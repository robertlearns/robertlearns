∑ Mathematical Foundations · Module 03

Fourier, Laplace & Transforms · Fourier- & Laplace-Transformation

This module contains the single most powerful idea in all of electrical engineering: every reasonable signal can be written as a sum of sinusoids, and sinusoids sail through linear systems unchanged except in size and timing. Once you own that idea, filters, amplifiers, radio, control loops and data links stop being separate subjects — they become one subject viewed at different frequencies.

Corresponds to: Höhere Mathematik III / Systemtheorie-Grundlagen (2nd–3rd semester) Prerequisites: Modules 01, 02 Study time: ≈ 6–8 h

1. The grand idea — why sinusoids rule everything

Start with an experiment you can do with a signal generator and an oscilloscope. Feed a sine wave into any linear, time-invariant system — an RC filter, a 50 km cable, an audio amplifier, a loudspeaker driven gently. What comes out is always another sine wave of exactly the same frequency. The system may make it smaller or larger (amplitude change) and may delay it (phase shift, Phasenverschiebung) — but it can never bend a sine into any other shape. Feed in a square wave, and the output is generally distorted; feed in a sinusoid, never.

Mathematically: the exponentials \(e^{j\omega t}\) from Module 01 are eigenfunctions (Eigenfunktionen) of every linear time-invariant (LTI, lineares zeitinvariantes System) system. Differentiation, integration, delay — everything an RLC circuit can do to a signal — merely multiplies \(e^{j\omega t}\) by a complex constant:

\[ \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}t}\, e^{j\omega t} = j\omega \, e^{j\omega t}, \qquad \int e^{j\omega t}\,\mathrm{d}t = \frac{1}{j\omega}\, e^{j\omega t}. \]
🔑Key idea: the divide-and-conquer strategy of EE

If (1) every signal is a sum of sinusoids, and (2) a linear system treats each sinusoid independently — multiplying it by a known complex factor — then analyzing any signal through any linear system takes three steps: decompose the input into sinusoids (Fourier), multiply each by the system's factor at that frequency, reassemble (inverse transform). Calculus becomes multiplication. This module builds the decompose/reassemble machinery; Modules 05–07 use it on circuits, and half of the rest of the degree runs on it.

💡Intuition: the prism and the graphic equalizer

A glass prism performs a physical Fourier transform: white light in, rainbow out — the "spectrum" is literal. A graphic equalizer is the whole strategy in hardware: it splits audio into frequency bands, lets you scale each band with a slider, and sums them back. Your cochlea does the same thing mechanically — you hear in the frequency domain (Frequenzbereich), which is why a chord sounds like three notes and not like a complicated wiggle.

⚠️ω versus f — keep the 2π where it belongs

The angular frequency \(\omega = 2\pi f\) (in rad/s) and the ordinary frequency \(f\) (in Hz) both appear constantly. Formulas in this module use \(\omega\); lab equipment and datasheets use \(f\). A dropped factor \(2\pi\) is the most common transform-exam error after sign mistakes. When a result feels off by "about 6", check the \(2\pi\).

2. Fourier series — periodic signals as harmonic sums

Joseph Fourier's claim (1807): every periodic function of period \(T\) — square waves, sawtooths, rectified sines, anything a lab generator makes — can be written as a DC value plus sinusoids at the fundamental frequency (Grundfrequenz) \(f_0 = 1/T\) and its integer multiples, the harmonics (Oberschwingungen, Harmonische):

\[ \boxed{\; f(t) = \frac{a_0}{2} + \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \Big[ a_k \cos(k\omega_0 t) + b_k \sin(k\omega_0 t) \Big] \;}, \qquad \omega_0 = \frac{2\pi}{T}. \]

The term \(a_0/2\) is simply the mean value (Gleichanteil, DC component). The claim only becomes useful because the coefficients can be computed, thanks to orthogonality: the average of \(\sin(k\omega_0 t)\sin(m\omega_0 t)\) over one period is zero unless \(k = m\). Multiplying \(f(t)\) by one particular sinusoid and averaging therefore "sieves out" exactly that harmonic's coefficient:

\[ a_k = \frac{2}{T}\int_{0}^{T} f(t)\cos(k\omega_0 t)\,\mathrm{d}t, \qquad b_k = \frac{2}{T}\int_{0}^{T} f(t)\sin(k\omega_0 t)\,\mathrm{d}t . \]

2.1 Symmetry shortcuts — read the coefficients off the waveform

Before integrating anything, look at the wave:

  • Even function \(f(-t)=f(t)\) (mirror-symmetric about \(t=0\)): only cosines survive, all \(b_k = 0\).
  • Odd function \(f(-t)=-f(t)\): only sines survive, all \(a_k = 0\).
  • Half-wave symmetry \(f(t+T/2) = -f(t)\) (second half = inverted first half): only odd harmonics \(k = 1, 3, 5, \dots\) appear. Square and triangle waves have this — which is why you will never find a 2nd harmonic in an ideal square wave.
Worked example The square wave, fully derived

Take the symmetric square wave (Rechteckschwingung) of amplitude \(A\): \(f(t) = +A\) for \(0 < t < T/2\) and \(-A\) for \(T/2 < t < T\). Find its Fourier series.

Step 1 — exploit symmetry: the wave is odd (choose \(t=0\) at a rising edge) → all \(a_k = 0\), including the DC term (the mean is obviously zero). It also has half-wave symmetry → only odd \(k\) can survive. We only need \(b_k\) for odd \(k\).
Step 2 — set up the integral: split at \(T/2\), where the sign flips: \[ b_k = \frac{2}{T}\left[ \int_0^{T/2} A \sin(k\omega_0 t)\,\mathrm{d}t + \int_{T/2}^{T} (-A) \sin(k\omega_0 t)\,\mathrm{d}t \right] = \frac{4A}{T} \int_0^{T/2} \sin(k\omega_0 t)\,\mathrm{d}t, \] where the second integral equals the first by the half-wave symmetry.
Step 3 — integrate: \[ b_k = \frac{4A}{T} \cdot \left[ -\frac{\cos(k\omega_0 t)}{k\omega_0} \right]_0^{T/2} = \frac{4A}{T\,k\omega_0}\big( 1 - \cos(k\pi) \big) = \frac{2A}{\pi k}\big(1 - (-1)^k\big), \] using \(\omega_0 T = 2\pi\) and \(\cos(k\pi) = (-1)^k\). The bracket is 0 for even \(k\) and 2 for odd \(k\) — exactly as the symmetry predicted.
Step 4 — write the series: \[ \boxed{\; f(t) = \frac{4A}{\pi}\left( \sin\omega_0 t + \frac{\sin 3\omega_0 t}{3} + \frac{\sin 5\omega_0 t}{5} + \cdots \right) \;} \]
Odd harmonics only, amplitudes falling as \(1/k\). Sanity check: the fundamental has amplitude \(4A/\pi \approx 1.27A\) — larger than the square wave itself! The higher harmonics must partly cancel it near the peaks; watch this happen in the widget below.

2.2 Convergence speed and the Gibbs phenomenon

How fast the coefficients decay is dictated by how smooth the signal is:

  • Square wave (jump discontinuities): \(b_k \propto 1/k\) — slow. You need many harmonics.
  • Sawtooth (jumps too): \(\propto 1/k\), all harmonics present.
  • Triangle wave (continuous, kinked derivative): \(\propto 1/k^2\) — already 9× smaller by the 3rd harmonic.

Rule of thumb: each extra degree of smoothness buys one extra power of \(1/k\). This has hard engineering consequences: sharp edges mean strong high-frequency content, which is why fast digital edges radiate interference (EMC, Module 21) and why switching converters need filters (Module 18).

At a jump, something curious happens. The partial sum overshoots by about 9 % of the jump height, and as \(N\) grows the spike gets narrower but never shorter: the Gibbs phenomenon (Gibbssches Phänomen). The series still converges at every point of continuity — the misbehaving spike just keeps retreating toward the jump. You will meet Gibbs again as "ringing" in filter design (Module 12) and image compression artifacts.

🚫Classic pitfall: "more terms will fix the overshoot"

No finite — or infinite — number of terms removes the Gibbs overshoot at a discontinuity; its peak value tends to ≈ 8.95 % of the jump, forever. Increasing \(N\) only squeezes it into a thinner sliver of time. Exams love this: convergence in energy (the error's power → 0) is not the same as uniform convergence of the peak value.

3. The complex form — one formula instead of two

Module 01 taught us \(\cos x = \tfrac12(e^{jx} + e^{-jx})\) and \(\sin x = \tfrac{1}{2j}(e^{jx} - e^{-jx})\). Substituting these into the real series merges the two coefficient families \(a_k, b_k\) into a single complex one:

\[ \boxed{\; f(t) = \sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty} \underline{c}_k \, e^{jk\omega_0 t}, \qquad \underline{c}_k = \frac{1}{T}\int_{0}^{T} f(t)\, e^{-jk\omega_0 t}\,\mathrm{d}t \;} \]

with the dictionary

\[ \underline{c}_0 = \frac{a_0}{2}, \qquad \underline{c}_k = \frac{a_k - j b_k}{2}, \qquad \underline{c}_{-k} = \underline{c}_k^{\,*} \ \ \text{(for real signals)}. \]

Three things to internalize:

  • The sum now runs over negative \(k\) too. Negative frequencies are not mystical: \(e^{-j\omega t}\) is just a phasor rotating clockwise, and a real cosine needs one of each, \(\cos\omega_0 t = \tfrac12 e^{j\omega_0 t} + \tfrac12 e^{-j\omega_0 t}\). The two-sided spectrum splits each real amplitude half-and-half onto \(\pm k\).
  • \(|\underline{c}_k|\) is the (half-)amplitude of harmonic \(k\), \(\arg \underline{c}_k\) its phase — magnitude and phase spectrum in one complex number, exactly like the phasors of Module 05.
  • Every calculation gets shorter: one integral, one exponential, and the algebra of \(e^{jx}\) instead of trigonometric identities. From here on we use the complex form.
📜Fourier's scandalous claim

When Fourier presented his heat-conduction paper in 1807, the committee — including Lagrange and Laplace — refused publication: the claim that arbitrary functions, even ones with corners, equal sums of smooth sinusoids seemed absurd. Making it rigorous took mathematics a century (Dirichlet's conditions, 1829; Carleson's theorem, 1966!) and en route created much of modern analysis. Engineers, characteristically, used it the whole time. Every audio codec, DSL modem and MRI scanner is downstream of that rejected paper.

4. From series to transform — letting the period go to infinity

Series handle periodic signals. But a single voice burst, a radar echo, one logic pulse — real signals often never repeat. The trick: view a single pulse as a periodic signal whose period \(T\) has been stretched to infinity. Watch what happens as \(T\) grows:

  • The line spacing \(f_0 = 1/T\) shrinks — the spectral lines crowd together;
  • each individual \(\underline{c}_k \propto 1/T\) shrinks toward zero,
  • but the envelope shape of \(T \cdot \underline{c}_k\) stays put.

In the limit, the sum over discrete lines becomes an integral over a continuous frequency variable, and \(T\underline{c}_k\) becomes a function \(F(j\omega)\) — the Fourier transform (Fourier-Transformation) and its inverse (Rücktransformation):

\[ \boxed{\; F(j\omega) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(t)\, e^{-j\omega t}\, \mathrm{d}t \;} \qquad\qquad f(t) = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} F(j\omega)\, e^{j\omega t}\, \mathrm{d}\omega. \]

Read the pair as a conversation: the forward transform asks "how much of frequency \(\omega\) is in \(f(t)\)?" (by correlating with \(e^{-j\omega t}\)); the inverse reassembles \(f(t)\) from all its frequency components. \(|F(j\omega)|\) is the amplitude spectrum (Amplitudenspektrum) — note its unit: if \(f\) is a voltage in V, then \(F\) is in V·s = V/Hz, a spectral density. A single pulse spreads finite energy over a continuum of frequencies, so each individual frequency carries an infinitesimal amount; only frequency bands carry finite energy.

5. The Dirac impulse and the transform pairs that matter

5.1 The Dirac delta — the idealized bang

Circuit theory constantly needs the idealized "infinitely short, infinitely strong kick": the hammer tap, the lightning strike, the switch-closing spark. Model it as a rectangular pulse of width \(\varepsilon\) and height \(1/\varepsilon\) (unit area), then let \(\varepsilon \to 0\). The limit is the Dirac impulse (Dirac-Stoß, Deltafunktion) \(\delta(t)\) — not a function in the classical sense (it is a distribution), defined entirely by what it does inside integrals:

\[ \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \delta(t)\,\mathrm{d}t = 1, \qquad \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(t)\,\delta(t - t_0)\,\mathrm{d}t = f(t_0) \quad \text{(sifting property)}. \]

The second identity is the sifting property (Ausblendeigenschaft): integrating against a shifted delta simply samples the function at the impulse instant. It makes the delta's transform trivial — the integral \(\int \delta(t)\,e^{-j\omega t}\,\mathrm{d}t\) just evaluates the exponential at \(t = 0\):

\[ \delta(t) \;\circ\!\!-\!\!\bullet\; 1 \qquad \text{— an impulse contains all frequencies, equally.} \]

This is why you test a linear system by hitting it with an impulse: the response (the Impulsantwort \(h(t)\), Module 12) probes every frequency at once. It is also why a spark plug jams AM radio across the whole band. The delta is furthermore the derivative of the unit step (Einheitssprung) \(\sigma(t)\) — the switch-on function that is 0 for \(t<0\) and 1 for \(t>0\).

5.2 rect ↔ sinc — the most important pair in communications

Worked example Transform of a rectangular pulse

Compute \(F(j\omega)\) of a pulse of amplitude \(A\) and duration \(T\), centered at \(t = 0\): \(f(t) = A\) for \(|t| < T/2\), else 0. Then evaluate: what bandwidth does a 1 µs logic pulse occupy?

Step 1 — integrate (the integrand is nonzero only on the pulse): \[ F(j\omega) = \int_{-T/2}^{T/2} A\, e^{-j\omega t}\,\mathrm{d}t = A\,\frac{e^{-j\omega t}}{-j\omega}\Bigg|_{-T/2}^{T/2} = A\,\frac{e^{j\omega T/2} - e^{-j\omega T/2}}{j\omega} . \]
Step 2 — recognize Euler (Module 01): the numerator is \(2j\sin(\omega T/2)\), so \[ F(j\omega) = A\,\frac{2\sin(\omega T/2)}{\omega} = A\,T\,\operatorname{si}\!\left(\frac{\omega T}{2}\right), \qquad \operatorname{si}(x) := \frac{\sin x}{x}, \] the "sinc" function (Spaltfunktion).
Step 3 — read the shape: a central hump of height \(AT\) (= pulse area) with first nulls where \(\omega T/2 = \pi\), i.e. at \( f_{\text{null}} = 1/T \), then decaying side lobes. Almost all energy (≈ 90 %) sits in the main lobe \(|f| < 1/T\).
Step 4 — plug in numbers: \(T = 1\,\mathrm{\mu s} \Rightarrow f_{\text{null}} = 1\,\mathrm{MHz}\). A 1 µs pulse "occupies" roughly 1 MHz. A 1 ns pulse: 1 GHz.
\( \mathrm{rect} \;\circ\!\!-\!\!\bullet\; \mathrm{si} \), with first-null bandwidth \(B = 1/T\). The moral: bandwidth × duration ≈ 1. Fast pulses are expensive in spectrum — the fundamental cost law of digital communications.
🧪Interactive — pulse-width ↔ bandwidth duality simulation

The spectrum \(X(f) = T\operatorname{si}(\pi f T)\) of a 1 V rectangular pulse of duration \(T\). Shrink \(T\): the spectrum gets wider and flatter (approaching the delta's flat spectrum). Stretch \(T\): the spectrum squeezes toward a narrow spike at 0 Hz (approaching DC). The product of duration and first-null bandwidth stays exactly 1 — no slider position can beat it.

5.3 Duality, the Gaussian, and the uncertainty principle

The essential pairs, worth memorizing:

Notice the pattern running down the table: whatever is narrow on one side is wide on the other. This time–frequency duality (Zeit-Frequenz-Dualität) is not an accident of these examples but a theorem. Scaling time by \(a\) scales frequency by \(1/a\):

\[ f(at) \;\circ\!\!-\!\!\bullet\; \frac{1}{|a|}\,F\!\left(\frac{j\omega}{a}\right). \]

Its sharpest form is the uncertainty / bandwidth–duration principle (Zeitdauer-Bandbreite-Produkt): for suitable width definitions, \(\Delta t \cdot \Delta f \gtrsim \tfrac{1}{4\pi}\), with the Gaussian as the unique optimum. No signal is simultaneously short and narrow-band. Consequences everywhere: a radar that must resolve targets 15 m apart needs 0.1 µs pulses and therefore ≥ 10 MHz of spectrum; a piano's lowest notes take longer to "identify" by ear; and in quantum mechanics the very same mathematics, applied to position/momentum, is Heisenberg's principle.

5.4 Parseval — power is the same in both domains

Decomposing a signal does not change its energy, only its bookkeeping. For a periodic signal, the mean power is the sum of the harmonics' powers (each sinusoid of amplitude \(\hat{u}\) contributes \(\hat{u}^2/2\), cf. RMS in Module 05):

\[ P = \frac{1}{T}\int_0^T f^2(t)\,\mathrm{d}t = \left(\frac{a_0}{2}\right)^{\!2} + \frac{1}{2}\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\big(a_k^2 + b_k^2\big) = \sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty} |\underline{c}_k|^2 , \]

and for pulses the energy version reads \(\int f^2 \mathrm{d}t = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int |F(j\omega)|^2 \mathrm{d}\omega\) (Parsevalsche Gleichung). Practical use: chop off harmonics and you know exactly how much power you lost — exercise E7 shows the square wave's fundamental alone carries 81 % of the total.

6. The Laplace transform — Fourier's rugged sibling

Fourier is perfect for steady-state signals, but engineering is full of switch-on events: close a relay at \(t = 0\), and what happens? Two problems appear. First, signals like the unit step \(\sigma(t)\) or a growing exponential do not decay, so the Fourier integral \(\int f(t) e^{-j\omega t}\mathrm{d}t\) does not converge in the ordinary sense. Second, Fourier has no natural slot for initial conditions — the capacitor charge already present when the switch closes.

The fix is beautifully pragmatic: multiply the signal by a decaying exponential \(e^{-\sigma t}\) first, so the integral converges, and start the clock at \(t = 0\) since switch-on problems have no prehistory (or rather: the prehistory is compressed into initial conditions). Combining \(e^{-\sigma t}\,e^{-j\omega t} = e^{-(\sigma + j\omega)t} = e^{-st}\) with the complex frequency (komplexe Frequenz) \(s = \sigma + j\omega\) gives the (one-sided) Laplace transform (Laplace-Transformation):

\[ \boxed{\; F(s) = \mathcal{L}\{f(t)\} = \int_{0^-}^{\infty} f(t)\, e^{-st}\, \mathrm{d}t \;}, \qquad s = \sigma + j\omega. \]

The lower limit \(0^-\) (just before zero) makes sure an impulse at \(t=0\) is caught. The variable \(s\) has units 1/s — a "frequency" with a real part: \(e^{st} = e^{\sigma t}(\cos\omega t + j\sin\omega t)\) describes growing or decaying oscillations, not just steady ones. That extra dimension is exactly what transient analysis needs.

6.1 Region of convergence — lightly

The integral converges only if \(e^{-\sigma t}\) out-decays the signal: for \(f(t) = e^{2t}\) you need \(\sigma > 2\). Each transform therefore comes with a region of convergence (Konvergenzbereich) \(\operatorname{Re}(s) > \sigma_0\), a right half-plane. For the one-sided transform of the signals in this course, you can safely leave the ROC implicit — the formulas below "just work" — but remember it exists: it is what decides whether the Fourier transform can be recovered from \(F(s)\) (Section 10).

💡Intuition: what Laplace buys you

Fourier asks "which steady sinusoids make up this signal?" Laplace asks the more general question "which growing/decaying sinusoids \(e^{\sigma t}\cos\omega t\) make it up?" — and those happen to be precisely the shapes that RLC circuits produce naturally when disturbed (Module 02's homogeneous solutions!). Laplace speaks the native language of transients. The payoff is the derivative rule below: differential equations turn into polynomial algebra, with initial conditions built in for free.

🔑Key idea: the derivative rule

\(\mathcal{L}\{f'\} = sF(s) - f(0^-)\): differentiation in time becomes multiplication by \(s\), and the initial condition drops in automatically as a bonus term. Apply it twice for \(f''\): \(s^2F(s) - s f(0^-) - f'(0^-)\). Every linear ODE with constant coefficients — i.e. every LTI circuit — thus becomes a polynomial equation in \(s\). Solve it with school algebra, transform back with the table. Module 02's guess-the-ansatz method is hereby retired for circuits.

Worked example The derivative rule at work: \(\mathcal{L}\{\cos\}\) from \(\mathcal{L}\{\sin\}\)

Given \(\mathcal{L}\{\sin\omega_0 t\} = \omega_0/(s^2+\omega_0^2)\), derive the cosine transform without integrating.

Step 1 — relate the functions: \( \cos\omega_0 t = \frac{1}{\omega_0}\,\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}t}\sin\omega_0 t \).
Step 2 — apply the derivative rule with \(f(t) = \sin\omega_0 t\), \(f(0^-) = 0\): \[ \mathcal{L}\{\cos\omega_0 t\} = \frac{1}{\omega_0}\Big[ s \cdot \frac{\omega_0}{s^2+\omega_0^2} - 0 \Big] = \frac{s}{s^2+\omega_0^2}. \]
Matches the table — no integral computed. This is the everyday style of s-domain work: combine a few known pairs with the rules instead of ever touching \(\int_0^\infty\) again.

8. Solving an ODE end-to-end: the RC step response, again

In Module 02 we solved the RC charging circuit with the classical ansatz method. Same circuit, new machinery — watch how the whole procedure becomes mechanical.

Worked example RC step response via Laplace, end to end

KVL around the loop with \(i = C\,u_C'\) gives the ODE from Module 02: \( RC\,u_C'(t) + u_C(t) = U_0\,\sigma(t) \), with \(u_C(0^-) = 0\). Solve it by Laplace. Numbers: \(U_0 = 10\) V, \(R = 1\) kΩ, \(C = 1\) µF.

Step 1 — transform the whole equation (derivative rule on \(u_C'\), table on \(\sigma\)): \[ RC\big[s\,U_C(s) - \underbrace{u_C(0^-)}_{=0}\big] + U_C(s) = \frac{U_0}{s}. \]
Step 2 — solve algebraically (this is the entire "solving" step!): \[ U_C(s)\,(1 + sRC) = \frac{U_0}{s} \quad\Rightarrow\quad U_C(s) = \frac{U_0}{s\,(1 + sRC)} = \frac{U_0/RC}{s\,(s + 1/RC)}. \]
Step 3 — partial fractions (Partialbruchzerlegung): with \(a = 1/RC\), \[ \frac{a}{s(s+a)} = \frac{1}{s} - \frac{1}{s+a} \quad\text{(check: } \tfrac{(s+a) - s}{s(s+a)} = \tfrac{a}{s(s+a)} \checkmark\text{)}. \]
Step 4 — transform back with the table (\(1/s \to \sigma(t)\), \(1/(s+a) \to e^{-at}\)): \[ \boxed{\; u_C(t) = U_0\left(1 - e^{-t/RC}\right), \quad t \ge 0. \;} \]
Step 5 — numbers & sanity: \(\tau = RC = 1\,\mathrm{ms}\). At \(t = 1\) ms: \(u_C = 10(1 - e^{-1}) \approx 6.32\) V; at \(5\tau\): ≈ 9.93 V, effectively charged. Identical to Module 02's result — but note what we did not do: no homogeneous/particular split, no ansatz, no fitting constants to initial conditions afterwards. The initial condition entered in Step 1 and everything else was algebra.
\(u_C(t) = U_0(1 - e^{-t/RC})\) — same physics, one-third of the effort, and the method scales to circuits where guessing fails.

9. Transfer functions and the s-plane — a preview with teeth

Redo Step 2 above with a general input \(U_{in}(s)\) instead of \(U_0/s\) (and zero initial conditions). You get \(U_C(s) = H(s)\, U_{in}(s)\) with

\[ H(s) = \frac{U_C(s)}{U_{in}(s)} = \frac{1}{1 + sRC}. \]

This ratio — output over input in the s-domain — is the transfer function (Übertragungsfunktion), the single most used object in circuits, filters and control engineering (Modules 06, 12, 14). It is a property of the system alone, independent of the input. For any LTI circuit, \(H(s)\) is a ratio of polynomials in \(s\); the roots of the denominator are the poles (Polstellen). Our RC circuit has one pole, at

\[ s_p = -\frac{1}{RC} = -\frac{1}{\tau}. \]

Here is the punchline: partial fractions decompose every response into terms \(k_i/(s - s_{p,i})\), and each such term transforms back to \(k_i\,e^{s_{p,i} t}\). So the pole positions in the complex s-plane completely determine the shape of the natural time response:

  • Pole on the negative real axis, \(s_p = \sigma < 0\): pure decay \(e^{\sigma t}\), time constant \(\tau = 1/|\sigma|\). Farther left = faster.
  • Complex pair \(s_p = \sigma \pm j\omega\), \(\sigma < 0\): damped oscillation \(e^{\sigma t}\cos(\omega t + \varphi)\) — decay rate from the real part, ringing frequency from the imaginary part.
  • Poles on the \(j\omega\) axis (\(\sigma = 0\)): sustained oscillation (or constant, at the origin) — the marginal case.
  • Any pole with \(\sigma > 0\): the response grows exponentially — the system is unstable (instabil). A microphone screeching next to its loudspeaker is a pole that wandered into the right half-plane.

Place a pole (pair) at \(s = \sigma \pm j\omega\) and see the time response \(e^{\sigma t}\cos(\omega t)\) it generates. Try: \(\sigma\) far left (fast decay), \(\sigma = 0\) (pure oscillation — the marginal case), \(\sigma > 0\) (runaway). With \(\omega = 0\) you get the pure exponentials of Module 02; the dashed envelope \(\pm e^{\sigma t}\) is the "decay budget" set by the real part alone.

10. One family: Fourier ↔ Laplace ↔ z

Compare the two kernels: Fourier uses \(e^{-j\omega t}\), Laplace uses \(e^{-st}\) with \(s = \sigma + j\omega\). So the Fourier transform is the Laplace transform evaluated on the imaginary axis:

\[ F(j\omega) = F(s)\big|_{s = j\omega}, \qquad \text{valid when the ROC includes the } j\omega \text{ axis} \]

— i.e. when all poles lie strictly in the left half-plane, which for signals means \(f(t)\) is absolutely integrable (it decays). Conversely, Laplace is the Fourier transform of the pre-damped signal \(f(t)e^{-\sigma t}\). The two are one tool with two lenses: Laplace sees the whole s-plane and excels at transients and stability; Fourier lives on the \(j\omega\) axis and excels at spectra and steady state. That is why the frequency response of a circuit is simply \(H(j\omega) = H(s)|_{s = j\omega}\) — the Bode plots of Module 06 are a walk up the imaginary axis of Fig. 9.1.

One sibling remains. Digital systems (Module 13) know the signal only at sample instants \(t = nT_s\). Replacing the integral by a sum over samples and substituting \(z = e^{sT_s}\) yields the z-transform (z-Transformation) \(X(z) = \sum_n x[n]\,z^{-n}\): the Laplace transform's discrete-time twin. Everything transfers: the derivative rule becomes a delay rule (\(z^{-1}\) = one sample delay), transfer functions become ratios of polynomials in \(z^{-1}\), and the stable left half-plane maps to the inside of the unit circle \(|z| = 1\). If you understand this module, Module 13's DSP mathematics will feel like a change of notation rather than a new subject.

12. Exercises

Do them on paper first — transform tables allowed, that is how the pros work too.

E1A 50 Hz square wave switches between ±325 V (roughly the peak of the 230 V mains). Give the amplitude and frequency of its fundamental and of its 3rd harmonic. Which is the lowest harmonic above 1 kHz? easy

Show solution
From the square-wave series \(b_k = \frac{4A}{\pi k}\) (odd \(k\)): fundamental \(b_1 = \frac{4 \cdot 325}{\pi} \approx 414\,\mathrm{V}\) at 50 Hz; third harmonic \(b_3 = 414/3 \approx 138\,\mathrm{V}\) at 150 Hz. Harmonics sit at odd multiples of 50 Hz: 50, 150, 250, … The lowest one above 1 kHz is \(k = 21\), i.e. 1050 Hz with amplitude \(414/21 \approx 19.7\,\mathrm{V}\). Real-world remark: this is why cheap "modified sine" inverters (which output near-square waves) make transformers and motors hum audibly — those harmonics are big.

E2Using only the table and linearity, find \(F(s)\) for \(f(t) = 5 - 3e^{-2t} + 2\sin 4t\) (for \(t \ge 0\)). Where are the poles? easy

Show solution
Term by term: \( \mathcal{L}\{5\} = \frac{5}{s} \), \( \mathcal{L}\{3e^{-2t}\} = \frac{3}{s+2} \), \( \mathcal{L}\{2\sin 4t\} = \frac{2 \cdot 4}{s^2 + 16} \). So \[ F(s) = \frac{5}{s} - \frac{3}{s+2} + \frac{8}{s^2+16}. \] Poles: \(s = 0\) (the constant), \(s = -2\) (the decay), \(s = \pm j4\) (the oscillation) — each time-domain behavior announces itself as a pole location. Sanity check via the pole map of Fig. 9.1: constant ↔ origin, decay ↔ negative real axis, sustained sine ↔ \(j\omega\) axis. ✓

E3Transform back: \(F(s) = \dfrac{10}{s(s+5)}\). What is \(f(t)\), its initial value and its final value? medium

Show solution
Partial fractions: \( \frac{10}{s(s+5)} = \frac{A}{s} + \frac{B}{s+5} \) with \(A = \frac{10}{5} = 2\) (cover-up at \(s=0\)) and \(B = \frac{10}{-5} = -2\) (cover-up at \(s=-5\)). Hence \[ f(t) = 2\left(1 - e^{-5t}\right). \] Initial value \(f(0) = 0\), final value \(f(\infty) = 2\), time constant \(\tau = 1/5 = 0.2\,\mathrm{s}\). This is exactly the shape of the RC step response — unsurprising, since the s-domain expression \( \frac{\text{const}}{s(s+a)} \) is "step through a one-pole system". Recognizing such fingerprints is the real skill.

E4A radar must resolve targets 300 m apart, requiring pulses of at most \(T = 2\,\mathrm{\mu s}\). What first-null bandwidth do these pulses occupy? What happens to the bandwidth if resolution is improved to 30 m? medium

Show solution
From rect ↔ sinc: \(B = 1/T = 1/(2\,\mathrm{\mu s}) = 500\,\mathrm{kHz}\). For 30 m resolution the pulse must shrink to 0.2 µs, so \(B = 5\,\mathrm{MHz}\) — 10× the resolution costs 10× the spectrum, with no way around it (bandwidth–duration principle). This is why radio spectrum is auctioned for billions: resolution and data rate are literally made of bandwidth. (Modern radars cheat elegantly with long coded pulses — pulse compression — but the time-bandwidth law still holds for the code.)

E5An RL circuit (\(R = 10\,\Omega\), \(L = 50\,\mathrm{mH}\)) with zero initial current is switched onto \(U_0 = 20\,\mathrm{V}\) at \(t=0\): \(L\,i' + R\,i = U_0\,\sigma(t)\). Solve for \(i(t)\) via Laplace. medium

Show solution
Transform (derivative rule, \(i(0^-)=0\)): \( L s I(s) + R I(s) = \frac{U_0}{s} \) → \( I(s) = \frac{U_0}{s(R + sL)} = \frac{U_0/L}{s(s + R/L)} \). Partial fractions with \(a = R/L = 200\,\mathrm{s^{-1}}\): \( I(s) = \frac{U_0}{R}\left(\frac1s - \frac{1}{s+a}\right) \), hence \[ i(t) = \frac{U_0}{R}\left(1 - e^{-t R/L}\right) = 2\,\mathrm{A}\left(1 - e^{-t/5\,\mathrm{ms}}\right). \] Sanity: at \(t \to \infty\) the inductor is a short, so \(i = 20/10 = 2\) A ✓; \(\tau = L/R = 5\) ms; initial slope \(U_0/L = 400\) A/s (the inductor takes the full voltage first — dual of the capacitor).

E6Derive the Fourier series of the triangle wave by integrating the square-wave series term by term (integration is allowed inside a convergent Fourier series). Use it to prove \(1 + \frac{1}{9} + \frac{1}{25} + \cdots = \frac{\pi^2}{8}\). challenge

Show solution
The integral of a ±\(A\) square wave is a triangle wave of peak \(\hat{T} = A\,T/4\) (it rises at slope \(A\) for a quarter period from its minimum… choose the odd square wave; its integral is an even triangle). Integrating the series term by term: \[ \int \frac{4A}{\pi} \sum_{k\,\mathrm{odd}} \frac{\sin k\omega_0 t}{k}\,\mathrm{d}t = -\frac{4A}{\pi \omega_0} \sum_{k\,\mathrm{odd}} \frac{\cos k\omega_0 t}{k^2} + C. \] Each \(1/k\) became \(1/k^2\): integration smooths, and smoother signals have faster-decaying coefficients — the convergence rule of Section 2.2 in action. Normalizing to a unit-amplitude triangle (peak 1) gives \[ f_\triangle(t) = \frac{8}{\pi^2} \left( \cos\omega_0 t + \frac{\cos 3\omega_0 t}{9} + \frac{\cos 5\omega_0 t}{25} + \cdots \right)\!, \] (up to sign/phase conventions). Now evaluate at the peak \(t = 0\), where \(f_\triangle = 1\) and every cosine equals 1: \[ 1 = \frac{8}{\pi^2} \sum_{k\,\mathrm{odd}} \frac{1}{k^2} \;\Rightarrow\; \sum_{k\,\mathrm{odd}} \frac{1}{k^2} = \frac{\pi^2}{8}. \] A Fourier series just summed an infinite numerical series for you — Euler's Basel-problem trick in circuit-lecture clothing. (The full Basel sum over all \(k\) is \(\pi^2/6\); the odd terms carry \(3/4\) of it: \(\frac{3}{4} \cdot \frac{\pi^2}{6} = \frac{\pi^2}{8}\) ✓.)

E7Using Parseval, what fraction of a square wave's power is carried by its fundamental alone? By the first three nonzero harmonics (\(k = 1, 3, 5\))? challenge

Show solution
Total power of a ±\(A\) square wave directly in the time domain: \(P = \frac1T \int_0^T A^2\,\mathrm{d}t = A^2\) (the value is always ±\(A\), so the square is always \(A^2\)). Each harmonic contributes \(b_k^2/2\): \[ \frac{P_1}{P} = \frac{(4A/\pi)^2 / 2}{A^2} = \frac{8}{\pi^2} \approx 81.1\,\%. \] Adding \(k = 3\): \(+\frac{8}{9\pi^2} \approx 9.0\,\%\) → 90.1 %. Adding \(k = 5\): \(+\frac{8}{25\pi^2} \approx 3.2\,\%\) → 93.3 %. Bonus check: Parseval demands \(\frac{8}{\pi^2}\sum_{k\,\mathrm{odd}} \frac{1}{k^2} = 1\), which is exactly the \(\pi^2/8\) identity from E6 — the two exercises verify each other. Practical reading: a filter passing only the fundamental keeps 81 % of the power; that is how a resonant tank turns a square-wave drive into a near-sine (Module 18, resonant converters).
