Signals & Systems · Signale & Systeme

This is the module where electrical engineering acquires its universal language. A microphone voltage, a share price, an ECG trace, a radar echo — all are signals; an amplifier, a filter, a car suspension, a national economy — all are systems. One compact theory — impulse response, convolution, poles, Bode plots, sampling — describes them all, and every later module (filters, control, communications, DSP) speaks this language fluently.

Corresponds to: Signale und Systeme (3rd–4th semester) Prerequisites: Modules 03, 05 Study time: ≈ 7–9 h

1. Signals — the raw material

A signal (Signal) is a function that carries information: a physical quantity varying over time (or space, or both). Mathematically we simply write \(x(t)\). A system (System) is anything that transforms one signal into another: \(y(t) = T\{x(t)\}\). Signals are functions; systems are functions of functions. Before analyzing systems we need a taxonomy of their inputs.

1.1 The five classification axes

A CD stream is both; a sampled-but-not-yet-quantized voltage in an ADC front end is discrete-time but still analog in amplitude. This module works mostly with continuous-time deterministic signals; Module 13 goes discrete, and random signals get their own treatment in stochastic signal theory.

1.2 Energy signals vs. power signals

Borrowing from circuits: if \(x(t)\) were a voltage across a 1 Ω resistor, the instantaneous power would be \(x^2(t)\). We define the (normalized) signal energy and signal power:

\[ E = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} |x(t)|^2 \,\mathrm{d}t, \qquad P = \lim_{T\to\infty} \frac{1}{2T}\int_{-T}^{T} |x(t)|^2 \,\mathrm{d}t . \]
  • Energy signal (Energiesignal): \(0 < E < \infty\). Then necessarily \(P = 0\) — finite energy smeared over infinite time. Every finite-duration pulse, every decaying transient.
  • Power signal (Leistungssignal): \(0 < P < \infty\) (so \(E = \infty\)). Every periodic signal: for \(x(t) = \hat{x}\sin(\omega t)\), \(P = \hat{x}^2/2\) — precisely the RMS-squared from Module 05.

The classes are mutually exclusive, and some signals (e.g. the ramp \(t\cdot u(t)\)) are neither. The distinction matters later: energy signals get Fourier transforms, power signals get Fourier series or power spectral densities.

💡Intuition: rain shower vs. climate

An energy signal is a rain shower: you can meaningfully ask "how much water fell in total?" (finite energy), but the long-run average rate is zero. A power signal is the climate: total rainfall since forever is infinite and meaningless, but the average rate — millimeters per year — is finite and exactly the useful number. Ask each signal the question it can answer.

2. The elementary signals — a small cast of characters

A handful of standard signals act as building blocks and as test inputs for systems. Learn their names, shapes, and relationships.

2.1 Step, ramp, rectangular pulse

The unit step (Einheitssprung, Sprungfunktion) models switching something on at \(t=0\):

\[ u(t) = \begin{cases} 0, & t < 0 \\ 1, & t > 0 \end{cases} \]

(the value at exactly \(t=0\) is a convention; \(\tfrac12\) is common and never matters in practice). The unit ramp \(r(t) = t\,u(t)\) is its integral — it models quantities that grow linearly after switch-on. The rectangular pulse (Rechteckimpuls) of width \(T\),

\[ \operatorname{rect}\!\left(\tfrac{t}{T}\right) = u\!\left(t+\tfrac{T}{2}\right) - u\!\left(t-\tfrac{T}{2}\right), \]

models anything of finite duration: a camera flash, one bit on a wire, a gate window. Steps build pulses; pulses build everything else — as we are about to see.

2.2 The Dirac impulse — handle with care

Take a rectangular pulse of width \(\varepsilon\) and height \(1/\varepsilon\): its area is always exactly 1. Now let \(\varepsilon \to 0\). The result — infinitely short, infinitely tall, area 1 — is the Dirac impulse (Dirac-Stoß, Deltafunktion):

\[ \delta(t) = \lim_{\varepsilon\to 0}\, \frac{1}{\varepsilon}\operatorname{rect}\!\left(\frac{t}{\varepsilon}\right), \qquad \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \delta(t)\,\mathrm{d}t = 1 . \]

Strictly, \(\delta\) is not a function but a distribution (recall the careful treatment in Module 03): it is only defined by what it does inside integrals. And what it does there is the single most-used identity of this module, the sifting property (Ausblendeigenschaft):

\[ \boxed{\;\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x(t)\,\delta(t - t_0)\,\mathrm{d}t = x(t_0)\;} \]

The impulse "sifts out" the value of \(x\) at the instant where it sits. Why it must be true: the shrinking rectangle at \(t_0\) samples \(x\) over an ever-narrower window in which \(x\) is essentially constant at \(x(t_0)\); height \(1/\varepsilon\) times width \(\varepsilon\) leaves exactly that value. Two more facts to keep close:

\[ \delta(t) = \frac{\mathrm{d}u(t)}{\mathrm{d}t}, \qquad u(t) = \int_{-\infty}^{t} \delta(\tau)\,\mathrm{d}\tau, \qquad \delta(at) = \frac{1}{|a|}\,\delta(t). \]
⚠️The impulse has units — and other fine print

If \(t\) is in seconds, \(\delta(t)\) has units \(1/\mathrm{s}\) (its integral over time must be dimensionless). A current impulse \(Q\,\delta(t)\) in amperes therefore carries the charge \(Q\) — an idealized instantaneous charge dump. Also: expressions like \(\delta^2(t)\) are mathematically meaningless, and a "value" \(\delta(0)=\infty\) should never appear in your calculations. If your \(\delta\) is not under an integral (or about to be), be suspicious.

2.3 The complex exponential — one signal to rule them all

The most important signal in this entire theory is

\[ x(t) = e^{st}, \qquad s = \sigma + j\omega \in \mathbb{C}. \]

By Euler's formula (Module 03), \(e^{st} = e^{\sigma t}\left(\cos\omega t + j\sin\omega t\right)\) — an oscillation at \(\omega\) inside an exponential envelope \(e^{\sigma t}\). One two-parameter family covers every signal we care about:

Choice of \(s\)Signal
\(s = 0\)DC (constant)
\(s = \sigma\) real, \(\sigma<0\)decaying exponential (every RC discharge)
\(s = j\omega\) purely imaginarysteady sinusoid (all of Module 05!)
\(s = \sigma + j\omega,\ \sigma<0\)damped oscillation (every ringing circuit, Module 06)
\(s = \sigma + j\omega,\ \sigma>0\)growing oscillation (every unstable feedback loop)

Why this family and not, say, polynomials? Because \(e^{st}\) is the eigensignal of every linear time-invariant system: send it through, and it comes out as itself, merely scaled by a complex constant. Section 6 proves this in three lines, and the entire frequency-domain worldview — phasors, transfer functions, Bode plots — is the payoff. The parameter \(s\) even gets its own home, the s-plane, which we will map thoroughly in Section 7.

3. Systems and their properties

A system \(y = T\{x\}\) can be anything from two resistors to a jet engine. What makes system theory possible is that a few structural properties — not the physics — determine which mathematical tools apply. For each property you need three things: the definition, a test recipe, and a feel for examples and counterexamples.

3.1 Linearity — Linearität

A system is linear if superposition holds: for all signals and constants,

\[ T\{a\,x_1 + b\,x_2\} = a\,T\{x_1\} + b\,T\{x_2\}. \]

Test recipe: feed \(a x_1 + b x_2\) through the system definition and expand; separately compute \(a y_1 + b y_2\); compare the two expressions term by term.

Example: the differentiator \(y = \dot{x}\) — the derivative of a sum is the sum of derivatives. ✓ Also \(y(t) = t\,x(t)\): time-varying gain, but still linear in \(x\). ✓
Counterexample: the squarer \(y = x^2\): \((x_1 + x_2)^2 = x_1^2 + 2x_1x_2 + x_2^2 \ne x_1^2 + x_2^2\). ✗ Subtler: \(y = x + 1\) — it looks harmless, but scaling fails: input \(2x\) gives \(2x+1\), not \(2(x+1)\). Affine is not linear (zero in must give zero out!).

3.2 Time-invariance — Zeitinvarianz

A system is time-invariant if its behavior does not depend on when the experiment is run: delaying the input by \(t_0\) delays the output by \(t_0\) and changes nothing else,

\[ T\{x(t - t_0)\} = y(t - t_0) \quad \text{for all } t_0. \]

Test recipe: compute (a) the response to the pre-shifted input \(x(t-t_0)\), and (b) the shifted output \(y(t-t_0)\) by replacing every \(t\) in the output formula. Equal for all \(t_0\) → time-invariant. Watch for any explicit \(t\) in the system law — that is the usual culprit.

Example: \(y = \dot{x}\), \(y(t) = x(t-3)\), any RC network with fixed components. ✓
Counterexample: \(y(t) = t\,x(t)\): response to \(x(t-t_0)\) is \(t\,x(t-t_0)\), but \(y(t-t_0) = (t-t_0)\,x(t-t_0)\). ✗ (A gain knob someone is turning during the measurement.) Also \(y(t) = x(2t)\): time-scaling squeezes any shift. ✗

3.3 Causality — Kausalität

A system is causal if the output at time \(t\) depends only on the input at times \(\le t\). No physical real-time system can be non-causal — effects cannot precede causes.

Test recipe: ask "to compute \(y(t_1)\), do I need \(x\) at any \(\tau > t_1\)?" For LTI systems there is a one-look criterion coming in Section 4: \(h(t) = 0\) for all \(t < 0\).

Counterexample: \(y(t) = x(t+1)\) (a predictor), or the centered moving average \(y(t) = \tfrac{1}{2T}\int_{t-T}^{t+T} x(\tau)\mathrm{d}\tau\). Non-causal systems are not useless — offline processing (an audio file, a stored image) has the "future" already on disk, and image filters are routinely non-causal in the spatial coordinates.

3.4 Memory — Gedächtnis

A system is memoryless (gedächtnislos) if \(y(t)\) depends only on \(x(t)\) at the very same instant: \(y(t) = f\big(x(t)\big)\). A resistor is memoryless (\(u = Ri\), now). A capacitor is not: \(u_C = \tfrac1C \int_{-\infty}^{t} i\,\mathrm{d}\tau\) — its voltage remembers the entire current history. Memory is not a defect; it is exactly what makes filters, integrators and dynamics possible. Memoryless + linear + time-invariant leaves only \(y = k\,x\): a plain gain.

3.5 BIBO stability — Stabilität

A system is BIBO stable (bounded input → bounded output) if every bounded input \(|x(t)| \le M_x < \infty\) produces a bounded output. Test recipe: to prove instability, one witness suffices — find a single bounded input that makes the output run away. To prove stability of an LTI system, check the absolute integrability of the impulse response (Section 4):

\[ \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} |h(t)|\,\mathrm{d}t < \infty \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad \text{BIBO stable.} \]

Counterexample: the ideal integrator \(y(t)=\int_{-\infty}^t x\,\mathrm{d}\tau\). Feed it the humble bounded step \(u(t)\): out comes the ramp \(t\,u(t)\) — unbounded. ✗ This is why "just integrate" controllers must be handled with care in Module 14.

🔑Key idea: LTI is the sweet spot

Linear + time-invariant (LTI, LZI-System) is the combination that unlocks everything in the next five sections: complete characterization by one function \(h(t)\), convolution, transfer functions, Bode plots. Real devices are neither perfectly linear nor eternally time-invariant — but around an operating point, over the time scales we care about, LTI is an astonishingly accurate and utterly indispensable approximation. Learn the LTI theory deeply; treat everything else as a perturbation of it.

4. LTI systems — why one function tells you everything

Here is the central magic trick. Define the impulse response (Impulsantwort) \(h(t)\) as the system's output when the input is a single unit impulse:

\[ h(t) = T\{\delta(t)\}. \]

Claim: for an LTI system, \(h(t)\) determines the response to every input. The derivation is a three-step argument worth internalizing, not memorizing.

Step 1 — chop the input into pulses. Approximate \(x(t)\) by a staircase of narrow rectangles of width \(\Delta\) (Fig. 4.1). The rectangle at \(\tau = k\Delta\) has height \(x(k\Delta)\); writing it via the unit-area pulse \(\delta_\Delta(t) = \tfrac1\Delta \operatorname{rect}(t/\Delta)\):

\[ x(t) \approx \sum_k x(k\Delta)\; \delta_\Delta(t - k\Delta)\, \Delta . \]

Step 2 — use linearity and time-invariance. Time-invariance: the response to \(\delta_\Delta(t-k\Delta)\) is \(h_\Delta(t-k\Delta)\), the same pulse response shifted. Linearity: the response to the weighted sum is the weighted sum of responses:

\[ y(t) \approx \sum_k x(k\Delta)\; h_\Delta(t - k\Delta)\, \Delta . \]

Step 3 — let \(\Delta \to 0\). The pulse becomes the Dirac impulse, \(h_\Delta \to h\), the sum becomes an integral:

\[ \boxed{\; y(t) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x(\tau)\, h(t-\tau)\,\mathrm{d}\tau \;=\; (x * h)(t) \;} \]

This is the convolution integral (Faltungsintegral) — the single formula that computes any LTI output from any input. Its meaning: the output now is a weighted sum of all past (and, if non-causal, future) input values, and \(h\) is the weighting pattern — the system's memory profile.

🔑Key idea: h(t) is the system's fingerprint

One measurement — hit the system with an impulse, record what comes out — and you know its response to everything. This is why acousticians fire starter pistols in concert halls (the recorded reverberation is \(h(t)\), and convolving any dry recording with it lets you "play" that music in that hall), and why network analyzers fully characterize amplifiers. In practice one often measures the step response (Sprungantwort) \(g(t) = T\{u(t)\}\) instead — a step is easier to generate than an impulse — and uses \(h(t) = \mathrm{d}g/\mathrm{d}t\).

5. Convolution — the mechanics

5.1 The graphical method: flip — shift — multiply — integrate

The integral \(y(t) = \int x(\tau)h(t-\tau)\mathrm{d}\tau\) confuses everyone at first because of the argument \(h(t-\tau)\): as a function of the integration variable \(\tau\), it is \(h\) flipped (mirror at the vertical axis, because of \(-\tau\)) and shifted by \(t\). So for each output instant \(t\):

  1. Flip (falten = to fold — hence the German name!): draw \(h(-\tau)\).
  2. Shift it by \(t\): the flipped copy now sits at position \(t\); this is \(h(t-\tau)\).
  3. Multiply pointwise with \(x(\tau)\).
  4. Integrate: the area under the product is the single number \(y(t)\).

Slide the flipped copy from \(t=-\infty\) to \(+\infty\) and the areas trace out the whole output. The widget below animates exactly this.

Worked example rect ∗ rect = triangle, fully by hand

Let \(x(t) = h(t) = 1\) for \(0 \le t \le T\) (with \(T = 1\,\mathrm{ms}\)), zero elsewhere. Compute \(y = x * h\) graphically, case by case. The flipped-shifted copy \(h(t-\tau)\) occupies \(\tau \in [t-T,\; t]\); the signal \(x(\tau)\) occupies \(\tau \in [0, T]\). Everything reduces to: how much do these two intervals overlap?

Case 1 — no contact yet (\(t < 0\)): the intervals \([t-T, t]\) and \([0,T]\) are disjoint. Zero overlap: \(y(t) = 0\).
Case 2 — sliding in (\(0 \le t < T\)): overlap runs from \(0\) to \(t\), so \[ y(t) = \int_0^t 1 \cdot 1 \,\mathrm{d}\tau = t . \] The output ramps up linearly as the pulses engage.
Case 3 — sliding out (\(T \le t < 2T\)): overlap runs from \(t - T\) to \(T\): \[ y(t) = \int_{t-T}^{T} \mathrm{d}\tau = 2T - t . \] A linear ramp back down.
Case 4 — past each other (\(t \ge 2T\)): disjoint again, \(y(t) = 0\).
\(y(t)\) is a triangle: peak value \(T = 1\,\mathrm{ms}\cdot(1)^2\) at \(t = T\), total width \(2T = 2\,\mathrm{ms}\). Sanity checks: the output duration is the sum of the input durations (\(T + T\)); the output area equals the product of the areas (\(T \cdot T = T^2\) — the triangle's area, ✓); and the output is smoother than the inputs (convolution always smooths — two discontinuous rects gave a continuous triangle).
🚫Classic pitfall: forgetting to flip

Sliding an unflipped \(h(\tau - t)\) across \(x\) computes the correlation, not the convolution — for symmetric signals you get away with it, and then the exam presents an asymmetric \(h\) and the answer is time-reversed. Second classic: getting the integration limits wrong in the case analysis. Always determine the limits from "where are both factors nonzero?", sketching the two intervals — never from memory. Third: forgetting that the result's support starts at the sum of the two starting points.

The associativity result deserves a pause: an entire chain of amplifiers, cables and filters collapses into one equivalent LTI system. In the frequency domain (Section 6 onward) this becomes even better — convolution turns into multiplication, and cascades simply multiply their transfer functions.

6. The eigenfunction property — why engineers think in frequency

6.1 Three lines that create a worldview

Feed the complex exponential \(x(t) = e^{st}\) into an LTI system with impulse response \(h(t)\), using commutativity \(y = h * x\):

\[ y(t) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} h(\tau)\, e^{s(t-\tau)}\,\mathrm{d}\tau = e^{st} \underbrace{\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} h(\tau)\, e^{-s\tau}\,\mathrm{d}\tau}_{\displaystyle H(s)} \] \[ \boxed{\; x(t) = e^{st} \;\Longrightarrow\; y(t) = H(s)\, e^{st} \;} \]

The exponential passes through unchanged in shape — the system can only multiply it by the complex number \(H(s)\). In linear-algebra language (Module 01): \(e^{st}\) is an eigenfunction (Eigenfunktion) of every LTI system, with eigenvalue \(H(s)\). The function \(H(s)\), the Laplace transform of \(h(t)\), is the transfer function (Übertragungsfunktion).

6.2 The frequency response H(jω)

Set \(s = j\omega\) — a pure sinusoid — and you get the frequency response (Frequenzgang) \(H(j\omega)\). Because a real cosine is a pair of complex exponentials, a real input \(\hat{x}\cos(\omega t)\) produces, in steady state,

\[ y(t) = |H(j\omega)|\;\hat{x}\,\cos\!\big(\omega t + \varphi(\omega)\big), \qquad \varphi(\omega) = \angle H(j\omega). \]

Read that carefully — it is the most consequential sentence of the module: a sinusoid in gives a sinusoid out, at the same frequency; the system can only change its amplitude (by the magnitude response \(|H(j\omega)|\), Amplitudengang) and its phase (by the phase response \(\varphi(\omega)\), Phasengang). No new frequencies are ever created by an LTI system. Distortion that adds harmonics — an overdriven guitar amp — is by definition non-linearity.

💡Intuition: the system as a graphic equalizer

Decompose any input into sinusoids (Fourier, Module 03). The LTI system is then just a wall of independent volume-and-delay knobs, one per frequency: \(|H(j\omega)|\) is the volume setting, \(\varphi(\omega)\) the delay setting. Convolution in time is this per-frequency multiplication seen from the other side: \(Y(j\omega) = H(j\omega)X(j\omega)\). When convolution feels opaque, switch domains — that freedom is the whole point of the transform machinery.

Worked example The eigenfunction property in action: an RC lowpass

An RC lowpass (\(R = 1.6\,\mathrm{k\Omega},\ C = 100\,\mathrm{nF}\), output across C) is driven by \(x(t) = 5\,\mathrm{V}\cdot\cos(2\pi \cdot 3\,\mathrm{kHz}\cdot t)\). Find the steady-state output.

Step 1 — transfer function: the complex voltage divider from Module 05: \( H(j\omega) = \frac{1/(j\omega C)}{R + 1/(j\omega C)} = \frac{1}{1 + j\omega RC} \), with \(\tau = RC = 0.16\,\mathrm{ms}\) and corner frequency \(f_c = \frac{1}{2\pi RC} \approx 1\,\mathrm{kHz}\).
Step 2 — evaluate at the signal frequency: \( \omega RC = f/f_c = 3 \): \( |H| = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 + 3^2}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{10}} \approx 0.316, \qquad \varphi = -\arctan 3 \approx -71.6^\circ. \)
Step 3 — write down the output: \( y(t) \approx 1.58\,\mathrm{V}\cdot\cos(2\pi\cdot 3\,\mathrm{kHz}\cdot t - 71.6^\circ) \). Same frequency, smaller, later.
\(y\) is a 3 kHz cosine of amplitude ≈ 1.58 V lagging by ≈ 72°. No convolution integral was harmed: one complex multiplication replaced it. That trade — integral for multiplication — is what you bought with the eigenfunction property.

7. The transfer function — poles, zeros, and the s-plane map

7.1 Poles and zeros

For every circuit made of R, L, C and amplifiers — indeed for every system described by a linear ODE with constant coefficients — \(H(s)\) is a rational function: a ratio of polynomials,

\[ H(s) = \frac{N(s)}{D(s)} = k\,\frac{(s - z_1)(s - z_2)\cdots}{(s - p_1)(s - p_2)\cdots} \]

The roots \(z_i\) of the numerator are the zeros (Nullstellen) — frequencies the system completely blocks. The roots \(p_i\) of the denominator are the poles (Polstellen) — the system's natural resonances. A partial-fraction expansion (Module 03) turns \(H(s)\) back into time:

\[ H(s) = \sum_i \frac{A_i}{s - p_i} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad h(t) = \sum_i A_i\, e^{p_i t}\,u(t) \]

(for simple poles, causal system). Each pole \(p_i = \sigma_i + j\omega_i\) contributes one natural mode \(e^{\sigma_i t}e^{j\omega_i t}\): the pole's real part sets the decay/growth, its imaginary part sets the oscillation frequency. Complex poles come in conjugate pairs and combine into real damped cosines \(e^{\sigma t}\cos(\omega t + \varphi)\).

7.2 The s-plane map

Plot the poles (×) and zeros (○) in the complex plane and you can read off the system's temperament at a glance:

Three rules of thumb summarize the geography:

  • Left = decay, right = growth. \(\mathrm{Re}(p) = \sigma < 0\) gives \(e^{\sigma t} \to 0\); \(\sigma > 0\) explodes.
  • Height = pitch. \(\mathrm{Im}(p) = \omega\) is the ringing frequency in rad/s; poles on the real axis do not oscillate at all.
  • Close to the axis = slow. A pole hugging the imaginary axis decays sluggishly and rings long — think of a bell.

7.3 Stability and dominant poles

Because \(h(t)\) is a sum of pole modes, BIBO stability has a beautifully clean criterion:

\[ \boxed{\;\text{causal LTI system stable} \iff \text{all poles satisfy } \mathrm{Re}(p_i) < 0\;} \]

(zeros are irrelevant for stability — they cannot make anything grow). Poles exactly on the imaginary axis are the knife's edge: marginally stable (grenzstabil), oscillating forever, and tipped into instability by the slightest perturbation — an ideal oscillator is exactly this, deliberately.

Among stable poles, the one closest to the imaginary axis decays slowest and therefore rules the long-term behavior: the dominant pole (dominanter Pol). Practical consequence: a fifth-order amplifier with poles at \(-10^3\) and four poles beyond \(-10^6\) (all in 1/s) behaves, for settling-time purposes, like a first-order system with \(\tau = 1\,\mathrm{ms}\). Engineers approximate high-order systems by their one or two dominant poles constantly — it is the standard first move in Module 14.

🧪Interactive — pole explorer simulation

📜Poles run the world

Pole placement is arguably the most economically important activity in engineering: flight control laws place the poles of an intrinsically unstable airframe into the left half-plane hundreds of times per second (Module 14); phase-locked loops in every radio place poles for lock speed vs. jitter (Module 21); the 2019–2021 redesigns of grid-inverter controls were, at heart, arguments about poles drifting rightward as solar penetration grew (Module 19). When this module says "where are the poles?", it is asking the professional's first question.

8. Bode plots — frequency response you can sketch on a napkin

8.1 Decibels, recapped in one paragraph

Magnitude responses span factors of millions, so we plot them logarithmically. The gain in decibels: \(|H|_{\mathrm{dB}} = 20\log_{10}|H|\). Anchors to memorize: \(\times 1 = 0\,\mathrm{dB}\), \(\times\sqrt{2} \approx 3\,\mathrm{dB}\), \(\times 2 \approx 6\,\mathrm{dB}\), \(\times 10 = 20\,\mathrm{dB}\), \(\times 100 = 40\,\mathrm{dB}\); division flips the sign. The payoff of logs: cascaded gains multiply, so their dB values — and their Bode curves — simply add. With a log frequency axis as well, the pieces of any rational \(H(s)\) become straight-line segments. That is the Bode plot (Bode-Diagramm): magnitude in dB and phase in degrees, both over \(\log \omega\).

8.2 The asymptotic construction rules

Write \(H(s)\) in normal form — every factor as \((1 + s/\omega_c)\) — and read off the corner frequencies \(\omega_c\) (Knickfrequenzen). Then each factor contributes:

Construction procedure: (1) normal form, list corners left to right; (2) find the low-frequency asymptote from the constant and any \(s^{\pm n}\) factors; (3) walk rightward, bending the slope by ±20 dB/dec at each corner; (4) round the corners by ∓3 dB; (5) sketch phase by adding the S-curves. Total time: two minutes. Accuracy: within ~1–3 dB of exact — which is why analog designers still sketch Bode plots before touching a simulator.

Worked example Full Bode construction of \(H(s) = \dfrac{100\,(s + 10)}{(s+1)(s+100)}\)
Step 1 — normal form: pull each root out as \((1 + s/\omega_c)\): \[ H(s) = \frac{100 \cdot 10\,(1 + s/10)}{1\cdot(1 + s/1)\cdot 100\,(1 + s/100)} = 10\;\frac{1 + s/10}{(1 + s/1)(1 + s/100)} . \] DC gain \(H(0) = 10 \;\widehat{=}\; 20\,\mathrm{dB}\). Corners, left to right: pole at 1, zero at 10, pole at 100 (all rad/s).
Step 2 — magnitude asymptotes: start flat at 20 dB. At \(\omega = 1\) the pole bends the slope to −20 dB/dec; over the decade to \(\omega = 10\) the curve falls 20 dB → reaches 0 dB at ω = 10. There the zero bends the slope back to 0 dB/dec: flat at 0 dB until \(\omega = 100\), where the second pole bends it to −20 dB/dec for good. (Check the far end: at \(\omega \gg 100\), \(|H| \approx 100\,\omega/\omega^2 = 100/\omega\) — at \(\omega = 1000\) that is 0.1 = −20 dB. The asymptote agrees. ✓)
Step 3 — corrections: −3 dB at ω = 1 (→ 17 dB), +3 dB at ω = 10 (→ 3 dB), −3 dB at ω = 100 (→ −3 dB). Done — that is already accurate to a fraction of a dB.
Step 4 — phase: each corner contributes its S-curve: pole@1 (0→−90°), zero@10 (0→+90°), pole@100 (0→−90°). Low-frequency phase 0°; around \(\omega \approx 1\) the phase dips (−45° at ω = 1); the zero pulls it back up toward −45°…0° in the mid-band; the last pole takes it down to the high-frequency limit \((n_z - n_p)\cdot 90° = -90°\).
A "shelf" response: 20 dB below 1 rad/s, 0 dB between 10 and 100 rad/s, falling beyond. The −3 dB bandwidth relative to the DC gain (20 dB → 17 dB line) is read off at \(\omega \approx 1\) rad/s. Five minutes, no computer.

8.3 The second-order response and resonance peaking

🧪Interactive — second-order Bode explorer simulation

Sweep the damping ratio \(\zeta\): below \(\zeta = 1/\sqrt{2} \approx 0.707\) a resonance peak grows at \(\omega \approx \omega_0\) — at \(\zeta = 0.05\) it towers 20 dB above the passband. 9. First- and second-order prototypes — the two systems you must know cold

9.1 First order: one pole, one time constant

Prototype: \(H(s) = \dfrac{1}{1 + s\tau}\) — every RC lowpass, every thermal lag, every dominant-pole amplifier. Its three faces:

\[ h(t) = \frac{1}{\tau}e^{-t/\tau}u(t), \qquad g(t) = \left(1 - e^{-t/\tau}\right)u(t), \qquad |H(j\omega)| = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 + (\omega\tau)^2}} . \]

The numbers every engineer carries in their head: the step response reaches 63 % at \(t = \tau\), 95 % at \(3\tau\), 99 % at \(5\tau\); the −3 dB corner sits at \(\omega_c = 1/\tau\); and the 10–90 % rise time (Anstiegszeit) obeys

\[ t_r \approx 2.2\,\tau \quad\Longrightarrow\quad t_r \cdot f_{3\mathrm{dB}} \approx 0.35 . \]

That last identity — bandwidth × rise time ≈ 0.35 — lets you infer an oscilloscope's bandwidth from how it renders a sharp edge, and vice versa. A 100 MHz scope cannot show edges faster than ≈ 3.5 ns, no matter what the circuit does.

9.2 Second order: two poles, and the personality parameter ζ

Prototype (normalized DC gain 1):

\[ H(s) = \frac{\omega_0^2}{s^2 + 2\zeta\omega_0 s + \omega_0^2}, \qquad p_{1,2} = -\zeta\omega_0 \pm \omega_0\sqrt{\zeta^2 - 1}, \]

with undamped natural frequency \(\omega_0\) (Kennkreisfrequenz) and damping ratio \(\zeta\) (Dämpfungsgrad). The value of \(\zeta\) sets the system's entire personality:

RegimePolesStep responseFrequency response
\(\zeta > 1\) overdamped (Kriechfall)two distinct real polessluggish creep, no overshootdroops early, no peak
\(\zeta = 1\) critically damped (aperiodischer Grenzfall)double real pole at \(-\omega_0\)fastest response without overshoot−6 dB at \(\omega_0\), no peak
\(0 < \zeta < 1\) underdamped (Schwingfall)conjugate pair, \(\omega_d = \omega_0\sqrt{1-\zeta^2}\)overshoot \(M_p = e^{-\pi\zeta/\sqrt{1-\zeta^2}}\), ringing at \(\omega_d\)peak if \(\zeta < 1/\sqrt2\)

For \(\zeta < 1/\sqrt{2}\) the magnitude response peaks at \(\omega_r = \omega_0\sqrt{1 - 2\zeta^2}\) with height

\[ |H|_{\max} = \frac{1}{2\zeta\sqrt{1 - \zeta^2}} \;\approx\; Q = \frac{1}{2\zeta} \ \ (\zeta \ll 1), \]

connecting directly to the resonance and quality factor \(Q\) of the series RLC circuit in Module 06, where \(\zeta = \frac{R}{2}\sqrt{C/L}\). Design lore: \(\zeta \approx 0.7\) (poles at 45°) is the classic engineering compromise — fast, ~4–5 % overshoot, flattest possible passband (Butterworth!); car suspensions aim slightly under 1; measurement instruments nearer 1 to avoid overshoot lying about the measured value.

🚫Classic pitfall: three different "resonance frequencies"

Keep apart: \(\omega_0\) (undamped natural frequency, from the poles' distance to the origin), \(\omega_d = \omega_0\sqrt{1-\zeta^2}\) (actual ringing frequency of the step response) and \(\omega_r = \omega_0\sqrt{1-2\zeta^2}\) (where the magnitude response peaks). For small \(\zeta\) all three nearly coincide, and exam setters love the regime where they do not. At \(\zeta = 0.6\): \(\omega_d = 0.8\,\omega_0\) but \(\omega_r = 0.53\,\omega_0\) — far from interchangeable.

10. Filters — ideal shapes vs. buildable reality

The most common job description for an LTI system: pass some frequencies, block others — a filter. The four ideal shapes:

10.1 Why the brick wall is impossible

Take the ideal lowpass: \(|H| = 1\) for \(|f| < f_c\), zero beyond, zero phase. Inverse-transforming this rectangle in frequency (Module 03) gives the impulse response

\[ h(t) = 2f_c\,\operatorname{si}(2\pi f_c t) = 2f_c\,\frac{\sin(2\pi f_c t)}{2\pi f_c t}, \]

a sinc ripple extending over all time — including \(t < 0\). The ideal filter responds before the impulse arrives: it is non-causal, hence unbuildable in real time (its sinc tails decaying only as \(1/t\) also mean it is not even BIBO stable). Every real filter is a compromise triangle between (a) flat passband, (b) steep transition, (c) benign phase/transient behavior — you may pick roughly two. The classical approximation families (Butterworth: maximally flat; Chebyshev: steeper but rippled; Bessel: best phase, laziest rolloff) and their realization as circuits and algorithms are the business of Module 13.

💡Intuition: sharp in frequency = long in time

Frequency selectivity requires observation time. To decide whether a tone is 999 Hz or 1001 Hz you must listen for on the order of half a second — the reciprocal of the 2 Hz difference. A filter that separates frequencies sharply must therefore "listen" for a long time: a long, ringing impulse response. The brick wall takes this to the limit — infinitely sharp needs infinitely long, including infinitely early. This time–frequency duality (bandwidth × duration ≳ constant) is the engineering face of the uncertainty principle, and it will return in windowing (Module 13) and digital communications (Module 21).

11. Sampling — the bridge to the digital world

Every phone call, every measurement in this decade eventually becomes numbers. Sampling (Abtastung) reads the signal every \(T_s\) seconds — sampling frequency \(f_s = 1/T_s\) (Abtastfrequenz). The astonishing claim of the sampling theorem: under one condition, the samples contain everything — the continuous signal can be reconstructed exactly, not approximately.

11.1 The spectrum-replication picture

Derivation sketch. Model sampling as multiplying \(x(t)\) by an impulse train \(\text{ш}(t) = \sum_n \delta(t - nT_s)\):

\[ x_s(t) = x(t)\cdot \sum_{n} \delta(t - nT_s) = \sum_n x(nT_s)\,\delta(t - nT_s) \]

(the sifting property keeps exactly the sample values). The impulse train is periodic, so it has a Fourier series — and a remarkable one: all coefficients equal, \(\text{ш}(t) = \frac{1}{T_s}\sum_k e^{jk\omega_s t}\). Multiplication by \(e^{jk\omega_s t}\) shifts a spectrum by \(k\omega_s\) (Module 03). Therefore:

\[ \boxed{\;X_s(f) = \frac{1}{T_s}\sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty} X(f - k f_s)\;} \]

Sampling replicates the spectrum at every multiple of \(f_s\) (Fig. 11.1). If \(x\) is band-limited to \(f_{\max}\) and the replicas do not touch, the original spectrum sits intact between them — a lowpass filter can cut it back out, losslessly. The replicas stay clear of each other exactly when \(f_s - f_{\max} > f_{\max}\):

\[ \boxed{\; f_s > 2 f_{\max} \;} \quad\text{(Shannon/Nyquist sampling theorem, } \textit{Abtasttheorem}\text{)} \]

The threshold \(2 f_{\max}\) is the Nyquist rate; \(f_s/2\) is the Nyquist frequency — the highest frequency a given sampler can represent.

11.2 Aliasing — when frequencies lie about their identity

Sample too slowly and a high frequency \(f > f_s/2\) becomes indistinguishable from a low one: its samples are identical to those of the alias (Rückfaltung, Aliasing) at

\[ f_a = \left| f - k f_s \right|, \qquad k = \operatorname{round}(f/f_s), \]

i.e. the frequency folded back into \([0, f_s/2]\). Once sampled, the information about which of the two it "really" was is gone forever — no algorithm can undo aliasing.

💡Intuition: the wagon wheel and the stroboscope

In old westerns, stagecoach wheels seem to spin slowly backwards: the film camera samples at 24 frames/s, and a spoke pattern rotating slightly faster than an integer multiple of that appears folded down to a slow (or negative — backwards!) frequency. The same effect makes a strobe light "freeze" machinery: flash at exactly the rotation rate and the shaft's apparent frequency aliases to zero. Mechanics use this deliberately; your measurement chain suffers it accidentally — a 50 Hz hum sampled at 49 Hz shows up as a mysterious 1 Hz wobble in the data.

🧪Interactive — aliasing demonstrator simulation

A sine of frequency \(f\) (solid) is sampled at \(f_s\) (dots). The dashed curve is the sine a reconstructor would infer from the dots. Keep \(f < f_s/2\): both curves agree. Push \(f\) past \(f_s/2\): the dots are perfectly straddled by a slower impostor — watch the apparent frequency fold back down, hit 0 at \(f = f_s\), and rise again. That is the wagon wheel, quantified.

Worked example Computing alias frequencies

A data logger samples at \(f_s = 8\,\mathrm{kHz}\) with no input filter. What apparent frequencies do components at (a) 3 kHz, (b) 6.5 kHz and (c) 15.5 kHz produce?

Step (a) — 3 kHz: below the Nyquist frequency \(f_s/2 = 4\,\mathrm{kHz}\) → appears correctly at 3 kHz (\(k = \operatorname{round}(3/8) = 0\)).
Step (b) — 6.5 kHz: \(k = \operatorname{round}(6.5/8) = 1\): \(f_a = |6.5 - 8| = 1.5\,\mathrm{kHz}\). A 6.5 kHz whistle is recorded as a 1.5 kHz tone.
Step (c) — 15.5 kHz: \(k = \operatorname{round}(15.5/8) = 2\): \(f_a = |15.5 - 16| = 0.5\,\mathrm{kHz}\). Even far-ultrasonic content folds right into the audio band.
Apparent tones: 3 kHz, 1.5 kHz, 0.5 kHz — the last two are pure fiction. Sanity check: every alias lies in \([0, 4\,\mathrm{kHz}]\), as it must. Moral: what you see in sampled data is always in \([0, f_s/2]\), whatever the input was actually doing.

11.3 Anti-aliasing and reconstruction

Two practical consequences frame every ADC and DAC on Earth:

  • Before sampling: an analog anti-aliasing filter (Anti-Aliasing-Filter) must remove content above \(f_s/2\) before the sampler — afterwards is too late, the folding is irreversible. Because real filters need a transition band (Section 10), one samples faster than the naked theorem demands: CD audio keeps 20 kHz and samples at 44.1 kHz, leaving a 2.05 kHz guard band for the filter's rolloff. Modern systems oversample massively and filter digitally — but an analog first-stage filter is always there.
  • After the DAC: ideal reconstruction would convolve the sample impulses with the sinc of an ideal lowpass — non-causal, so real DACs instead hold each sample constant for \(T_s\): the zero-order hold (Halteglied). Its staircase output has the spectrum of the ideal one multiplied by a \(\operatorname{si}(\pi f/f_s)\) droop plus leftover replicas — which a gentle analog "smoothing" lowpass then cleans up. The full digital signal chain built on this — quantization, DSP, digital filters — is Module 13.

13. Exercises

Work these on paper first. Graphical convolution in particular is a hand skill — the widget can check you, but only your pencil teaches you.

E1Classify each signal (periodic? energy or power signal?) and compute \(E\) or \(P\): (a) \(x_1(t) = 3\cos(100\pi t)\), (b) \(x_2(t) = e^{-t}u(t)\), (c) \(x_3(t) = u(t)\), (d) \(x_4(t) = t\,u(t)\). easy

Show solution
(a) Periodic (\(T = 20\,\mathrm{ms}\)) → power signal, \(P = \hat{x}^2/2 = 4.5\) (in V² for x in V). (b) Aperiodic, decaying: \(E = \int_0^\infty e^{-2t}\mathrm{d}t = \tfrac12\) → energy signal. (c) The step: \(E = \infty\), but \(P = \lim \frac{1}{2T}\int_0^T 1\,\mathrm{d}t = \tfrac12\) → power signal. (d) The ramp: even the power average diverges (\(\int_0^T t^2 \mathrm{d}t = T^3/3\), divided by \(2T\) still \(\to\infty\)) → neither. Sanity check: every finite-duration or decaying signal you will meet is an energy signal; everything periodic is a power signal.

E2Evaluate using the sifting property: (a) \(\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}(t^2+1)\,\delta(t-2)\,\mathrm{d}t\); (b) \(\int_{0}^{5}\cos(\pi t)\,\delta(t-4)\,\mathrm{d}t\); (c) \(\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\delta(t+1)\,u(t)\,\mathrm{d}t\); (d) \(\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-3t}\,\delta(2t)\,\mathrm{d}t\). easy

Show solution
(a) The impulse sits at \(t=2\): result \(2^2+1 = 5\). (b) Impulse at \(t=4\), which lies inside [0, 5]: \(\cos(4\pi) = 1\). (c) Impulse at \(t=-1\): sift out \(u(-1) = 0\). (d) Scaling rule \(\delta(2t) = \tfrac12\delta(t)\): result \(\tfrac12 e^{0} = \tfrac12\). The traps were all in the fine print: integration limits must contain the impulse (b), and the argument scaling changes the weight (d).

E3For (a) \(y(t) = t\,x(t)\) and (b) \(y(t) = x(2t)\), test linearity, time-invariance and causality with full arguments. medium

Show solution
(a) Linear: \(t\,(a x_1 + b x_2) = a\,t x_1 + b\,t x_2\) ✓. Not time-invariant: input \(x(t-t_0)\) gives \(t\,x(t-t_0)\), but the shifted output is \((t-t_0)\,x(t-t_0)\) — different whenever \(t_0 \ne 0\). Causal (even memoryless): \(y(t)\) uses only \(x(t)\) now. (b) Linear ✓ (same substitution argument). Not time-invariant: input \(x(t-t_0)\) yields \(x(2t - t_0)\), but \(y(t-t_0) = x(2t - 2t_0)\) ✗. Not causal: \(y(1) = x(2)\) — the output at t = 1 s needs the input one second in the future. Moral: any explicit \(t\) or time-rescaling in the system law should make your time-invariance alarm ring.

E4An RC lowpass has \(h(t) = \frac{1}{\tau}e^{-t/\tau}u(t)\) with \(\tau = 1\,\mathrm{ms}\). Compute the step response \(g = u * h\) by convolution, and find when the output reaches 95 % and 99 % of its final value. medium

Show solution
For \(t \ge 0\), the flipped step \(u(t-\tau')\) overlaps \(h\) on \([0, t]\): \[ g(t) = \int_0^t \tfrac{1}{\tau}e^{-\tau'/\tau}\mathrm{d}\tau' = 1 - e^{-t/\tau}. \] 95 %: \(e^{-t/\tau} = 0.05 \Rightarrow t = \tau\ln 20 \approx 3.0\,\tau = 3.0\,\mathrm{ms}\). 99 %: \(t = \tau \ln 100 \approx 4.6\,\tau \approx 4.6\,\mathrm{ms}\). These are the "3τ / 5τ" settling rules of thumb, now derived rather than memorized. Sanity: \(g(\infty) = 1\) matches the DC gain \(H(0) = 1\), and \(\mathrm{d}g/\mathrm{d}t = h\) ✓.

E5Sketch the Bode magnitude plot of \(H(s) = \dfrac{100\,s}{(s+10)(s+1000)}\): normal form, corner frequencies, all asymptote slopes, mid-band gain, and the phase at \(\omega = 100\) rad/s. medium

Show solution
Normal form: \(H = \frac{100\,s}{10\cdot 1000\,(1+s/10)(1+s/1000)} = 0.01\,\frac{s}{(1+s/10)(1+s/1000)}\). Low frequencies: the zero at the origin gives +20 dB/dec; at \(\omega = 1\): \(|H| \approx 0.01\) = −40 dB. Corner ω = 10: pole flattens the slope → mid-band plateau \(|H| \approx 0.01 \times 10 = 0.1\) = −20 dB between 10 and 1000 rad/s. Corner ω = 1000: second pole → −20 dB/dec falloff. Shape: a band-pass. Phase at \(\omega = 100\): zero at origin +90°; pole at 10 (a decade below) ≈ −84°; pole at 1000 (a decade above) ≈ −6°. Sum ≈ 0° — the classic mid-band phase of a bandpass. Sanity: at the geometric band center the two corner phases nearly cancel the +90° of the zero; exact calculation gives +0.0° because \(100 = \sqrt{10 \cdot 1000}\).

E6A vibration monitor needs content up to 400 Hz; the machine also emits a strong 900 Hz component. The ADC samples at \(f_s = 1\,\mathrm{kHz}\). (a) Where does the 900 Hz line appear in the data? Why is that disastrous? (b) An anti-aliasing lowpass (−20 dB/dec per order above its 400 Hz corner) must suppress everything that folds into the 0–400 Hz band by ≥ 40 dB. Estimate the required filter order, and show how resampling at 4 kHz changes the answer. challenge

Show solution
(a) \(k = \mathrm{round}(900/1000) = 1\): \(f_a = |900 - 1000| = 100\,\mathrm{Hz}\) — dead center in the measurement band, indistinguishable from a genuine 100 Hz bearing vibration. A maintenance decision could be made on a fictitious signal. (b) With \(f_s = 1\,\mathrm{kHz}\), everything above \(f_s - 400 = 600\,\mathrm{Hz}\) folds into the useful band, so the stopband must start at 600 Hz. An n-th order filter cornered at 400 Hz gives roughly \(20n\log_{10}(600/400) \approx 3.5\,n\) dB there — 40 dB needs \(n \approx 12\): absurd. At \(f_s = 4\,\mathrm{kHz}\) the folding edge moves to \(3.6\,\mathrm{kHz}\): \(20n\log_{10}(3600/400) \approx 19\,n\) dB → \(n = 3\) suffices (and 900 Hz no longer aliases at all). Moral: oversampling buys cheap analog filters — the design trade at the heart of every modern ADC front end (Module 13).

E7Derive the impulse response of the ideal lowpass \(H(j2\pi f) = \operatorname{rect}\!\left(\frac{f}{2f_c}\right)\) by inverse Fourier transform, and prove that the filter is (a) non-causal and (b) not even BIBO stable. challenge

Show solution
\[ h(t) = \int_{-f_c}^{f_c} e^{j2\pi f t}\,\mathrm{d}f = \frac{e^{j2\pi f_c t} - e^{-j2\pi f_c t}}{j2\pi t} = \frac{\sin(2\pi f_c t)}{\pi t} = 2f_c\,\operatorname{si}(2\pi f_c t). \] (a) \(h(t) \ne 0\) for \(t < 0\) (the sinc is even!) — the filter outputs signal before the impulse arrives. Non-causal, hence not realizable in real time; any realizable approximation must delay and truncate the sinc. (b) \(\int|h|\,\mathrm{d}t\): over the k-th half-period lobe, \(|h| \sim \frac{1}{\pi t}\), so the lobe areas fall off like \(1/k\) — the harmonic series — and the integral diverges. Not BIBO stable: there exists a bounded input (one that flips sign in step with the sinc lobes) producing an unbounded output. So the brick wall fails on two independent counts — which is why even offline digital "ideal" filters are windowed, truncated compromises (Module 13).
