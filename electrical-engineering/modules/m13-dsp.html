Digital Signal Processing · Digitale Signalverarbeitung

Every phone call you make, every song you stream, every photo you take is a stream of numbers being multiplied and added — billions of times per second. This module is about that arithmetic: how to filter, analyze and transform signals after they have become numbers, and why the FFT — arguably the most important algorithm of the 20th century — makes it all affordable.

Corresponds to: Digitale Signalverarbeitung (Master / late Bachelor) Prerequisites: Module 12 Study time: ≈ 6–8 h

1. Why process digitally? The software-defined world

Module 12 built the continuous-time theory: Fourier, Laplace, filters made of R, L and C. So why does virtually every modern system — audio, radio, radar, medical imaging — convert its signals to numbers as early as possible and back to analog as late as possible? Three reasons, each decisive on its own:

  • Perfect reproducibility. An analog filter is made of components with ±1 % tolerance that drift with temperature and age. A digital filter is a list of coefficients: every unit built behaves identically, forever, on every continent. Copying a digital signal is lossless — the millionth copy of a file equals the first, something no tape or vinyl ever achieved.
  • Flexibility. Changing an analog filter means a soldering iron. Changing a digital filter means writing new numbers into memory — a firmware update. The same hardware can be an FM demodulator today and a 5G receiver tomorrow. This is the meaning of software-defined: the product is code.
  • Cost and capability. Riding on 60 years of Moore's law, a 1 € microcontroller today performs hundreds of millions of multiply-accumulate operations per second — the workload of a room full of 1970s analog gear. And some operations are only possible digitally: exactly linear-phase filters (Section 7), adaptive filters that redesign themselves in milliseconds, and spectral analysis via the FFT.
📜The algorithm that started it all

In 1965 James Cooley and John Tukey published a way to compute the discrete Fourier transform in \(N\log N\) instead of \(N^2\) operations — motivated, it later emerged, by the need to detect Soviet nuclear tests from seismometer data. (Gauss had found the trick in 1805 and left it in an unpublished notebook.) The Fast Fourier Transform turned spectral analysis from an overnight batch job into a real-time operation and effectively founded the discipline of DSP. Today FFTs run inside every Wi-Fi and LTE modem — OFDM (Module 21) literally transmits data as an inverse FFT.

2. Discrete-time signals and the processing chain

A discrete-time signal (zeitdiskretes Signal) is a sequence of numbers \(x[n]\), \(n \in \mathbb{Z}\), usually obtained by sampling (Abtastung) an analog signal every \(T = 1/f_s\) seconds, where \(f_s\) is the sampling rate (Abtastrate):

\[ x[n] = x(nT), \qquad n \in \mathbb{Z}. \]

Square brackets signal "integer index"; the sequence knows nothing about seconds anymore. That has a profound consequence: a sampled sinusoid \(\cos(2\pi f\, n T)\) depends only on the ratio \(f/f_s\). We therefore work with the normalized angular frequency (normierte Kreisfrequenz)

\[ \boxed{\;\Omega = \omega T = 2\pi \frac{f}{f_s}\;} \qquad \Omega \in (-\pi, \pi] \;\;\Leftrightarrow\;\; f \in \left(-\tfrac{f_s}{2}, \tfrac{f_s}{2}\right]. \]

\(\Omega = \pi\) is the Nyquist frequency \(f_s/2\) — the highest frequency a sampled system can represent (Module 12). Discrete-time spectra are always periodic in \(\Omega\) with period \(2\pi\): adding \(2\pi\) to \(\Omega\) changes nothing, because \(e^{j(\Omega + 2\pi)n} = e^{j\Omega n}\) for integer \(n\). Aliasing is this periodicity seen from the analog side.

The standard real-time DSP system is a sandwich:

🔑Key idea: the sample index is the only clock

Inside the DSP block there are no seconds — only "current sample", "previous sample", "sample before that". Every algorithm in this module is built from three primitives: delay by one sample, multiply by a constant, add. A billion-transistor 5G modem does nothing else, just very fast. The z-transform, coming next, is precisely the algebra of these three operations.

3. The z-transform — Laplace for sequences

3.1 Definition

The z-transform (z-Transformation) of a sequence \(x[n]\) is

\[ \boxed{\;X(z) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} x[n]\, z^{-n}\;}, \qquad z \in \mathbb{C}, \]

defined wherever the sum converges (the region of convergence; for causal signals, the outside of a circle \(|z| > r\)). It plays exactly the role for discrete systems that the Laplace transform plays for continuous ones. The single most important property follows by re-indexing the sum:

\[ x[n-1] \;\circ\!\!-\!\!\bullet\; z^{-1} X(z). \]
🔑Key idea: \(z^{-1}\) = one sample of delay

Read \(z^{-1}\) not as an abstract variable but as a machine: a one-sample delay register (Verzögerungsglied). Then any block diagram of delays, gains and adders translates line-by-line into an algebraic expression in \(z\) — and back. Digital filter design is algebra on \(z^{-1}\).

3.2 Relation to Laplace: \(z = e^{sT}\)

Sample the Laplace kernel: \(e^{st}\big|_{t=nT} = (e^{sT})^n\). Calling \(z = e^{sT}\) turns the sampled Laplace transform into the z-transform. This exponential map warps the familiar s-plane geography (Module 12) into the z-plane:

3.4 Difference equations ↔ transfer functions

A causal LTI discrete system is described by a difference equation (Differenzengleichung) — the discrete sibling of a differential equation:

\[ y[n] = \sum_{k=0}^{M} b_k\, x[n-k] \;-\; \sum_{k=1}^{K} a_k\, y[n-k]. \]

Transforming term by term with the shift theorem and solving for \(Y/X\) gives the rational transfer function

\[ H(z) = \frac{Y(z)}{X(z)} = \frac{b_0 + b_1 z^{-1} + \dots + b_M z^{-M}}{1 + a_1 z^{-1} + \dots + a_K z^{-K}}. \]

Numerator roots are zeros, denominator roots are poles (Pol- und Nullstellen). The frequency response is \(H(z)\) evaluated on the unit circle, \(H(e^{j\Omega})\) — and its magnitude has a beautiful geometric reading: the product of distances from \(e^{j\Omega}\) to all zeros, divided by the product of distances to all poles. A pole near the unit circle creates a resonance peak; a zero on the circle creates a perfect null. (Widget in Section 8 lets you feel this.)

3.5 Stability

The impulse response of a single pole at \(z=p\) is \(p^n\): geometric decay if \(|p|<1\), geometric explosion if \(|p|>1\). Hence the discrete stability criterion:

\[ \boxed{\;\text{BIBO-stable (causal)} \iff \text{all poles satisfy } |p_k| < 1\;} \]

— strictly inside the unit circle, the image of "left half-plane" under \(z=e^{sT}\). Example: \(y[n] = a\,y[n-1] + x[n]\) has \(H(z) = 1/(1-az^{-1})\), pole at \(a\), impulse response \(a^n u[n]\). For \(a = 0.9\) it is a smoothing lowpass with time constant \(\approx 1/(1-a) = 10\) samples; for \(a = 1.01\) the output grows without bound — a 1 % coefficient error away from disaster. Filters with poles need care (Section 9).

⚠️Convention trap: \(\Omega\) vs \(\omega\), \(z^{-1}\) vs \(z\)

German texts write the normalized frequency as \(\Omega\) (capital omega); many US texts write \(\omega\) for the same thing and use \(\hat\omega\) or \(2\pi f/f_s\) elsewhere. Also, filter coefficients are quoted either for \(H(z)\) in powers of \(z^{-1}\) (usual) or of \(z\); and some tools (MATLAB) define \(a_k\) with the opposite sign. Before copying coefficients from anywhere, check the convention — a sign flip on \(a_1\) turns a lowpass into a highpass-ish mess.

4. From DTFT to DFT — spectra you can compute

4.1 The DTFT: continuous spectrum of a sequence

Evaluate the z-transform on the unit circle and you get the discrete-time Fourier transform (DTFT):

\[ X(e^{j\Omega}) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} x[n]\, e^{-j\Omega n}, \]

a continuous, \(2\pi\)-periodic function of \(\Omega\). Beautiful for analysis — useless for a computer, on two counts: the sum is infinite, and \(\Omega\) is a continuum. The DFT fixes both with one stroke.

4.2 The DFT: sampling the spectrum

Take a finite record of \(N\) samples \(x[0], \dots, x[N-1]\), and evaluate its spectrum at exactly \(N\) equally spaced frequencies \(\Omega_k = \tfrac{2\pi k}{N}\). This is the discrete Fourier transform (diskrete Fourier-Transformation):

\[ \boxed{\;X[k] = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} x[n]\, e^{-j 2\pi k n / N}\;}, \quad k = 0, \dots, N-1, \qquad x[n] = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{k=0}^{N-1} X[k]\, e^{+j 2\pi k n / N}. \]

Conventions to burn in: \(n\) indexes time samples, \(k\) indexes frequency bins (Frequenzstützstellen), the exponent's sign is negative in the forward direction, and the \(1/N\) sits (here, and in most texts and libraries) on the inverse. Bin \(k\) corresponds to the physical frequency

\[ f_k = k\,\frac{f_s}{N}, \qquad \Delta f = \frac{f_s}{N} \;\;\text{(frequency resolution)}. \]

Bins \(k = 0 \dots N/2\) cover DC to Nyquist; bins above \(N/2\) are the negative frequencies in disguise (\(k \equiv k - N\)), and for real signals they carry no new information: \(X[N-k] = X^*[k]\) (conjugate symmetry). A real full-scale cosine landing exactly on bin \(k_0\) produces \(|X[k_0]| = |X[N-k_0]| = N/2\) — remember the \(N/2\), everyone forgets it once.

💡Intuition: the DFT as a bank of correlators

Each output \(X[k]\) asks one question: "how much does the record look like a complex oscillation with exactly \(k\) whole cycles in my \(N\) samples?" — computed as a correlation (multiply and sum). \(N\) questions, \(N\) answers. The DFT implicitly assumes the record repeats periodically forever; every strange DFT phenomenon (leakage, circular convolution) is this hidden assumption talking.

Worked example A 4-point DFT, entirely by hand

Compute the DFT of \(x = \{1, 1, 0, 0\}\). With \(N = 4\), the kernel is \(e^{-j2\pi kn/4} = (-j)^{kn}\) — only the values \(1, -j, -1, j\) ever occur.

Step 1 — k = 0 (DC bin): \( X[0] = 1 + 1 + 0 + 0 = 2 \). Always just the sum of samples; average = \(2/4 = 0.5\). ✓
Step 2 — k = 1: \( X[1] = 1\cdot 1 + 1\cdot(-j) + 0 + 0 = 1 - j = \sqrt{2}\,e^{-j\pi/4} \).
Step 3 — k = 2 (Nyquist bin): \( X[2] = 1\cdot 1 + 1\cdot(-1) + 0 - 0 = 0 \) — the record contains no \(+1,-1,+1,-1\) component.
Step 4 — k = 3: \( X[3] = 1 + 1\cdot(j) = 1 + j \). Check: \(X[3] = X^*[1]\) ✓ — conjugate symmetry, as required for a real input.
Step 5 — sanity check via Parseval: \( \sum |x[n]|^2 = 2 \) and \( \frac{1}{N}\sum |X[k]|^2 = \frac{1}{4}(4 + 2 + 0 + 2) = 2 \) ✓
\( X = \{2,\; 1-j,\; 0,\; 1+j\} \) — DC-heavy, no Nyquist content, symmetric. Sixteen multiplications did the job; Section 5 shows why that scaling is a problem and how the FFT dodges it.

5. The FFT — the trick worth a million

Computed literally, each of the \(N\) bins needs \(N\) complex multiply-adds: \(N^2\) operations. The fast Fourier transform is not a different transform — it is the same DFT computed cleverly. The divide-and-conquer idea (decimation in time): split \(x[n]\) into its even and odd samples. Each is a DFT of length \(N/2\), and the full spectrum is a cheap combination of the two:

\[ X[k] = E[k] + e^{-j2\pi k/N}\, O[k], \qquad X[k + N/2] = E[k] - e^{-j2\pi k/N}\, O[k]. \]

Each half splits again, and again, until only trivial 1-point DFTs remain — \(\log_2 N\) stages of \(N/2\) "butterfly" operations each:

\[ \boxed{\;\text{FFT cost} \sim N \log_2 N \quad \text{vs} \quad \text{DFT cost} \sim N^2\;} \]
\(N\)direct DFT \(\sim N^2\)FFT \(\sim N\log_2 N\)speed-up
644 096384≈ 11×
25665 5362 04832×
1 024≈ 1.05 million≈ 10 240≈ 100×
4 096≈ 16.8 million≈ 49 000≈ 340×
1 048 576 (2²⁰)≈ 1.1 × 10¹²≈ 21 million≈ 52 000×

The bold row is the one to remember: a 1024-point spectrum for ten thousand operations instead of a million. At an audio rate of \(f_s = 48\) kHz, a 1024-sample block arrives every 21 ms; even a modest microcontroller executes 10 k operations in microseconds — real-time spectral processing with power to spare. The \(N^2\) version would already strain it, and at radar/5G rates (hundreds of MHz) the FFT is the difference between "impossible" and "routine". One condition: the radix-2 algorithm wants \(N\) to be a power of two, which is why FFT sizes are always 256, 1024, 4096 …

6. Spectral leakage, windows, and the spectrogram

6.1 Why finite observation smears the spectrum

Chopping \(N\) samples out of an endless signal is multiplying it by a rectangular window (Fensterfunktion). Multiplication in time is convolution in frequency (Module 12): every spectral line gets convolved with — smeared into — the window's own spectrum, a \(\mathrm{sinc}\)-like Dirichlet kernel with a main lobe two bins wide and side lobes decaying from only −13 dB.

If the tone completes an integer number of cycles in the record, its frequency sits exactly on a bin, all other bins land precisely on the kernel's nulls, and the spectrum looks perfectly clean. Move the tone half a bin and the nulls miss: energy leaks into every bin of the spectrum (Leckeffekt). The tall −13 dB side lobes of the rectangular window can easily bury a genuine weak signal 40 dB down.

6.2 Windowing: trading resolution for dynamic range

The cure is to fade the record in and out smoothly instead of cutting it — multiply by a tapered window before the FFT. Smooth tapering slashes the side lobes but widens the main lobe: a fundamental, inescapable trade-off between resolution (separating two close tones) and dynamic range (seeing a weak tone next to a strong one).

🚫Classic pitfall: zero padding "improves resolution"

Appending zeros to a record before the FFT gives more, finer-spaced bins — and students conclude the resolution improved. It did not. Zero padding merely interpolates the same underlying DTFT more densely: the main lobe width, set by the true observation time \(N/f_s\), is unchanged. Two tones closer than the main lobe stay merged no matter how many zeros you append. To genuinely resolve them you need more signal, not more zeros. Zero padding is still useful — it makes peak positions easier to read off — just be honest about what it is: a plotting nicety, not new information.

6.3 The STFT and the spectrogram

Real signals — speech, music, radar echoes — change over time, and one FFT of a long record averages all that history into mush. The short-time Fourier transform (STFT) slides a window along the signal and takes an FFT of each (usually overlapping) segment. Plotting the magnitudes as columns — time across, frequency up, intensity as color — gives the spectrogram (Spektrogramm), DSP's single most-used visualization. The window length sets an uncertainty trade-off: long windows give fine frequency detail but blur when things happen; short windows pinpoint events but blur pitch. Speech analysis typically uses 20–30 ms frames — short enough to catch phonemes, long enough to resolve formants.

7. FIR filters — convolution you design

7.1 The moving average: the simplest filter that works

A finite impulse response (FIR) filter (Filter mit endlicher Impulsantwort) has no feedback — the output is a weighted sum of the last \(M{+}1\) inputs, i.e. a convolution with a finite tap vector \(b_k\):

\[ y[n] = \sum_{k=0}^{M} b_k\, x[n-k]. \]

The humblest choice, all taps equal, is the moving average (gleitender Mittelwert) — the thing every spreadsheet user invents independently. It deserves a real analysis:

Worked example Frequency response of the N-point moving average

Derive \(H(e^{j\Omega})\) for \(h[n] = \frac{1}{N}\) for \(n = 0 \dots N-1\), and locate its nulls for \(N = 4\) at \(f_s = 48\) kHz.

Step 1 — geometric sum: \[ H(e^{j\Omega}) = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{k=0}^{N-1} e^{-j\Omega k} = \frac{1}{N}\,\frac{1 - e^{-j\Omega N}}{1 - e^{-j\Omega}}. \]
Step 2 — factor out half-angles: writing \(1 - e^{-j\theta} = e^{-j\theta/2}(e^{j\theta/2} - e^{-j\theta/2}) = 2j\,e^{-j\theta/2}\sin(\theta/2)\) top and bottom: \[ H(e^{j\Omega}) = e^{-j\Omega (N-1)/2}\; \frac{\sin(N\Omega/2)}{N \sin(\Omega/2)}. \]
Step 3 — read it: the magnitude is a Dirichlet kernel: 1 at DC, first crossing zero at \(\Omega = 2\pi/N\), i.e. \(f = f_s/N\), then ripples with slowly falling peaks. The phase factor \(e^{-j\Omega(N-1)/2}\) is exactly linear in \(\Omega\): a pure delay of \((N-1)/2\) samples.
Step 4 — numbers: \(N = 4\), \(f_s = 48\) kHz → perfect nulls at \(12\) and \(24\) kHz; −3 dB point at \(\approx 0.443 f_s/N \approx 5.3\) kHz.
A moving average is a lowpass with nulls at \(k \cdot f_s/N\) and linear phase. Choosing \(N\) to drop a null onto an interfering frequency (mains hum!) is a classic one-line filter design — see exercise E5.

7.2 Linear phase — why FIR filters are special

The moving average's linear phase was no accident: any FIR filter whose taps are symmetric, \(b_k = b_{M-k}\), has exactly linear phase — a frequency-independent group delay of \(M/2\) samples. Every frequency component is delayed by the same time, so the waveform's shape passes through undistorted (only amplitudes change). No causal analog filter, and no IIR filter, can do this exactly.

🔑Key idea: why linear phase matters

A filter with curved phase delays bass differently from treble: a crisp pulse comes out smeared even if the magnitude response is flat — this is dispersion. In audio mastering, in data transmission (where a smeared pulse overlaps its neighbors — intersymbol interference, Module 21), and in ECG machines (where a distorted QRS waveform is a misdiagnosis waiting to happen), waveform fidelity is the whole point. That is why these fields run on symmetric FIR filters despite their higher cost.

7.3 Design by windowing the ideal response

Want a sharp lowpass with cutoff \(\Omega_c\)? The ideal brick wall has the impulse response \(h_{id}[n] = \frac{\sin(\Omega_c n)}{\pi n}\) — a sinc: infinitely long and two-sided, hence unrealizable. The window design method: truncate the sinc to \(M{+}1\) taps around its center, shift right to make it causal, and multiply by a taper (Hann, Hamming — the same windows as Section 6, for the same reason: truncation ripples). More taps → sharper transition; heavier taper → smaller passband/stopband ripple but wider transition. Crude but robust; the workhorse alternatives (Parks–McClellan equiripple) optimize the same trade-off numerically.

Increase \(N\): the passband narrows (first null at \(f/f_s = 1/N\)), but the stopband side lobes never drop below −13 dB relative to the neighboring null spacing — averaging is a mediocre lowpass, which is exactly why windowed-sinc designs exist.

8. IIR filters — poles for efficiency

8.1 Feedback buys steepness

An infinite impulse response (IIR) filter feeds outputs back (Rückkopplung): \(a_k \neq 0\), poles appear, and the impulse response rings on forever (geometrically decaying, if stable). The payoff is dramatic: a resonant pole pair near the unit circle produces a steep, selective response that an FIR filter would need dozens of taps to imitate. Rule of thumb: an elliptic IIR of order 6 matches the magnitude selectivity of an FIR with 100+ taps. The price: possible instability, coefficient sensitivity, and phase that is never exactly linear.

8.2 From analog prototypes: the bilinear transform

Decades of analog filter craft (Butterworth, Chebyshev, elliptic — Module 12) are recycled digitally via the bilinear transform (Bilineartransformation): substitute

\[ \boxed{\;s \;\to\; \frac{2}{T}\,\frac{1 - z^{-1}}{1 + z^{-1}}\;} \]

into the analog \(H(s)\). This maps the entire \(j\omega\) axis one-to-one onto the unit circle — no aliasing of the response, stability preserved (left half-plane → circle interior). The cost is frequency warping: the infinite analog frequency axis is squeezed into \(0 \dots f_s/2\) via \(\omega_a = \frac{2}{T}\tan(\Omega/2)\). The fix is prewarping: design the analog prototype at the warped frequency \(\omega_a = \frac{2}{T}\tan(\pi f_c / f_s)\) so that after the squeeze, the critical frequency lands exactly where you wanted it.

Worked example Digitizing an RC lowpass by bilinear transform

Design a first-order digital lowpass with \(f_c = 1\) kHz at \(f_s = 8\) kHz from the analog prototype \(H(s) = \frac{1}{1 + s/\omega_c}\).

Step 1 — prewarp the cutoff: \( \omega_c' = \frac{2}{T}\tan\!\left(\pi \frac{f_c}{f_s}\right) = 16\,000 \cdot \tan(22.5°) = 6627\,\mathrm{rad/s} \) (5.5 % above the naive \(2\pi \cdot 1000 = 6283\) — the warp is already visible at \(f_c = f_s/8\)).
Step 2 — substitute: with \(\frac{2}{T} = 16\,000\), let \(\alpha = \frac{16\,000}{6627} = 2.414\): \[ H(z) = \frac{1}{1 + \alpha\,\frac{1-z^{-1}}{1+z^{-1}}} = \frac{1 + z^{-1}}{(1+\alpha) + (1-\alpha)z^{-1}} = \frac{0.2929\,(1 + z^{-1})}{1 - 0.4142\, z^{-1}}. \]
Step 3 — sanity checks: pole at \(z = 0.414\) — inside the unit circle, stable ✓. DC (\(z=1\)): \(H = \frac{0.5858}{0.5858} = 1\) ✓. Nyquist (\(z=-1\)): numerator zero → \(H = 0\), a bonus brick-wall at \(f_s/2\) ✓.
Step 4 — verify the cutoff: at \(\Omega = \pi/4\) (1 kHz): \(|H| = 0.7071\) — exactly −3 dB. Prewarping did its job to four digits.
\( y[n] = 0.4142\,y[n-1] + 0.2929\,x[n] + 0.2929\,x[n-1] \) — three MACs per sample replace a physical RC pair, with a cutoff that will never drift.

8.3 Biquads: second-order sections

High-order IIR filters are never implemented as one big polynomial ratio — root positions become absurdly sensitive to coefficient rounding (a 10th-order polynomial's roots can move drastically when coefficients are stored in 16 bits). Instead the filter is factored into cascaded second-order sections ("biquads", Filterkaskade aus Teilfiltern 2. Ordnung):

\[ H(z) = \prod_i \frac{b_{0i} + b_{1i} z^{-1} + b_{2i} z^{-2}}{1 + a_{1i} z^{-1} + a_{2i} z^{-2}}, \]

each section owning one pole pair (and zero pair). Every audio equalizer band, every anti-hum notch, every crossover in a powered speaker is a handful of biquads. A single all-pole biquad with poles at \(r e^{\pm j\Omega_0}\) is the digital resonator: difference equation \(y[n] = 2r\cos\Omega_0\, y[n-1] - r^2 y[n-2] + x[n]\), peak near \(f_0\), bandwidth shrinking as \(r \to 1\).

9. Implementation: structures and fixed point

A difference equation can be wired in several algebraically identical but numerically different structures: direct form I (separate delay lines for \(x\) and \(y\)), direct form II (shared delay line — minimum memory), and their transposed twins. On paper they are equal; in finite-precision arithmetic they differ in noise and overflow behavior (transposed direct form II is the common default for biquads).

Small processors compute in fixed-point arithmetic (Festkommaarithmetik), typically Q15: 16-bit words interpreted as fractions in \([-1, 1)\). Three demons follow:

  • Coefficient quantization — rounding coefficients moves poles; a pole intended at \(r = 0.999\) may land at \(1.001\). Biquad cascading (Section 8.3) exists to contain this.
  • Overflow — sums exceeding ±1 wrap around catastrophically unless saturated; internal signals must be scaled with headroom.
  • Limit cycles — rounding inside a feedback loop can sustain small permanent oscillations even with zero input; a fixed-point IIR filter can literally hum to itself. Cures: more internal bits, error feedback, or dither.

Floating point (Gleitkomma) makes most of this vanish and is why desktop audio tools are carefree — but at 10 mW battery budgets, fixed point still rules.

10. Quantization — the price of going digital

10.1 The uniform quantizer and its SNR

An ideal \(b\)-bit ADC maps its full-scale range \(\pm A\) onto \(2^b\) levels spaced \(\Delta = 2A/2^b\) apart (Quantisierung). Each sample acquires an error \(e = x_q - x\) confined to \(\pm\Delta/2\). For busy signals the error behaves like uniform white noise, with power

\[ \sigma_e^2 = \frac{1}{\Delta}\int_{-\Delta/2}^{\Delta/2} e^2\, \mathrm{d}e = \frac{\Delta^2}{12}. \]

Now feed in the loudest undistorted test signal, a full-scale sine of amplitude \(A = 2^{b-1}\Delta\), power \(A^2/2 = 2^{2b}\Delta^2/8\). The signal-to-quantization-noise ratio:

\[ \mathrm{SNR} = \frac{2^{2b}\Delta^2/8}{\Delta^2/12} = \frac{3}{2}\cdot 2^{2b} \;\;\Rightarrow\;\; \boxed{\;\mathrm{SNR}_{\mathrm{dB}} = 6.02\,b + 1.76\;} \]

Six dB per bit — each extra bit halves \(\Delta\), quartering the noise power. This one line prices every ADC datasheet and every audio format.

Worked example How many bits does music need?

Evaluate CD audio (16 bit) and studio audio (24 bit), and find the bit depth needed to span the ~120 dB from the threshold of hearing to the threshold of pain.

Step 1 — CD: \( \mathrm{SNR} = 6.02 \cdot 16 + 1.76 = 98.1\,\mathrm{dB} \). On a 2 V full-scale converter, \(\Delta = 2/2^{16} = 30.5\,\mathrm{\mu V}\) — comparable to the thermal noise of the analog stages that follow.
Step 2 — studio: \( 6.02 \cdot 24 + 1.76 = 146.2\,\mathrm{dB} \) — more than any microphone or ear. The extra bits are headroom: engineers record 20 dB below full scale without touching the noise floor.
Step 3 — full auditory range: \( b = (120 - 1.76)/6.02 = 19.6 \Rightarrow 20\,\mathrm{bits} \). Real 24-bit ADCs deliver ~21 effective bits — just enough, and why 24-bit became the studio standard.
16 bits ≈ 98 dB suits playback; 24 bits ≈ 146 dB (theoretical) buys recording headroom; the ear itself is a ~20-bit instrument.

10.2 Dither: adding noise on purpose

For quiet signals the error is no longer noise-like: a sine hovering around one step gets truncated into a square-ish wave, producing harsh, correlated distortion. The counterintuitive cure is dither: adding a tiny random noise (~1 LSB) before quantizing. The distortion decorrelates into a smooth, benign hiss, and — remarkably — signals below one LSB become audible, encoded in the statistics of the toggling last bit. Every professional mastering chain dithers when reducing 24-bit masters to 16-bit CDs. Trading distortion for noise is a recurring DSP bargain, and dither is its purest form.

11. Multirate DSP — changing the sampling rate in software

Different subsystems want different rates: a Wi-Fi front-end samples at 160 MHz, the demodulator needs 20 MHz; a 48 kHz studio file must play on 44.1 kHz hardware. Multirate techniques (Abtastratenumsetzung) convert rates digitally:

  • Decimation (Dezimierung) by \(M\): keep every \(M\)-th sample. The new Nyquist limit is \(f_s/(2M)\) — any content above it aliases irreversibly into the kept band. Therefore, iron law: lowpass-filter first, then downsample. The filter runs at the high rate but (in efficient polyphase form) only computes the outputs that survive.
  • Interpolation by \(L\): insert \(L-1\) zeros between samples (creating spectral images at multiples of the old \(f_s\)), then lowpass-filter with gain \(L\) to erase the images and "fill in" smooth values between the originals.
  • Rational conversion \(L/M\): interpolate then decimate, sharing one filter. \(44.1 \to 48\) kHz is \(L/M = 160/147\).

Multirate thinking underlies delta-sigma converters (sample at MHz with 1 bit, decimate to kHz with 24 bits — Module 10), SDR channelizers, and every "resample" button in an audio editor.

🚫Classic pitfall: "just take every 4th sample"

Downsampling 48 kHz audio to 12 kHz by simply keeping every 4th sample folds everything between 6 and 24 kHz right on top of the speech band — cymbals become growls, and no later processing can undo it, because two different inputs now produce identical samples. Aliasing destroys information; the anti-alias filter before the decimator is not optional polish, it is the whole trick. (Same law as at the ADC in Fig. 2.1 — decimation is resampling.)

12. Where DSP runs your life

  • Audio coding (MP3/AAC). A filter bank (an MDCT — a cousin of the DFT) splits audio into subbands; a psychoacoustic model computes, per band, how much quantization noise the ear will mask — a loud tone at 1 kHz renders quiet content in neighboring bands literally inaudible. Bits are spent only where the ear would notice their absence: ~10× compression with little audible loss. The compression is not in the math but in the model of you.
  • Active noise cancellation. Headphone microphones capture ambient noise; an adaptive filter (coefficients updated every sample by the LMS algorithm) predicts the noise at the eardrum and emits its negative. The whole loop must run in tens of microseconds — sound travels ~1 cm in 30 µs, so latency directly limits how high in frequency cancellation works. Pure real-time DSP under a hard deadline.
  • Software-defined radio. Digitize a wide chunk of spectrum as early as possible; then mixing (multiply by \(e^{-j\Omega_0 n}\)), channel filtering, decimation and demodulation are all software. One hardware platform receives FM, ADS-B aircraft beacons, or LTE depending on which program is loaded — Section 1's promise made literal. A 30 € USB dongle does this on your desk.
  • Medical. ECG front-ends run a 50 Hz IIR notch plus a linear-phase FIR bandpass (waveform shape is the diagnosis — Section 7.2). Ultrasound machines beamform by digitally delaying and summing dozens of transducer channels. And MRI reconstructs images by, essentially, a giant 2-D inverse FFT of the acquired k-space data — no FFT, no MRI as we know it.

14. Exercises

Work these on paper (and, where tempting, verify with a few lines of Python) before opening the solutions.

E1A 3 kHz tone is sampled at \(f_s = 48\) kHz and a 1024-point DFT is taken. What is the frequency resolution, and in which bin does the tone appear? Is there leakage? easy

Show solution
\( \Delta f = f_s/N = 48\,000/1024 = 46.875\,\mathrm{Hz} \). Bin: \( k = f/\Delta f = 3000/46.875 = 64 \) — exactly an integer, so the tone sits precisely on bin 64 and (with any window) shows no leakage: 64 whole cycles fit in the record. A 3.01 kHz tone would give \(k = 64.21\) — off-bin — and smear. Sanity check: bin 64 of 1024 ≙ \(64/1024 = 1/16\) of \(f_s\) = 3 kHz ✓.

E2A processor performs 100 million real MACs per second. A 4096-point spectrum of a 48 kHz signal must be produced for every block (one block = 4096 samples). Check real-time feasibility for (a) the direct DFT (\(\approx 4N^2\) real MACs) and (b) the FFT (\(\approx 4 N\log_2 N\)). easy

Show solution
Block duration: \(4096/48\,000 = 85.3\,\mathrm{ms}\) — the compute budget.
(a) DFT: \(4 \cdot 4096^2 = 67.1\) million MACs → \(671\,\mathrm{ms}\) per block. Almost 8× too slow — impossible.
(b) FFT: \(4 \cdot 4096 \cdot 12 = 197\,\mathrm{k}\) MACs → \(2.0\,\mathrm{ms}\) per block: uses 2.3 % of the budget, leaving 97 % for the actual application. The same chip is hopeless or bored depending on the algorithm — that is the FFT's entire historical significance in one arithmetic line.

E3For the "leaky integrator" \(y[n] = 0.9\,y[n-1] + 0.1\,x[n]\): find \(H(z)\), the pole, the DC gain, the impulse response, and the approximate time constant in samples. Is it stable? medium

Show solution
Transform: \(Y(1 - 0.9z^{-1}) = 0.1X\) → \(H(z) = \frac{0.1}{1 - 0.9 z^{-1}}\). One pole at \(z = 0.9\): inside the unit circle → stable. DC gain: \(H(1) = 0.1/0.1 = 1\) — sensible for an averager. Impulse response: \(h[n] = 0.1 \cdot 0.9^n u[n]\), a sampled exponential. Time constant: \(0.9^n = e^{-n/\tau}\) → \(\tau = -1/\ln 0.9 \approx 9.5\) samples ≈ \(1/(1-a)\). This two-multiply filter is the ubiquitous "exponential smoothing" of sensor readings, stock charts and load averages — now you know its pole.

E4Compute the 4-point DFT of \(x = \{1, 0, 1, 0\}\) and interpret each bin physically. medium

Show solution
\(X[0] = 1+0+1+0 = 2\). \(X[1] = 1 + 0\cdot(-j) + 1\cdot(-1) + 0 = 0\). \(X[2] = 1 - 0 + 1 - 0 = 2\). \(X[3] = 1 + 0 + 1\cdot(-1) + 0 = 0\).
So \(X = \{2, 0, 2, 0\}\): the signal is a DC level of \(0.5\) (bin 0: \(2/4\)) plus a Nyquist-rate alternation \(\pm 0.5\) (bin 2) — indeed \(x[n] = 0.5 + 0.5(-1)^n\) reproduces \(\{1,0,1,0\}\) exactly ✓. No bin-1/3 content: the record contains no once-per-record oscillation. A two-line signal, a two-line spectrum.

E5A sensor sampled at \(f_s = 1\) kHz suffers from 50 Hz mains hum. Choose the length \(N\) of a moving-average filter that removes the hum and all its harmonics exactly. How much does it attenuate a wanted 10 Hz signal? medium

Show solution
Moving-average nulls sit at \(k \cdot f_s/N\). Demanding the first null at 50 Hz: \(N = f_s/50 = 20\) — one full mains period per average. Then nulls fall at 50, 100, 150, … Hz: every harmonic dies, because each contributes a whole number of cycles that average to zero. At 10 Hz (\(\Omega\)-argument \(\nu = f/f_s = 0.01\)): \( |H| = \left|\frac{\sin(20\pi\nu)}{20\sin(\pi \nu)}\right| = \frac{\sin(0.2\pi)}{20\sin(0.01\pi)} = \frac{0.588}{0.628} = 0.936 \) → only \(-0.58\) dB. A 20-tap filter, designed with one division, kills the interference completely while barely touching the signal — the professional's favorite party trick.

E6Derive \( \mathrm{SNR}_{\mathrm{dB}} = 6.02\,b + 1.76 \) from first principles, stating every assumption. Then explain why a sine at 10 % of full scale measures ~20 dB worse. challenge

Show solution
Assumptions: uniform quantizer, step \(\Delta\); error \(e\) uniformly distributed on \([-\Delta/2, \Delta/2]\) and uncorrelated with the signal (valid for busy signals spanning many steps). Noise power: \( \sigma_e^2 = \frac{1}{\Delta}\int_{-\Delta/2}^{\Delta/2} e^2\,\mathrm{d}e = \frac{\Delta^2}{12} \). Full-scale sine: amplitude \(A = 2^{b-1}\Delta\), power \(P_s = A^2/2 = 2^{2b}\Delta^2/8\). \( \mathrm{SNR} = P_s/\sigma_e^2 = \tfrac{3}{2}\,2^{2b} \); in dB: \(10\log_{10}1.5 + 2b \cdot 10\log_{10}2 = 1.76 + 6.02\,b\) ✓. At 10 % amplitude the signal power drops by \(20\log_{10}10 = 20\) dB while the noise floor \(\Delta^2/12\) stays fixed — SNR follows the signal down. Moral: quantization SNR is a property of the level, not just the converter; recording too quietly throws bits away, which is exactly what recording engineers mean by "use your headroom".

E7A 48 kHz signal must be decimated to 8 kHz (\(M = 6\)) for a speech codec. (a) Specify the anti-alias filter. (b) A colleague skips the filter "because the signal is mostly speech below 4 kHz anyway" — a 15 kHz whistle from a CRT monitor is present at −30 dB. Where does it land, and why is this fatal? challenge

Show solution
(a) New Nyquist: \(8/2 = 4\) kHz. The filter (running at 48 kHz, before ↓6) needs passband ≈ 0–3.4 kHz (speech), stopband from 4 kHz with enough attenuation that aliases fall below the codec's noise floor — typically ≥ 60 dB, e.g. a ~60-tap linear-phase FIR.
(b) Aliasing folds frequencies about multiples of the new \(f_s = 8\) kHz: the 15 kHz whistle appears at \(|15 - 2\cdot 8| = 1\) kHz — dead center of the speech band, at −30 dB: clearly audible, and indistinguishable from a genuine 1 kHz tone after decimation, so no later filter can remove it without removing speech. Filter first is not advice; it is information theory. (Bonus check: the filtered path attenuates 15 kHz by ≥ 60 dB, pushing the alias to −90 dB — inaudible.)
