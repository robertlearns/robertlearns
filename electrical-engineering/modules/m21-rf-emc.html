Above a certain frequency, wires stop being wires: they become waveguides, antennas, and troublemakers. This module is where circuit theory meets Maxwell — transmission lines, reflections, the Smith chart, antennas, and the dark art of making electronics that neither pollutes the airwaves nor gets jammed by them. Every WiFi router, every 5G base station, and every CE-marked product on a European shelf exists because someone mastered exactly this material.

1. When ordinary circuit theory dies — the λ/10 rule

Everything you did in Modules 04 and 05 rested on a hidden assumption: that a voltage applied at one end of a wire appears at the other end instantly and identically. In reality the signal travels as an electromagnetic wave at (roughly) the speed of light. Kirchhoff's laws are the limit of Maxwell's equations for circuits that are electrically small — small compared to the wavelength

\[ \lambda = \frac{v}{f}, \qquad v = \frac{c_0}{\sqrt{\varepsilon_r}} \ \ \text{(in a dielectric)}, \quad c_0 = 3\cdot 10^8\,\mathrm{m/s}. \]

The standard engineering boundary is the λ/10 rule: if the physical size \(l\) of a circuit or connection satisfies \(l < \lambda/10\), voltages and currents are essentially the same everywhere along it at any instant, and you may use lumped-element (konzentrierte Bauelemente) circuit theory. Beyond that, the phase of the wave varies noticeably along the structure and you must switch to distributed (verteilte Elemente) thinking — transmission-line theory.

🔑Key idea: it's the edge, not the clock

For digital signals the relevant frequency is not the clock rate but the spectral content of the edges. A common estimate for the highest significant frequency ("knee frequency") of an edge with rise time \(t_r\) is \(f_{\text{knee}} \approx 0.35/t_r\). A leisurely 10 MHz clock with 100 ps edges is, electromagnetically, a multi-GHz signal. This is why signal integrity (Signalintegrität) became a mainstream digital-design discipline.

Worked example λ/10 boundary for a 100 ps edge

A modern FPGA output switches with \(t_r = 100\,\mathrm{ps}\). Above what trace length on FR-4 (\(v \approx 1.5\cdot 10^8\,\mathrm{m/s}\), i.e. 15 cm/ns) must the trace be treated as a transmission line?

Step 1 — edge bandwidth: \( f_{\text{knee}} \approx 0.35/t_r = 0.35/(100\,\mathrm{ps}) = 3.5\,\mathrm{GHz} \).
Step 2 — wavelength in FR-4: \( \lambda = v/f = 1.5\cdot 10^8 / 3.5\cdot 10^9 \approx 4.3\,\mathrm{cm} \).
Step 3 — λ/10: critical length \( \approx 4.3\,\mathrm{mm} \). Cross-check with the time-domain rule of thumb "critical if longer than 1/6 of the edge's spatial extent": the edge occupies \( t_r \cdot v = 15\,\mathrm{mm} \) of trace, and \(15/6 = 2.5\,\mathrm{mm}\) — same order.
Any trace longer than a few millimetres carrying this edge is a transmission line. On a modern board that is every trace — hence termination resistors everywhere (Section 4).

2. Transmission lines — Leitungstheorie

2.1 The distributed L′C′ ladder

Take any uniform two-conductor structure — coax cable, PCB trace over a ground plane, twisted pair. Every short slice \(\Delta x\) of it has a little series inductance (current loop ⇒ magnetic flux, Module 07) and a little shunt capacitance (two conductors with a dielectric between them). Per unit length we write \(L'\) (H/m) and \(C'\) (F/m), plus loss terms \(R'\) (conductor resistance) and \(G'\) (dielectric leakage):

2.2 The telegrapher's equations

Apply KVL and KCL to one cell of Fig. 2.1 and let \(\Delta x \to 0\) (exactly the limit process of Module 12's field theory, done on a circuit). KVL along the top: the voltage drops by the inductor's \(L'\Delta x \cdot \partial i/\partial t\) plus the resistive drop. KCL at the node: the current drops by what leaks into the capacitor, \(C'\Delta x \cdot \partial u/\partial t\), plus dielectric leakage. Dividing by \(\Delta x\):

\[ \boxed{\;-\frac{\partial u}{\partial x} = R' i + L' \frac{\partial i}{\partial t}, \qquad -\frac{\partial i}{\partial x} = G' u + C' \frac{\partial u}{\partial t}\;} \]

These are the telegrapher's equations (Leitungsgleichungen), derived by Oliver Heaviside in 1876 for undersea telegraph cables. For the lossless case (\(R' = G' = 0\)), differentiate the first with respect to \(x\), substitute the second, and you get the wave equation:

\[ \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2} = L'C' \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial t^2} \quad\Rightarrow\quad u(x,t) = u^+\!\left(t - \tfrac{x}{v}\right) + u^-\!\left(t + \tfrac{x}{v}\right), \qquad v = \frac{1}{\sqrt{L'C'}}. \]

Any shape travels undistorted in both directions at velocity \(v\). For lines with homogeneous dielectric, \(v = c_0/\sqrt{\varepsilon_r}\) regardless of geometry — coax with PE dielectric gives \(v \approx 0.66\,c_0\) ("velocity factor 0.66"), FR-4 microstrip roughly \(0.5\,c_0\), i.e. the handy number 15 cm/ns, or ~6.7 ps per millimetre.

2.3 Characteristic impedance Z₀ — the ratio the wave wants

Substituting the traveling-wave solution back into the telegrapher's equations shows that for a single traveling wave, voltage and current are locked in proportion:

\[ \boxed{\;\frac{u^+}{i^+} = Z_0 = \sqrt{\frac{L'}{C'}}\;} \qquad \text{(lossless line, real number!)} \]

This is the characteristic impedance (Wellenwiderstand). Physically: to charge each successive cell's capacitance through each cell's inductance, the wavefront must carry charge and flux in a fixed ratio — that ratio is \(Z_0\). Three things it is not:

  • It is not a resistance you can measure with an ohmmeter — an infinite (or matched) line looks like \(Z_0\) from its input, but no power is dissipated in "\(Z_0\)"; it is carried away by the wave.
  • It does not depend on line length — only on geometry and dielectric.
  • It is not the DC loop resistance \(R'l\), which is a loss term.
📜Why 50 Ω? The 30/77 compromise

For an air-dielectric coax cable you can optimize the diameter ratio \(D/d\): minimum attenuation occurs at \(Z_0 \approx 77\,\Omega\), maximum power handling (before breakdown) at \(Z_0 \approx 30\,\Omega\), maximum voltage rating at ≈ 60 Ω. In the 1930s, radar engineers split the difference — the geometric mean \(\sqrt{30 \cdot 77} \approx 48\,\Omega\) — and the world standardized on 50 Ω. Cable TV chose 75 Ω instead: reception needs low loss, not power handling, and 75 Ω sits near the loss optimum and near the 73 Ω of a half-wave dipole (Section 8). Two numbers, and you now understand every RF connector catalog.

3. Reflections and standing waves

3.1 The reflection coefficient Γ — derivation

Terminate a line \(Z_0\) with a load \(Z_L\). At the load, two constraints must hold simultaneously: the wave physics demands \(u^+/i^+ = Z_0\) and \(u^-/i^- = -Z_0\) (the backward wave carries current the other way), while Ohm's law at the load demands \(u/i = Z_L\) for the totals \(u = u^+ + u^-\), \(i = i^+ + i^-\). If \(Z_L \neq Z_0\), a single forward wave cannot satisfy both — the load must generate a reflected wave. How big? Divide the two totals:

\[ Z_L = \frac{u^+ + u^-}{\frac{u^+}{Z_0} - \frac{u^-}{Z_0}} = Z_0\,\frac{1 + u^-/u^+}{1 - u^-/u^+}. \]

Solve for the ratio \(\underline{\Gamma} = u^-/u^+\):

\[ \boxed{\;\underline{\Gamma} = \frac{Z_L - Z_0}{Z_L + Z_0}\;} \qquad |\underline{\Gamma}| \le 1 \ \text{for passive loads}. \]

Three canonical cases, worth memorizing with their bounce intuition:

LoadΓBounce intuition
Matched, \(Z_L = Z_0\) (angepasst)0the load "feels like more line" — the wave is fully absorbed, no echo
Open, \(Z_L \to \infty\) (Leerlauf)+1current must be zero at the end; the reflected wave cancels the current and doubles the voltage
Short, \(Z_L = 0\) (Kurzschluss)−1voltage must be zero; the wave flips sign and bounces back inverted, current doubles
💡Intuition: the rope-and-wall picture

Send a pulse down a rope. Tied to a rigid wall (short circuit): the pulse reflects inverted. Free end (open circuit): it reflects upright, and the free end whips to double amplitude. Tied to a second rope of identical weight (matched): the pulse passes through, no echo. Impedance mismatch on a cable behaves exactly like a change of rope density — reflections are the universal behavior of waves at boundaries, from ropes to ultrasound to light hitting glass.

3.2 Standing waves, VSWR, return loss

In sinusoidal steady state the forward and reflected waves superpose along the line. Where they arrive in phase, the amplitude envelope is \( |u^+|(1 + |\Gamma|) \); a quarter wavelength away they oppose, giving \( |u^+|(1 - |\Gamma|) \). The interference pattern does not move — a standing wave (stehende Welle) with maxima and minima locked to the load position, repeating every \(\lambda/2\). The classic quality measure is the voltage standing wave ratio:

\[ \boxed{\;\mathrm{VSWR} = \frac{U_{max}}{U_{min}} = \frac{1 + |\underline{\Gamma}|}{1 - |\underline{\Gamma}|}\;} \in [1, \infty). \]

VSWR = 1 means matched; VSWR = ∞ means total reflection. An antenna-tuner's "SWR meter" measures exactly this by sampling the forward and reflected waves with a directional coupler. Equivalent, and preferred in datasheets, is the return loss (Rückflussdämpfung):

\[ RL = -20 \log_{10} |\underline{\Gamma}| \ \ \mathrm{dB} \qquad\text{(bigger = better; matched} \to \infty\text{)}. \]

Since power goes with amplitude squared, the reflected power fraction is \(|\Gamma|^2\) and the power delivered to the load is \(1 - |\Gamma|^2\).

Worked example Γ, VSWR and return loss for a complex load

A load \(Z_L = 25 + \mathrm{j}25\,\Omega\) terminates a 50 Ω line. Find \(\underline{\Gamma}\), VSWR, return loss, and the reflected power fraction.

Step 1 — reflection coefficient: \[ \underline{\Gamma} = \frac{(25 + \mathrm{j}25) - 50}{(25 + \mathrm{j}25) + 50} = \frac{-25 + \mathrm{j}25}{75 + \mathrm{j}25} = \frac{-1 + \mathrm{j}}{3 + \mathrm{j}} = \frac{(-1+\mathrm{j})(3-\mathrm{j})}{10} = -0.2 + \mathrm{j}\,0.4 . \]
Step 2 — magnitude and angle: \( |\underline{\Gamma}| = \sqrt{0.04 + 0.16} = 0.447\), \(\ \arg\underline{\Gamma} = 117^\circ \).
Step 3 — VSWR: \( \mathrm{VSWR} = \frac{1 + 0.447}{1 - 0.447} = \frac{1.447}{0.553} \approx 2.6 \). An SWR meter would read "2.6 : 1".
Step 4 — return loss and power: \( RL = -20\log_{10} 0.447 = 7.0\,\mathrm{dB} \); reflected power \( |\Gamma|^2 = 20\,\% \), delivered \(80\,\%\).
\(\underline{\Gamma} = 0.447\angle 117^\circ\), VSWR ≈ 2.6, RL = 7 dB — a poor match by modern standards (specs typically demand RL > 10 dB, i.e. VSWR < 2).

🚫Classic pitfall: "reflected power heats the transmitter"

Reflected power does not automatically burn up the source. What actually happens: the reflected wave re-reflects at the source (if the source is also mismatched) and the steady state settles into whatever the total circuit dictates. The real dangers of high VSWR are (1) voltage maxima on the line exceeding breakdown, (2) a transmitter output stage seeing an impedance far from its design load and overheating or folding back power, and (3) in the exam: forgetting that \(|\Gamma| > 1\) is impossible for passive loads — if you compute one, you made a sign error.

4. Time domain: bounce diagrams and signal integrity

Now the digital designer's view. Launch a voltage step into a mismatched line and watch the echoes. A driver \(U_q\) with source resistance \(R_S\) launches, by voltage divider against the line's input impedance (which is exactly \(Z_0\) until the first echo returns!),

\[ u_1 = U_q \frac{Z_0}{R_S + Z_0}. \]

This step travels for one line delay \(T = l/v\), reflects at the load with \(\Gamma_L\), travels back, reflects at the source with \(\Gamma_S = \frac{R_S - Z_0}{R_S + Z_0}\), and so on — each end's voltage is the running sum of every wave that has arrived. The bookkeeping tool is the bounce diagram (Impulsfahrplan — "pulse timetable", a wonderfully German word):

Worked example The first three reflections, by hand

A 3.3 V step from a driver with \(R_S = 25\,\Omega\) enters a \(Z_0 = 50\,\Omega\), \(T = 1\,\mathrm{ns}\) trace, open at the far end (a CMOS gate input ≈ open). Compute the load-end voltage for the first few nanoseconds.

Step 1 — reflection coefficients and launch: \( \Gamma_L = +1 \) (open), \( \Gamma_S = \frac{25-50}{25+50} = -\tfrac13 \), launched step \( u_1 = 3.3 \cdot \frac{50}{75} = 2.20\,\mathrm{V} \).
Step 2 — first arrival, t = T: incident 2.20 V + reflected \(1 \cdot 2.20\) V ⇒ load jumps to 4.40 V — an overshoot beyond the 3.3 V supply!
Step 3 — echo returns, t = 2T (source), t = 3T (load): the 2.20 V echo hits the source, re-reflects as \( -\tfrac13 \cdot 2.20 = -0.733\,\mathrm{V} \); source end reads \(2.20 + 2.20 - 0.733 = 3.67\,\mathrm{V}\). At \(t = 3T\) that −0.733 V wave arrives at the open end and doubles: load drops by 1.467 V to 2.93 V.
Step 4 — the pattern: each round trip multiplies the circulating wave by \(\Gamma_S \Gamma_L = -\tfrac13\): the load rings 4.40 → 2.93 → 3.42 → … converging geometrically on \(U_q = 3.30\,\mathrm{V}\) (open load ⇒ full source voltage finally).
Ringing between 4.4 V and 2.9 V for several nanoseconds — enough to double-clock an edge-triggered input and to overstress protection diodes. This is why fast digital lines are terminated.

🔑Key idea: why termination resistors exist

You can kill reflections at either end. Series termination (Serienterminierung): make \(R_S + R_{driver} = Z_0\); the half-amplitude step doubles at the open far end to full swing, and the returning echo dies at the source. Costs one resistor, no static power — the default for point-to-point digital (DDR address lines, clocks). Parallel termination: put \(Z_0\) at the far end; clean incident wave, but continuous DC current — used where the line must be matched at all times (high-speed serial links use variants: Thévenin, AC-coupled, on-die termination). The 120 Ω resistors at both ends of every CAN bus are exactly this.

5. The Smith chart — the RF engineer's map

Impedance along a mismatched line is a moving target: the input impedance of a line of length \(l\) terminated in \(Z_L\) is

\[ Z_{in} = Z_0\, \frac{Z_L + \mathrm{j} Z_0 \tan\beta l}{Z_0 + \mathrm{j} Z_L \tan\beta l}, \]

which repeats every \(\lambda/2\) and swaps short↔open every \(\lambda/4\). Computing with this in 1939 (no calculators) was misery, so Phillip Smith drew the calculation instead. The Smith chart (Smith-Diagramm) is simply the complex Γ-plane — a unit circle, since \(|\Gamma| \le 1\) — with a curvilinear grid showing which normalized impedance \(z = Z/Z_0\) corresponds to each point via \(\Gamma = (z-1)/(z+1)\). How to read it:

  • Center = \(\Gamma = 0\) = perfect match (\(z = 1\), i.e. 50 Ω). Distance from center = \(|\Gamma|\); the rim = total reflection.
  • Circles of constant resistance \(r\): nested circles all tangent at the right edge. Arcs of constant reactance \(x\): upper half inductive (+j), lower half capacitive (−j). The horizontal axis is purely resistive; its left end is the short (\(\Gamma = -1\)), right end the open (\(\Gamma = +1\)).
  • Moving along a lossless line toward the generator rotates the point clockwise around the center at constant \(|\Gamma|\) — one full turn per \(\lambda/2\). (That's the \(e^{-\mathrm{j}2\beta l}\) factor the reflected wave picks up.) Circles about the center are therefore constant-VSWR circles.
  • Adding a series L or C moves along the constant-\(r\) circle; adding a shunt element moves along a constant-conductance circle (the chart mirrored left-right). Matching = choreographing moves that end at the center.

6. Impedance matching — Anpassung

Why match? Two reasons, one from each half of this course. From Module 04: a source delivers maximum power into a conjugate-matched load, \(Z_L = Z_i^*\) — and at RF, where every dB of signal is precious, you cannot afford the mismatch loss. From Section 3: matching eliminates reflections, hence no standing waves, no ringing, no frequency ripple from echo interference. The generic toolkit:

6.1 L-networks

Two reactive elements (no loss!) in an L shape can match any two resistances \(R_{hi} > R_{lo}\). Design formulas — define the network Q:

\[ Q = \sqrt{\frac{R_{hi}}{R_{lo}} - 1}, \qquad X_{ser} = Q\,R_{lo} \ \ (\text{series side, at } R_{lo}), \qquad X_{par} = \frac{R_{hi}}{Q} \ \ (\text{shunt side, across } R_{hi}). \]

Choose L or C for each position to taste (the choice flips which frequencies are rejected — a low-pass L-match doubles as a harmonic filter for transmitters). The catch: the match is exact at one frequency, with fractional bandwidth roughly \(1/Q\). The 100 Ω → 50 Ω example above is this formula with \(Q = 1\).

6.2 The quarter-wave transformer

Set \(l = \lambda/4\) in the \(Z_{in}\) formula: \(\tan\beta l \to \infty\) and

\[ \boxed{\;Z_{in} = \frac{Z_0^2}{Z_L} \quad\Rightarrow\quad Z_T = \sqrt{Z_0 \, Z_L}\;} \]

A quarter-wave line of impedance \(Z_T\) equal to the geometric mean makes \(Z_L\) look like \(Z_0\). Intuition: the reflections from the two ends of the transformer are equal in size and, after the extra \(2 \times \lambda/4 = \lambda/2\) round trip, opposite in phase — they cancel.

Worked example Quarter-wave transformer for a patch antenna

A 2.45 GHz microstrip patch antenna presents \(Z_L = 200\,\Omega\) at its edge. Design a quarter-wave microstrip transformer to a 50 Ω feed line (FR-4, effective permittivity \(\varepsilon_{eff} \approx 3.3\) for a narrow trace).

Step 1 — transformer impedance: \( Z_T = \sqrt{50 \cdot 200} = \sqrt{10\,000} = 100\,\Omega \) — a conveniently thin trace.
Step 2 — guided wavelength: \( \lambda = \frac{c_0}{f\sqrt{\varepsilon_{eff}}} = \frac{3\cdot 10^8}{2.45 \cdot 10^9 \cdot \sqrt{3.3}} \approx \frac{122.4\,\mathrm{mm}}{1.82} \approx 67\,\mathrm{mm} \).
Step 3 — physical length: \( l = \lambda/4 \approx 16.8\,\mathrm{mm} \).
A 100 Ω, ≈ 17 mm trace section. Sanity check: at 2.45 GHz exactly, \(Z_{in} = 100^2/200 = 50\,\Omega\) ✓. Narrowband by nature (the length is only λ/4 at one frequency) — fine for WiFi's few-percent band.

Stub matching (Stichleitungsanpassung): instead of discrete L and C, short lengths of open- or short-circuited line provide any desired reactance (\(Z_{in} = \mathrm{j}Z_0\tan\beta l\) for a shorted stub) — pure copper, no components, standard above a few GHz where lumped parts stop behaving. Design is the same two-move Smith-chart dance.

7. S-parameters — how RF networks are actually characterized

Low-frequency two-port descriptions (Z, Y, h parameters, Module 05) require terminating ports in opens or shorts to measure. At GHz frequencies you cannot build a clean open (fringing capacitance, radiation) or short (lead inductance), and many devices oscillate when terminated that way. The fix: describe the network by what it does to waves, with every port terminated in the system impedance \(Z_0\) — the one termination that is easy and stable. The scattering parameters (Streuparameter) relate incoming wave amplitudes \(a_i\) to outgoing \(b_i\):

\[ \begin{pmatrix} b_1 \\ b_2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} S_{11} & S_{12} \\ S_{21} & S_{22} \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} a_1 \\ a_2 \end{pmatrix} \]
  • \(S_{11}\): input reflection — precisely the \(\underline{\Gamma}\) of Section 3, seen at port 1 with port 2 matched.
  • \(S_{21}\): forward transmission — gain of an amplifier, insertion loss of a filter or cable.
  • \(S_{12}\): reverse transmission (isolation — you want ≈ 0 for an amplifier, or it may oscillate).
  • \(S_{22}\): output reflection.

All are complex and frequency-dependent; datasheets quote magnitudes in dB: \(20\log_{10}|S_{ij}|\). Because S-parameters are wave amplitude ratios, powers go with the square:

\(|S_{11}|\) in dBreflected powerVSWRverdict for an antenna port
−3 dB50 %5.8broken
−6 dB25 %3.0poor
−10 dB10 %1.92the standard "good enough" spec line

The instrument that measures S-parameters is the vector network analyzer (VNA, Netzwerkanalysator) — it launches a swept sinusoid, separates forward and reflected waves with directional couplers, and records complex ratios. Before measuring, you calibrate with known standards (short, open, load, through) to move the measurement reference plane to the tips of your cables; removing the effect of fixturing beyond that plane is called de-embedding. A 100 € NanoVNA does today what filled a rack in 1990 — buy one; it makes this entire module tangible.

8. Antennas — Antennen

8.1 How a wire radiates

A static charge has a static field; a charge in uniform motion has a field that moves along with it. But accelerate a charge and the field lines cannot update everywhere instantly — the "news" of the changed position propagates outward at \(c\), and the kink between old and new field patterns is a transverse pulse of E and H that carries energy away forever: radiation (Module 12 gives the honest field-theory version). An antenna is a structure shaped so that the accelerating charges (AC currents) create kinks that reinforce in useful directions. Radiated power grows with frequency (for a short wire, \(P \propto f^2 l^2 I^2\)) — which is why your 50 Hz mains cord doesn't radiate measurably but the same cord carrying 100 MHz noise is an excellent transmitter. Remember that sentence; it is half of EMC.

8.2 The half-wave dipole and the monopole

Cut a wire to \(\lambda/2\) total, feed it in the middle: the half-wave dipole (Halbwellendipol). It is a resonant structure — the current standing wave fits exactly, zero at the tips, maximum at the feed — so the reactance cancels and the feed sees a nearly pure resistance:

\[ Z_{dipole} \approx 73\,\Omega \ (+\,\mathrm{j}42\,\Omega \text{ exactly at } l = \lambda/2\text{; trimmed} \approx 5\,\% \text{ shorter for resonance}). \]

Its pattern is a donut around the wire: maximum broadside, zero off the ends. Gain over an isotropic radiator: 2.15 dBi. Slice a dipole in half and stand it on a conducting plane — the monopole (Monopol/Viertelwellenstrahler): the plane mirrors the missing half. Length \(\lambda/4\), impedance half a dipole's ≈ 36 Ω, and it explains every car antenna and the ground-plane pads under WiFi PCB antennas. The ground plane is half the antenna — a "bad ground" is a bad antenna.

8.3 Gain, directivity, EIRP, polarization

Directivity \(D\): how much stronger the antenna radiates in its best direction than an ideal isotropic radiator spreading the same power evenly. Gain \(G = \eta D\) additionally counts ohmic/dielectric losses (η is often > 90 % for good antennas); quoted in dBi (relative to isotropic) or dBd (relative to a dipole, = dBi − 2.15). Antennas don't create power — they focus it, trading beamwidth for gain. EIRP (equivalent isotropically radiated power) \(= P_{tx} \cdot G_{tx}\) is what regulations limit (e.g. 100 mW = 20 dBm EIRP for 2.4 GHz WiFi in the EU). Polarization (Polarisation): the orientation of the E-field — linear (vertical/horizontal) or circular (GPS, to survive orientation changes and ionospheric rotation). Cross-polarized antennas can lose 20+ dB: mounting a receiver antenna horizontal under a vertical transmitter is a classic field failure.

9. Propagation and the link budget

9.1 Free-space path loss — derived in three lines

An isotropic transmitter of power \(P_t\) spreads its energy over an ever-growing sphere; at distance \(d\) the power density is \(S = P_t/(4\pi d^2)\) — the inverse-square law, pure geometry, no absorption. A receiving antenna collects power proportional to its effective aperture, which for an isotropic antenna is \(A_e = \lambda^2/4\pi\) (a beautiful result from reciprocity — bigger wavelengths are "easier to catch"). Received power:

\[ P_r = S \cdot A_e = \frac{P_t}{4\pi d^2}\cdot\frac{\lambda^2}{4\pi} = P_t \left(\frac{\lambda}{4\pi d}\right)^2 . \]

The factor is the free-space path loss (Freiraumdämpfung) \( \mathrm{FSPL} = (4\pi d/\lambda)^2 \). With antenna gains at both ends we get the Friis transmission equation (Harald Friis, Bell Labs 1946):

\[ \boxed{\;P_r = P_t\, G_t\, G_r \left(\frac{\lambda}{4\pi d}\right)^{\!2}\;} \qquad\text{in dB: } P_r = P_t + G_t + G_r - \underbrace{\left(20\log_{10} d_{km} + 20\log_{10} f_{MHz} + 32.45\right)}_{\mathrm{FSPL\ in\ dB}} . \]

Note the structure: +6 dB per doubling of distance or frequency of loss. The frequency term is not absorption — it's the shrinking capture aperture of a fixed-gain receive antenna. (Fix the antenna areas instead, as satellite dishes do, and higher frequency actually helps.)

Worked example How far does WiFi really reach?

2.4 GHz WiFi: \(P_t = 100\,\mathrm{mW} = 20\,\mathrm{dBm}\), antenna gains 2 dBi each, receiver sensitivity \(-85\,\mathrm{dBm}\) (for a mid-rate modulation). Free-space range?

Step 1 — allowed path loss: \( L_{max} = 20 + 2 + 2 - (-85) = 109\,\mathrm{dB} \).
Step 2 — FSPL at 1 m reference: \( \mathrm{FSPL}(1\,\mathrm{m}) = 20\log_{10}(4\pi \cdot 1 / 0.125) \approx 40\,\mathrm{dB} \). Every decade of distance adds 20 dB.
Step 3 — solve: \( 20\log_{10}(d/1\,\mathrm{m}) = 109 - 40 = 69\,\mathrm{dB} \Rightarrow d = 10^{3.45} \approx 2.8\,\mathrm{km} \).
≈ 2.8 km in free space — yet your router dies two rooms away. The difference is walls (5–15 dB each), multipath fading (plan 20–30 dB margin), body loss, and interference. Link budgets are exercises in honest pessimism.
🧪Interactive — Friis link budget simulation

Received power vs. distance on a log axis — a straight line at −20 dB/decade. The dashed horizontal line is the receiver sensitivity; where the curve crosses it is your free-space range. Watch the range shrink as you raise the frequency at fixed gains, and grow 10× for every 20 dB you add to the budget.

Reality beyond free space: signals reach the receiver over many paths (reflections off walls, ground, buildings) that interfere — multipath fading (Mehrwegeausbreitung): moving half a wavelength can swing the signal 30 dB (why your car radio goes quiet at exactly one position at the traffic light). Statistics (Rayleigh/Rice fading), OFDM, and MIMO are the countermeasures — Module 22's territory. And "line of sight" needs more than a visual line: most of the energy travels in the first Fresnel zone, an ellipsoid of radius \(r = \sqrt{\lambda d_1 d_2/(d_1+d_2)}\) around the direct ray (≈ 17 m mid-path for a 10 km link at 2.4 GHz!) — clip it with a treetop and the "clear" link loses several dB.

10. EMC — Elektromagnetische Verträglichkeit

Everything so far was about signals you want. EMC is the same physics applied to signals you don't: every product must (a) not disturb others — emission (Störaussendung) — and (b) function despite disturbances — immunity (Störfestigkeit). In the EU this is law (the EMC Directive behind the CE mark); in engineering it is the discipline that decides whether your prototype becomes a product. Every EMC problem — every single one — decomposes into the model:

The four coupling paths, each with its physics and its fix:

  1. Conducted / common-impedance coupling (galvanische Kopplung): two circuits share a piece of conductor (a "ground" wire, a supply trace). Circuit A's return current × shared impedance = voltage injected into circuit B. Fix: don't share — star grounding for low frequency, and at high frequency a solid ground plane, whose spreading inductance is minuscule. This is why "ground" bounce plagues bad layouts (recall the ground symbol is a fiction — copper has impedance, Module 04's ideal node does not exist at 100 MHz).
  2. Capacitive coupling (kapazitive Kopplung): parasitic capacitance between nearby conductors injects current \(i = C_{par}\,\mathrm{d}V/\mathrm{d}t\). Driven by fast voltage edges; the victim sees more the higher its impedance. Fix: distance, and a grounded shield between aggressor and victim (the injected current drains to ground instead) — the reason sensitive analog nodes get guard traces.
  3. Inductive coupling (induktive Kopplung): a changing current's magnetic field threads the victim's loop, inducing \(u = M\,\mathrm{d}I/\mathrm{d}t\) (Module 07). Driven by fast current edges; received in proportion to loop area. Fix: minimize loop area on both sides — run signal and return adjacent, use ground planes (the return current flows directly under the trace), and twist pairs: each twist reverses the induced polarity, cancelling pickup. Twisted pair is loop-area engineering, nothing more.
  4. Radiated coupling (Strahlungskopplung): at distances beyond ≈ λ/2π the field detaches and propagates as a wave; cables and slots act as the antennas of Section 8 — unintentionally. Fix: kill the antennas (short cables, filtered at entry) and shield (Section 12).
⚠️Ask "where does the return current flow?"

The single most valuable EMC habit. Every signal current returns to its source — through some path. At DC it takes the path of least resistance; above ≈ 100 kHz it takes the path of least inductance, which means directly underneath its trace in the ground plane (that hugging minimizes loop area automatically). Cut a slot in the plane and the return current must detour around it — you have built a large loop, i.e. an antenna, out of pure absent-mindedness. Split ground planes "for cleanliness" cause more EMC failures than they cure; if you must split, never route signals across the gap.

11. Decoupling and PCB layout — EMC you can solder

11.1 The decoupling capacitor, explained properly

When a logic gate switches, it demands an ampere-scale current spike within nanoseconds. The power supply is centimetres (= many nanohenries) away and cannot respond in time: \(u = L\,\mathrm{d}i/\mathrm{d}t\) with 10 nH and 1 A/ns is a 10 V droop — game over. The decoupling capacitor (Abblockkondensator) is a local charge reservoir: placed millimetres from the IC's supply pins, it delivers the spike over a tiny loop and gets refilled slowly by the supply. Its two jobs: hold the local voltage up, and — the EMC job — keep the high-frequency current loop small so it can't couple or radiate.

But a real capacitor is a series C–L–R chain: its leads and internal structure add an equivalent series inductance (ESL, ~1 nH for an 0603 ceramic). Impedance:

\[ |Z(f)| = \left| \frac{1}{\mathrm{j}\omega C} + \mathrm{j}\omega L_{ESL} + R_{ESR} \right|, \qquad f_{res} = \frac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{L_{ESL}\, C}} . \]

Below the self-resonant frequency it is a capacitor; above, it is an inductor — a 100 nF ceramic with 1 nH resonates at \( f_{res} = 1/(2\pi\sqrt{10^{-9}\cdot 10^{-7}}) \approx 16\,\mathrm{MHz} \) and is inductive at the frequencies of your 100 MHz clock's harmonics. Countermeasures: smaller packages (less ESL), shortest possible via connections, and paralleling — many small capacitors halve the ESL with each doubling of count. (Paralleling different values, e.g. 100 nF ∥ 1 nF, broadens coverage but creates an anti-resonance peak between the two resonances where the pair is briefly worse — modern practice often prefers many identical 100 nF parts plus the plane capacitance.) Rule of thumb that survives all fashion cycles: one ~100 nF ceramic per supply pin, as close as physically possible, via straight to the plane.

11.2 PCB layout rules for EMC

  • Use an unbroken ground plane adjacent to every signal layer. It gives controlled \(Z_0\), minimal loop areas, and low common impedance — one measure that attacks three coupling paths at once.
  • Keep fast loops tiny: the switching loop of a DC/DC converter (switch–diode–input cap) is the #1 emission source on most boards; place those three parts touching each other.
  • Route critical signals first (clocks, RF, resets), short and over solid plane; never across plane splits; guard analog inputs.
  • Filter at the border: every cable leaving the enclosure is an antenna. π-filters, ferrites, and ESD diodes belong at the connector, not 5 cm inland.
  • Slow down what you can: use the slowest edge rate that still works (series resistors, driver strength settings). Emissions follow \( \mathrm{d}i/\mathrm{d}t \) — free EMC margin.
💡Intuition: EMC is transmission lines + antennas, replayed as a horror film

Nothing in Sections 10–12 is new physics. A noisy trace over a slot is a transmission line with its return path amputated. A heatsink is a patch antenna at some frequency. An enclosure seam is a slot antenna. Every EMC "mystery" yields to the questions this module already taught: What is the loop? What is the wavelength? Where does the return current flow? What is the antenna?

12. Shielding, filtering, standards & testing

12.1 Shielding and the treachery of apertures

A conductive enclosure (Schirmung) attenuates fields by reflection (impedance mismatch between wave and metal — the same Γ physics as Section 3!) and absorption (skin-effect decay inside the metal, Module 12). Solid metal gives 100+ dB effortlessly. The problem is never the metal — it is the holes. A slot of length \(l\) behaves as a slot antenna (Babinet's complement of a dipole): it radiates efficiently as \(l\) approaches \(\lambda/2\), and a useful estimate of the shielding effectiveness that remains is

\[ SE \approx 20 \log_{10}\!\left(\frac{\lambda}{2\,l}\right) \mathrm{dB} \quad (l < \lambda/2), \]

reaching 0 dB — no shield at all — at \(l = \lambda/2\). A 15 cm seam is resonant at 1 GHz. That is why RF enclosures use conductive gaskets, why shielded rooms have finger-stock doors, and why one long thin gap is far worse than many small round holes of the same total area. Cable shields continue the enclosure: a coax shield grounded through a 5 cm pigtail has nanohenries in series — at 100 MHz the pigtail, not the shield, sets the performance (360° gland terminations exist for this reason).

12.2 Filtering, common mode, ESD

Noise on cables travels in two modes: differential (out on one wire, back on the other — small loop) and common mode (Gleichtakt: same direction on all wires, returning through the earth/chassis — a huge loop, hence the dominant radiator above ~30 MHz). The common-mode choke (Gleichtaktdrossel) — both conductors wound on one ferrite core — presents high impedance to common mode while differential flux cancels: your laptop brick's mains filter and the ferrite blob on the monitor cable are exactly this. Feedthrough capacitors mounted in the shield wall filter right at the boundary with near-zero ESL. And ESD/surge (Überspannungsschutz): human-body discharges reach 8–15 kV with sub-ns edges (an EMC event par excellence); TVS diodes at every external connector, with short fat traces to chassis, are the standard armor — IEC 61000-4-2 defines the test gun.

12.3 Standards and the test day

The EU EMC Directive 2014/30/EU makes conformity mandatory for the CE mark; harmonized standards define the numbers — emissions largely per CISPR (e.g. CISPR 32/EN 55032 for multimedia: Class A industrial vs. the ~10 dB stricter Class B residential limits), immunity per the IEC 61000-4-x series (ESD, radiated fields, burst, surge). The measurement narrative, so it's not abstract: your device sits on a turntable in an anechoic chamber (Absorberhalle — a shielded room lined with pyramidal ferrite/foam absorbers, a lab-made free space). A calibrated antenna 3 m or 10 m away feeds an EMI receiver that sweeps 30 MHz–1 GHz+ while the table rotates and the antenna height scans, hunting the worst case. Emission peaks poking above the limit line are your clocks' harmonics, each traceable to a loop or cable you now know how to fix. Test-lab days cost four figures — which is why the habits of Sections 10–11 are cheaper than any retrofit. Radiated immunity is the mirror image: the chamber blasts your product with 3–10 V/m while you watch it for misbehavior.

14. Exercises

Work these on paper — Γ and dB arithmetic must become reflex.

E1A 10 cm microstrip trace on FR-4 (\(\varepsilon_{eff} = 3.24\)) carries a 1.8 GHz signal. Find the propagation velocity, the wavelength, the trace's electrical length in wavelengths, and its delay. easy

Show solution
\( v = c_0/\sqrt{3.24} = 3\cdot10^8/1.8 = 1.67\cdot 10^8\,\mathrm{m/s} \). \( \lambda = v/f = 1.67\cdot 10^8 / 1.8\cdot 10^9 = 9.3\,\mathrm{cm} \). Electrical length: \( 10/9.3 \approx 1.08\,\lambda \) — more than a full wavelength, emphatically a transmission line (λ/10 would be 9.3 mm!). Delay: \( \tau = l/v = 0.1/1.67\cdot10^8 = 0.60\,\mathrm{ns} \). Sanity check with the 6 ps/mm rule: 10 cm × 6 ps/mm = 0.6 ns ✓.

E2A 75 Ω TV antenna is connected (lazily) to a 50 Ω receiver input. Compute Γ, VSWR, return loss, and the fraction of power lost to the mismatch. easy

Show solution
\( \Gamma = \frac{75-50}{75+50} = 0.2 \), \( \mathrm{VSWR} = \frac{1.2}{0.8} = 1.5 \), \( RL = -20\log_{10}0.2 = 14\,\mathrm{dB} \). Reflected power: \( |\Gamma|^2 = 4\,\% \) → mismatch loss \( -10\log_{10}(0.96) = 0.18\,\mathrm{dB} \). Remark: 0.18 dB is negligible for TV reception — which is why 50/75 Ω adapters often contain no matching network at all. Mismatch matters when Γ is large or when reflections cause ripple/ghosting, not for every small deviation.

E3Design a quarter-wave coaxial transformer to feed a 73 Ω dipole from 50 Ω coax at 435 MHz (amateur band). The transformer coax has velocity factor 0.66. Give \(Z_T\) and the physical length. medium

Show solution
\( Z_T = \sqrt{50 \cdot 73} = \sqrt{3650} \approx 60.4\,\Omega \) — awkward, but 60 Ω coax exists; in practice one often accepts VSWR 1.46 instead, or uses a 75 Ω section for partial matching. Wavelength in the cable: \( \lambda = 0.66 \cdot c_0/f = 0.66 \cdot 0.690\,\mathrm{m} = 0.455\,\mathrm{m} \) → \( l = \lambda/4 = 11.4\,\mathrm{cm} \). Check: \( Z_{in} = 60.4^2/73 = 50.0\,\Omega \) ✓. Note the length is λ/4 in the cable, not in air — forgetting the velocity factor is the classic build error.

E4A 5 V step from a source with \(R_S = 75\,\Omega\) drives a 50 Ω line, open at the far end. Tabulate the load-end voltage after the 1st, 2nd, and 3rd arrivals, and give the final value. medium

Show solution
Launch: \( u_1 = 5\cdot\frac{50}{125} = 2\,\mathrm{V} \); \( \Gamma_L = +1 \), \( \Gamma_S = \frac{75-50}{125} = +0.2 \). Load arrivals add \( u_1(\Gamma_S\Gamma_L)^k (1+\Gamma_L) \): at \(T\): \(2\cdot 2 = 4.00\,\mathrm{V}\); at \(3T\): \(+2\cdot 0.2\cdot 2 = +0.80 \Rightarrow 4.80\,\mathrm{V}\); at \(5T\): \(+2\cdot 0.04\cdot2 = +0.16 \Rightarrow 4.96\,\mathrm{V}\). Final value: open load, no DC current → \(5\,\mathrm{V}\) exactly. Because \(\Gamma_S > 0\), the approach is monotonic (a "slow staircase"), not ringing — an over-damped-looking edge on the oscilloscope. Ringing needs reflection coefficients of opposite sign at the two ends.

E5A 433 MHz remote transmits 10 dBm through a 0 dBi antenna. The receiver (0 dBi antenna) needs −100 dBm. Compute the received power at 50 m free space and the link margin; then the maximum free-space range. medium

Show solution
FSPL(50 m) \( = 20\log_{10}(50) + 20\log_{10}(433\cdot10^6) - 147.55 = 34.0 + 172.7 - 147.55 = 59.1\,\mathrm{dB} \). \( P_r = 10 - 59.1 = -49.1\,\mathrm{dBm} \) → margin \( = -49.1-(-100) \approx 51\,\mathrm{dB} \). Huge — as expected for a garage-door range check. Max range: allowed FSPL = 110 dB → \( 20\log_{10} d = 110 + 147.55 - 172.7 = 84.8 \Rightarrow d \approx 17\,\mathrm{km} \). Reality: buildings, ground reflection and a 20–30 dB fade margin shrink this to hundreds of metres — but it shows why sub-GHz ISM bands (433/868 MHz) are loved for IoT: at 2.4 GHz the same budget gives ~5× less range (FSPL +15 dB).

E6Match a 10 Ω electrically-short antenna to 50 Ω at 27 MHz with an L-network (series element at the antenna, shunt element on the 50 Ω side). Find Q, both reactances, and component values. challenge

Show solution
\( Q = \sqrt{50/10 - 1} = 2 \). Series side (at \(R_{lo} = 10\,\Omega\)): \( X_{ser} = Q R_{lo} = 20\,\Omega \). Choose an inductor: \( L = X/\omega = 20/(2\pi\cdot 27\cdot 10^6) = 118\,\mathrm{nH} \). Shunt side (across \(R_{hi} = 50\,\Omega\)): \( X_{par} = R_{hi}/Q = 25\,\Omega \). Choose a capacitor: \( C = 1/(\omega\cdot 25) = 236\,\mathrm{pF} \). Verify: \(10 + \mathrm{j}20\,\Omega\) in parallel-with-C: admittance \( \frac{1}{10+\mathrm{j}20} + \mathrm{j}\frac{1}{25} = \frac{10-\mathrm{j}20}{500} + \mathrm{j}0.04 = 0.02 + \mathrm{j}0\ \mathrm{S} = 1/50\,\Omega \) ✓. Bandwidth ≈ \( f/Q \approx 13\,\mathrm{MHz} \) — comfortably wide here; matching 1 Ω would need Q = 7 and get touchy. (A real short antenna also has capacitive reactance to cancel first — this exercise matched only the radiation resistance.)

E7An enclosure has a 5 cm ventilation slot. (a) At what frequency does the slot leak like a half-wave antenna? (b) Estimate its shielding effectiveness at 1 GHz. (c) The same open area is provided as five 1 cm slots — new SE at 1 GHz? What does this teach about seam design? challenge

Show solution
(a) \( l = \lambda/2 \Rightarrow \lambda = 10\,\mathrm{cm} \Rightarrow f = 3\,\mathrm{GHz} \) — right in the WiFi/Bluetooth/radar neighborhood. (b) At 1 GHz, \( \lambda = 30\,\mathrm{cm} \): \( SE \approx 20\log_{10}\!\left(\frac{30}{2\cdot 5}\right) = 20\log_{10}3 \approx 9.5\,\mathrm{dB} \) — a nearly worthless shield. (c) Each 1 cm slot: \( SE \approx 20\log_{10}(30/2) = 23.5\,\mathrm{dB} \); five slots add ≈ \(10\log_{10}5 \approx 7\,\mathrm{dB}\) of leakage → ≈ 16.5 dB net, still ~7 dB better — and the advantage grows at higher f. Lesson: many short openings beat one long one; it is the longest dimension of an aperture that radiates, not its area. Hence honeycomb vents and closely-spaced screws along seams.
