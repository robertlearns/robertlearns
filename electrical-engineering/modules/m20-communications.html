📶 Communications & High Frequency · Module 20

Communications Engineering · Nachrichtentechnik

Every phone call, WiFi packet, satellite image and podcast is the same engineering problem: push information through a channel that is too narrow, too noisy and shared with everyone else. This module builds the whole discipline — modulation, noise, digital transmission, and Shannon's astonishing theory that tells us the exact speed limit of any channel and promises we can drive right up to it, error-free.

Corresponds to: Nachrichtentechnik / Übertragungstechnik (4th–6th semester) Prerequisites: Modules 12, 13 Study time: ≈ 7–9 h

1. The communication problem

In 1948 Claude Shannon opened A Mathematical Theory of Communication with one diagram, and the field has never needed a better one. A source (Quelle) produces a message; a transmitter (Sender) converts it into a signal suited to the physical medium; the channel (Kanal) — cable, fiber, or free space — distorts it and adds noise (Rauschen); the receiver (Empfänger) tries to undo the damage and deliver the message to the sink (Senke). Everything in this module is a refinement of one of these five blocks.

Every design in this field trades three resources against each other — the resource triangle:

  • Bandwidth \(B\) (Bandbreite): the slice of spectrum you may occupy. Regulated, auctioned (German 5G spectrum sold for €6.5 billion in 2019), and physically scarce.
  • Power \(P\) (equivalently SNR): more transmit power beats down the noise — but batteries drain, regulations cap emissions, and other users suffer interference.
  • Complexity: signal-processing effort at both ends. Moore's law made this the cheapest resource — which is precisely why modern systems (5G, WiFi 6) burn enormous computation to save bandwidth and power.
🔑Key idea: one problem, one currency

Every technique in this module — AM, FM, QAM, Hamming codes, OFDM — is a different way of spending bandwidth, power and complexity to buy the same commodity: reliable information rate. Shannon's capacity formula (Section 9) is the exchange rate table, and it is exact.

2. Baseband, passband — why modulate at all?

A microphone signal lives in the baseband (Basisband): roughly 300 Hz – 3.4 kHz for telephone speech, 20 Hz – 20 kHz for music. Why not just amplify it and feed an antenna? Three killer reasons:

1. Antenna size. An antenna radiates efficiently only when its length is comparable to the wavelength, typically \(\lambda/4\) (Module 21). With \(\lambda = c/f\):

SignalFrequencyWavelength \(\lambda\)\(\lambda/4\) antenna
Speech, directly3 kHz100 km25 km — absurd
MW broadcast (AM)1 MHz300 m75 m — the classic radio masts
FM broadcast100 MHz3 m75 cm — a car antenna
WiFi2.4 GHz12.5 cm3.1 cm — printed on the PCB

2. Multiplexing. If everyone transmitted at baseband, all signals would sit on top of each other. Shifting each user to a different carrier frequency lets thousands share one medium (Section 11).

3. Channel allocation. Physics and regulation assign each service a frequency window where propagation behaves well (MW bends around the Earth, GHz penetrates the ionosphere for satellites). You must move your signal to where your channel is.

Modulation (Modulation) is therefore the act of imprinting the message \(m(t)\) onto a high-frequency carrier (Träger) \(\cos(2\pi f_c t)\). A cosine has exactly three adjustable properties — amplitude, frequency, phase — so there are exactly three modulation families: AM, FM, PM. Recall from Fourier theory (Module 12) that multiplying by \(\cos(2\pi f_c t)\) shifts a spectrum from around 0 up to around \(\pm f_c\): baseband becomes passband (Bandpasslage).

3. Amplitude modulation — radio you could build in 1920

3.1 Time domain and modulation index

With a single-tone message \(m(t) = \cos(2\pi f_m t)\), conventional AM transmits

\[ s_{AM}(t) = \hat{U}_c\,\bigl(1 + m\cos(2\pi f_m t)\bigr)\,\cos(2\pi f_c t), \]

where \(m\) is the modulation index (Modulationsgrad), \(0 \le m \le 1\). The message rides as the envelope (Hüllkurve) of the carrier. If \(m > 1\) (Übermodulation) the factor \(1 + m\cos(\cdot)\) goes negative, the envelope no longer equals the message, and a simple receiver hears gross distortion — try it in the widget below.

3.2 The spectrum — carrier plus two sidebands

Expand the product with \(\cos a \cos b = \tfrac12[\cos(a{-}b) + \cos(a{+}b)]\):

\[ s_{AM}(t) = \hat{U}_c \cos(2\pi f_c t) \;+\; \frac{m\hat{U}_c}{2}\cos\bigl(2\pi (f_c{-}f_m) t\bigr) \;+\; \frac{m\hat{U}_c}{2}\cos\bigl(2\pi (f_c{+}f_m) t\bigr). \]

Three spectral lines: the carrier at \(f_c\), plus a lower and an upper sideband (unteres/oberes Seitenband) at \(f_c \mp f_m\). A real message occupying \(0\dots f_{m,\max}\) produces mirror-image sideband bands, so the occupied RF bandwidth is

\[ \boxed{\,B_{AM} = 2 f_{m,\max}\,} \]

— twice the message bandwidth. An MW station carrying 4.5 kHz audio occupies 9 kHz, which is exactly the European MW channel spacing.

3.3 Power — where AM wastes watts

The carrier term contains no information whatsoever — it is the same cosine whether you broadcast Beethoven or silence. Yet it eats most of the transmit power:

\[ P_{\text{carrier}} = \frac{\hat{U}_c^2}{2R}, \qquad P_{\text{sidebands}} = 2\cdot\frac{(m\hat{U}_c/2)^2}{2R} = \frac{m^2}{2} P_{\text{carrier}} \;\;\Rightarrow\;\; \frac{P_{\text{sidebands}}}{P_{\text{total}}} = \frac{m^2}{2+m^2}. \]
Worked example AM power split of a broadcast transmitter

An MW transmitter runs a 100 kW carrier. Compute the useful (sideband) power for full modulation \(m = 1\) and for a realistic program average \(m = 0.3\).

Step 1 — full modulation: \( P_{SB} = \frac{m^2}{2}P_c = 0.5 \cdot 100 = 50\,\mathrm{kW} \); total 150 kW, useful fraction \( \frac{1}{3} \approx 33\,\% \). That is the best case.
Step 2 — realistic modulation: \( P_{SB} = \frac{0.09}{2}\cdot 100 = 4.5\,\mathrm{kW} \); total 104.5 kW, useful fraction \( \frac{4.5}{104.5} \approx 4.3\,\% \).
Step 3 — interpret: over 95 % of the electricity bill of a typical AM station heats the ether with a pure, information-free cosine. Both sidebands also carry the same information (mirror images), doubling the waste.
Useful power: 33 % at \(m=1\), only ≈ 4 % at \(m=0.3\). AM buys receiver simplicity with transmitter watts.

3.4 Envelope detection — why AM won the 1920s

So why did the world start with such a wasteful scheme? Because the receiver is almost free: a diode rectifies the RF signal and an RC low-pass (Hüllkurvendemodulator, envelope detector) traces the envelope — which, for \(m \le 1\), is the message. Diode + capacitor + headphones: the crystal radio (Detektorempfänger), buildable at a kitchen table in 1920, needing no battery at all. The transmitted carrier is exactly what makes this trick work: it keeps the envelope positive.

📜DSB, SSB — spending complexity to save power and bandwidth

Suppress the carrier and you get DSB-SC (double sideband, suppressed carrier): all power in the sidebands, but the receiver now needs a synchronized local oscillator (coherent detection) — no more crystal radios. Transmit only one sideband and you get SSB (Einseitenbandmodulation): half the bandwidth, all power useful. SSB became the mode for long-haul telephony and amateur radio from the 1950s. The historical arc — AM → DSB → SSB — is the resource triangle in action: each step trades receiver complexity for power and bandwidth efficiency.

4. Frequency modulation — trading bandwidth for quiet

4.1 Instantaneous frequency

Instead of the amplitude, wiggle the frequency. Define the instantaneous frequency (Momentanfrequenz) as the derivative of the total phase:

\[ f_i(t) = \frac{1}{2\pi}\frac{\mathrm{d}\varphi(t)}{\mathrm{d}t} = f_c + k_f\, m(t), \]

where \(k_f\) (Hz/V) sets how far the message pushes the frequency. The peak excursion \(\Delta f = k_f\,\hat{m}\) is the frequency deviation (Frequenzhub). For a single tone \(m(t)=\cos(2\pi f_m t)\), integrating the phase gives

\[ s_{FM}(t) = \hat{U}_c \cos\!\bigl(2\pi f_c t + \beta \sin(2\pi f_m t)\bigr), \qquad \boxed{\,\beta = \frac{\Delta f}{f_m}\,} \]

with the FM modulation index \(\beta\). Crucially, the amplitude is constant: all information lives in the zero crossings. Phase modulation (PM) is the sibling: \(\varphi(t) = 2\pi f_c t + k_p m(t)\) — FM of the differentiated message. PM matters less for analog audio but becomes the backbone of digital transmission (PSK, Section 6).

4.2 Bandwidth — Carson's rule

An exact FM spectrum requires Bessel functions (the sidebands at \(f_c \pm n f_m\) have amplitudes \(J_n(\beta)\)) — infinitely many sidebands! In practice their amplitudes die off quickly beyond the deviation, and John Carson's 1922 rule captures ≈ 98 % of the power:

\[ \boxed{\,B_{FM} \approx 2\,(\Delta f + f_m) = 2 f_m (\beta + 1).\,} \]
Worked example Carson's rule for broadcast FM

European FM broadcasting uses deviation \(\Delta f = 75\,\mathrm{kHz}\) and transmits audio (plus stereo pilot/subcarrier) up to \(f_m = 15\,\mathrm{kHz}\). Find \(\beta\), the Carson bandwidth, and compare with the channel raster.

Step 1 — modulation index: \( \beta = \Delta f / f_m = 75/15 = 5 \) — “wideband FM”.
Step 2 — Carson bandwidth: \( B \approx 2(\Delta f + f_m) = 2(75 + 15) = 180\,\mathrm{kHz} \).
Step 3 — sanity check: FM stations are spaced on a 100 kHz raster but assigned ≥ 200–300 kHz apart in one region — a 180 kHz signal fits a 200 kHz allocation with a small guard band. ✓
\(\beta = 5\), \(B \approx 180\,\mathrm{kHz}\) — twelve times the 15 kHz message. FM deliberately spends bandwidth.

4.3 What the bandwidth buys: noise robustness

Why spend 12× bandwidth? Because additive noise mostly perturbs the amplitude of a signal, and FM ignores amplitude — the receiver hard-limits the signal and only evaluates zero crossings. Analysis (and Module 13's noise theory) shows the demodulated SNR of wideband FM improves roughly as \(3\beta^2(\beta+1)\) relative to AM at equal received power — for \(\beta = 5\), a gain of order 20 dB, further helped by pre-/de-emphasis filtering. This is why music radio moved to FM: the medium is quiet enough for high fidelity. It is our first concrete instance of Shannon's grand bargain — bandwidth can be exchanged for SNR.

FM also exhibits the capture effect (Capture-Effekt): when two FM stations land on one frequency, the receiver locks onto the stronger one and suppresses the weaker almost completely (a few dB difference suffices), instead of mixing both as AM does. Great for cellular reuse of frequencies; occasionally startling on a road trip when the station "flips".

5. Noise — the adversary, quantified

5.1 Thermal noise: N = kTB

Every resistor at temperature \(T\) generates thermal noise (Johnson–Nyquist noise, Wärmerauschen, Module 13) — the Brownian motion of its electrons. The available noise power in a bandwidth \(B\) is beautifully simple:

\[ \boxed{\,N = k\,T\,B\,}, \qquad k = 1.38\cdot 10^{-23}\,\mathrm{J/K}. \]

At the reference temperature \(T_0 = 290\,\mathrm{K}\): \(kT_0 = 4.0\cdot10^{-21}\,\mathrm{W/Hz} = -174\,\mathrm{dBm/Hz}\) — a number every RF engineer knows by heart. Worked numbers for a 20 MHz WiFi channel:

\[ N = -174\,\mathrm{dBm/Hz} + 10\log_{10}(20\cdot10^6)\,\mathrm{dB} = -174 + 73 = -101\,\mathrm{dBm} \approx 0.08\,\mathrm{pW}. \]

If your laptop receives a −70 dBm WiFi signal, the physics-given signal-to-noise ratio (Signal-Rausch-Abstand) is \(\mathrm{SNR} = -70-(-101) = 31\,\mathrm{dB}\) — before the receiver adds its own noise. Other noise sources (shot noise in junctions, flicker noise, man-made interference) add on top, but \(kTB\) is the floor nature never waives.

5.2 Noise figure and the Friis formula — why the LNA comes first

A real amplifier amplifies the input noise and adds its own. Its noise figure (Rauschzahl) \(F\) says by what factor it degrades the SNR: \(F = \mathrm{SNR_{in}}/\mathrm{SNR_{out}}\) (as a ratio; NF \(= 10\log_{10}F\) in dB). For a cascade of stages with gains \(G_i\) and noise factors \(F_i\), Friis' formula gives

\[ \boxed{\,F_{\text{total}} = F_1 + \frac{F_2 - 1}{G_1} + \frac{F_3 - 1}{G_1 G_2} + \dots\,} \]

Each later stage's noise is divided by all the gain before it. Consequence: the first stage dominates. Numbers: a low-noise amplifier (rauscharmer Vorverstärker, LNA) with NF = 1 dB (\(F_1 = 1.26\)), gain 20 dB (\(G_1 = 100\)), followed by a noisy mixer with NF = 8 dB (\(F_2 = 6.3\)):

\[ F = 1.26 + \frac{6.3 - 1}{100} = 1.31 \;\Rightarrow\; \mathrm{NF} = 1.2\,\mathrm{dB}. \]

Swap the order and \(F = 6.3 + (1.26-1)/G_{mixer} \approx 6.4\) → NF ≈ 8 dB: a 7 dB catastrophe, identical parts. This is why the LNA of a satellite dish sits at the dish (in the LNB), not at the TV end of 20 m of lossy cable — attenuation before the first amplifier adds dB-for-dB to the noise figure.

5.3 The AWGN channel model

For analysis we abstract the channel as AWGN — additive white Gaussian noise: \(r(t) = s(t) + n(t)\), with \(n(t)\) white (flat spectral density \(N_0/2\)) and Gaussian (Central Limit Theorem: it is the sum of countless independent electron jitters, Module 13). AWGN is the fruit-fly of communications: simple enough to compute everything, realistic enough for satellite, deep-space and cable links. Mobile radio adds multipath fading on top (Section 11).

🚫Classic pitfall: dB, dBm, and adding them

dBm is an absolute power level (referenced to 1 mW); dB is a ratio. You may add dB to dBm (gain applied to a level) but never dBm to dBm — “−70 dBm + (−101 dBm)” is nonsense; two −70 dBm signals sum to −67 dBm (powers add, \(+3\,\mathrm{dB}\)). In exams, convert to linear when in doubt: \(P/\mathrm{mW} = 10^{(x\,\mathrm{dBm})/10}\).

6. Digital modulation — bits, symbols, constellations

6.1 From bits to waveforms

Digital transmission sends a discrete alphabet: the bit stream is chopped into groups of \(\log_2 M\) bits, each group selects one of \(M\) symbols, and each symbol is one allowed combination of carrier amplitude and phase, held for a symbol duration \(T_s\). Two rates must never be confused:

\[ \text{symbol rate } R_s = \frac{1}{T_s} \;\;[\mathrm{Baud}], \qquad \text{bit rate } R_b = R_s \cdot \log_2 M \;\;[\mathrm{bit/s}]. \]

Bandwidth is governed by the symbol rate (Section 8). So to push more bits through fixed bandwidth, you enlarge the alphabet \(M\) — packing symbols closer together.

6.2 The constellation diagram — THE picture of digital communications

Write each symbol as \(s(t) = I\cos(2\pi f_c t) - Q\sin(2\pi f_c t)\): a point \((I, Q)\) in the complex plane (the phasor language of Module 12, frozen per symbol). Plotting all \(M\) allowed points gives the constellation diagram (Konstellationsdiagramm):

  • ASK / OOK (on-off keying): two amplitudes, 0 and 1 — how the very first digital radio and today's cheapest remote controls work; also, in intensity form, most fiber optics.
  • BPSK: two phases, \(\pm 1\) on the real axis. 1 bit/symbol, maximally robust — used by GPS and deep-space probes.
  • QPSK: four phases (corners of a square), 2 bit/symbol at the same error rate per bit as BPSK — a free lunch, which is why almost nothing uses BPSK when QPSK is available.
  • 16 / 64 / 256-QAM (quadrature amplitude modulation, Quadraturamplitudenmodulation): square grids of 16/64/256 points, 4/6/8 bit/symbol.

The trade-off is geometric: with fixed average power, more points means smaller distances between neighbors, so less noise suffices to push a received point across a decision boundary. Denser = faster but noise-hungrier. Quantitatively, each step 16→64→256-QAM costs roughly 6 dB more required SNR for the same symbol-error rate.

🔑Key idea: what your WiFi actually does

Your router measures the channel every few milliseconds and picks the densest constellation the current SNR supports — QPSK across the garden, 256-QAM next to the router (WiFi 6 dares 1024-QAM at short range). Move your laptop and the link rate changes not because "the WiFi is weak" but because the modem has rationally retreated to a sparser constellation. LTE/5G do exactly the same (adaptive modulation and coding, AMC).

7. Receiving bits: matched filter, decisions, BER

7.1 The matched filter — correlate with what you sent

Buried in noise, how do you best detect a known pulse shape \(g(t)\)? Filter the received signal with the time-reversed pulse itself, \(h(t) = g(T_s - t)\) — the matched filter (signalangepasstes Filter), equivalent to correlating the input with a template of what the transmitter sent. It maximizes the sampling-instant SNR over all linear filters, achieving \(\mathrm{SNR}_{\max} = 2E_s/N_0\) — depending only on the symbol energy \(E_s\), not the pulse shape. Intuition: the filter re-aligns and adds up every part of the signal coherently, while the noise adds only incoherently (random-walk style).

7.2 Decision regions and the BER waterfall

After matched filtering and sampling, the receiver holds a noisy constellation point and simply picks the nearest ideal point — decision regions (Entscheidungsgebiete) are the cells around each constellation point. An error occurs when Gaussian noise carries the point across a boundary; the probability is governed by the Gaussian tail function \(Q(x)\) (Q-Funktion). For BPSK:

\[ P_b = Q\!\left(\sqrt{\tfrac{2E_b}{N_0}}\right), \]

with \(E_b\) the energy spent per bit and \(N_0\) the noise density — \(E_b/N_0\) is the universal "SNR per bit" currency for comparing schemes. Plot \(P_b\) against \(E_b/N_0\) in dB and you get the famous waterfall curves: flat and hopeless at low SNR, then plunging many decades per few dB. BPSK needs ≈ 9.6 dB for \(P_b = 10^{-5}\); 16-QAM needs ≈ 4 dB more; each denser QAM shifts the waterfall right. Channel coding (Section 10) shifts it left — that shift is the "coding gain".

8. ISI and pulse shaping — the art of not smearing

8.1 Why rectangular pulses fail

A crisp rectangular symbol pulse has a \(\mathrm{sinc}\)-shaped spectrum stretching to infinity (Module 12). A real channel is band-limited; it truncates that spectrum, and the time-domain pulse smears into its neighbors: intersymbol interference (ISI, Zwischensymbolinterferenz) — the received value of each symbol is contaminated by the tails of previous and following ones. At high symbol rates this, not noise, is often the binding constraint.

8.2 Nyquist's criterion and the raised cosine

Nyquist (1928) asked: which pulse shapes have tails that pass through zero at every other symbol's sampling instant? Answer: any pulse whose spectrum folds (aliases) to a constant when sampled at rate \(R_s\) — zero-ISI needs at least \(B \ge R_s/2\), and the minimum-bandwidth solution is the ideal \(\mathrm{sinc}\) pulse. But sinc decays as \(1/t\) and any timing error unleashes huge ISI. Practice uses the raised-cosine family (Kosinus-roll-off-Filter) with roll-off factor \(\alpha \in [0,1]\):

\[ \boxed{\,B = \frac{R_s}{2}(1+\alpha)\,} \qquad \text{(baseband; RF bandwidth } = R_s(1+\alpha)). \]

\(\alpha = 0\) is the fragile sinc; \(\alpha = 1\) doubles the bandwidth but gives fast-decaying, timing-tolerant tails. Real systems pick \(\alpha \approx 0.2\dots0.35\) and split the filtering into a root-raised-cosine at each end — so the cascade is raised-cosine and each half is the other's matched filter. Elegant.

8.3 The eye diagram — the engineer's oscilloscope diagnostic

Overlay the received waveform in slices of two symbol periods and an eye diagram (Augendiagramm) appears. Open eye: sample anywhere near the middle and decide safely. ISI, noise and jitter each close the eye in a characteristic way — one oscilloscope picture diagnoses the whole link.

9. Information theory — the crown of the discipline

9.1 Information and entropy — measuring surprise

Shannon's first stroke of genius: information is surprise. A message that was certain anyway carries none. The information content of a symbol with probability \(p\) is

\[ I = \log_2 \frac{1}{p} \;\;[\mathrm{bit}], \]

and the average over a source's alphabet is its entropy (Entropie):

\[ \boxed{\,H = \sum_i p_i \log_2 \frac{1}{p_i}\,} \quad [\mathrm{bit/symbol}]. \]

Worked source example — a weather station emitting one symbol per day: ☀ with \(p=0.5\), ☁ with \(p=0.25\), 🌧 with \(p=0.125\), ❄ with \(p=0.125\):

\[ H = 0.5\cdot 1 + 0.25 \cdot 2 + 0.125\cdot 3 + 0.125 \cdot 3 = 1.75\ \mathrm{bit/symbol}, \]

less than the \(\log_2 4 = 2\) bits a naive fixed-length code would spend — because the source is predictable. (Uniform probabilities maximize entropy; certainty gives \(H=0\).)

9.2 Source coding — compressing to the entropy limit

Shannon's source coding theorem (Quellencodierungstheorem): a source of entropy \(H\) can be encoded losslessly with an average of arbitrarily close to, but never fewer than, \(H\) bits per symbol. Entropy is the hard floor of compression. Huffman's algorithm (1952) builds an optimal prefix-free code by repeatedly merging the two least probable symbols. For our weather source:

Symbol\(p_i\)Huffman codeLength\(p_i \cdot l_i\)
☀ sun0.5010.5
☁ clouds0.251020.5
🌧 rain0.12511030.375
❄ snow0.12511130.375

Average length \(\bar{L} = 1.75\) bit/symbol \(= H\) exactly — Huffman hits the entropy floor whenever the probabilities are powers of ½. ZIP, PNG, MP3, H.265: all are elaborate machines for approaching \(H\) (MP3 additionally lowers \(H\) first by discarding what your ear cannot hear — lossy coding).

9.3 Channel capacity — the speed limit of the universe’s channels

The masterstroke. For the band-limited AWGN channel, the Shannon–Hartley capacity (Kanalkapazität) is

\[ \boxed{\,C = B \cdot \log_2\!\left(1 + \mathrm{SNR}\right)\,} \quad [\mathrm{bit/s}]. \]

And its meaning is astonishing: for any rate \(R < C\) there exist codes making the error probability arbitrarily small — not "small", but as close to zero as you like, over a noisy channel, at finite power. Conversely, above \(C\), reliable communication is impossible, full stop. Before 1948 engineers believed noise imposed a quality-vs-speed trade-off forever; Shannon proved you can have perfection, below a hard speed limit. Note the asymmetry: capacity grows linearly in bandwidth but only logarithmically in power — doubling \(B\) doubles \(C\); doubling \(P\) adds just 1 bit/s/Hz at high SNR. That is why the industry fights for spectrum, not for bigger amplifiers.

Worked example Capacity of a 20 MHz, 30 dB channel

A WiFi link has \(B = 20\,\mathrm{MHz}\) and SNR = 30 dB. Find \(C\) and compare with a real modem.

Step 1 — SNR to linear: \( \mathrm{SNR} = 10^{30/10} = 1000 \).
Step 2 — capacity: \( C = 20\cdot10^6 \cdot \log_2(1001) \approx 20\cdot10^6 \cdot 9.97 \approx 199\,\mathrm{Mbit/s} \).
Step 3 — reality check: a single-stream 802.11n radio in 20 MHz peaks at 72 Mbit/s — about 36 % of capacity. Modern WiFi 6 with LDPC codes and 1024-QAM gets much closer to the line. Nobody gets above it. Ever.
\(C \approx 199\,\mathrm{Mbit/s}\) — ≈ 10 bit/s per Hz of spectrum at 30 dB.

One more jewel: let \(B \to \infty\) at fixed power and the capacity saturates; rearranging yields a minimum energy per bit for reliable communication at all:

\[ \frac{E_b}{N_0} \ge \ln 2 = 0.693 \;\;\widehat{=}\; -1.59\,\mathrm{dB} \]

— the Shannon limit. Modern LDPC/turbo codes operate within a few tenths of a dB of it.

10. Channel coding — spending bits to buy reliability

Shannon proved good codes exist but not how to build them; the next 60 years of coding theory chased that promise. Channel coding (Kanalcodierung) adds structured redundancy so the receiver can detect and correct errors. A code mapping \(k\) data bits into \(n\) coded bits has code rate \(R_c = k/n\).

10.1 The repetition code — and why it is terrible

Send every bit three times, decide by majority. With raw bit-error probability \(p = 10^{-2}\), a block fails only if ≥ 2 of 3 bits flip:

\[ P_e = 3p^2(1-p) + p^3 \approx 3\cdot10^{-4}. \]

Errors drop 30-fold — but the rate drops to \(R_c = 1/3\). To reach \(10^{-9}\) you would need ever more repetitions, with rate crawling toward zero. Yet Shannon says rates near \(C\) (a healthy constant!) suffice for any reliability. Repetition wastes its redundancy protecting each bit in isolation; good codes protect many bits jointly, so that each parity bit says something about the whole block.

10.2 Hamming(7,4) — jointly protecting a block, fully worked

Richard Hamming (1950, annoyed that his weekend batch jobs crashed on single bit errors) designed the first single-error-correcting code. Hamming(7,4) packs \(k=4\) data bits \(d_1 d_2 d_3 d_4\) and 3 parity bits into \(n=7\), rate \(R_c = 4/7 \approx 0.57\). Place parity bits at positions 1, 2, 4 (the powers of two); each parity bit covers the positions whose binary index contains its power:

\[ p_1 = d_1 \oplus d_2 \oplus d_4, \qquad p_2 = d_1 \oplus d_3 \oplus d_4, \qquad p_3 = d_2 \oplus d_3 \oplus d_4 \]

(⊕ = XOR; codeword order \(p_1 p_2 d_1 p_3 d_2 d_3 d_4\)). The magic: recomputing the three parity checks at the receiver yields a 3-bit syndrome (Syndrom) that spells out the binary address of the flipped bit.

Worked example Hamming(7,4): encode, corrupt, correct

Encode the data nibble \(d_1d_2d_3d_4 = 1011\), flip one bit in transit, and let the receiver repair it.

Step 1 — parity bits: \( p_1 = 1{\oplus}0{\oplus}1 = 0 \), \( p_2 = 1{\oplus}1{\oplus}1 = 1 \), \( p_3 = 0{\oplus}1{\oplus}1 = 0 \). Codeword (positions 1…7 = \(p_1 p_2 d_1 p_3 d_2 d_3 d_4\)): 0110011.
Step 2 — the channel strikes: position 5 (\(d_2\)) flips → received 0110111.
Step 3 — syndrome: recompute each check over its circle, including the parity bit itself:
\( s_1 = p_1{\oplus}d_1{\oplus}d_2{\oplus}d_4 = 0{\oplus}1{\oplus}1{\oplus}1 = 1 \) (violated)
\( s_2 = p_2{\oplus}d_1{\oplus}d_3{\oplus}d_4 = 1{\oplus}1{\oplus}1{\oplus}1 = 0 \) (ok)
\( s_3 = p_3{\oplus}d_2{\oplus}d_3{\oplus}d_4 = 0{\oplus}1{\oplus}1{\oplus}1 = 1 \) (violated)
Step 4 — decode the syndrome: read \((s_3 s_2 s_1) = 101_2 = 5\) — the corrupted position, directly as a binary number. Flip bit 5 back → 0110011 ✓, extract \(1011\).
One flipped bit located and corrected using only 3 extra bits for 4 data bits. Two flips, however, would mislead the decoder — Hamming(7,4) corrects 1 error or detects 2 (minimum distance 3).

10.3 Coding gain and the modern arsenal

A code's coding gain (Codierungsgewinn) is how many dB less \(E_b/N_0\) you need for a target BER compared to uncoded transmission — the leftward shift of the waterfall curve. The modern lineup, name and role:

11. Sharing the channel — multiplexing, OFDM, MIMO

11.1 Three orthogonalities: frequency, time, code

FDM (Frequenzmultiplex): each user gets a frequency slice — classic radio dials, analog telephony's 12-channel carrier groups, the guard bands paying the rent. TDM (Zeitmultiplex): everyone uses the full band, but in rotating time slots — E1 telephone lines (32 slots), GSM (8 slots per carrier); requires tight synchronization. CDMA (Codemultiplex): all users transmit simultaneously over the full band, each spreading its data with a unique fast pseudo-random code; the receiver correlates with "its" code and all other users average out as mild noise. Spreading also buries the signal below the noise floor and shrugs off narrowband interference — invented for military radios, then powering 3G/UMTS and, in essence, GPS.

11.2 OFDM — the winner takes all

Radio signals reach the receiver via many paths (direct, wall-reflected, building-diffracted): multipath (Mehrwegeausbreitung). Path-delay differences of ~1 µs smear symbols into each other; equivalently, the channel's frequency response develops deep notches — frequency-selective fading. For a single fast carrier at 20 Msym/s (50 ns symbols), 1 µs of delay spread means each symbol collides with 20 neighbors. Equalizing that is brutal.

OFDM (orthogonal frequency-division multiplexing, orthogonales Frequenzmultiplexverfahren) flips the problem: split the data over hundreds of parallel, slow subcarriers — e.g. 20 MHz → 64 subcarriers of 312.5 kHz each, symbols now 4 µs long, so 1 µs of smear touches only a fraction of each symbol. Prepend each symbol with a cyclic prefix (Guard-Intervall) — a copy of its own tail — that absorbs the multipath transient entirely; within each narrow subcarrier the channel is just a complex constant, fixed by one multiplication. Subcarriers are spaced exactly \(1/T_s\) apart, so each one's spectral peak lands on all the others' nulls — overlapping yet orthogonal, no guard bands wasted. Best of all, modulating/demodulating all subcarriers at once is a single (I)FFT — computationally almost free. WiFi, LTE, 5G NR, DVB-T2 and DSL (as DMT) all chose OFDM: it converts an intractable equalization problem into trivial per-subcarrier arithmetic.

11.3 MIMO — more antennas, more channels

MIMO (multiple input, multiple output) uses several antennas at both ends. If the multipath environment is rich enough, the matrix channel between \(N_t\) transmit and \(N_r\) receive antennas has multiple independent eigenmodes — effectively parallel spatial channels in the same band at the same time. Capacity scales up to \(\min(N_t, N_r)\)-fold: spatial multiplexing. This — not a Shannon violation, but Shannon applied per spatial stream — is how LTE doubles and WiFi quadruples the single-channel numbers. 5G's massive MIMO base stations carry 64+ antenna elements and use beamforming (Strahlformung): phasing the elements so the radiated energy focuses on each user (Module 21's array theory), boosting SNR and letting many users reuse the same resource blocks in different directions.

12. Systems in practice — the link budget and a grand tour

12.1 The link budget — one addition that designs the radio

Will the link work? Add up, in dB: transmit power, all gains, minus all losses; compare with what the receiver needs. That sum is the link budget (Streckenbilanz). The main loss is free-space path loss (FSPL, Freiraumdämpfung) — really just the \(1/(4\pi d^2)\) thinning of power over a sphere, referenced to an isotropic antenna:

\[ \mathrm{FSPL} = 20\log_{10}(d_{\mathrm{km}}) + 20\log_{10}(f_{\mathrm{MHz}}) + 32.44 \;\mathrm{dB}. \]
Worked example WiFi link budget through the house

Router: 20 dBm EIRP (EU legal limit at 2.4 GHz; EIRP = transmit power + antenna gain). Laptop 30 m away, two brick walls in the path (≈ 7 dB each), laptop antenna gain 2 dBi. Receiver sensitivity for 65 Mbit/s (64-QAM, MCS 7): −75 dBm. Does it work?

Step 1 — free-space path loss: \( \mathrm{FSPL} = 20\log_{10}(0.03) + 20\log_{10}(2400) + 32.44 = -30.5 + 67.6 + 32.4 \approx 69.5\,\mathrm{dB} \).
Step 2 — total losses: \( 69.5 + 2\cdot 7 = 83.5\,\mathrm{dB} \).
Step 3 — received power: \( P_r = 20 - 83.5 + 2 = -61.5\,\mathrm{dBm} \).
Step 4 — margin: \( -61.5 - (-75) = 13.5\,\mathrm{dB} \) of fade margin — comfortable; fading dips and a closed door still fit. One more floor (~15 dB) would eat it, and the modem would down-shift to QPSK: fewer Mbit/s, same physics.
Link closes with ≈ 13.5 dB margin at 65 Mbit/s. Every radio system ever fielded was designed by exactly this addition.

14. Exercises

Work these on paper before opening the solutions — the struggle is the learning.

E1Compute the \(\lambda/4\) antenna length for (a) direct transmission of a 10 kHz audio signal, (b) the same audio modulated onto a 100 MHz FM carrier. Comment. easy

Show solution
(a) \( \lambda = c/f = 3\cdot10^8/10^4 = 30\,\mathrm{km} \) → \(\lambda/4 = 7.5\,\mathrm{km}\) — a tower taller than Mount Everest is high. (b) \( \lambda = 3\,\mathrm{m} \) → \(\lambda/4 = 75\,\mathrm{cm}\) — a car whip antenna. Same information, 100 000× smaller antenna: this single ratio is the founding business case for modulation.

E2Find the thermal noise floor (290 K) of a 20 MHz WiFi channel in dBm. The receiver has NF = 6 dB and needs SNR = 25 dB for 64-QAM. What is its sensitivity? easy

Show solution
Floor: \( -174 + 10\log_{10}(2\cdot10^7) = -174 + 73 = -101\,\mathrm{dBm} \). The receiver behaves as if the floor were \( -101 + 6 = -95\,\mathrm{dBm} \) (noise figure). Sensitivity: \( -95 + 25 = -70\,\mathrm{dBm} \). Sanity check: real 802.11 datasheets quote −70…−75 dBm for 64-QAM rates — our three-line estimate lands right on top of them.

E3A narrowband FM voice radio uses \(\Delta f = 5\,\mathrm{kHz}\) and \(f_m = 3\,\mathrm{kHz}\). Find \(\beta\) and the Carson bandwidth. Does it fit the standard 25 kHz channel raster? How many such voice channels fit where ONE broadcast-FM station sits? medium

Show solution
\( \beta = 5/3 \approx 1.67 \); \( B \approx 2(5+3) = 16\,\mathrm{kHz} \) — fits a 25 kHz raster with guard space. ✓ Broadcast FM occupies ≈ 200 kHz (Section 4), so \(200/25 = 8\) two-way voice channels fit in one music station's slot. Spectrum regulators perform exactly this arithmetic all day.

E4A receive chain: antenna cable with 3 dB loss (a passive loss of \(L\) has \(F = L\), \(G = 1/L\)), then an LNA (NF 1 dB, gain 20 dB), then a mixer (NF 10 dB). Compute the total NF. Then move the LNA in front of the cable (mast-mounted) and recompute. medium

Show solution
Linear values: cable \(F=2, G=0.5\); LNA \(F=1.26, G=100\); mixer \(F=10\).
Cable first: \( F = 2 + \frac{1.26-1}{0.5} + \frac{10-1}{0.5 \cdot 100} = 2 + 0.52 + 0.18 = 2.70 \) → NF ≈ 4.3 dB.
LNA first: \( F = 1.26 + \frac{2-1}{100} + \frac{10-1}{100 \cdot 0.5} = 1.26 + 0.01 + 0.18 = 1.45 \) → NF ≈ 1.6 dB.
Same parts, 2.7 dB better — nearly double the noise performance, i.e. the same link works at ~1.4× the distance. This is why satellite LNBs and cell-tower TMAs sit at the antenna.

E5A cable modem transmits 8 Msym/s with raised-cosine roll-off \(\alpha = 0.25\) using 256-QAM. Find the occupied RF bandwidth, the raw bit rate, and the spectral efficiency. Compare with the capacity limit if SNR = 35 dB. medium

Show solution
RF bandwidth: \( B = R_s(1+\alpha) = 8 \cdot 1.25 = 10\,\mathrm{MHz} \). Bit rate: \( R_b = R_s \log_2 256 = 8 \cdot 8 = 64\,\mathrm{Mbit/s} \); efficiency \(64/10 = 6.4\,\mathrm{bit/s/Hz}\). Capacity: \( C = 10^7 \log_2(1+10^{3.5}) = 10^7 \cdot 11.63 \approx 116\,\mathrm{Mbit/s} \) — the modem uses 55 % of it; the gap pays for the roll-off excess bandwidth, coding overhead and implementation margin. (DOCSIS 3.1 closes much of it with 4096-QAM + LDPC + OFDM.)

E6With raw error probability \(p = 10^{-3}\): (a) block error probability of the (3,1) repetition code; (b) probability that Hamming(7,4) fails (≥ 2 errors in 7 bits); (c) compare the rates. Which protects 4 data bits with fewer transmitted bits at better reliability? challenge

Show solution
(a) \( P_e = 3p^2(1-p)+p^3 \approx 3\cdot10^{-6} \) per data bit. (b) \( P_{fail} = 1 - (1-p)^7 - 7p(1-p)^6 \approx \binom{7}{2}p^2 = 21\cdot10^{-6} \) per 7-bit block, i.e. ≈ \(5\cdot10^{-6}\) per data bit — same order as repetition. (c) Rates: repetition sends \(4\times3 = 12\) bits for 4 data bits (\(R_c = 1/3\)); Hamming sends 7 (\(R_c = 4/7\)). Comparable protection for almost half the bits — the profit from protecting bits jointly. Longer modern codes push this advantage to the Shannon limit; repetition never gets better per redundant bit.

E7Satellite TV link budget: a GEO satellite (36 000 km) radiates EIRP = 52 dBW at 12 GHz. Your 60 cm dish has 37 dB gain; system noise temperature 100 K; transponder bandwidth 33 MHz. Find the received power, the noise power, and the SNR. Is DVB-S2 with 8PSK (needs ~11 dB) feasible? challenge

Show solution
FSPL: \( 20\log(36000) + 20\log(12000) + 32.44 = 91.1 + 81.6 + 32.4 = 205.1\,\mathrm{dB} \). Received: \( P_r = 52 - 205.1 + 37 = -116.1\,\mathrm{dBW} = -86.1\,\mathrm{dBm} \) — about 2.5 picowatts collected by the dish. Noise: \( N = kTB = 1.38\cdot10^{-23}\cdot 100 \cdot 33\cdot10^6 = 4.6\cdot10^{-14}\,\mathrm{W} = -103.4\,\mathrm{dBm} \). SNR \( = -86.1+103.4 \approx 17\,\mathrm{dB} \) — comfortably above the ~11 dB needed: ✓, with ~6 dB of rain-fade margin. Heavy thunderstorms eat exactly that margin, which is why satellite TV pixelates in a downpour.
