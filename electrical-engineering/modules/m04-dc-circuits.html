⚡ Fundamentals of Electrical Engineering · Module 04

DC Circuit Analysis · Gleichstromtechnik

Everything in electrical engineering — from a phone charger to a national grid — is ultimately analyzed with the concepts in this module: charge, current, voltage, resistance, and two deceptively simple conservation laws. Master these and every later circuit, however exotic, becomes bookkeeping.

Corresponds to: Grundlagen der Elektrotechnik I (1st semester) Prerequisites: Module 01 (linear systems of equations) Study time: ≈ 4–6 h

1. Charge and current — what actually flows

Electricity begins with electric charge (elektrische Ladung) \(Q\), measured in coulombs (C). Charge is a property of matter, like mass: electrons carry \(-e\), protons \(+e\), with the elementary charge

\[ e = 1.602 \cdot 10^{-19}\,\mathrm{C}. \]

One coulomb is therefore the charge of about \(6.24 \cdot 10^{18}\) electrons — an enormous number, which is why we work with the aggregate quantity rather than individual particles.

Electric current (elektrischer Strom) \(I\) is the rate at which charge passes through a cross-section of a conductor:

\[ I = \frac{\mathrm{d}Q}{\mathrm{d}t}, \qquad [I] = \frac{\mathrm{C}}{\mathrm{s}} = \mathrm{A} \ \text{(ampere)}. \]
💡Intuition: the water analogy

Picture a closed water-pipe system. Charge is the water itself, current is the flow rate (liters per second through a pipe cross-section), and voltage — coming next — is the pressure difference that pushes the water around. A pump (the source) creates pressure; narrow pipes (resistors) impede flow. The analogy is imperfect but survives an astonishing amount of first-year electrical engineering.

⚠️Conventional current vs. electron flow

By historical convention (predating the discovery of the electron), current arrows point in the direction positive charge would flow — from + to − outside the source. In a metal wire, the electrons actually drift the opposite way. All of circuit analysis uses the conventional direction (technische Stromrichtung); electron flow direction is irrelevant for calculation. And surprisingly: the electrons themselves drift at only ~0.1 mm/s — it is the electric field, propagating near light speed, that makes the lamp light "instantly".

2. Voltage and potential — why charge moves

Charge does not flow on its own; it needs an energy difference. The electric potential \(\varphi\) at a point is the potential energy per unit charge at that point. The voltage (Spannung) between two points A and B is the potential difference:

\[ U_{AB} = \varphi_A - \varphi_B = \frac{W_{AB}}{Q}, \qquad [U] = \frac{\mathrm{J}}{\mathrm{C}} = \mathrm{V} \ \text{(volt)}. \]

\(U_{AB} = 5\,\mathrm{V}\) means: transporting 1 C of charge from A to B releases 5 J of energy. Two consequences worth internalizing:

  • Voltage is always between two points. "The voltage at point A" is meaningless unless a reference point (ground, Masse, 0 V) has been agreed.
  • Voltage can exist without current (a battery on the shelf); current cannot flow without a driving voltage (in ordinary resistive circuits).

3. Resistance and Ohm's law

Push charge through a material and it collides with the atomic lattice, dissipating energy as heat. The material "resists". For metallic conductors at constant temperature, Georg Simon Ohm found (1826) that current is proportional to the applied voltage:

\[ \boxed{\,U = R \cdot I\,} \qquad R = \frac{U}{I}, \quad [R] = \frac{\mathrm{V}}{\mathrm{A}} = \Omega \ \text{(ohm)}. \]

The proportionality constant \(R\) is the resistance (Widerstand). Its reciprocal \(G = 1/R\) is the conductance (Leitwert), in siemens (S). For a homogeneous wire of length \(l\), cross-section \(A\) and material resistivity \(\rho\) (spezifischer Widerstand):

\[ R = \rho \, \frac{l}{A}. \]

Copper (\(\rho \approx 0.0178\,\Omega\,\mathrm{mm^2/m}\)) conducts ~60× better than steel; that is why your household wiring is copper.

🧪Interactive — Ohm's law as a line simulation

A resistor's \(I\)–\(U\) characteristic is a straight line through the origin — the defining picture of a linear component. Drag \(R\) and watch the slope: large \(R\) → flat line (little current per volt). The dot marks the operating point at the chosen source voltage.

🔑Key idea: linearity

Ohm's law makes the resistor a linear element: double the cause, double the effect. Entire solution techniques (superposition, Thévenin, nodal analysis as a linear system) exist because R, and later L and C, are linear. Diodes and transistors will break this linearity — and that is exactly what makes them useful (Module 08).

4. Energy and power

A charge \(Q\) falling through a voltage \(U\) releases energy \(W = U Q\). Per unit time, that is the power (Leistung) converted in a component:

\[ P = U \cdot I \qquad [P] = \mathrm{V \cdot A} = \mathrm{W} \ \text{(watt)}. \]

Combining with Ohm's law gives the two workhorse forms for resistors:

\[ P = I^2 R = \frac{U^2}{R}. \]
Worked example A kettle, seriously analyzed

A kettle is rated 2000 W at 230 V. Find its current draw, its element resistance, and the energy to heat 1 L of water from 15 °C to 100 °C (ideal case), given \(c_w = 4187\,\mathrm{J/(kg\,K)}\).

Step 1 — current: \( I = P/U = 2000/230 \approx 8.7\,\mathrm{A} \). (This is why kettles need a 10 A+ circuit.)
Step 2 — resistance: \( R = U^2/P = 230^2/2000 \approx 26.5\,\Omega \).
Step 3 — energy and time: \( W = m\,c_w\,\Delta T = 1 \cdot 4187 \cdot 85 \approx 356\,\mathrm{kJ} \). At 2000 J/s: \( t = 356\,000/2000 \approx 178\,\mathrm{s} \approx 3\,\mathrm{min} \) — matching everyday experience.
\(I \approx 8.7\,\mathrm{A}\), \(R \approx 26.5\,\Omega\), ideal heating time ≈ 3 minutes.

5. Sources — ideal and real

A circuit needs something to maintain the potential difference: a source (Quelle).

  • An ideal voltage source holds \(U = U_q\) fixed regardless of the current drawn.
  • An ideal current source forces \(I = I_q\) regardless of the voltage across it.

Real sources are neither. A real battery is modeled as an ideal source \(U_q\) in series with an internal resistance \(R_i\) (Innenwiderstand). Its terminal voltage sags under load:

\[ U_{\text{terminal}} = U_q - R_i \, I. \]

This one equation explains why your car's headlights dim while cranking the starter (the starter draws ~100 A through \(R_i \approx 10\,\mathrm{m\Omega}\), dropping a full volt or more inside the battery).

6. Kirchhoff's laws — the two rules that solve everything

Ohm's law describes one component. To analyze networks of components we need just two more facts, both conservation laws, formulated by Gustav Kirchhoff (1845) — as a 21-year-old student, incidentally.

6.1 Current law (KCL) — Knotenregel

Charge cannot pile up at a junction ("node", Knoten). Whatever flows in must flow out, at every instant:

\[ \sum_{k} I_k = 0 \quad \text{at every node (inflows +, outflows −).} \]

Water analogy: at a pipe junction, liters-in per second equals liters-out per second.

6.2 Voltage law (KVL) — Maschenregel

Potential is a well-defined height. Walk any closed loop (Masche) in a circuit and you return to the height you started at:

\[ \sum_{k} U_k = 0 \quad \text{around every closed loop.} \]

Analogy: hike any closed path in the mountains — climbs and descents must cancel.

Worked example Solving Fig. 6.1 numerically

Let \(U_q = 12\,\mathrm{V}\), \(R_1 = 1\,\mathrm{k\Omega}\), \(R_2 = 2\,\mathrm{k\Omega}\), \(R_3 = 2\,\mathrm{k\Omega}\). Find all currents.

Step 1 — set up the three equations: \[ I_1 = I_2 + I_3, \qquad 12 = 1000\,I_1 + 2000\,I_2, \qquad 2000\,I_2 = 2000\,I_3 . \]
Step 2 — exploit symmetry: the third equation gives \(I_2 = I_3\), so \(I_1 = 2 I_2\).
Step 3 — substitute: \( 12 = 1000 (2 I_2) + 2000 I_2 = 4000\, I_2 \Rightarrow I_2 = 3\,\mathrm{mA} \).
\( I_2 = I_3 = 3\,\mathrm{mA},\; I_1 = 6\,\mathrm{mA} \). Check (KVL M₁): \(12 - 6 - 6 = 0\) ✓
🚫Classic pitfall: sign discipline

90 % of wrong answers in GET exams are sign errors. The cure: before writing any equation, draw an assumed arrow for every current and voltage (direction is your free choice). Then apply the rules mechanically. If a result comes out negative, the quantity simply flows opposite to your arrow — that is not an error. German convention: in the Verbraucherzählpfeilsystem (load reference convention), U and I arrows point the same way through a load, and then \(P = UI > 0\) means the component absorbs power.

7. Series, parallel, and the two dividers

7.1 Series connection (Reihenschaltung)

Same current through all elements; voltages add:

\[ R_{\text{total}} = R_1 + R_2 + \dots + R_n \]

The applied voltage splits proportionally to resistance — the voltage divider (Spannungsteiler), arguably the most-used formula in all of electronics:

\[ \boxed{\;\frac{U_2}{U_{\text{total}}} = \frac{R_2}{R_1 + R_2}\;} \]

7.2 Parallel connection (Parallelschaltung)

Same voltage across all elements; currents add. Conductances add, hence:

\[ \frac{1}{R_{\text{total}}} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \dots \qquad \text{two resistors: } R_{\text{total}} = \frac{R_1 R_2}{R_1 + R_2} \]

The parallel total is always smaller than the smallest member — adding a path can only make flow easier. Current splits in inverse proportion to resistance (current divider, Stromteiler): the smaller resistor takes the larger share.

🧪Interactive — the voltage divider simulation

Output voltage of the divider as a function of \(R_2\), for your chosen \(R_1\) and input voltage. The dot is your current design point. Note the curve is not linear in \(R_2\) — it saturates toward \(U_{in}\) because for \(R_2 \gg R_1\) nearly all voltage drops across \(R_2\).

🔑Why the divider matters

Sensor interfaces (a thermistor forming \(R_2\)), volume knobs (a potentiometer is a variable divider), reference voltages, level shifters — all voltage dividers. Its one weakness: connect a load across \(R_2\) and the ratio changes, because the load sits in parallel with \(R_2\). Quantifying that "loading effect" is precisely what Thévenin's theorem (next section) is for.

8. Thévenin & Norton — any network becomes two components

Here is the most elegant result of DC circuit theory. Take any linear network of sources and resistors, with two terminals sticking out. As seen from those terminals, the whole network behaves exactly like:

  • one ideal voltage source \(U_{Th}\) in series with one resistor \(R_{Th}\)  (Thévenin, Ersatzspannungsquelle), or equivalently
  • one ideal current source \(I_N = U_{Th}/R_{Th}\) in parallel with the same \(R_{Th}\)  (Norton, Ersatzstromquelle).

Recipe:

  1. \(U_{Th}\) = open-circuit voltage at the terminals (Leerlaufspannung).
  2. \(R_{Th}\) = resistance seen looking into the terminals with all sources "switched off" (voltage sources → short circuit, current sources → open circuit). Alternatively \(R_{Th} = U_{Th}/I_{SC}\) with the short-circuit current.
Worked example Thévenin equivalent of a loaded divider

A divider with \(U_q = 10\,\mathrm{V}\), \(R_1 = 6\,\mathrm{k\Omega}\), \(R_2 = 4\,\mathrm{k\Omega}\); terminals across \(R_2\). What voltage does a \(10\,\mathrm{k\Omega}\) load actually see?

Step 1 — open-circuit voltage: \( U_{Th} = 10 \cdot \frac{4}{6+4} = 4\,\mathrm{V} \).
Step 2 — dead network: replace \(U_q\) with a short. From the terminals you now see \(R_1\) parallel to \(R_2\): \( R_{Th} = \frac{6 \cdot 4}{6 + 4} = 2.4\,\mathrm{k\Omega} \).
Step 3 — attach the load to the equivalent: a simple two-resistor divider now: \( U_L = 4 \cdot \frac{10}{2.4 + 10} \approx 3.23\,\mathrm{V} \).
The "4 V" divider delivers only ≈ 3.23 V into 10 kΩ — a 19 % sag. Rule of thumb: keep the load ≥ 10× \(R_{Th}\) to stay within ~10 %.

9. Maximum power transfer & efficiency

Connect a load \(R_L\) to a real source \((U_q, R_i)\). The load power is

\[ P_L = I^2 R_L = \frac{U_q^2 \, R_L}{(R_i + R_L)^2}. \]

Small \(R_L\): lots of current but tiny voltage — little power. Large \(R_L\): full voltage but a trickle of current — little power again. The maximum lies exactly at

\[ \boxed{\;R_L = R_i\;} \quad\text{(matching, } P_{max} = \frac{U_q^2}{4 R_i}\text{)}, \]

where, however, the efficiency is only \(\eta = 50\,\%\) — half the energy heats the source. Hence two opposite design philosophies: communications engineers match impedances (maximum signal power matters, Module 21); power engineers do the opposite and make \(R_i \ll R_L\) (efficiency matters — a matched power plant would burn half its output in its own generators).

10. Systematic analysis: nodal analysis

Kirchhoff plus Ohm always works, but ad-hoc loop-hunting scales terribly. The industry-standard systematic method — the one inside SPICE and every circuit simulator — is nodal analysis (Knotenpotentialverfahren):

  1. Pick a reference node (ground); every other node \(k\) gets an unknown potential \(\varphi_k\).
  2. Write KCL at each non-reference node, expressing every branch current via Ohm's law as \((\varphi_a - \varphi_b)/R\).
  3. Solve the resulting linear system \(\mathbf{G}\boldsymbol{\varphi} = \mathbf{i}\) — with the linear algebra of Module 01.

For \(n\) non-reference nodes you get exactly \(n\) equations, mechanically. The conductance matrix \(\mathbf{G}\) even has a fill-in-by-inspection structure: diagonal entry = sum of conductances at the node; off-diagonal = minus the conductance between the two nodes; symmetric for resistor networks.

Worked example Fig. 6.1 again — by nodal analysis, one line

Only node A is unknown (B is ground). KCL at A, currents out of A positive:

\[ \frac{\varphi_A - 12}{1\,\mathrm{k}} + \frac{\varphi_A}{2\,\mathrm{k}} + \frac{\varphi_A}{2\,\mathrm{k}} = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; \varphi_A \left(\tfrac1{1\mathrm{k}} + \tfrac1{2\mathrm{k}} + \tfrac1{2\mathrm{k}}\right) = \tfrac{12}{1\mathrm{k}} \;\Rightarrow\; \varphi_A = 6\,\mathrm{V}. \]
\(\varphi_A = 6\) V, so \(I_2 = I_3 = 6\,\mathrm{V}/2\,\mathrm{k\Omega} = 3\) mA — same result as Section 6, but one equation instead of three. That efficiency gain grows with circuit size.

12. Exercises

Work these on paper before opening the solutions — the struggle is the learning.

E1An LED needs 2.0 V at 20 mA; the supply is 5 V. Size the series resistor and its minimum power rating. easy

Show solution
The resistor must drop \(5 - 2 = 3\) V at 20 mA: \( R = 3/0.02 = 150\,\Omega \). Its dissipation: \( P = U I = 3 \cdot 0.02 = 60\,\mathrm{mW} \) — a standard 0.25 W resistor has ample margin. This tiny calculation is probably the most frequently performed circuit design task on Earth.

E2Three resistors: \(R_1 = 100\,\Omega\) in series with the parallel combination of \(R_2 = 300\,\Omega\) and \(R_3 = 150\,\Omega\). Total resistance? Current from a 12 V source? Voltage across the parallel pair? easy

Show solution
\( R_2 \| R_3 = \frac{300 \cdot 150}{450} = 100\,\Omega \) → \( R_{tot} = 200\,\Omega \) → \( I = 12/200 = 60\,\mathrm{mA} \). Divider: the parallel pair gets \( 12 \cdot \frac{100}{200} = 6\,\mathrm{V} \). (Check via currents: \(I_2 = 6/300 = 20\) mA, \(I_3 = 6/150 = 40\) mA, sum 60 mA ✓)

E3A battery shows 12.6 V unloaded and 11.8 V while delivering 40 A. Find \(R_i\), the short-circuit current, and the power wasted internally at 40 A. medium

Show solution
Sag: \( \Delta U = 0.8\,\mathrm{V} \) at 40 A → \( R_i = 0.8/40 = 20\,\mathrm{m\Omega} \). Short-circuit current: \( I_{sc} = 12.6/0.02 = 630\,\mathrm{A} \) (why battery terminals must never be shorted — and why a car battery can crank a starter). Internal loss: \( P_i = I^2 R_i = 1600 \cdot 0.02 = 32\,\mathrm{W} \) of heat inside the battery.

E4Solve by nodal analysis: node 1 connects to a 24 V source through \(2\,\Omega\), to ground through \(4\,\Omega\), and to node 2 through \(4\,\Omega\); node 2 connects to ground through \(8\,\Omega\). Find \(\varphi_1, \varphi_2\). medium

Show solution
KCL at node 1: \( \frac{\varphi_1 - 24}{2} + \frac{\varphi_1}{4} + \frac{\varphi_1 - \varphi_2}{4} = 0 \) → \( \varphi_1 - 0.25\,\varphi_2 = 12 \) (after ×1... simplifying: multiply by 4: \(2\varphi_1 - 48 + \varphi_1 + \varphi_1 - \varphi_2 = 0\) → \(4\varphi_1 - \varphi_2 = 48\)).
KCL at node 2: \( \frac{\varphi_2 - \varphi_1}{4} + \frac{\varphi_2}{8} = 0 \) → \( -2\varphi_1 + 3\varphi_2 = 0 \) → \( \varphi_1 = 1.5\,\varphi_2 \).
Substitute: \( 6\varphi_2 - \varphi_2 = 48 \Rightarrow \varphi_2 = 9.6\,\mathrm{V},\; \varphi_1 = 14.4\,\mathrm{V} \). Check at node 2: \((9.6-14.4)/4 = -1.2\) A in, \(9.6/8 = 1.2\) A out ✓

E5Prove that the maximum-power condition is \(R_L = R_i\) by differentiating \(P_L(R_L)\). challenge

Show solution
\( P_L = \frac{U_q^2 R_L}{(R_i + R_L)^2} \). Quotient rule: \[ \frac{\mathrm{d}P_L}{\mathrm{d}R_L} = U_q^2 \, \frac{(R_i + R_L)^2 - R_L \cdot 2 (R_i + R_L)}{(R_i + R_L)^4} = U_q^2 \, \frac{R_i - R_L}{(R_i + R_L)^3}. \] This is positive for \(R_L < R_i\), zero at \(R_L = R_i\), negative beyond — a maximum. Physically: below matching you lose voltage, above it you lose current; the product peaks in the middle.

E6Twelve identical 1 Ω resistors form the edges of a cube. What is the resistance between opposite corners? challenge

Show solution
Classic symmetry argument: by the cube's symmetry, the three nodes adjacent to the input corner are at equal potential, as are the three adjacent to the output corner. Equal-potential nodes can be merged without changing anything. The network collapses to: 3 resistors in parallel (⅓ Ω), then 6 in parallel (⅙ Ω), then 3 in parallel (⅓ Ω), all in series: \[ R = \tfrac13 + \tfrac16 + \tfrac13 = \tfrac56\,\Omega \approx 0.83\,\Omega. \] Moral: before grinding through nodal analysis, look for symmetry — it can trivialize a 7-unknown problem.
