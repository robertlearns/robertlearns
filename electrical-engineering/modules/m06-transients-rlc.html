⚡ Fundamentals of Electrical Engineering · Module 06

Transients & RLC Networks · Ausgleichsvorgänge & Schwingkreise

Flip any switch and, for a few microseconds or milliseconds, the circuit is neither in its old state nor its new one — it is in between, and that in-between is where camera flashes fire, ignition coils make 30 kV from a 12 V battery, and radios pick one station out of thousands. This module is about what circuits do in time: exponential settling, the violent revenge of interrupted inductors, and the beautiful, sometimes dangerous phenomenon of resonance.

Corresponds to: Grundlagen der Elektrotechnik II/III (2nd–3rd semester) Prerequisites: Modules 02, 04, 05 Study time: ≈ 5–7 h

1. What is a transient?

In Module 04 every circuit sat calmly in a steady state (eingeschwungener Zustand): all voltages and currents constant (DC) or, as in Module 05, settled into fixed-amplitude sinusoids. But circuits do not live in steady state forever — switches close, loads connect, lightning strikes. A transient (Ausgleichsvorgang, literally "equalization process") is the finite-time journey between two steady states, triggered by a switching event (Schaltvorgang) at some instant we conventionally call \(t = 0\).

Why is the journey not instantaneous? Because two of our components store energy: the capacitor (Kondensator) stores it in its electric field, the inductor (Spule) in its magnetic field. Energy cannot teleport — it must flow in or out through finite power, and that takes time. Resistor-only circuits have no storage, hence no transients: they jump to the new state instantly. Add one storage element and you get first-order exponential behavior; add two and the circuit can oscillate — energy sloshing back and forth between \(C\) and \(L\) like water between two connected tanks.

🔑Key idea: transients = energy bookkeeping in time

The number of independent energy-storage elements equals the order of the differential equation that governs the circuit. One \(C\) or one \(L\): first order, pure exponential decay, characterized entirely by one number — the time constant \(\tau\) (Zeitkonstante). One \(C\) and one \(L\): second order, and a brand-new possibility appears — oscillation.

2. The two continuity rules and stored energy

Everything in this module rests on two facts, each forced by an energy argument.

2.1 Capacitor voltage cannot jump

The capacitor's stored energy is

\[ W_C = \tfrac{1}{2}\,C\,u_C^2 . \]

If \(u_C\) jumped discontinuously, \(W_C\) would change by a finite amount in zero time — infinite power. Equivalently, from \(i_C = C\,\mathrm{d}u_C/\mathrm{d}t\): a voltage jump would require an infinite current. Neither exists in nature, so:

\[ \boxed{\,u_C(0^+) = u_C(0^-)\,} \]

The capacitor voltage immediately after switching equals the voltage immediately before. The capacitor current, by contrast, may jump freely — and usually does.

2.2 Inductor current cannot jump

Dually, the inductor stores

\[ W_L = \tfrac{1}{2}\,L\,i_L^2 , \]

and \(u_L = L\,\mathrm{d}i_L/\mathrm{d}t\) says a current jump would demand an infinite voltage. Hence:

\[ \boxed{\,i_L(0^+) = i_L(0^-)\,} \]

The inductor current is continuous; the inductor voltage may jump — and when you try to force the current to jump anyway (opening a switch on a live coil), the inductor generates whatever voltage it takes to keep its current flowing. That single sentence explains sparking switches, ignition coils and destroyed transistors; we will quantify it in Section 5.

💡Intuition: the flywheel and the water tower

Think of a capacitor as a water tower: its water level (voltage) changes only as fast as pipes can fill or drain it — you cannot teleport water in. Think of an inductor as a flywheel: its rotation speed (current) has inertia. You can throttle a flywheel gently, but try to stop it dead with your hand and it will tear your hand off — that violent reaction is the inductive voltage spike.

🚫Classic pitfall: continuity of the wrong quantity

Exam scripts are littered with "\(i_C(0^+) = i_C(0^-)\)" and "\(u_L(0^+) = u_L(0^-)\)". Both are false in general. Only the state variables — capacitor voltage and inductor current, the quantities carrying the stored energy — are continuous. Everything else (resistor voltages, capacitor currents, inductor voltages) is allowed to jump at \(t=0\), and you must recompute them from the circuit at \(t = 0^+\).

3. First order: the RC circuit, derived properly

The drosophila of transients: a source \(U_0\), a switch closing at \(t=0\), a resistor \(R\), a capacitor \(C\), initially uncharged (Fig. 3.1).

3.1 Setting up and solving the ODE

KVL around the loop for \(t > 0\), with \(i = C\,\mathrm{d}u_C/\mathrm{d}t\):

\[ U_0 = R\,i + u_C = RC\,\frac{\mathrm{d}u_C}{\mathrm{d}t} + u_C . \]

This is exactly the first-order linear ODE with constant coefficients you solved in Module 02: general solution = particular (steady-state) solution + homogeneous (decaying) solution. The particular solution is the new steady state \(u_{C,p} = U_0\) (in DC steady state no current flows into \(C\), so no drop across \(R\)). The homogeneous equation \(RC\,\dot u + u = 0\) has the solution \(K e^{-t/RC}\). Fitting the initial condition \(u_C(0)=0\) (continuity rule!) gives \(K = -U_0\):

\[ \boxed{\;u_C(t) = U_0\left(1 - e^{-t/\tau}\right), \qquad \tau = RC\;} \]

and the current, which does jump at \(t=0\):

\[ i(t) = C\,\frac{\mathrm{d}u_C}{\mathrm{d}t} = \frac{U_0}{R}\,e^{-t/\tau}. \]

At \(t=0^+\) the uncharged capacitor looks like a short circuit (it holds its voltage, 0 V) and the current leaps to \(U_0/R\); as \(t \to \infty\) the capacitor looks like an open circuit and the current dies to zero.

3.2 The time constant τ — Zeitkonstante

Check the units: \([\tau] = \Omega \cdot \mathrm{F} = \frac{\mathrm{V}}{\mathrm{A}}\cdot\frac{\mathrm{A\,s}}{\mathrm{V}} = \mathrm{s}\). One time constant is how long the circuit would take to finish if it kept its initial pace — geometrically, the initial tangent of \(u_C(t)\) hits \(U_0\) exactly at \(t=\tau\). Because the pace itself decays, after one \(\tau\) the transition is only

\[ 1 - e^{-1} \approx 0.632 = 63.2\,\% \]

complete. Memorize the ladder:

elapsed time\(1\tau\)\(2\tau\)\(3\tau\)\(4\tau\)\(5\tau\)
fraction of transition completed63.2 %86.5 %95.0 %98.2 %99.3 %
remaining36.8 %13.5 %5.0 %1.8 %0.7 %

The exponential mathematically never finishes, but engineering practice declares the transient over after five time constants (< 1 % remaining). "Settled to \(5\tau\)" is the standard rule of thumb (Faustregel) in datasheets and lab work.

3.3 Discharge

Now charge the capacitor to \(U_0\) and at \(t = 0\) connect it to a plain resistor. The same ODE with \(U_0\) replaced by the new steady state (zero) gives pure decay:

\[ u_C(t) = U_0\,e^{-t/\tau}, \qquad i(t) = -\frac{U_0}{R}\,e^{-t/\tau}, \]

the minus sign flagging that current now flows out of the capacitor. After one \(\tau\), 36.8 % of the voltage remains; the stored energy — going as \(u^2\) — is already down to \(e^{-2} \approx 13.5\,\%\).

Worked example Camera flash: seconds to charge, kilowatts to fire

A compact-camera flash stores energy in an electrolytic capacitor \(C = 1000\,\mathrm{\mu F}\) charged to \(U_0 = 330\,\mathrm{V}\) (via a boost converter whose output behaves like a source with \(R \approx 500\,\Omega\)). The xenon tube, once triggered, behaves like \(R_{tube} \approx 0.6\,\Omega\). Find the stored energy, the recycle time, the flash duration, and the average power during the flash.

Step 1 — stored energy: \( W = \tfrac12 C U_0^2 = \tfrac12 \cdot 10^{-3} \cdot 330^2 \approx 54\,\mathrm{J} \). (Enough to be genuinely dangerous — flash caps bite hours after the battery is removed.)
Step 2 — recycle (charge) time: \( \tau_{ch} = RC = 500 \cdot 10^{-3} = 0.5\,\mathrm{s} \) → practically full at \(5\tau = 2.5\,\mathrm{s}\). That is exactly the familiar wait between flashes.
Step 3 — flash duration: \( \tau_{dis} = R_{tube}\,C = 0.6 \cdot 10^{-3} = 0.6\,\mathrm{ms} \) → the flash is essentially over after \(5\tau \approx 3\,\mathrm{ms}\).
Step 4 — average power: \( P \approx 54\,\mathrm{J} / 3\,\mathrm{ms} \approx 18\,\mathrm{kW} \) — nine kettles' worth, from two AA batteries, because the capacitor decouples energy delivery rate from energy collection rate.
\(W \approx 54\) J, recycle ≈ 2.5 s, flash ≈ 3 ms, peak-ish power ≈ 18 kW. Same energy, four orders of magnitude apart in power — that asymmetry is the whole business model of the capacitor.

4. The universal first-order recipe

Look again at charging, \(u_C = U_0(1-e^{-t/\tau})\), and discharging, \(u_C = U_0 e^{-t/\tau}\). Both are special cases of one formula. In any circuit with a single storage element, every voltage and every current moves from its initial value to its final value along the same exponential:

\[ \boxed{\;x(t) = x_\infty + \left(x_0 - x_\infty\right)e^{-t/\tau}\;} \]

where \(x_0 = x(0^+)\) is the initial value just after switching, \(x_\infty\) is the value in the new steady state, and \(\tau\) is the circuit's one time constant. Read it as: "final value, plus the initial surplus, decaying away."

🔑Key idea: three numbers solve every first-order switching problem

You never need to set up the ODE again. Any first-order switching problem — any topology, any element voltage or current you are asked for — is fully determined by three numbers: \(x_0\), \(x_\infty\), \(\tau\). Find them from three DC circuit analyses (Module 04 skills!) and write the answer down. The recipe is the single highest-leverage formula of this module; Section 6 turns it into a checklist.

Solving the recipe for time also answers "how long until \(x\) reaches a threshold \(x_1\)?":

\[ t_1 = \tau \,\ln\!\frac{x_0 - x_\infty}{x_1 - x_\infty}. \]

This little formula is the working heart of RC timers (the classic 555 chip), of switch debouncing (Entprellen) — where an RC low-pass with \(\tau \approx 10\,\mathrm{ms}\) rides out the mechanical chatter of a pressed button before a logic gate looks at the level — and of every "press and hold for 2 seconds" feature ever built.

5. RL circuits and the inductive kick

5.1 The RL time constant

Swap the capacitor for an inductor: source \(U_0\), resistor \(R\), inductor \(L\), switch closes at \(t=0\), \(i_L(0)=0\). KVL: \(U_0 = Ri + L\,\mathrm{d}i/\mathrm{d}t\). Same mathematics, new state variable — the current:

\[ i(t) = \frac{U_0}{R}\left(1 - e^{-t/\tau}\right), \qquad \boxed{\;\tau = \frac{L}{R}\;} \]

Note the duality throughout: the inductor at \(t=0^+\) looks like an open circuit (it insists on its old current, zero) and in DC steady state like a short circuit (constant current → no voltage). Larger \(L\) means more inertia → longer \(\tau\); larger \(R\) here means faster settling, the opposite of RC.

5.2 Interrupting an inductor: the kick — Abschaltüberspannung

Now the dangerous half. Let the current reach \(I_0 = U_0/R\), then open the switch. The current path is gone, but the continuity rule says \(i_L(0^+) = I_0\) — the flywheel will not stop. The inductor drives its current into whatever is available. If that is only the tiny stray capacitance \(C_s\) of the wiring (picofarads), the magnetic energy converts to electric energy in that minuscule capacitor:

\[ \tfrac12 L I_0^2 = \tfrac12 C_s \hat u^2 \quad\Rightarrow\quad \hat u = I_0 \sqrt{\frac{L}{C_s}} . \]

With \(C_s\) tiny, \(\hat u\) is huge — kilovolts from a 12 V circuit. In practice the voltage rises until something conducts: the air in the switch gap breaks down and you see a spark. That is why mechanical switches on inductive loads (motors, relays, transformers) spark and erode, and why a transistor switching a relay coil without protection dies on the first turn-off.

The standard cure is the flyback diode (Freilaufdiode, "free-wheeling diode") in parallel with the coil, oriented so it is blocking during normal operation. The instant the switch opens, the coil's voltage reverses, the diode conducts, and the current circulates harmlessly through the coil–diode loop, decaying with \(\tau = L/R_{coil}\) while the energy burns off in the coil's own resistance. The voltage across the switch is clamped to \(U_0 + 0.7\,\mathrm{V}\).

Worked example How big is the kick? A relay without its diode

A 12 V relay coil: \(L = 100\,\mathrm{mH}\), \(R_{coil} = 100\,\Omega\), driven from 12 V. The wiring's stray capacitance is \(C_s \approx 100\,\mathrm{pF}\). Estimate the turn-off voltage spike without a flyback diode, and the current-decay time constant with one.

Step 1 — steady current before switching: \( I_0 = 12/100 = 120\,\mathrm{mA} \); stored energy \( W = \tfrac12 \cdot 0.1 \cdot 0.12^2 = 0.72\,\mathrm{mJ} \).
Step 2 — energy transfer estimate: \( \hat u = I_0\sqrt{L/C_s} = 0.12\sqrt{0.1/10^{-10}} = 0.12 \cdot 31\,600 \approx 3.8\,\mathrm{kV}. \)
Step 3 — reality check: air breaks down at ~3 kV/mm, so the switch gap arcs long before 3.8 kV is reached — the "spike" is clamped by the spark itself. A transistor, however, has no merciful arc: it simply avalanches and dies. (An ignition coil is this exact circuit on purpose, with a transformer to multiply the kick to ~30 kV for the spark plug.)
Step 4 — with flyback diode: the current free-wheels with \( \tau = L/R_{coil} = 0.1/100 = 1\,\mathrm{ms} \), gone after ~5 ms. The switch never sees more than \(12 + 0.7 \approx 12.7\,\mathrm{V}\).
Unprotected: multi-kilovolt spike (arc). Protected: 12.7 V and a gentle 1 ms decay. One 5-cent diode.
📜The kick, put to work

The inductive kick is a nuisance turned into an industry: car ignition systems (Kettering, 1910) interrupt a coil current to fire spark plugs; boost converters (Module 14) interrupt an inductor thousands of times per second to step voltages up; electric fence energizers and xenon-flash trigger transformers do the same. And every relay driver, motor bridge and solenoid valve you will ever design needs its freewheeling path — look at any motor-driver IC datasheet and you will find the diodes built in.

6. The systematic switching-problem method

Here is the checklist that, combined with the recipe of Section 4, mechanically solves every first-order switching problem (Schaltaufgabe):

  1. Analyze \(t = 0^-\) (old steady state): replace \(C\) by an open circuit, \(L\) by a short circuit; do DC analysis (Module 04). Extract the state variables \(u_C(0^-)\), \(i_L(0^-)\).
  2. Apply continuity: \(u_C(0^+) = u_C(0^-)\), \(i_L(0^+) = i_L(0^-)\). If you need a non-state quantity at \(0^+\), analyze the switched circuit with the storage element replaced by a source of its initial value.
  3. Analyze \(t \to \infty\) (new steady state): again \(C\) → open, \(L\) → short, but now with the switched topology. Read off \(x_\infty\).
  4. Find \(\tau\): deactivate all sources and compute the Thévenin resistance \(R_{Th}\) seen from the storage element's terminals (Module 04, Section 8). Then \(\tau = R_{Th}\,C\) or \(\tau = L/R_{Th}\).
  5. Write the answer: \(x(t) = x_\infty + (x_0 - x_\infty)\,e^{-t/\tau}\). Sketch it. Sanity-check both endpoints.
⚠️τ belongs to the circuit, not to the component

The most common error in step 4: using whatever resistor happens to be drawn next to the capacitor. \(\tau\) is set by the total Thévenin resistance seen from the storage element in the post-switching circuit — often a series/parallel combination, and it changes if the switching changed the topology. One circuit can even have different \(\tau\) values before and after switching.

Worked example The full method on a two-resistor circuit

A 10 V source feeds node A through \(R_1 = 4\,\mathrm{k\Omega}\). From A, a capacitor \(C = 10\,\mathrm{\mu F}\) goes to ground. A resistor \(R_2 = 6\,\mathrm{k\Omega}\) from A to ground is connected through a switch that closes at \(t=0\) (open before). Find \(u_C(t)\) and the time at which \(u_C = 7\,\mathrm{V}\).

Step 1 — \(t=0^-\): switch open, steady state: no current flows (C is open, R₂ disconnected) → no drop across \(R_1\) → \(u_C(0^-) = 10\,\mathrm{V}\).
Step 2 — continuity: \(u_C(0^+) = 10\,\mathrm{V}\).
Step 3 — \(t\to\infty\): C open, switch closed: plain voltage divider, \(u_\infty = 10 \cdot \frac{6}{4+6} = 6\,\mathrm{V}\).
Step 4 — τ: kill the source (short it). From the capacitor's terminals you see \(R_1 \parallel R_2 = \frac{4\cdot 6}{10} = 2.4\,\mathrm{k\Omega}\), so \(\tau = 2.4\,\mathrm{k\Omega}\cdot 10\,\mathrm{\mu F} = 24\,\mathrm{ms}\).
Step 5 — recipe: \[ u_C(t) = 6 + (10-6)\,e^{-t/24\,\mathrm{ms}} = 6 + 4\,e^{-t/24\,\mathrm{ms}}\ \mathrm{V}. \] Threshold: \(7 = 6 + 4e^{-t/\tau} \Rightarrow e^{-t/\tau} = \tfrac14 \Rightarrow t = 24\,\mathrm{ms}\cdot\ln 4 \approx 33\,\mathrm{ms}\).
\(u_C\) sags exponentially from 10 V to 6 V with \(\tau = 24\) ms, crossing 7 V at ≈ 33 ms. Endpoint check: \(t=0\): 10 V ✓, \(t\to\infty\): 6 V ✓.

7. Second order: the series RLC circuit

Now put both storage elements in one loop (Fig. 7.1): a step source \(U_0\), resistor \(R\), inductor \(L\), capacitor \(C\) in series. Two energy stores → second-order dynamics → the possibility of oscillation: the capacitor discharges into the inductor, the inductor's inertia overshoots and recharges the capacitor the other way, and so on — Section 2's sloshing animation, now with friction \(R\).

7.1 The ODE and its characteristic equation

KVL with \(i = C\,\dot u_C\):

\[ U_0 = L\,\frac{\mathrm{d}i}{\mathrm{d}t} + R\,i + u_C \;\Rightarrow\; LC\,\frac{\mathrm{d}^2 u_C}{\mathrm{d}t^2} + RC\,\frac{\mathrm{d}u_C}{\mathrm{d}t} + u_C = U_0 . \]

The exponential ansatz \(u_h = K e^{st}\) (Module 02) turns the homogeneous equation into the characteristic equation (charakteristische Gleichung):

\[ s^2 + \frac{R}{L}\,s + \frac{1}{LC} = 0 \qquad\Rightarrow\qquad s_{1,2} = -\alpha \pm \sqrt{\alpha^2 - \omega_0^2}, \]

with two parameters that deserve names and a permanent place in your head — the undamped natural frequency (Kennkreisfrequenz) and the decay rate:

\[ \boxed{\;\omega_0 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{LC}}\;}, \qquad \alpha = \frac{R}{2L}. \]

Their ratio is the dimensionless damping ratio (Dämpfungsgrad, in German texts often \(d\) or \(\vartheta\)):

\[ \boxed{\;\zeta = \frac{\alpha}{\omega_0} = \frac{R}{2}\sqrt{\frac{C}{L}}\;} \]

7.2 The trichotomy: over-, critically, underdamped

Everything hinges on the sign of \(\alpha^2 - \omega_0^2\), i.e. on whether \(\zeta \gtrless 1\). For the step response with \(u_C(0)=0\), \(i(0)=0\):

The boundary sits at \(\zeta = 1\), i.e. at the critical resistance

\[ R_{crit} = 2\sqrt{\frac{L}{C}} . \]
🔑Key idea: which damping do you want?

Each case is somebody's design goal. Critically damped (or slightly under, \(\zeta \approx 0.7\)): measurement instruments, car suspensions, oscilloscope probes, defibrillator discharge pulses — you want to arrive at the new value fast without overshooting it. Overdamped: when overshoot is unacceptable at any price (some precision analog steps). Strongly underdamped (\(\zeta \ll 1\)): oscillators, radio tuners, MRI coils — there the "defect" of ringing is the product, and you fight to make \(\zeta\) as small as possible. Note \(\zeta \approx 0.7\) (not 1) is the usual sweet spot in practice: ~4 % overshoot buys distinctly faster settling into a tolerance band.

Worked example A complete second-order solve, no steps skipped

Series RLC: \(R = 40\,\Omega\), \(L = 10\,\mathrm{mH}\), \(C = 10\,\mathrm{\mu F}\), step \(U_0 = 10\,\mathrm{V}\) at \(t=0\), initially at rest. Classify the response, find \(u_C(t)\), and compute the first-peak overshoot.

Step 1 — parameters: \( \omega_0 = 1/\sqrt{LC} = 1/\sqrt{10^{-2}\cdot 10^{-5}} = 3162\,\mathrm{s^{-1}} \); \( \alpha = R/2L = 40/0.02 = 2000\,\mathrm{s^{-1}} \); \( \zeta = 2000/3162 = 0.632 \lt 1 \) → underdamped. (Check: \(R_{crit} = 2\sqrt{10^{-2}/10^{-5}} = 63.2\,\Omega \gt 40\,\Omega\) ✓)
Step 2 — damped frequency: \( \omega_d = \sqrt{\omega_0^2 - \alpha^2} = \sqrt{10^7 - 4\cdot 10^6} = 2449\,\mathrm{s^{-1}} \) → \(f_d \approx 390\,\mathrm{Hz}\).
Step 3 — solution (initial conditions \(u_C(0)=0\), \(\dot u_C(0) = i(0)/C = 0\)): \[ u_C(t) = 10\left[1 - e^{-2000t}\left(\cos 2449t + \tfrac{2000}{2449}\sin 2449t\right)\right]\,\mathrm{V}. \]
Step 4 — first peak: it occurs half a damped period in, \(t_p = \pi/\omega_d = 1.28\,\mathrm{ms}\), and the overshoot is \( e^{-\alpha t_p} = e^{-2000\cdot 0.00128} = e^{-2.57} \approx 0.077 \), so \( \hat u_C = 10\,(1 + 0.077) \approx 10.8\,\mathrm{V} \).
Underdamped, \(f_d \approx 390\) Hz, peaks at ≈ 10.8 V (7.7 % overshoot) after 1.28 ms, settled after ≈ \(5/\alpha = 2.5\) ms. Sanity: \(\zeta = 0.63\) is near the practical sweet spot — mild ringing, quick settling.

8. The underdamped case up close

For \(\zeta < 1\) the roots are \(s_{1,2} = -\alpha \pm j\omega_d\) with the damped natural frequency (Eigenkreisfrequenz)

\[ \boxed{\;\omega_d = \omega_0\sqrt{1 - \zeta^2}\;} \]

— always a bit below \(\omega_0\): friction slows the pendulum. The free response is a sinusoid trapped inside a decaying envelope (Hüllkurve) \(\pm A\,e^{-\alpha t}\): the oscillation's amplitude shrinks by the factor \(e^{-\alpha T_d}\) every damped period \(T_d = 2\pi/\omega_d\).

That per-period shrink factor is worth a name. The logarithmic decrement (logarithmisches Dekrement) is the log of the ratio of successive same-side peaks:

\[ \Lambda = \ln\frac{x_n}{x_{n+1}} = \alpha\,T_d = \frac{2\pi\zeta}{\sqrt{1-\zeta^2}} . \]

Its practical value: measure two successive peak heights on an oscilloscope, take the log of their ratio, and you have determined \(\zeta\) of a physical system without knowing R, L or C at all. This trick works on circuits, buildings and aircraft wings alike, and it is the standard lab method for extracting damping from a ring-down trace.

⚠️Three frequencies, keep them apart

\(\omega_0 = 1/\sqrt{LC}\) is the undamped natural frequency (and, as we will see, the resonance frequency of the series circuit). \(\omega_d = \omega_0\sqrt{1-\zeta^2}\) is the frequency the damped circuit actually rings at. And the excitation frequency \(\omega\) of a driving source is a third, independent thing. For high-\(Q\) circuits (\(\zeta \lesssim 0.05\)) they are numerically almost identical — which is exactly why students merge them into one concept and then lose points when \(\zeta\) is not small.

9. Resonance — Resonanz

So far the source was a step. Now drive the series RLC with a sinusoid of adjustable frequency and use Module 05's phasor impedance:

\[ \underline{Z}(\omega) = R + j\left(\omega L - \frac{1}{\omega C}\right). \]

The inductive and capacitive reactances have opposite signs — and at exactly one frequency they cancel:

\[ \omega_0 L = \frac{1}{\omega_0 C} \quad\Rightarrow\quad \boxed{\;\omega_0 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{LC}}, \qquad f_0 = \frac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{LC}}\;} \]

(the same \(\omega_0\) as in Section 7 — the Thomson formula, Thomsonsche Schwingungsgleichung). At series resonance:

  • \(|\underline{Z}|\) is at its minimum, equal to plain \(R\); the circuit looks purely resistive;
  • the current is maximal, \(I_{max} = U/R\), and in phase with the source;
  • \(\underline{U}_L\) and \(\underline{U}_C\) are individually huge, equal in magnitude and opposite in phase — they cancel in the loop sum but each one is very real across its component.

9.1 Voltage magnification: the surprising overvoltage

How huge? At resonance \(I = U/R\), so the capacitor voltage is

\[ U_C = \frac{I}{\omega_0 C} = \frac{U}{\omega_0 C R} = Q\,U, \qquad U_L = \omega_0 L\, I = \frac{\omega_0 L}{R}\,U = Q\,U, \]

where \(Q\) is the quality factor defined next section. A resonant circuit with \(Q = 100\) driven by 1 V develops 100 V across its capacitor — from a 1 V source. This is not a paradox: over many cycles, the source keeps topping up the sloshing energy, and the stored amplitude builds until losses balance input, exactly like pushing a playground swing a little every period (Resonanzüberhöhung). It is also a genuine hazard: series resonances in power grids and in carelessly designed filters produce insulation-destroying overvoltages. And it is a genuine tool: MRI receive coils and antenna matching networks use precisely this magnification to lift microvolt signals above the noise before any amplifier touches them.

9.2 Parallel resonance — the dual

Swap the topology: \(R\), \(L\), \(C\) in parallel, driven by a current source. Everything dualizes. The admittance \( \underline{Y} = \frac1R + j(\omega C - \frac{1}{\omega L}) \) is minimal at the same \(\omega_0\), hence \(|\underline{Z}|\) is at a maximum: the parallel circuit rejects current at resonance (Sperrkreis, "blocking circuit"), while internally a magnified circulating current \(I_L = I_C = Q\,I\) sloshes between \(L\) and \(C\) — current magnification. Series circuit: voltage magnification, impedance minimum (Saugkreis, "suction circuit" — it sucks in its resonance frequency, used to trap specific harmonics in power systems). Parallel circuit: current magnification, impedance maximum — the standard frequency-selective load in radio-frequency amplifiers and oscillators (Module 13).

10. Quality factor and bandwidth

10.1 Q — Güte

The quality factor measures how underdamped, how "ringy", a resonator is. The fundamental, technology-independent definition is energetic:

\[ \boxed{\;Q = 2\pi\,\frac{\text{energy stored}}{\text{energy dissipated per cycle}}\;} \]

For the series RLC this evaluates to the circuit forms you will actually compute with:

\[ Q = \frac{\omega_0 L}{R} = \frac{1}{\omega_0 C R} = \frac{1}{R}\sqrt{\frac{L}{C}}, \qquad Q = \frac{1}{2\zeta}. \]

The last identity ties Sections 7–8 to this one: high \(Q\) ⇔ low damping ⇔ many ring-down cycles (a resonator rings for roughly \(Q/\pi\) periods before decaying to 4 %). Typical values: an RC filter has no resonance at all; a decent LC tank \(Q \sim 100\); a quartz crystal \(Q \sim 10^4\)–\(10^6\) (why your watch keeps time); a superconducting cavity \(Q \sim 10^{10}\).

10.2 Bandwidth — Bandbreite

Drive the series circuit off-resonance and the current falls. The two frequencies \(f_1, f_2\) where the current has dropped to \(I_{max}/\sqrt{2}\) — i.e. where the dissipated power has halved (the −3 dB points, Module 07) — bracket the bandwidth:

\[ \boxed{\;B = f_2 - f_1 = \frac{f_0}{Q}\;} \]

High \(Q\) means a needle-sharp resonance: enormous response at \(f_0\), nothing next door. That trade — magnification versus selectivity versus bandwidth, all set by one number — is the design equation of every tuned system. (At \(f_1\) and \(f_2\) the reactive term equals \(\pm R\), so the current phase is \(\mp 45^\circ\) there — a useful lab signature.)

🧪Interactive — the resonance curve simulation

Current versus frequency for a series RLC driven by 1 V. Lower \(R\) and watch the peak grow taller and narrower simultaneously — \(Q\) rises, bandwidth \(B = f_0/Q\) shrinks (dashed −3 dB level, marked \(f_1, f_2\)). Then nudge \(C\): the whole peak slides in frequency without changing shape much — that is literally what turning an old radio's tuning knob did.

Worked example Designing an AM radio tuner

An AM tuner must select the station at \(f_0 = 1.0\,\mathrm{MHz}\) while rejecting the neighbors 10 kHz away (AM channel spacing), so we need \(B \le 10\,\mathrm{kHz}\). The coil is fixed at \(L = 100\,\mathrm{\mu H}\). Find \(C\), the required \(Q\), the maximum tolerable series resistance, and the voltage across \(C\) for a 10 µV antenna signal.

Step 1 — tuning capacitor (Thomson): \( C = \frac{1}{\omega_0^2 L} = \frac{1}{(2\pi\cdot 10^6)^2 \cdot 10^{-4}} \approx 253\,\mathrm{pF} \) — right in the range of a classic variable air capacitor (~10–350 pF), which is why those values were standard.
Step 2 — required Q: \( Q = f_0/B = 10^6 / 10^4 = 100 \).
Step 3 — resistance budget: \( R \le \frac{\omega_0 L}{Q} = \frac{2\pi\cdot 10^6\cdot 10^{-4}}{100} \approx 6.3\,\Omega \). The coil's wire and losses must stay under ~6 Ω at 1 MHz — this, not the capacitor, is the hard part (skin effect, Module 05, raises wire resistance at RF).
Step 4 — magnification: \( U_C = Q\,U = 100 \cdot 10\,\mathrm{\mu V} = 1\,\mathrm{mV} \) — a free, noiseless ×100 "amplifier" before the first transistor. This is why every classic receiver starts with a tuned circuit.
\(C \approx 253\) pF, \(Q = 100\), \(R \le 6.3\,\Omega\), and the tuner hands the detector 1 mV from a 10 µV antenna. Sanity: \(\zeta = 1/2Q = 0.005\) — extremely underdamped, exactly as a tuner should be.

📜Where you'll meet all this again

Resonant circuits were radio's founding technology — Hertz's 1887 spark experiments were underdamped RLC transients, and Tesla's coils pushed \(Q\)-magnification to spectacular extremes. Today: every filter you design in Module 07 is shaped RLC behavior; switch-mode power supplies (Module 14) are deliberately managed inductor transients, millions per second; digital signal integrity (Module 16) is the fight against underdamped ringing on every PCB trace; control theory (Module 17) reuses \(\zeta\), \(\omega_0\) and overshoot verbatim for any second-order system; and an MRI scanner is a superb resonator at the proton's 64–300 MHz Larmor frequency — the received signal exists only because a high-\(Q\) coil magnifies it out of the noise.

12. Exercises

Work these on paper before opening the solutions — sketch every response before computing it.

E1An RC circuit has \(R = 10\,\mathrm{k\Omega}\) and \(C = 100\,\mathrm{\mu F}\). Find \(\tau\), the time to reach 95 % of the final voltage when charging, and the initial charging current from a 24 V source. easy

Show solution
\( \tau = RC = 10^4 \cdot 10^{-4} = 1\,\mathrm{s} \). 95 % is the \(3\tau\) landmark: \(t \approx 3\,\mathrm{s}\) (exactly \(\tau\ln 20 = 3.0\,\mathrm{s}\)). At \(t=0^+\) the empty capacitor is a short, so \(i(0^+) = 24\,\mathrm{V}/10\,\mathrm{k\Omega} = 2.4\,\mathrm{mA}\). Real-world remark: 1 s is a long electrical time constant — RC pairs like this build power-on-reset delays.

E2A coil with \(L = 50\,\mathrm{mH}\) and winding resistance \(25\,\Omega\) is connected to 12 V. Find \(\tau\), the final current, and the current after one time constant. easy

Show solution
\( \tau = L/R = 0.05/25 = 2\,\mathrm{ms} \). Final current (inductor → short in steady state): \(I_\infty = 12/25 = 480\,\mathrm{mA}\). After one \(\tau\): \(i = 0.632 \cdot 480 \approx 303\,\mathrm{mA}\). Sanity check: recipe with \(x_0 = 0\): \(i(\tau) = 480(1 - e^{-1})\) ✓. This 2 ms rise is why relays have a specified "operate time" — the contacts can't move until the current (hence force) has built up.

E3A 24 V source feeds node A through \(R_1 = 3\,\mathrm{k\Omega}\); \(C = 5\,\mathrm{\mu F}\) connects A to ground. At \(t=0\) a switch connects \(R_2 = 6\,\mathrm{k\Omega}\) from A to ground (previously disconnected; the circuit was in steady state). Find \(u_C(t)\) and the time to reach 18 V. medium

Show solution
Method of Section 6. \(t=0^-\): C open, no current → \(u_C(0^-) = 24\,\mathrm{V}\); continuity → \(u_C(0^+)=24\,\mathrm{V}\). \(t\to\infty\): divider, \(u_\infty = 24\cdot\frac{6}{9} = 16\,\mathrm{V}\). \(\tau\): source shorted → \(R_{Th} = 3\,\mathrm{k}\parallel 6\,\mathrm{k} = 2\,\mathrm{k\Omega}\), \(\tau = 2\,\mathrm{k}\cdot 5\,\mathrm{\mu} = 10\,\mathrm{ms}\). \[ u_C(t) = 16 + 8\,e^{-t/10\,\mathrm{ms}}\ \mathrm{V}. \] Threshold: \(18 = 16 + 8e^{-t/\tau} \Rightarrow e^{-t/\tau} = 1/4 \Rightarrow t = 10\ln 4 \approx 13.9\,\mathrm{ms}\). Endpoint check: 24 V at \(t=0\), 16 V at \(\infty\) ✓ — and note \(\tau\) used the parallel combination, not \(R_1\) or \(R_2\) alone.

E4A solenoid valve (\(L = 0.5\,\mathrm{H}\), operating current 2 A) is switched off by a transistor; the stray capacitance at the node is ≈ 200 pF. Estimate the theoretical voltage peak without protection, the stored energy, and state what a flyback diode limits the voltage to on a 24 V supply. medium

Show solution
Energy: \(W = \tfrac12 \cdot 0.5 \cdot 4 = 1\,\mathrm{J}\) (a lot!). Peak: \( \hat u = I_0\sqrt{L/C_s} = 2\sqrt{0.5/(2\cdot 10^{-10})} = 2\cdot 50\,000 = 100\,\mathrm{kV} \) — theoretical, ignition-coil territory; in reality the transistor avalanches or an arc forms at a few hundred volts and clamps it, usually destroying the transistor in the process. With a flyback diode the node is clamped to \(24 + 0.7 \approx 24.7\,\mathrm{V}\) and the 1 J dissipates gently in the coil resistance. Moral: the estimate being wildly "wrong" (100 kV never appears) is exactly the point — something must absorb that joule, and you choose what.

E5Series RLC: \(R = 500\,\Omega\), \(L = 2\,\mathrm{mH}\), \(C = 8\,\mathrm{nF}\). Classify the damping, and find \(\omega_0\), \(\zeta\), and (if it exists) \(f_d\). What R would give critical damping? medium

Show solution
\( \omega_0 = 1/\sqrt{2\cdot 10^{-3}\cdot 8\cdot 10^{-9}} = 1/\sqrt{1.6\cdot 10^{-11}} = 2.5\cdot 10^5\,\mathrm{s^{-1}} \) (\(f_0 \approx 39.8\,\mathrm{kHz}\)). \( R_{crit} = 2\sqrt{L/C} = 2\sqrt{2.5\cdot 10^5} = 1000\,\Omega \), so \( \zeta = R/R_{crit} = 0.5 \) → underdamped. \( \omega_d = \omega_0\sqrt{1 - 0.25} = 2.165\cdot 10^5\,\mathrm{s^{-1}} \) → \( f_d \approx 34.5\,\mathrm{kHz} \). Critical damping needs \(R = 1\,\mathrm{k\Omega}\). Check: \(\zeta = 0.5\) → overshoot \(e^{-\pi\zeta/\sqrt{1-\zeta^2}} = e^{-1.814} \approx 16\,\%\) — visible ringing, as expected for ζ well below 0.7.

E6A series resonant circuit: \(L = 100\,\mathrm{\mu H}\), \(C = 250\,\mathrm{pF}\), \(R = 8\,\Omega\), driven by 5 mV. Find \(f_0\), \(Q\), \(B\), and the capacitor voltage at resonance. challenge

Show solution
\( f_0 = \frac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{LC}} = \frac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{2.5\cdot 10^{-14}}} = \frac{1}{2\pi\cdot 1.581\cdot 10^{-7}} \approx 1.007\,\mathrm{MHz} \). \( Q = \frac{\omega_0 L}{R} = \frac{6.33\cdot 10^6 \cdot 10^{-4}}{8} \approx 79 \). \( B = f_0/Q \approx 12.7\,\mathrm{kHz} \). \( U_C = Q\,U = 79 \cdot 5\,\mathrm{mV} \approx 0.40\,\mathrm{V} \) — an 80-fold passive voltage gain. Sanity: current at resonance \(I = 5\,\mathrm{mV}/8\,\Omega = 625\,\mathrm{\mu A}\), and \(U_C = I/(\omega_0 C) = 625\,\mathrm{\mu A}/(6.33\cdot 10^6 \cdot 2.5\cdot 10^{-10}) \approx 0.40\,\mathrm{V}\) ✓ — both routes agree.

E7The two-capacitor paradox: a capacitor \(C\) charged to \(U_0\) is connected (through a wire of resistance \(R\)) to an identical uncharged capacitor. Find the final voltage, the initial and final stored energy, and show that exactly half the energy is lost — independent of R. Where did it go, and what happens as \(R \to 0\)? challenge

Show solution
Charge is conserved: \(Q_0 = C U_0\) redistributes over \(2C\) → \(U_\infty = U_0/2\). Energy before: \(W_0 = \tfrac12 C U_0^2\). After: \(W_\infty = \tfrac12 (2C)(U_0/2)^2 = \tfrac14 C U_0^2 = \tfrac12 W_0\). Half is gone. Where: the equalizing current is \(i(t) = \frac{U_0}{R} e^{-t/\tau}\) with \(\tau = R\cdot\frac{C}{2}\) (the two capacitors in series as seen by R). Dissipation: \( \int_0^\infty i^2R\,\mathrm{d}t = \frac{U_0^2}{R}\cdot\frac{\tau}{2} = \tfrac14 C U_0^2 \) — exactly the missing half, for every R. Smaller R just loses it faster (bigger current spike, shorter time; the \(R\) cancels). As \(R \to 0\) the lumped model breaks down: with real wiring the loop is an underdamped RLC (wire inductance!) and the "lost" energy partly radiates as a burst of electromagnetic waves — you can hear it as a click on an AM radio. Moral: energy bookkeeping exposes physics that idealized schematics hide.
