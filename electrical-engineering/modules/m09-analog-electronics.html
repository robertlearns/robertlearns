Analog Circuit Design · Analoge Schaltungstechnik

Every microphone signal, every sensor reading, every radio wave arrives as a few millivolts of fragile analog reality — and something must make it bigger, cleaner, and stronger without wrecking it. This module builds the art of amplification from a single transistor up to the operational amplifier, and then reveals the one idea — negative feedback — that turns imperfect silicon into circuits of almost mathematical precision.

Corresponds to: Elektronik / Schaltungstechnik (3rd–4th semester) Prerequisites: Modules 05, 08 Study time: ≈ 7–9 h

1. What amplification actually means

An amplifier (Verstärker) takes a small signal and produces a larger copy of it. That sounds like getting something for nothing — it is not. The extra energy comes from a DC power supply; the input signal merely controls how that supply energy is delivered to the load. The defining figure of merit is the gain (Verstärkung):

\[ A_u = \frac{u_{\text{out}}}{u_{\text{in}}} \quad\text{(voltage gain)}, \qquad A_i = \frac{i_{\text{out}}}{i_{\text{in}}}, \qquad A_p = \frac{P_{\text{out}}}{P_{\text{in}}} = A_u \cdot A_i . \]
💡Intuition: the control valve

Think of a huge water main with a valve. Your hand on the valve wheel expends a few watts; the water flow it controls carries kilowatts. Nobody claims the valve "creates" power — it modulates a pre-existing flow. A transistor is precisely such a valve for electrons: a small gate/base signal modulates a large supply current. Amplification is controlled energy conversion, never energy creation. This is also why every amplifier schematic has supply rails: no supply, no gain.

A real amplifier is characterized by more than gain. As seen from outside it behaves like a two-port (Module 05) with three headline parameters:

  • Input resistance \(r_{\text{in}}\) (Eingangswiderstand) — the load the amplifier presents to whatever drives it. Should usually be high, so the source is not loaded down (recall the loaded voltage divider, Module 04).
  • Output resistance \(r_{\text{out}}\) (Ausgangswiderstand) — the internal resistance of the amplifier acting as a source. Should usually be low, so gain does not collapse when a load is attached.
  • Bandwidth (Bandbreite) — the frequency range over which the gain holds up. No amplifier is fast forever (Section 6).

The ideal voltage amplifier is therefore: \(r_{\text{in}} \to \infty\), \(r_{\text{out}} \to 0\), gain constant at all frequencies. Keep this wish list in mind — the op-amp will come astonishingly close.

2. The decibel — the logarithmic language of gain

Amplifier stages are cascaded, and cascaded gains multiply: a ×20 preamp into a ×50 power stage gives ×1000. Multiplying awkward numbers all day is miserable, so engineering adopted a logarithmic measure in which cascading becomes addition: the decibel (Dezibel, dB). The definition is fundamentally a power ratio:

\[ A_p\big|_{\mathrm{dB}} = 10 \, \log_{10}\frac{P_2}{P_1}. \]

For voltages the story has a factor-of-two twist. Since \(P = U^2/R\), a voltage ratio enters the power ratio squared (assuming equal resistance levels), and \(\log(x^2) = 2\log x\):

\[ \boxed{\; A_u\big|_{\mathrm{dB}} = 20 \, \log_{10}\left|\frac{U_2}{U_1}\right| \;} \qquad A_p\big|_{\mathrm{dB}} = 10 \, \log_{10}\frac{P_2}{P_1}. \]

The two definitions are deliberately consistent: doubling the voltage into a fixed resistor quadruples the power, and indeed \(20\log 2 = 10 \log 4 \approx 6\;\mathrm{dB}\). One physical situation, one dB number — that is the whole point of the 20-vs-10 rule.

⚠️20 for voltage, 10 for power — never mix them

"The filter attenuates by 3 dB" means the power is halved and the voltage is down to \(1/\sqrt{2} \approx 70.7\,\%\). Both statements are the same 3 dB. If you compute \(10\log\) of a voltage ratio you are off by exactly a factor 2 in dB — a classic exam and lab-report error. Also note: dB is a ratio, dimensionless. Absolute levels need a reference suffix: dBm (power re 1 mW — ubiquitous in RF and telecom), dBV (voltage re 1 V), dB(A) (acoustics, weighted).

Fluency in dB means knowing a handful of anchor values cold — everything else is composition, e.g. \(\times 40 = \times 10 \times 4 \;\hat{=}\; 20\,\mathrm{dB} + 12\,\mathrm{dB} = 32\,\mathrm{dB}\):

Worked example dB fluency: a signal chain

A microphone delivers 2 mV. The chain: preamp +26 dB → tone filter −3 dB (at the frequency of interest) → power stage +34 dB. What voltage reaches the loudspeaker terminal, and what is the total gain as a plain factor?

Step 1 — add the dB values: \( 26 - 3 + 34 = 57\,\mathrm{dB} \). Cascaded stages just add — this is why dB exists.
Step 2 — back to a linear factor: \( A_u = 10^{57/20} = 10^{2.85} \approx 708 \). Sanity check by anchors: \(57 = 20 + 20 + 20 - 6 + 3 \Rightarrow 10\cdot10\cdot10\cdot\tfrac12\cdot\sqrt2 \approx 707\) ✓
Step 3 — output voltage: \( u_{\text{out}} = 708 \cdot 2\,\mathrm{mV} \approx 1.42\,\mathrm{V} \).
Total gain 57 dB ≙ ×708; output ≈ 1.4 V. Notice how the anchor-value decomposition let us verify \(10^{2.85}\) without a calculator.

3. Biasing and the operating point

3.1 Why an amplifier needs DC bias

Here is the problem Module 08 left us with: a transistor is a one-quadrant, nonlinear device. An NPN transistor or n-MOSFET conducts only when its input voltage exceeds a threshold (\(U_{BE} \approx 0.7\,\mathrm{V}\), or \(U_{GS} > U_{th}\)), and even then the current grows exponentially or quadratically — nothing like the proportional behavior an amplifier must deliver. Feed a small audio sine (±50 mV around zero) directly into a bare transistor and the output is silence: the device never turns on.

The fix: superimpose the small signal on a constant DC level that holds the transistor in the middle of its useful region. This DC condition — a chosen quiescent current \(I_D\) (or \(I_C\)) and quiescent voltages with no signal applied — is the operating point (Arbeitspunkt), often marked Q (quiescent). Designing the resistor network that establishes and stabilizes it is called biasing (Arbeitspunkteinstellung, the bias voltage itself: Vorspannung).

3.2 The small-signal idea — linearization at the operating point

Now the beautiful trick. Around the operating point, expand the transistor's nonlinear characteristic \(i_D(u_{GS})\) as a Taylor series (Module 02):

\[ i_D(U_{GS,Q} + u_{gs}) = I_{D,Q} + \underbrace{\left.\frac{\mathrm{d}i_D}{\mathrm{d}u_{GS}}\right|_{Q}}_{\textstyle g_m} u_{gs} \;+\; \frac{1}{2}\left.\frac{\mathrm{d}^2 i_D}{\mathrm{d}u_{GS}^2}\right|_Q u_{gs}^2 + \dots \]

If the signal excursion \(u_{gs}\) is small, the quadratic and higher terms are negligible and the device behaves — for the signal — like a perfectly linear element with one parameter, the transconductance (Steilheit):

\[ \boxed{\; i_d = g_m \, u_{gs} \;} \qquad g_m = \left.\frac{\mathrm{d}i_D}{\mathrm{d}u_{GS}}\right|_{Q} \]

For a MOSFET in saturation (\(i_D = \tfrac{k}{2}(u_{GS}-U_{th})^2\)): \(g_m = k\,(U_{GS,Q}-U_{th}) = \dfrac{2 I_{D,Q}}{U_{GS,Q}-U_{th}}\). For a BJT the exponential characteristic gives the remarkably clean \(g_m = I_{C,Q}/U_T\) with the thermal voltage \(U_T \approx 25\,\mathrm{mV}\) — at 1 mA collector current, every BJT on Earth has \(g_m = 40\,\mathrm{mS}\).

Notation convention (worth internalizing now, it is used everywhere): \(u_{GS} = U_{GS,Q} + u_{gs}\) — capital letter + capital subscript = total DC + signal; capital + Q = operating point; lowercase + lowercase subscript = small signal only.

🔑Key idea: two circuits in one

Every transistor amplifier is analyzed twice. First the DC analysis (Großsignal): capacitors open, signal source off — find the operating point. Then the small-signal analysis (Kleinsignal): DC sources become their internal resistances (ideal voltage supplies → AC ground!), capacitors become shorts (at signal frequencies), and the transistor becomes its linearized model \(i_d = g_m u_{gs}\). The nonlinear problem splits into two linear ones. All the linear machinery of Modules 04–05 — superposition, Thévenin, nodal analysis — now applies to a transistor circuit.

3.3 Coupling capacitors

One practical question remains: how do we add the AC signal to the DC bias without the signal source disturbing the bias (or the bias pushing DC into the source)? With a coupling capacitor (Koppelkondensator). A capacitor blocks DC completely (\(Z_C \to \infty\) at \(f = 0\)) but is nearly a short at signal frequencies (\(|Z_C| = 1/\omega C\) small). It is a frequency-selective wall: bias stays inside the stage, signal passes through. The price: together with the input resistance it forms a high-pass with cutoff \(f_c = 1/(2\pi r_{\text{in}} C)\), so \(C\) must be sized large enough that \(f_c\) lies below the lowest signal frequency — this is why audio amplifiers historically bristled with 10 µF electrolytics.

4. One stage in depth: the common-source amplifier

The workhorse single-transistor voltage amplifier is the common-source stage (Sourceschaltung) — for BJTs, the identical-in-spirit common-emitter stage (Emitterschaltung). Signal enters the gate, leaves the drain, and the source terminal is common to input and output loops:

4.1 The gain, derived in three lines

Small-signal reasoning, walking the signal through the stage:

  1. The input voltage appears (through C₁) directly between gate and source: \(u_{gs} = u_{\text{in}}\).
  2. The transistor converts it to a drain-current wiggle: \(i_d = g_m u_{gs}\).
  3. That current flows through \(R_D\) from the supply. The supply rail is AC ground (its voltage does not wiggle), so the drain voltage moves by \(u_{\text{out}} = -\,i_d R_D\): when the current increases, the drop across \(R_D\) increases and the drain is pulled down.
\[ \boxed{\; A_u = \frac{u_{\text{out}}}{u_{\text{in}}} = -\,g_m R_D \;} \]

The minus sign means the stage inverts: a rising input gives a falling output — a 180° phase shift, not a defect. Typical numbers: \(I_{D,Q} = 1\,\mathrm{mA}\) at an overdrive of 0.2 V gives \(g_m = 2 \cdot 1\,\mathrm{mA}/0.2\,\mathrm{V} = 10\,\mathrm{mS}\); with \(R_D = 2\,\mathrm{k\Omega}\), \(A_u = -20\) (26 dB). One transistor, one resistor: a ×20 amplifier.

4.2 Input and output resistance

Looking into the gate, a MOSFET draws essentially no current — the stage's input resistance is set by the bias divider, \(r_{\text{in}} = R_1 \parallel R_2\) (hundreds of kΩ if you choose generously). Looking back into the output, the signal source you "see" is the drain current source behind \(R_D\); killing the sources leaves \(r_{\text{out}} \approx R_D\) — a few kΩ, uncomfortably high. Load the stage with anything comparable to \(R_D\) and the gain sags exactly like a loaded voltage divider. Single transistor stages are good at voltage gain but poor at driving; fixing that is one of the op-amp's jobs.

4.3 Source degeneration — feedback before we name it

The gain \(-g_m R_D\) has a flaw: \(g_m\) depends on temperature and varies transistor-to-transistor by tens of percent. Insert a resistor \(R_S\) between source and ground and something remarkable happens. If \(i_d\) tries to grow, the drop \(i_d R_S\) raises the source potential, which reduces \(u_{gs}\), which pulls \(i_d\) back down — the stage resists its own variations. Quantitatively (KVL around the input loop: \(u_{\text{in}} = u_{gs} + i_d R_S\), with \(i_d = g_m u_{gs}\)):

\[ A_u = -\,\frac{g_m R_D}{1 + g_m R_S} \;\xrightarrow{\;g_m R_S \gg 1\;}\; -\,\frac{R_D}{R_S}. \]

For large loop strength the transistor's wobbly \(g_m\) cancels out: the gain is set by a ratio of resistors, which can be matched to 0.1 %. We traded raw gain for precision and stability. Remember this trade — it is negative feedback in embryo, and Section 6 will make it the central principle of all analog design.

5. The operational amplifier

Chaining transistor stages to get high gain, high \(r_{\text{in}}\) and low \(r_{\text{out}}\) is routine but tedious — so the industry did it once, extremely well, and put it in a five-pin package. The operational amplifier (Operationsverstärker, OpAmp, German abbreviation "OPV") is a differential-input voltage amplifier with enormous gain:

\[ u_{\text{out}} = A_0 \,(u_+ - u_-), \qquad A_0 \approx 10^5 \dots 10^7 \ \text{(open-loop gain, } 100{-}140\ \mathrm{dB}). \]

The name comes from analog computers of the 1940s–60s, where these amplifiers performed mathematical operations — summing, integrating — on voltages. Used bare, an op-amp is nearly useless as an amplifier: with \(A_0 = 10^5\) and ±12 V rails, any input difference beyond ±120 µV slams the output into a supply rail. The magic appears only when we close a feedback loop around it. For analysis with negative feedback, two golden rules describe the ideal op-amp:

🔑Key idea: the two golden rules

Rule 1 — no input current: the inputs draw \(i_+ = i_- = 0\) (infinite input resistance).
Rule 2 — virtual short: with negative feedback in place, the output does whatever is necessary to drive the input difference to zero: \(u_+ = u_-\) (virtueller Kurzschluss).
Rule 2 is not a property of the chip but of the feedback loop: if \(u_-\) dipped below \(u_+\), the huge gain would raise \(u_{\text{out}}\), and the feedback network would push \(u_-\) back up. Equilibrium exists only at \(u_+ \approx u_-\) (the residual difference is \(u_{\text{out}}/A_0\) — microvolts). Every op-amp circuit in this section is solved with just these two rules plus KCL.

5.1 The inverting amplifier — full derivation

Derivation, using nothing but the golden rules and KCL:

  1. The + input is grounded, so Rule 2 forces \(u_- = u_+ = 0\). The inverting input is a virtual ground (virtuelle Masse): 0 V, yet no current flows into ground there.
  2. Current through \(R_1\): \(\;i_1 = \dfrac{u_{\text{in}} - 0}{R_1}\).
  3. Rule 1: none of it enters the op-amp, so all of it must continue through \(R_2\): \(i_2 = i_1\).
  4. Output voltage: \(\;u_{\text{out}} = 0 - i_2 R_2 = -\dfrac{R_2}{R_1}\,u_{\text{in}}\).
\[ \boxed{\; A_u = -\frac{R_2}{R_1} \;} \]

The gain is set purely by a resistor ratio — the op-amp's own \(A_0\), with all its temperature drift and unit-to-unit spread, has vanished from the formula (exactly the source-degeneration effect of Section 4.3, taken to its logical conclusion). Input resistance: the source looking in sees the virtual ground behind \(R_1\), so \(r_{\text{in}} = R_1\) — finite, and a genuine drawback of this topology. Output resistance: milliohms, thanks to feedback (Section 6).

🧪Interactive — inverting amplifier with real rails simulation

Set the gain with \(R_1\), \(R_2\) and watch the output sine (inverted!). Then raise the input amplitude or the gain until the ideal output would exceed the supply rails: the op-amp clips — the tops of the sine are sliced flat at the rails (saturation, heard as harsh distortion in audio). No feedback trickery can push the output beyond its power supply.

🚫Classic pitfall: golden rules without negative feedback

Students happily write \(u_+ = u_-\) for any op-amp circuit. Rule 2 holds only when negative feedback is active: a path from output back to the inverting input, and an output that is not stuck at a rail. In a comparator (no feedback, Section 7) or a Schmitt trigger (positive feedback) the inputs are not equal — applying the virtual short there produces confident nonsense. Always check where the feedback goes before invoking the rules.

5.2 Non-inverting amplifier and the voltage follower

Feed the signal into the + input instead, with the feedback divider \(R_2\)/\(R_1\) from output to − input. Rule 2: \(u_- = u_+ = u_{\text{in}}\). Rule 1 means the divider is unloaded, so \(u_- = u_{\text{out}} \dfrac{R_1}{R_1 + R_2}\). Equate:

\[ \boxed{\; A_u = 1 + \frac{R_2}{R_1} \;} \qquad \text{(non-inverting, } A_u \ge 1) \]

No inversion, and — because the signal enters the op-amp's input directly — nearly infinite input resistance. Note the gain can never be below 1.

The special case \(R_2 = 0\) (direct feedback wire, \(R_1 \to \infty\)) is the voltage follower or buffer (Spannungsfolger / Impedanzwandler): \(A_u = 1\). Gain one sounds pointless until you recall Section 1: this circuit converts a feeble source (say a pH electrode with \(r_{\text{out}} = 100\,\mathrm{M\Omega}\), able to supply nanoamps) into a stiff source with \(r_{\text{out}}\) of milliohms that can drive a cable and an ADC. It amplifies not voltage but current capability — impedance conversion is half of real-world analog design.

Worked example Design: sensor 0–50 mV → ADC 0–5 V

A pressure sensor outputs 0–50 mV with \(r_{\text{out}} = 20\,\mathrm{k\Omega}\); the microcontroller's ADC wants 0–5 V. Design the amplifier and check it with a 3 MHz-GBW op-amp for signals up to 10 kHz.

Step 1 — required gain: \( A_u = 5\,\mathrm{V} / 50\,\mathrm{mV} = 100 \) (40 dB). Positive gain, no inversion wanted.
Step 2 — choose topology: with a 20 kΩ source, an inverting amp is out: its \(r_{\text{in}} = R_1\) would form a divider with the sensor and wreck the accuracy (to keep the loading error below 1 % we would need \(R_1 > 2\,\mathrm{M\Omega}\), forcing \(R_2 = 200\,\mathrm{M\Omega}\) — absurd). Take the non-inverting amp: \(r_{\text{in}} \approx \infty\), the sensor is unloaded.
Step 3 — resistor values: \( 1 + R_2/R_1 = 100 \Rightarrow R_2 = 99\,R_1 \). Choose \(R_1 = 1\,\mathrm{k\Omega}\), \(R_2 = 99\,\mathrm{k\Omega}\) (or E96 values 1.00 k / 99.0 k... practically 1 k + 100 k gives gain 101, then trim in software). kΩ-range values keep resistor noise and op-amp bias-current errors small without loading the output.
Step 4 — bandwidth check (anticipating Section 6): closed-loop bandwidth \( \approx \mathrm{GBW}/A_u = 3\,\mathrm{MHz}/100 = 30\,\mathrm{kHz} > 10\,\mathrm{kHz} \) ✓ (with 1 dB to spare at 10 kHz: \(f/f_c = 0.33\), droop \(= 10\log(1+0.11) \approx 0.5\,\mathrm{dB}\)).
Non-inverting, \(R_1 = 1\,\mathrm{k\Omega}\), \(R_2 = 99\,\mathrm{k\Omega}\), gain 100 — and the topology choice mattered as much as the arithmetic.

5.3 Summing and difference amplifiers

Because the inverting input is a virtual ground, currents from several sources arriving there simply add (KCL) without the sources interacting — each one sees ground. The summing amplifier (Summierverstärker):

\[ u_{\text{out}} = -R_f \left( \frac{u_1}{R_1} + \frac{u_2}{R_2} + \frac{u_3}{R_3} \right) \]

— a weighted sum with resistor-programmable weights. This is literally an audio mixing desk channel bus, and with weights 1, 2, 4, 8 it is a digital-to-analog converter (Module 14).

The difference amplifier (Differenzverstärker) applies \(u_1\) through \(R_1\) to the − input and \(u_2\) through an identical \(R_1\)–\(R_2\) divider to the + input. With both resistor pairs matched:

\[ u_{\text{out}} = \frac{R_2}{R_1}\,(u_2 - u_1). \]

It amplifies the difference and rejects what is common to both inputs — vital for sensors on long cables where both wires pick up the same 50 Hz hum. The rejection quality (CMRR, common-mode rejection ratio, Gleichtaktunterdrückung) depends brutally on resistor matching: 0.1 % resistors limit CMRR to ≈ 60 dB. For serious instrumentation one uses the three-op-amp instrumentation amplifier (Instrumentenverstärker): two followers buffer the inputs (infinite \(r_{\text{in}}\)), one difference stage subtracts, and the whole gain is set by a single resistor — bought as one laser-trimmed IC (INA128 and friends) with CMRR > 100 dB.

5.4 Integrator and differentiator

Replace \(R_2\) with a capacitor \(C\) and the feedback current charges it: \(i_1 = u_{\text{in}}/R\) flows onto \(C\), so \(u_{\text{out}} = -u_C\) accumulates:

\[ \boxed{\; u_{\text{out}}(t) = -\frac{1}{RC}\int_0^t u_{\text{in}}(\tau)\,\mathrm{d}\tau + u_{\text{out}}(0) \;} \]

The integrator (Integrierer). In the frequency domain \( \underline{H}(j\omega) = -\dfrac{1}{j\omega RC} \): magnitude falls at −20 dB/decade over all frequencies with unity gain at \(\omega = 1/RC\), phase −90° — an idealized low-pass taken to its limit. It is the core of function generators, analog computers, PID controllers (the "I") and delta-sigma ADCs. Practical caveat: at DC the gain is infinite, so the tiniest input offset integrates relentlessly until the output hits a rail; real integrators add a large resistor across \(C\) to leak the drift away.

Swapping R and C gives the differentiator (Differenzierer): \(u_{\text{out}} = -RC\,\dfrac{\mathrm{d}u_{\text{in}}}{\mathrm{d}t}\), gain rising at +20 dB/decade — a high-pass without end. That rising response amplifies high-frequency noise and invites oscillation, so pure differentiators are rare in practice; a series resistor is added to tame the response above the band of interest.

Worked example Integrator: square wave in, triangle out

A ±2 V square wave at 5 kHz drives an integrator with \(R = 10\,\mathrm{k\Omega}\), \(C = 10\,\mathrm{nF}\). Find the output waveform and its amplitude.

Step 1 — what shape? During each half-period the input is constant (±2 V), so the output ramps linearly with slope \( \mp u_{\text{in}}/(RC) \). Alternating ramps = a triangle wave (inverted relative to the square).
Step 2 — slope: \( RC = 10^4 \cdot 10^{-8} = 10^{-4}\,\mathrm{s} \), so \( |\mathrm{d}u/\mathrm{d}t| = 2\,\mathrm{V}/10^{-4}\,\mathrm{s} = 20\,\mathrm{V/ms} \).
Step 3 — amplitude: the ramp runs for a half-period \(T/2 = 100\,\mathrm{µs}\): \( \Delta u = 20\,\mathrm{V/ms} \cdot 0.1\,\mathrm{ms} = 2\,\mathrm{V} \) peak-to-peak, i.e. a ±1 V triangle centered on 0.
±1 V triangle at 5 kHz. Sanity check via the frequency domain: the square's fundamental (\(\hat u_1 = 4\cdot2/\pi \approx 2.55\,\mathrm{V}\)) sees gain \(1/(\omega RC) = 1/(2\pi \cdot 5000 \cdot 10^{-4}) \approx 0.32\) → \(\approx 0.81\,\mathrm{V}\) fundamental — consistent with a ±1 V triangle (whose fundamental is \(8/\pi^2 \approx 0.81\) of its peak ✓).

6. Negative feedback — the central principle

Step back and see what Sections 4.3 and 5 kept doing: sacrifice raw gain, get precision. Harold Black formalized this in 1927. Let a forward amplifier of gain \(A\) drive the output, and let a network \(\beta\) (typically a mere voltage divider) feed a fraction of the output back and subtract it from the input:

Solve the loop: \(u_{\text{out}} = A\,u_e = A\,(u_{\text{in}} - \beta u_{\text{out}})\), hence

\[ \boxed{\; A_{\text{cl}} = \frac{u_{\text{out}}}{u_{\text{in}}} = \frac{A}{1 + A\beta} \;\xrightarrow{\;A\beta \gg 1\;}\; \frac{1}{\beta} \;} \]

The product \(A\beta\) is the loop gain (Schleifenverstärkung) — the currency in which all feedback benefits are paid. If it is large, the closed-loop gain depends only on \(\beta\): only on resistors. Check with the non-inverting amp: \(\beta = R_1/(R_1+R_2)\Rightarrow A_{\text{cl}} = 1 + R_2/R_1\) ✓.

6.1 Gain desensitization, quantified

How much does \(A_{\text{cl}}\) care about \(A\)? Differentiate \(A_{\text{cl}} = A/(1+A\beta)\):

\[ \frac{\mathrm{d}A_{\text{cl}}}{A_{\text{cl}}} = \frac{1}{1 + A\beta}\,\frac{\mathrm{d}A}{A}. \]

Relative variations of the raw amplifier are divided by the loop gain. Worked numbers: an op-amp with \(A = 10^5\), specified only within ±50 % (open-loop gain really is that sloppy — it varies with temperature, supply and specimen), configured for \(A_{\text{cl}} = 10\) (\(\beta = 0.1\), loop gain \(A\beta = 10^4\)):

\[ \frac{\mathrm{d}A_{\text{cl}}}{A_{\text{cl}}} = \frac{\pm 50\,\%}{1 + 10^4} \approx \pm 0.005\,\% . \]

Verify with brute force: \(A = 10^5 \Rightarrow A_{\text{cl}} = \frac{10^5}{1+10^4} = 9.9990\); let \(A\) collapse to half, \(A = 5\cdot10^4 \Rightarrow A_{\text{cl}} = \frac{5\cdot10^4}{1+5\cdot10^3} = 9.9980\). The amplifier lost half its gain and the circuit changed by 0.01 %. That is why analog designers barely glance at the open-loop gain spec: any surplus is instantly converted into precision.

📜Black's ferry ride, 1927

Harold Black had spent years at Bell Labs fighting distortion in telephone repeater amplifiers — thousands of vacuum-tube stages in series across the continent, each adding its own nonlinearity. On the Lackawanna ferry to Manhattan on 6 August 1927 the idea struck him; he sketched the feedback equation on his newspaper. The patent office resisted for nine years ("an amplifier that throws away gain — absurd"), and colleagues warned the loop would always oscillate. The stability question was legitimate — answered by Nyquist (1932) and Bode (1940), giving us the stability theory you will meet in control engineering (Module 16). Today essentially every precision analog system — and every control loop from a chip's voltage regulator to a rocket's guidance — runs on Black's idea.

6.2 Bandwidth extension and the gain–bandwidth product

A real (internally compensated) op-amp has a single-pole open-loop response: huge DC gain \(A_0\) that starts falling at −20 dB/decade from a very low corner \(f_p\) (often just 10 Hz!). Above the corner \(|A(f)| \approx A_0 f_p / f\): gain × frequency is constant. This constant is the gain–bandwidth product (Verstärkungs-Bandbreite-Produkt):

\[ \mathrm{GBW} = A_0 f_p = |A(f)| \cdot f . \]

Close the loop and the same trade appears at your gain level: the closed-loop corner frequency is where the falling open-loop gain meets your demanded gain:

\[ \boxed{\; f_c \approx \frac{\mathrm{GBW}}{A_{\text{cl}}} \;} \]

Feedback buys bandwidth with surplus gain: the loop gain that desensitized \(A_{\text{cl}}\) also holds the response flat until there is no surplus left. Worked example: a µA741-class op-amp has GBW = 1 MHz. At gain 1000 (60 dB) it is flat only to 1 kHz — useless even for audio. At gain 10: 100 kHz. As a follower (gain 1): the full 1 MHz. Need 40 dB of gain across 100 kHz? Then you need GBW ≥ 10 MHz, or you split the job into two ×10 stages (each needs only 1 MHz — cascading relaxes the requirement multiplicatively, another reason multi-stage design survives).

6.3 Distortion reduction

Nonlinearity is a signal-dependent gain error, and feedback divides all gain errors by \(1 + A\beta\). An output stage with an ugly 10 % kink in its transfer curve, wrapped in a loop with a loop gain of 1000, shows a residual distortion of roughly 0.01 %. This — not exotic transistors — is why a 30 € audio amplifier IC achieves 0.003 % THD. The same division also applies to the output resistance (\(r_{\text{out}}\) drops by \(1+A\beta\), to milliohms) and to hum injected inside the loop. Feedback is a universal error-squasher; the only sin it cannot fix is clipping (once the output hits the rail, there is no gain left to close the loop — see the widget in Section 5).

7. Comparators and positive feedback

7.1 The comparator — an op-amp with no loop

Remove all feedback and the op-amp's huge gain becomes a feature: the output can only sit at one rail or the other, depending on the sign of \(u_+ - u_-\). Used this way the device is a comparator (Komparator) — a 1-bit answer to "is the signal above the threshold?", the bridge from analog to digital (Module 13 onward). Dedicated comparator ICs (LM311, LM393) are built for this job: they switch in nanoseconds and have logic-compatible outputs, but are not internally compensated and must never be used as amplifiers — just as a compensated op-amp makes a sluggish comparator (its compensation capacitor limits slewing, Section 10).

7.2 The Schmitt trigger — hysteresis from positive feedback

A bare comparator has a fatal flaw with real, noisy signals: near the threshold, every noise wiggle re-crosses it, and the output chatters — dozens of spurious edges per genuine transition. Imagine a thermostat set to 20.0 °C switching the heating on and off ten times a minute as the sensor reading flickers by ±0.1 K. The cure is deliberately applied positive feedback (Mitkopplung): feed a fraction of the output back to the non-inverting input, so the act of switching moves the threshold away from the signal. This is the Schmitt trigger (Otto Schmitt, 1937 — invented, delightfully, to model squid neurons).

Take the inverting configuration: signal on the − input, and a divider \(R_1\) (to ground), \(R_2\) (to output) setting \(u_+ = \beta\,u_{\text{out}}\) with \(\beta = \frac{R_1}{R_1+R_2}\). Derive the thresholds:

  1. Suppose the output is high, \(u_{\text{out}} = +U_{\text{sat}}\). Then the effective threshold is \(u_+ = +\beta U_{\text{sat}}\). The output flips only when the input rises past \(U_{T+} = +\beta U_{\text{sat}}\).
  2. The instant it flips, \(u_{\text{out}} = -U_{\text{sat}}\) and the threshold jumps down to \(U_{T-} = -\beta U_{\text{sat}}\). To flip back, the input must fall all the way below \(U_{T-}\).
\[ \boxed{\; U_{T\pm} = \pm\,\beta\, U_{\text{sat}}, \qquad \Delta U_H = U_{T+} - U_{T-} = 2\,\frac{R_1}{R_1+R_2}\,U_{\text{sat}} \;} \]

The gap \(\Delta U_H\) is the hysteresis (Hysterese): the circuit remembers its last decision and demands real evidence, not noise, to change its mind. Any noise smaller than \(\Delta U_H\) cannot cause chatter. The thermostat becomes: heat ON below 19.5 °C, OFF above 20.5 °C — a 1 K deadband, and the furnace cycles calmly.

🧪Interactive — Schmitt trigger vs. noisy signal simulation

A slow sine buried in noise drives an inverting comparator. With hysteresis at zero you get a plain comparator: count the transitions — it chatters at every crossing. Now widen the hysteresis until the transition count drops to the ideal 8 (two per period). Then increase the noise and see how much hysteresis you need to stay clean: the rule is \(\Delta U_H \gtrsim 2 \times\) peak noise.

8. Active filters

8.1 First order: an RC filter with benefits

Passive RC filters (Module 05) suffer when loaded — the load resistance becomes part of the filter and shifts the cutoff. Put the RC network into an op-amp circuit and the problem vanishes. The active low-pass: an inverting amplifier with \(C\) in parallel with \(R_2\):

\[ \underline{H}(j\omega) = -\frac{R_2}{R_1}\cdot\frac{1}{1 + j\omega R_2 C}, \qquad f_c = \frac{1}{2\pi R_2 C}. \]

At low frequencies the capacitor is open and the gain is \(-R_2/R_1\) — the filter can amplify while filtering. Above \(f_c\) the capacitor progressively shorts out \(R_2\): −20 dB/decade. Swap the capacitor into the input branch (in series with \(R_1\)) and you get the matching active high-pass. The op-amp's near-zero output resistance means the next stage never detunes this one: filters become LEGO bricks that cascade cleanly — the property that makes higher-order filters practical.

8.2 Second order: the Sallen–Key stage and Q

Steeper filtering needs second-order sections with complex pole pairs — something two cascaded RCs can never provide (two real poles give a droopy, gradual knee). Passively you would need an inductor; actively, a little positive feedback through a capacitor does the job. The classic Sallen–Key low-pass (1955): two Rs, two Cs, and a follower whose output bootstraps the first capacitor. Its transfer function is the standard second-order low-pass:

\[ \underline{H}(s) = \frac{1}{1 + \dfrac{s}{\omega_0 Q} + \dfrac{s^2}{\omega_0^2}}, \qquad \omega_0 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{R_1 R_2 C_1 C_2}}, \quad Q = \frac{\sqrt{R_1 R_2 C_1 C_2}}{(R_1 + R_2)\,C_2} \;\overset{R_1=R_2}{=}\; \frac12\sqrt{\frac{C_1}{C_2}} . \]

The quality factor \(Q\) (Güte, same Q as in resonant circuits, Module 05) shapes the knee: \(Q < 0.5\) — two real poles, sluggish; \(Q = 1/\sqrt{2} \approx 0.707\) — maximally flat passband; \(Q > 0.707\) — the response peaks by \(\approx 20\log_{10} Q\) dB near \(f_0\) before falling. High Q buys a sharper corner at the price of peaking (and ringing in the time domain — the two are the same physics).

🧪Interactive — Sallen–Key low-pass: the role of Q Bode plot

Sweep \(Q\) from overdamped to strongly peaking and watch the knee morph; the stopband slope is always −40 dB/decade. At \(Q = 0.707\) (Butterworth) the passband is maximally flat — nudge Q either way and you either droop early or grow a resonant bump. The phase always passes −90° at \(f_0\).

Filter families in one paragraph: a real specification ("flat to 1 kHz, −60 dB by 4 kHz") is met by cascading second-order sections with specific \((f_0, Q)\) recipes, and the recipes have names. Butterworth (Potenzfilter): maximally flat passband, honest all-rounder. Chebyshev: steepest cutoff for a given order, paid for with ripple in the passband and worse ringing. Bessel (Thomson-Filter): sacrifices steepness for a maximally flat group delay — all frequencies delayed equally, so pulses and square waves pass undistorted; the choice wherever waveform shape matters (audio crossovers, data acquisition anti-aliasing before precise time-domain measurements). Same circuit topology, different component values — the design tables live in every filter handbook and in tools like TI's FilterPro.

9. Oscillators — amplifiers that feed themselves

Positive feedback, dosed carefully, does more than make Schmitt triggers: it makes signal sources. Take an amplifier \(A\) and return its output through a frequency-selective network \(\beta(j\omega)\) to its input without any external signal. A self-sustaining oscillation at frequency \(f_0\) exists exactly when a signal completes the loop and arrives back as its identical self — the Barkhausen criterion (Schwingbedingung, Heinrich Barkhausen, 1921):

\[ \boxed{\; |\underline{A}\,\underline{\beta}(j\omega_0)| = 1 \quad\text{and}\quad \arg\big(\underline{A}\,\underline{\beta}(j\omega_0)\big) = 0° \;(n\cdot360°) \;} \]

Phase condition selects the frequency; magnitude condition sustains the amplitude. In practice the loop gain is set slightly above 1 so oscillation reliably starts from noise, and some gentle nonlinearity trims it back to exactly 1 as the amplitude grows.

Worked example Wien bridge oscillator

The Wien network — series RC feeding parallel RC (equal R, equal C) — is placed from the op-amp output back to the + input. Where does it oscillate, and what gain must the amplifier provide? Take \(R = 1.59\,\mathrm{k\Omega}\), \(C = 100\,\mathrm{nF}\).

Step 1 — feedback ratio of the Wien network: with \(\underline{Z}_s = R + \tfrac{1}{j\omega C}\) (series) and \(\underline{Z}_p = R \parallel \tfrac{1}{j\omega C} = \tfrac{R}{1+j\omega RC}\): \[ \underline{\beta} = \frac{\underline{Z}_p}{\underline{Z}_s + \underline{Z}_p} = \frac{1}{3 + j\left(\omega RC - \frac{1}{\omega RC}\right)} . \]
Step 2 — phase condition: \(\arg\underline{\beta} = 0\) requires the imaginary term to vanish: \(\omega_0 RC = 1/(\omega_0 RC)\), i.e. \( f_0 = \dfrac{1}{2\pi RC} = \dfrac{1}{2\pi \cdot 1590 \cdot 10^{-7}} \approx 1.0\,\mathrm{kHz} \).
Step 3 — magnitude condition: at \(f_0\), \(\underline{\beta} = 1/3\). The amplifier must therefore supply exactly \(A = 3\): a non-inverting amp with \(R_2/R_1 = 2\).
\(f_0 \approx 1\,\mathrm{kHz}\), amplifier gain 3.00. The catch: gain 2.99 → the oscillation dies; 3.01 → it grows until clipping. Real Wien oscillators use an amplitude-dependent element in \(R_1\) — classically a small incandescent lamp whose resistance rises as it warms — to regulate the gain to exactly 3 with a clean, low-distortion sine.
📜The oscillator that started Silicon Valley

The lamp-stabilized Wien bridge oscillator was Bill Hewlett's master's thesis at Stanford (1939). He and Dave Packard built it in a Palo Alto garage as the HP200A audio oscillator; Walt Disney bought eight of the follow-up model to mix the soundtrack of Fantasia. The garage is now a California Historic Landmark — "Birthplace of Silicon Valley". Elegant analog design, literally foundational.

Not every oscillator needs a sine. Relaxation oscillators (Kippschwingungen) charge a capacitor toward one Schmitt-trigger threshold, flip, discharge toward the other, flip back — producing square (and, at the capacitor, quasi-triangular) waves with \(f \approx \frac{1}{2RC\,\ln\frac{1+\beta}{1-\beta}}\) (derivation: Exercise E7 — it is Module 06's RC step response applied twice per period). The legendary 555 timer (1972, still produced by the hundreds of millions per year) is exactly this: two comparators at \(\tfrac13 U_{CC}\) and \(\tfrac23 U_{CC}\), a flip-flop, and a discharge transistor. For serious frequency stability, none of the above suffices: quartz crystal oscillators (Module 21) hold \(10^{-6}\) where RC oscillators drift at \(10^{-2}\).

10. Real op-amps — reading the datasheet

The golden rules describe a fictional device. Real op-amps deviate in ways that dominate practical design, so let us break each idealization honestly:

  • Input offset voltage \(U_{os}\) (Offsetspannung): the two inputs are never perfectly matched; the op-amp behaves as if a small DC source (0.1–5 mV, precision types < 25 µV) sat in series with one input. It is amplified by the full noise gain \(1 + R_2/R_1\): a 2 mV offset in a gain-100 stage produces a 0.2 V output error — catastrophic for the 0–5 V sensor design of Section 5. Drifts with temperature (µV/K); zero-drift (chopper) op-amps fix this by design.
  • Input bias current \(I_B\) (Eingangsruhestrom): the inputs do draw current — µA for old bipolar parts (µA741), pA for FET/CMOS inputs. Flowing through your feedback resistors it creates additional offset (\(I_B \cdot R\)); with a 1 µF integrator capacitor, 80 nA of bias current ramps the output by 80 mV per second all by itself. High-impedance sources (pH probes, photodiodes) demand FET inputs.
  • Finite GBW: Section 6.2 — check \(f_c = \mathrm{GBW}/A_{\text{cl}}\) against your signal band, with margin, because gain error grows well below \(f_c\).
  • Slew rate SR (Anstiegsgeschwindigkeit): the maximum rate of change of the output voltage, set by the internal compensation capacitor being charged with a finite current: \(\mathrm{SR} = (\mathrm{d}u/\mathrm{d}t)_{max}\), typically 0.5 V/µs (µA741) to >1000 V/µs. This is a large-signal, nonlinear limit, distinct from GBW: a sine \(u = \hat{U}\sin\omega t\) needs a peak slope \(\omega\hat{U}\), so undistorted output requires \[ \boxed{\; f \le f_{max} = \frac{\mathrm{SR}}{2\pi \hat{U}} \;} \] Beyond it, sines leave as triangles. Small signals may still be fine at that frequency — GBW limits the small, SR the large.
  • Output swing / rail-to-rail: classic op-amps leave 1.5–2 V unreachable near each rail; rail-to-rail output stages get within millivolts — essential at today's 3.3 V single supplies, where losing 2×1.5 V would leave almost nothing. "Rail-to-rail input" is a separate spec (common-mode range); read both.
Worked example Slew-rate limit of a 10 V, 100 kHz sine

An amplifier must deliver \(\hat{U} = 10\,\mathrm{V}\) at \(f = 100\,\mathrm{kHz}\). (a) What slew rate is required? (b) Up to what frequency could a µA741 (SR = 0.5 V/µs) deliver this amplitude? (c) Could the 741 still amplify a 100 mV signal at 100 kHz?

Step (a) — required SR: maximum slope of a sine is at the zero crossing: \( \left(\frac{\mathrm{d}u}{\mathrm{d}t}\right)_{max} = \omega\hat{U} = 2\pi \cdot 10^5 \cdot 10 \approx 6.28\cdot10^6\,\mathrm{V/s} = 6.3\,\mathrm{V/µs} \). We need a ≥ 10 V/µs part for margin (e.g. TL072: 13 V/µs ✓).
Step (b) — 741's full-power bandwidth: \( f_{max} = \frac{\mathrm{SR}}{2\pi\hat{U}} = \frac{0.5\cdot10^6\,\mathrm{V/s}}{2\pi \cdot 10\,\mathrm{V}} \approx 8.0\,\mathrm{kHz} \). Thirteen times too slow — a 10 V, 100 kHz "sine" from a 741 is a 0.5 V/µs triangle.
Step (c) — small signal: at \(\hat U = 0.1\,\mathrm{V}\): required SR \(= 2\pi\cdot10^5\cdot0.1 \approx 0.063\,\mathrm{V/µs}\) ✓ slew-safe. But now check GBW: 1 MHz / 100 kHz = 10 — only gain 10 is available with zero margin. The two limits are independent gates; a signal must pass both.
6.3 V/µs required; the 741 manages 10 V only to 8 kHz. Slew rate scales with amplitude × frequency — halve the swing, double the reachable frequency.
🔑Reading the datasheet — a 60-second protocol

Given any op-amp datasheet, extract in order: (1) supply range & rail-to-rail? — does it work at your voltage at all; (2) GBW vs. your gain × bandwidth; (3) slew rate vs. \(2\pi f \hat U\) of your largest, fastest output; (4) \(U_{os}\) × noise gain vs. your error budget; (5) \(I_B\) × your resistance level; (6) input voltage noise \(e_n\) (nV/√Hz) if you amplify µV signals; (7) output current vs. your load. Anything spec'd "typical" may legally be worse — design against the "max/min" column. And beware: the front-page headline numbers are chosen by marketing, the table on page 5 by engineers.

12. Exercises

Design on paper first — resistor values and all — then check against the solutions.

E1An inverting amplifier has \(R_1 = 2.2\,\mathrm{k\Omega}\), \(R_2 = 22\,\mathrm{k\Omega}\). Find the gain (linear and dB), the output for \(u_{in} = 0.3\sin(\omega t)\,\mathrm{V}\), and the input resistance. easy

Show solution
\( A_u = -22/2.2 = -10 \), i.e. \(20\log 10 = 20\,\mathrm{dB}\) (the sign does not enter the dB value). Output: \( u_{out} = -3.0\sin(\omega t)\,\mathrm{V} \) — ten times larger and inverted (180° phase). Input resistance: the source drives into the virtual ground through \(R_1\), so \( r_{in} = 2.2\,\mathrm{k\Omega} \) — modest; a high-impedance source would sag here, which is why Section 5.2's design chose the non-inverting topology.

E2Convert fluently: (a) a gain of ×355 to dB; (b) −34 dB to a voltage ratio; (c) an amplifier chain of +12 dB, +12 dB, −6 dB driving 8 Ω: if 0.5 V enters, what power reaches the load? easy

Show solution
(a) \( 20\log_{10} 355 = 20 \cdot 2.55 = 51\,\mathrm{dB} \). Anchor check: \(355 \approx 100 \cdot 2 \cdot \sqrt2 \cdot 1.25\), and \(40 + 6 + 3 + 2 = 51\,\mathrm{dB}\) ✓. (b) \( 10^{-34/20} = 10^{-1.7} \approx 0.02 \) (−34 = −40 + 6: \(0.01 \times 2 = 0.02\) ✓). (c) Total \(12+12-6 = 18\,\mathrm{dB} \Rightarrow \times 10^{0.9} \approx 7.94\). Output ≈ \(0.5 \cdot 7.94 = 3.97\,\mathrm{V}\); power \(P = U^2/R = 3.97^2/8 \approx 2.0\,\mathrm{W}\) (using RMS-in ⇒ RMS-out).

E3A common-source stage runs at \(I_{D,Q} = 0.5\,\mathrm{mA}\) with overdrive \(U_{GS,Q}-U_{th} = 0.25\,\mathrm{V}\) and \(R_D = 6.8\,\mathrm{k\Omega}\). Find \(g_m\), the gain (linear + dB), and the gain if a source resistor \(R_S = 250\,\Omega\) is added. What did \(R_S\) buy? medium

Show solution
\( g_m = \frac{2 I_D}{U_{ov}} = \frac{2\cdot0.5\,\mathrm{mA}}{0.25\,\mathrm{V}} = 4\,\mathrm{mS} \). Undegenerated: \( A_u = -g_m R_D = -4\,\mathrm{mS}\cdot6.8\,\mathrm{k\Omega} = -27.2 \) → \(20\log 27.2 \approx 28.7\,\mathrm{dB}\). With \(R_S\): \( A_u = \frac{-g_m R_D}{1+g_m R_S} = \frac{-27.2}{1+4\cdot0.25} = \frac{-27.2}{2} = -13.6 \). Bought: half the gain traded for stability. Sensitivity check — if temperature shifts \(g_m\) by +20 % (to 4.8 mS), the raw gain moves to −32.6 (+20 %), but the degenerated gain only to \(-32.6/(1+4.8\cdot0.25) = -14.8\) (+9 %): variations are divided by \(1 + g_m R_S\), exactly the feedback formula of Section 6. (Amusingly, here \(g_m R_S = 1\) makes the asymptotic limit \(-R_D/R_S = -27.2\) numerically equal to the raw gain — pure coincidence of the chosen values; the limit is approached only for \(g_m R_S \gg 1\).)

E4Design an inverting Schmitt trigger with \(U_{sat} = \pm 12\,\mathrm{V}\) whose thresholds are \(\pm 0.6\,\mathrm{V}\). Choose sensible resistors. How much peak noise can the input tolerate without chattering? medium

Show solution
Required \(\beta = U_{T+}/U_{sat} = 0.6/12 = 0.05 = \frac{R_1}{R_1+R_2}\) → \( R_2 = 19\,R_1 \). Pick \(R_1 = 1\,\mathrm{k\Omega}\), \(R_2 = 19\,\mathrm{k\Omega}\) (E24: 1 k / 18 k gives ±0.63 V, fine). kΩ-range keeps the divider stiff against bias currents without loading the output. Hysteresis width \(\Delta U_H = 1.2\,\mathrm{V}\): noise up to ±0.6 V peak riding on the signal cannot re-cross both thresholds, so the output stays clean — provided the signal itself swings well beyond ±0.6 V.

E5A non-inverting amplifier with gain 50 uses an op-amp with GBW = 1 MHz and \(U_{os} = 2\,\mathrm{mV}\). Find (a) the closed-loop bandwidth, (b) the DC output error from the offset, (c) the gain error at 10 kHz. medium

Show solution
(a) \( f_c = \mathrm{GBW}/A_{cl} = 10^6/50 = 20\,\mathrm{kHz} \). (b) The offset is amplified by the noise gain (= 50 here): \( 50 \cdot 2\,\mathrm{mV} = 100\,\mathrm{mV} \) of DC error at the output — 2 % of a 5 V full scale, before the signal even arrives. (c) Single-pole response: \( |A(f)|/A_{cl} = 1/\sqrt{1+(f/f_c)^2} = 1/\sqrt{1+0.25} = 0.894 \) → the gain is 10.6 % low (−0.97 dB) at 10 kHz. Moral: "bandwidth 20 kHz" does not mean "accurate to 20 kHz" — for 1 % flatness you must stay below \(f_c/7\) (since \(1/\sqrt{1+(1/7)^2}\approx0.99\)).

E6Design a unity-gain Sallen–Key Butterworth low-pass with \(f_0 = 1\,\mathrm{kHz}\) using equal resistors and \(C_1 = 22\,\mathrm{nF}\). Then state its attenuation at 10 kHz. challenge

Show solution
Butterworth ⇒ \(Q = 1/\sqrt2 = 0.707\). Equal-R design: \( Q = \tfrac12\sqrt{C_1/C_2} \Rightarrow C_1/C_2 = 4Q^2 = 2 \Rightarrow C_2 = 11\,\mathrm{nF} \) (use 10 nF, giving \(Q = 0.74\) — 0.4 dB of peaking, usually acceptable; or parallel 10 nF + 1 nF). Resistors: \( \omega_0 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{R^2 C_1 C_2}} \Rightarrow R = \frac{1}{2\pi f_0\sqrt{C_1 C_2}} = \frac{1}{2\pi\cdot10^3\cdot\sqrt{22\cdot11}\,\mathrm{nF}} = \frac{1}{2\pi\cdot10^3\cdot15.6\cdot10^{-9}} \approx 10.2\,\mathrm{k\Omega} \) → 10 kΩ, then \(f_0\) is 2 % high; fine. At 10 kHz (\(r = f/f_0 = 10\)): \( |H| = 1/\sqrt{(1-100)^2 + (10/0.707)^2} \approx 1/\sqrt{9801+200} = 1/100 \) → −40 dB, exactly the −40 dB/decade promise of a second-order filter.

E7Derive the oscillation frequency of the Schmitt-trigger relaxation oscillator: an inverting Schmitt trigger (\(\beta = 0.5\), \(U_{sat} = \pm10\,\mathrm{V}\)) whose output charges a capacitor through \(R = 10\,\mathrm{k\Omega}\), \(C = 10\,\mathrm{nF}\), the capacitor voltage driving the − input. challenge

Show solution
Say the output has just switched to +10 V with the capacitor at the lower threshold \(-\beta U_{sat} = -5\,\mathrm{V}\). The capacitor charges toward +10 V (RC exponential, Module 06): \( u_C(t) = 10 - 15e^{-t/RC} \). It reaches the upper threshold +5 V when \( e^{-t/RC} = \frac{10-5}{15} = \frac{1-\beta}{... } \) — generally \( t_{1/2} = RC\ln\frac{1+\beta}{1-\beta} \). Here: \( t_{1/2} = RC\ln 3 \). Full period \( T = 2RC\ln 3 = 2\cdot10^{-4}\cdot1.0986 \approx 220\,\mathrm{µs} \Rightarrow f \approx 4.55\,\mathrm{kHz} \). Sanity: larger hysteresis (β→1) → the capacitor must travel farther → slower; f depends only on RC and β, not on \(U_{sat}\) (both the travel distance and the drive scale with \(U_{sat}\) and cancel) — which is why this humble circuit is surprisingly supply-tolerant, just like its industrial cousin, the 555.
