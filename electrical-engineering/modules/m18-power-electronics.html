🏭 Power Engineering · Module 18

Power Electronics · Leistungselektronik

Every watt that reaches your phone, your laptop, an electric car or the grid from a solar roof has been chopped, transformed and re-assembled by transistors switching tens of thousands of times per second. Power electronics (Leistungselektronik) is the art of converting electrical energy between forms — AC↔DC, one voltage to another — at efficiencies above 95 %, and it is arguably the most consequential EE discipline of the electrified 21st century.

Corresponds to: Leistungselektronik (5th–6th semester) Prerequisites: Modules 06, 08, 17 Study time: ≈ 6–8 h

1. The central dogma: switch, don't drop

Suppose you must feed a 5 V / 2 A load from a 12 V supply. The classical answer is a linear regulator (Längsregler): a transistor in series with the load, operated in its active region, continuously "throttling" like a half-closed valve. It works, it is quiet and simple — and it is thermodynamically brutal. The full load current flows through the transistor while it drops the entire excess voltage:

\[ P_{\text{transistor}} = (U_{in} - U_{out})\, I_{out}, \qquad \eta_{\text{linear}} = \frac{U_{out} I_{out}}{U_{in} I_{out}} = \frac{U_{out}}{U_{in}}. \]

The efficiency depends only on the voltage ratio — no amount of circuit cleverness inside a linear regulator can beat \(U_{out}/U_{in}\).

Worked example Linear vs. switcher, 12 V → 5 V at 2 A

Compare a linear regulator and a switching converter (Schaltregler, measured \(\eta \approx 95\,\%\)) delivering 5 V / 2 A from a 12 V rail. Ambient 40 °C; the linear pass transistor has \(R_{th,ja} = 60\,\mathrm{K/W}\) without a heatsink and \(T_{j,max}=150\,°\mathrm{C}\).

Step 1 — linear regulator: \( P_{out} = 5 \cdot 2 = 10\,\mathrm{W} \), \( P_{in} = 12 \cdot 2 = 24\,\mathrm{W} \), so \( \eta = 10/24 \approx 42\,\% \). A full \( (12-5)\cdot 2 = 14\,\mathrm{W} \) — 58 % of everything drawn from the input — becomes heat in one transistor.
Step 2 — thermal consequence: without a heatsink \( T_j = 40 + 14 \cdot 60 = 880\,°\mathrm{C} \) — instant destruction. To keep \(T_j \le 125\,°\mathrm{C}\) we need \( R_{th} \le (125-40)/14 \approx 6\,\mathrm{K/W} \): a serious finned heatsink, roughly the volume of the entire circuit.
Step 3 — switching converter: \( P_{in} = 10/0.95 \approx 10.5\,\mathrm{W} \), loss \( \approx 0.53\,\mathrm{W} \) — spread over a MOSFET, an inductor and a diode. No heatsink at all; a patch of PCB copper suffices. Input current drops from 2 A to \(10.5/12 \approx 0.88\,\mathrm{A}\).
42 % vs. ≈ 95 % efficiency; 14 W vs. 0.5 W of heat. This factor-of-26 in dissipation is why essentially every power supply above a few watts switches.
🔑Key idea: a switch dissipates (almost) nothing

For any device, \(P = U \cdot I\). A transistor used as a switch lives in only two states: fully on (\(U \approx 0\), large \(I\) → \(P\approx 0\)) or fully off (\(I \approx 0\), large \(U\) → \(P\approx 0\)). Loss occurs only in the residual on-state drop and in the brief transitions between states. All of power electronics follows from this one observation: chop the energy flow with near-lossless switches, then smooth the chopped waveform with lossless reactive elements — inductors and capacitors — into what the load wants.

📜From mercury arcs to wide bandgap

Power conversion predates the transistor: rotary converters (motor-generator sets) and mercury-arc valves (Quecksilberdampfgleichrichter) ran railways and electrolysis plants from ~1900. The thyristor (1957) made conversion solid-state; the power MOSFET (late 1970s) and IGBT (1980s) made it fast; SiC and GaN (2010s) are now making it small enough to hide a 65 W charger in a plug. Modules 06 (inductors/capacitors), 08 (semiconductors) and 17 (three-phase AC) supply the ingredients we now assemble.

2. The switch toolbox

Real semiconductor switches are imperfect in characteristic ways; choosing the right one is half the design.

2.1 The power diode — the automatic switch

A diode conducts when forward-biased and blocks otherwise — a switch that operates itself. Silicon PN power diodes drop \(U_F \approx 0.8{-}1.1\,\mathrm{V}\); Schottky diodes drop ~0.4 V but are limited in voltage (Si-Schottky ≲ 200 V). The hidden defect is reverse recovery (Sperrverzögerung): a conducting PN diode is flooded with minority carriers, and when the circuit reverses the voltage, the diode briefly conducts backwards while the stored charge \(Q_{rr}\) is swept out. In a hard-switched converter this recovery current spikes through the transistor at every turn-on — a major loss and EMI source. Schottky and SiC diodes are (nearly) recovery-free, which is a big part of their appeal.

2.2 Power MOSFET — fast, resistive, low voltage

The power MOSFET (Module 08) conducts like a resistor: on-state loss is \(P = I^2 R_{DS(on)}\), with \(R_{DS(on)}\) from a few mΩ (30 V parts) to hundreds of mΩ (600 V parts). Being majority-carrier devices they switch in tens of nanoseconds. Weakness: \(R_{DS(on)}\) of silicon MOSFETs grows roughly with \(U_{BR}^{2.5}\), so above ~650 V silicon MOSFETs become uneconomic. Every MOSFET also contains an intrinsic anti-parallel body diode — free but slow (poor recovery).

2.3 IGBT — the high-power workhorse

The IGBT (insulated-gate bipolar transistor, Bipolartransistor mit isolierter Gate-Elektrode) is a MOSFET-driven bipolar device: it drops a roughly constant \(U_{CE(sat)} \approx 1.5{-}2.5\,\mathrm{V}\) like a diode rather than acting as a resistance. At hundreds of amps that fixed drop beats any silicon MOSFET's \(I^2R\). Ratings reach 6.5 kV / 3.6 kA per module. Price: minority-carrier storage causes a turn-off tail current, so switching is slower — practical frequencies of a few kHz to ~20 kHz. IGBTs drive trains, trams, industrial motor drives and (still, in many designs) EV inverters.

2.4 Thyristor / SCR — the latching veteran

The thyristor (SCR, Thyristor) turns on with a gate pulse and then latches: it cannot be turned off by its gate, only by the external current falling to zero. That marries perfectly with the AC mains, whose current crosses zero 100 times a second — line-commutated (netzgeführte) converters. Phase-controlled rectifiers built from SCRs powered a century of DC drives and electrolysis, and thyristors still rule the very top of the power scale: LCC-HVDC links at ±800 kV carry many gigawatts through valves of series-connected thyristors (Section 11).

2.5 SiC and GaN — the wide-bandgap revolution

Silicon carbide (SiC) and gallium nitride (GaN) have ~3× the bandgap of silicon and about 10× the critical electric field. A device blocking a given voltage can therefore be made ~10× thinner and more heavily doped, slashing on-resistance and stored charge. The consequences compound:

  • Higher voltage: SiC MOSFETs at 1200–3300 V behave like fast, low-loss switches where silicon offered only slow IGBTs.
  • Higher frequency: less stored charge → clean, fast edges; GaN converters run at MHz. Since inductor and capacitor size scales as \(1/f_{sw}\) (Section 3), passives shrink dramatically — the physics behind 65 W GaN phone chargers the size of a matchbox.
  • Higher temperature: wider bandgap → far smaller leakage at high \(T_j\); SiC junctions run at 175 °C+ comfortably.

The flagship application is transport: SiC traction inverters (Tesla Model 3 onward, and most 800-V platforms) cut inverter losses by roughly half versus IGBTs, and SiC on-board chargers shrank from bricks to books.

3. Losses, gate drive and the frequency trade-off

3.1 Conduction losses

While a switch is on it dissipates (Durchlassverluste):

  • MOSFET (resistive): \( P_{cond} = I_{rms}^2 \, R_{DS(on)} \) — note the RMS current, and note \(R_{DS(on)}\) roughly doubles from 25 °C to 125 °C.
  • IGBT / diode (fixed drop): \( P_{cond} \approx U_{CE(sat)} \cdot I_{avg} \) (plus a small resistive term).

3.2 Switching losses — the overlap picture

Transitions are not instantaneous. With an inductive load, at turn-on the current must rise before the voltage can fall (and vice versa at turn-off): for tens of nanoseconds the device carries both high voltage and high current. The instantaneous power \(p(t) = u(t)\,i(t)\) spikes to kilowatts; its time-integral is the switching energy per event, \(E_{on} + E_{off}\) (found in every datasheet). Since this happens \(f_{sw}\) times per second (Schaltverluste):

\[ \boxed{\; P_{sw} = (E_{on} + E_{off}) \cdot f_{sw} \;} \]

3.3 The efficiency-vs-frequency trade-off

Why not switch slowly, then? Because the passive components' job is to store energy for one switching period, and the energy to be buffered per cycle is \(P/f_{sw}\). Required inductance and capacitance for a given current/voltage ripple scale as \(1/f_{sw}\) (we derive this in Section 5). Doubling \(f_{sw}\) halves the size, weight and (roughly) cost of the magnetics — but doubles \(P_{sw}\). Every converter design balances on this seesaw:

\[ \eta \uparrow \;\Leftrightarrow\; f_{sw}\downarrow \qquad \text{size} \downarrow \;\Leftrightarrow\; f_{sw}\uparrow . \]

Wide-bandgap devices shift the whole trade-off because their \(E_{sw}\) is 5–10× smaller: same efficiency at 5× the frequency, one-fifth the magnetics. (Resonant "soft-switching" topologies like the LLC, Section 7, cheat the trade-off further by switching at zero voltage.)

3.4 Gate drivers and dead time

A MOSFET is "voltage-controlled", yet its gate is a capacitor of several nanofarads (specified as gate charge \(Q_g\), tens of nC). To traverse the switching transition in, say, 20 ns you must move that charge in 20 ns: \(I_{gate} = Q_g/t \approx 50\,\mathrm{nC}/20\,\mathrm{ns} = 2.5\,\mathrm{A}\) — amps, briefly. This is the job of the gate driver (Gate-Treiber). Mid-transition the gate voltage stalls on the Miller plateau: all driver current is consumed charging the gate-drain capacitance while the drain voltage swings, and a weak driver lingers there — right in the lossy overlap region. High-side switches additionally need a floating or isolated drive (bootstrap circuits, isolated gate drivers).

In any half-bridge (two switches stacked across the DC bus — the universal building block from Section 6 onward) both switches on simultaneously would short the bus: shoot-through (Brückenkurzschluss). Drivers therefore insert a dead time (Totzeit) of tens to hundreds of ns in which both are off and current flows through a diode. Too little dead time → shoot-through; too much → extra diode loss and waveform distortion (Section 8.4).

🚫Classic pitfall: "higher frequency = higher efficiency"

Students often assume faster switching must be better because the datasheet transistor is "fast". Backwards: raising \(f_{sw}\) increases loss (\(P_{sw} = E_{sw} f_{sw}\)) and buys you smaller passives, not efficiency. The exam-safe statement: frequency trades efficiency against size. If a question reports a converter mysteriously heating up after \(f_{sw}\) was raised — that is not a mystery.

4. Rectifiers: AC → DC

4.1 Half-wave — the recap

One diode passes only positive half-waves (M08): output pulses at \(f_{line}\), average \(\hat U/\pi\), terrible ripple, DC current through any upstream transformer. Educational, rarely built. The standard front end is the full bridge (Brückengleichrichter, B2): four diodes steer both half-waves the same way through the load, giving \(|u(t)|\) pulsing at \(2f_{line}\).

4.2 Full bridge with capacitor filter — and the ripple formula

Add a smoothing capacitor (Glättungskondensator) and the output stays near the peak \(\hat u = \sqrt{2}\,U_{rms}\) minus two diode drops. Between charging pulses — almost the whole half-period \(T/2 = 1/(2f_{line})\) — the capacitor alone supplies the load. From \(i = C\,\mathrm{d}u/\mathrm{d}t\), a constant load current \(I_{load}\) discharging \(C\) for time \(\approx 1/(2f_{line})\) sags by:

\[ \boxed{\; \Delta U_{pp} \approx \frac{I_{load}}{2 f_{line}\, C} \;} \]

(Slightly conservative, since recharging consumes part of the half-period — perfect for sizing.)

Worked example Sizing the bus capacitor of an offline supply

A 230 V / 50 Hz bridge rectifier feeds a 60 W converter stage. Allow 30 V peak-to-peak ripple on the DC bus. Size \(C\).

Step 1 — bus voltage and current: \( \hat u = \sqrt{2}\cdot 230 \approx 325\,\mathrm{V} \); with ripple the average bus is ≈ 310 V, so \( I_{load} \approx 60/310 \approx 0.19\,\mathrm{A} \).
Step 2 — apply the ripple formula: \( C \ge \dfrac{I_{load}}{2 f \, \Delta U} = \dfrac{0.19}{2 \cdot 50 \cdot 30} \approx 63\,\mathrm{\mu F} \).
Step 3 — pick a real part: next standard value 68 µF, rated 400 V (325 V peak + mains surges). Check hold-up: the stored energy \( \tfrac12 C \hat u^2 \approx 3.6\,\mathrm{J} \) can bridge a half-cycle mains dropout — a spec many supplies must meet.
C ≈ 68 µF / 400 V. Rule of thumb confirmed: offline supplies need roughly 1 µF per watt on the high-voltage bus.

4.3 Inrush current

At the instant of plug-in the capacitor is empty — through the diodes it looks like a short circuit across the mains, and the first charging spike (Einschaltstromstoß) can reach 50–100 A. Real supplies limit it with an NTC thermistor (high resistance cold, low when warm) or a relay-bypassed resistor. This is the "clack" you hear when big audio amplifiers power up.

4.4 Power factor correction (PFC)

The bridge-plus-capacitor draws current only in narrow spikes at the voltage peaks. Such current is rich in odd harmonics — poor power factor (Module 05), extra RMS current in every cable, and tripped limits: EN 61000-3-2 caps the mains harmonics of equipment above 75 W. The fix is power factor correction (Leistungsfaktorkorrektur): insert a boost converter (Section 6) between bridge and bus capacitor and control its input current to track the sinusoidal voltage. The mains then sees a nearly ideal resistor (\(\lambda > 0.99\)), while the boost output regulates the bus at ~390 V DC. Every laptop brick, LED driver and EV charger above 75 W contains one.

5. DC-DC converters I: the buck, derived properly

DC-DC converters (Gleichspannungswandler) are the heart of power electronics. The workhorse analysis tool is one statement about inductors in periodic steady state. Over one switching period \(T\) the inductor current returns to its starting value (otherwise it would ratchet up forever), so from \(u_L = L\,\mathrm{d}i_L/\mathrm{d}t\):

\[ \boxed{\; \langle u_L \rangle = \frac{1}{T}\int_0^T u_L \,\mathrm{d}t = 0 \;} \qquad \text{(volt-second balance, \textit{Spannungszeitflächen-Gleichgewicht})} \]
💡Intuition: volt-seconds are inductor bookkeeping

Volts across an inductor tilt its current up or down; volt·seconds are the accumulated tilt. Steady state means the books balance every cycle: the volt-seconds pushed in while the switch is on must exactly cancel those pulled out while it is off. Write that one equation and any converter's voltage ratio falls out in two lines — no differential equations needed.

5.1 Buck operation and conversion ratio

Let the switch be on for \(D\,T\) (duty cycle \(D\), Tastgrad) and off for \((1-D)T\). Assume the output holds a steady \(U_{out}\) (the LC filter guarantees small ripple) and continuous inductor current:

  • S on: switch node = \(U_{in}\);   \(u_L = U_{in} - U_{out}\) → \(i_L\) ramps up.
  • S off: \(i_L\) cannot stop; it pulls the switch node down until diode D conducts → node ≈ 0; \(u_L = -U_{out}\) → \(i_L\) ramps down.

Volt-second balance:

\[ (U_{in} - U_{out})\,D T \;=\; U_{out}\,(1-D)T \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \boxed{\; M = \frac{U_{out}}{U_{in}} = D \;} \]

The output is simply the time-average of the switch node — a chopped rectangle, low-pass filtered. Ideal efficiency: 100 %, since every element is a switch, an L or a C.

5.2 Ripple: sizing L and C

During the on-time, \(i_L\) rises linearly by the peak-to-peak current ripple (Stromwelligkeit):

\[ \boxed{\; \Delta I_L = \frac{(U_{in} - U_{out})\,D}{L\, f_{sw}} \;}\]

The triangular ripple component of \(i_L\) flows into \(C\) (the DC part goes to the load). Integrating the triangle gives the output voltage ripple:

\[ \boxed{\; \Delta U_{out} = \frac{\Delta I_L}{8\, f_{sw}\, C} \;} \qquad (+\, \Delta I_L \cdot ESR \text{ for real capacitors}) \]

Both formulas carry \(f_{sw}\) in the denominator — the quantitative form of "passives shrink with frequency" (Section 3.3). Designers typically choose \(\Delta I_L \approx 20{-}40\,\%\) of the load current: smaller wastes inductor size, larger raises RMS losses and risks…

5.3 …the CCM/DCM boundary

A diode conducts only forward: if the ripple valley \(I_{out} - \Delta I_L/2\) tries to go below zero, the diode simply turns off and \(i_L\) sits at zero for the rest of the period — discontinuous conduction mode (DCM, lückender Betrieb). Boundary:

\[ I_{out} > \frac{\Delta I_L}{2} \; \Leftrightarrow \; \text{CCM} . \]

In DCM the neat result \(M = D\) fails (\(U_{out}\) rises toward \(U_{in}\) at light load) and the control dynamics change — a classic trap for lightly-loaded designs. A synchronous buck (Section 6.3) replaces the diode with a MOSFET that can conduct backwards, staying in CCM at any load.

Worked example Complete buck design: 12 V → 5 V at 2 A

Design the power stage: \(U_{in}=12\,\mathrm{V}\), \(U_{out}=5\,\mathrm{V}\), \(I_{out}=2\,\mathrm{A}\), \(f_{sw}=500\,\mathrm{kHz}\). Targets: \(\Delta I_L = 30\,\%\) of \(I_{out}\), \(\Delta U_{out} \le 50\,\mathrm{mV}\).

Step 1 — duty cycle: \( D = U_{out}/U_{in} = 5/12 \approx 0.417 \). (Real converters run slightly higher to cover losses.)
Step 2 — inductor: \( \Delta I_L = 0.3 \cdot 2 = 0.6\,\mathrm{A} \); \[ L = \frac{(U_{in}-U_{out})\,D}{\Delta I_L\, f_{sw}} = \frac{7 \cdot 0.417}{0.6 \cdot 5\cdot 10^{5}} \approx 9.7\,\mathrm{\mu H} \;\rightarrow\; 10\,\mathrm{\mu H}. \] Saturation rating: \( \hat i_L = 2 + 0.3 = 2.3\,\mathrm{A} \) → pick a 3 A-rated part.
Step 3 — CCM check: \( \Delta I_L/2 = 0.3\,\mathrm{A} < 2\,\mathrm{A} \) ✓ — CCM down to 0.3 A load.
Step 4 — capacitor: \( C \ge \dfrac{\Delta I_L}{8 f_{sw} \Delta U_{out}} = \dfrac{0.6}{8 \cdot 5\cdot10^{5} \cdot 0.05} = 3\,\mathrm{\mu F} \). A 22 µF ceramic gives \(\Delta U_{out} \approx 6.8\,\mathrm{mV}\) plus ESR ripple — ceramics' few-mΩ ESR adds ~2 mV. Ample margin (the extra C also helps load transients).
Step 5 — semiconductor stress: switch and diode both block \(U_{in}=12\,\mathrm{V}\) (choose ≥ 20 V parts); diode average current \( (1-D) I_{out} \approx 1.17\,\mathrm{A} \).
D ≈ 0.42, L = 10 µH (3 A), C = 22 µF ceramic, 20 V FET + Schottky (or synchronous FET). This is, almost component for component, the point-of-load converter next to every CPU.

6. DC-DC converters II: boost, buck-boost, synchronous

6.1 Boost — stepping up

Swap the roles: inductor at the input, switch to ground, diode to the output (Hochsetzsteller). Switch on: \(u_L = U_{in}\), current ramps up, energy loads into \(L\); switch off: the inductor insists on its current, its voltage flips, and it lifts the node above \(U_{out}\) so the diode delivers. Volt-second balance:

\[ U_{in}\, D T = (U_{out} - U_{in})(1-D)T \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \boxed{\; M = \frac{U_{out}}{U_{in}} = \frac{1}{1-D} \;} \]

\(D = 0.5\) doubles the voltage; \(D = 0.9\) gives ×10 — on paper \(M \to \infty\) as \(D \to 1\). Reality disagrees: with the inductor's series resistance \(R_L\) included, \(M = \frac{1}{1-D}\cdot\frac{1}{1 + R_L/\big(R(1-D)^2\big)}\) — the gain peaks (typically around ×5–6) and then collapses, because at extreme \(D\) the inductor current grows as \(I_{out}/(1-D)\) and \(I^2 R_L\) losses eat everything. Boost converters also hide a right-half-plane zero: commanding more output first makes it dip — a control headache we quantify with the tools of Module 14.

⚠️The boost has no "off" and no short-circuit protection

Trace the path \(U_{in} \to L \to D \to U_{out}\): it conducts even with the switch never closing. A boost's output can never fall below \(U_{in} - U_F\), and a shorted output puts the full input across one inductor and diode — unlimited current. Any real boost (including every PFC stage) needs upstream fusing.

6.2 Buck-boost — inverting, both directions

Third permutation: switch first, then inductor to ground, diode to the output (Inverswandler). The inductor charges from \(U_{in}\) (on) and discharges into the output (off), which comes out negative:

\[ \boxed{\; M = -\frac{D}{1-D} \;} \]

\(|M|\) passes through 1 at \(D = 0.5\): one topology steps both up and down. The energy here is fully "trampolined" through the inductor each cycle — which is exactly the idea the flyback (Section 7) makes isolated.

6.3 Synchronous rectification

At 5 V output, a Schottky diode's 0.45 V drop costs \(0.45/5 = 9\,\%\) efficiency by itself; at 1 V core voltages it would cost 45 %. The cure: replace the diode with a MOSFET switched on exactly when the diode would conduct — synchronous rectification (Synchrongleichrichtung). A 5 mΩ FET carrying 2 A drops 10 mV: loss 20 mW instead of 0.9 W, a 45× reduction. Cost: a second gate driver and careful dead time (the body diode covers the gaps). Virtually every modern buck below 20 V output is synchronous.

7. Isolated converters: the flyback and friends

Anything plugged into the mains must keep 230 V AC galvanically separated from parts a human can touch: safety standards demand reinforced isolation between mains and output (galvanische Trennung). Isolation also breaks ground loops and lets outputs float. The tool is a high-frequency transformer — and because transformer size shrinks with frequency just like inductors, a 100 kHz transformer for 65 W is a 2 cm toroid rather than the fist-sized 50 Hz lump in an old wall wart.

7.1 Flyback — the phone-charger workhorse

Take the buck-boost and split its inductor into two magnetically-coupled windings: the flyback converter (Sperrwandler). Its "transformer" is really a coupled inductor: with the primary switch on, current builds in the primary and energy is stored in the core — specifically in a deliberate air gap (recall from Module 06: a gap holds magnetic energy at low flux density, preventing core saturation). The secondary diode blocks during this phase. Switch off → flux must continue → secondary voltage flips polarity, the diode conducts, and the stored energy dumps into the output. With turns ratio \(n = N_s/N_p\):

\[ \boxed{\; M = \frac{U_{out}}{U_{in}} = n\,\frac{D}{1-D} \;} \]

One switch, one magnetic part, one diode, isolation and any voltage ratio: unbeatable simplicity below ~100 W. Every phone charger, standby supply and LED bulb driver is a flyback. Modern USB-PD chargers add a GaN primary switch and synchronous secondary to hit 94 % in a plug-sized box.

Above ~100 W the flyback's peaky currents and gapped-core energy storage become inefficient. The family grows: the forward converter (Durchflusswandler) — a buck with a true transformer inserted, transferring energy while the switch is on; and the LLC resonant converter, which shapes currents into sinusoids so switches turn on at zero voltage (soft switching) — the standard for server supplies, TVs and EV on-board chargers at 90–98 % efficiency.

8. Inverters: DC → AC

8.1 The H-bridge

To make AC from DC you need to apply the DC bus to the load in either polarity. Four switches in an H-bridge (Vollbrücke) do exactly that:

Closing (S₁, S₄) applies \(+U_{dc}\) to the load; (S₂, S₃) applies \(-U_{dc}\). Toggle at 50 Hz and you have a square-wave inverter — functional (cheap "modified sine" UPSs work this way) but the output contains strong odd harmonics: \( u(t) = \frac{4 U_{dc}}{\pi}\left(\sin\omega t + \tfrac13 \sin 3\omega t + \tfrac15 \sin 5\omega t + \dots\right)\). Motors buzz and heat; electronics complain.

8.2 Sinusoidal PWM

The elegant fix reuses the buck insight — the filtered output follows the duty cycle — but now the duty cycle is modulated sinusoidally. Compare a sinusoidal reference (Sollwert) at the desired output frequency with a triangular carrier (Trägersignal) at \(f_c \gg f_1\): whenever reference > carrier, switch high, else low. The result is sinusoidal PWM: a pulse train whose local average traces the sine. With modulation index \(m_a = \hat u_{ref}/\hat u_{carrier}\), the fundamental of the output is

\[ \hat u_{1} = m_a \, U_{dc} \quad \text{(full bridge, } m_a \le 1). \]

The harmonics no longer sit at 3rd, 5th, 7th… but cluster around \(f_c\) and its multiples — far above the fundamental, so a small LC filter (or the motor's own inductance) removes them. Pushing \(m_a\) beyond 1 (overmodulation) buys a little more fundamental at the price of returning low-order harmonics.

Push \(m_a\) past 1.0 and watch the pulse pattern saturate (overmodulation: flat-topped, low-order harmonics return).

8.3 Three-phase inverters

Add a third half-bridge leg: six switches generate three phase voltages shifted 120° — the universal machine-side converter (Module 17 supplies the three-phase theory). Motor drives control it with space-vector modulation (SVM), a smarter cousin of sine-triangle PWM that treats the three legs as one rotating voltage vector and squeezes ~15 % more usable voltage from the same DC link. Every VFD (Frequenzumrichter), EV inverter and modern wind or solar grid converter is this six-switch bridge plus firmware.

8.4 Dead time, revisited

Each leg needs dead time between its top and bottom switch (Section 3.4). During dead time the leg's output voltage is set by the load current's direction (whichever diode it forces on), not by the controller — so each pulse is slightly wrong. At low output voltage these fixed-size errors dominate: dead-time distortion causes torque ripple in slow-turning motors and crossover distortion in class-D amplifiers. Good drives measure the current direction and pre-compensate each edge.

9. Closing the loop

Every converter so far ran open-loop, but \(U_{out}\) must survive input swings and load steps, so a controller adjusts \(D\) continuously. Two standard schemes: voltage-mode control — compare \(U_{out}\) to a reference, feed the error through a compensator (a shaped PI/PID, Module 14), let it set the PWM duty; and current-mode control — an inner loop regulates the inductor current peak-by-peak, the outer voltage loop merely commands that current. Current mode turns the inductor into a controlled current source: simpler outer-loop dynamics, inherent cycle-by-cycle overcurrent protection, easy paralleling — at the cost of current sensing and a subtle subharmonic instability above \(D = 0.5\) (cured by "slope compensation"). The loop-shaping mathematics — crossover frequency, phase margin, that boost RHP zero — is exactly Module 14 applied to a switching plant.

10. Thermal design: keeping the junction alive

Losses do not disappear; they exit through the package as heat, driving the junction temperature \(T_j\) (Sperrschichttemperatur) above ambient. Heat flow behaves like a DC circuit — temperature ↔ voltage, power ↔ current, thermal resistance \(R_{th}\) (K/W) ↔ resistance — so the junction-to-ambient path is a series chain:

\[ \boxed{\; T_j = T_{amb} + P_{loss}\,\big( R_{th,jc} + R_{th,cs} + R_{th,sa} \big) \;} \]

junction→case (package, fixed by the device), case→sink (thermal interface material), sink→ambient (your heatsink, Kühlkörper — the only term you control). Silicon must stay below \(T_{j,max}\) = 150–175 °C; designers derate to ~125 °C because failure rates roughly double per 10 K and power cycling fatigues solder and bond wires.

Worked example IGBT losses and heatsink selection

One IGBT in a motor-drive bridge conducts an average 20 A for half of each period with \(U_{CE(sat)} = 1.7\,\mathrm{V}\). Datasheet: \(E_{on}+E_{off} = 4\,\mathrm{mJ}\) at the working point; \(f_{sw} = 8\,\mathrm{kHz}\); \(R_{th,jc} = 0.40\,\mathrm{K/W}\), \(R_{th,cs} = 0.10\,\mathrm{K/W}\); \(T_{amb} = 40\,°\mathrm{C}\) in the cabinet; design limit \(T_j \le 125\,°\mathrm{C}\). Choose the heatsink.

Step 1 — conduction loss: \( P_{cond} = U_{CE(sat)} \cdot I_{avg} \cdot D = 1.7 \cdot 20 \cdot 0.5 = 17\,\mathrm{W} \).
Step 2 — switching loss: \( P_{sw} = (E_{on}+E_{off}) f_{sw} = 4\,\mathrm{mJ} \cdot 8000 = 32\,\mathrm{W} \). Note it dominates — typical for IGBTs even at modest frequency.
Step 3 — total and thermal budget: \( P_{loss} = 49\,\mathrm{W} \). Allowed total resistance: \( R_{th,ja} \le \dfrac{125-40}{49} \approx 1.73\,\mathrm{K/W} \).
Step 4 — heatsink spec: \( R_{th,sa} \le 1.73 - 0.40 - 0.10 = 1.23\,\mathrm{K/W} \). That is a substantial extruded profile (~10×10×4 cm) in still air, or a much smaller one with a fan (forced air cuts \(R_{th,sa}\) ~3×).
Heatsink ≤ 1.2 K/W. Sanity check: \(T_j = 40 + 49 \cdot 1.73 \approx 125\,°\mathrm{C}\) ✓ — and if the fan fails, \(T_j\) exceeds 150 °C, which is why drives monitor heatsink temperature.
🧪Interactive — the thermal resistance ladder simulation

Heat flows from junction to ambient through three series resistances; each drops \(\Delta T = P \cdot R_{th}\) like a voltage. Slide the loss power up or worsen the heatsink and watch the junction bar cross the 150 °C limit. Note how little the junction-to-case and interface terms matter once the heatsink is poor — the largest resistance dominates, exactly as in a series circuit.

11. Applications tour — where the watts go

11.1 The phone charger

Mains → bridge rectifier (+ small PFC above 75 W) → ~390 V bus → flyback with GaN primary switch at ~150 kHz → synchronous secondary → USB-C. A USB-PD controller negotiates the output (5/9/15/20 V) with the phone over the CC wire and trims the flyback's reference via an optocoupler. 65 W at ~94 % in 30 cm³ — a small monument to every section above.

11.2 The EV powertrain

The traction inverter is the crown jewel: 400–800 V bus, 150–300 kW peak, SiC modules at ~10–20 kHz, efficiency ≈ 98–99 %, water-cooled. In regenerative braking it runs backwards — the machine becomes a generator and the same six switches rectify power into the battery. Power electronic converters are inherently bidirectional if you build both current paths.

11.3 Solar: MPPT and the string inverter

A photovoltaic panel is a current source with a knee: its power \(P = U \cdot I\) has a single maximum power point (MPP) that wanders with sunlight and temperature. Connect the panel straight to a battery or fixed bus and you operate wherever the bus dictates — almost never at the MPP. A DC-DC converter between panel and bus can hold the panel at its best voltage regardless of the output — maximum power point tracking (MPPT): perturb the operating point, observe \(\mathrm{d}P\), walk uphill.

Worked example What MPPT is worth, in watts

A panel has \(U_{oc} = 40\,\mathrm{V}\), \(I_{sc} = 10\,\mathrm{A}\), MPP at \(U_{mp} = 33\,\mathrm{V}\), \(I_{mp} = 9\,\mathrm{A}\). Compare charging a 24 V battery (a) directly, (b) through an MPPT converter with \(\eta = 97\,\%\).

Step 1 — direct connection: the battery clamps the panel at 24 V, well left of the knee, where the panel still delivers nearly \(I_{sc}\): \(I \approx 9.9\,\mathrm{A}\) → \(P \approx 24 \cdot 9.9 \approx 238\,\mathrm{W}\).
Step 2 — MPPT: converter holds the panel at 33 V / 9 A = 297 W, converts to 24 V at 97 %: \(P_{batt} = 297 \cdot 0.97 \approx 288\,\mathrm{W}\) (i.e. ≈ 12 A into the battery — more output current than input current, which only a converter can do).
Step 3 — verdict: gain = \(288/238 \approx 1.21\).
MPPT harvests ≈ 21 % more energy from identical hardware — and far more when the mismatch is worse (cold mornings raise \(U_{mp}\)). That is why even 10 € charge controllers advertise it.

A rooftop string inverter chains it all: panels in series (~600 V DC) → MPPT boost stage → DC link → three-phase PWM inverter (Section 8) synchronised to the grid, injecting sinusoidal current at unity power factor, with anti-islanding protection. Efficiency ≈ 98 %.

11.4 UPS and HVDC — the extremes of scale

An uninterruptible power supply (unterbrechungsfreie Stromversorgung, USV) is rectifier + battery + inverter; in "double-conversion" units the load always runs from the inverter, so a mains failure changes nothing downstream. At the opposite extreme, HVDC (Hochspannungs-Gleichstromübertragung) converts entire power stations: classic thyristor (LCC) links at ±800 kV move 8+ GW across China; modern modular multilevel converters (MMC) stack hundreds of half-bridge cells per arm — IGBT technology synthesising near-perfect 400 kV sinusoids — to connect offshore wind farms and asynchronous grids (Module 19 covers the grid side). Same volt-second physics as the 10 µH buck, scaled by nine orders of magnitude.

13. Exercises

Reach for the formula table only after you have tried to reconstruct each formula from volt-second balance or from \(P = UI\).

E1A linear regulator drops 24 V to 3.3 V at 500 mA. Find its efficiency and dissipation, and the dissipation of a 92 %-efficient buck doing the same job. easy

Show solution
Linear: \( \eta = 3.3/24 = 13.8\,\% \); \( P_{loss} = (24-3.3)\cdot 0.5 = 10.35\,\mathrm{W} \) — needs a heatsink for a 1.65 W load! Buck: \( P_{out} = 1.65\,\mathrm{W} \), \( P_{loss} = 1.65\,(1/0.92 - 1) \approx 0.14\,\mathrm{W} \) — 72× less heat. Sanity check: the wider the conversion ratio, the more absurd the linear option becomes; that is why 24 V industrial systems never regulate logic rails linearly.

E2A buck converts 48 V to 12 V at \(f_{sw} = 200\,\mathrm{kHz}\) with \(L = 33\,\mathrm{\mu H}\). Find \(D\) and \(\Delta I_L\), and the minimum load current for CCM. easy

Show solution
\( D = 12/48 = 0.25 \). \( \Delta I_L = \frac{(48-12)\cdot 0.25}{33\cdot10^{-6} \cdot 2\cdot10^{5}} = \frac{9}{6.6} \approx 1.36\,\mathrm{A} \). CCM requires \( I_{out} > \Delta I_L/2 \approx 0.68\,\mathrm{A} \). Below that the converter enters DCM and the output would rise if the controller didn't shrink \(D\). Real 48 V→12 V telecom bricks show exactly this ripple order of magnitude.

E3A boost converter feeds a 48 V / 96 W load from a 12 V battery, \(\eta = 95\,\%\). Find \(D\) (ideal), the average input current, and the average diode current. medium

Show solution
\( M = 4 = 1/(1-D) \Rightarrow D = 0.75 \). Input power \( P_{in} = 96/0.95 \approx 101\,\mathrm{W} \) → \( I_{in} = 101/12 \approx 8.4\,\mathrm{A} \) — note the input current is 4× the 2 A output current: step-up converters stress their input side. The diode carries the output current on average: \( I_{D,avg} = I_{out} = 2\,\mathrm{A} \) (it conducts \(8.4\,\mathrm{A}\)-ish for the \(1-D = 25\,\%\) of the time: \(8.4 \cdot 0.25 \approx 2.1\,\mathrm{A}\) ✓, difference = losses/ripple).

E4A 230 V / 50 Hz bridge rectifier supplies 0.5 A DC. How much capacitance keeps the ripple below 20 V peak-to-peak? What is the approximate minimum bus voltage? medium

Show solution
\( C \ge \frac{I}{2 f \Delta U} = \frac{0.5}{2\cdot 50 \cdot 20} = 250\,\mathrm{\mu F} \) → choose 330 µF / 400 V. Peak: \( \hat u = \sqrt2 \cdot 230 - 2\cdot 1\,\mathrm{V} \approx 323\,\mathrm{V} \); valley ≈ \(323 - 20 = 303\,\mathrm{V}\). Remark: the downstream converter must regulate over this 303–323 V band — and over the mains tolerance (±10 %) on top. Designers spec input ranges like 90–264 V AC for exactly this reason.

E5A MOSFET in a buck carries \(I_{rms} = 3\,\mathrm{A}\) with \(R_{DS(on)} = 25\,\mathrm{m\Omega}\) (hot) and loses \(E_{on}+E_{off} = 8\,\mathrm{\mu J}\) per cycle. At what \(f_{sw}\) do switching losses equal conduction losses? What is the total loss there? medium

Show solution
Conduction: \( P_{cond} = 3^2 \cdot 0.025 = 0.225\,\mathrm{W} \) — independent of frequency. Switching: \( P_{sw} = 8\,\mathrm{\mu J} \cdot f_{sw} \). Equality: \( f_{sw} = \dfrac{0.225\,\mathrm{W}}{8\,\mathrm{\mu J}} = 28\,125\,\mathrm{Hz} \approx 28\,\mathrm{kHz} \). Total loss there: \(2 \cdot 0.225 = 0.45\,\mathrm{W}\). Interpretation: above ~28 kHz this design is switching-loss dominated; running it at 500 kHz would cost \(0.225 + 4 = 4.2\,\mathrm{W}\). To switch fast you need a device with smaller \(E_{sw}\) (better figure of merit, e.g. GaN) — frequency choices always trace back to this crossover.

E6A TO-247 SiC MOSFET (\(R_{th,jc} = 0.5\,\mathrm{K/W}\)) sits on a heatsink with \(R_{th,sa} = 2.0\,\mathrm{K/W}\) via a pad with \(R_{th,cs} = 0.5\,\mathrm{K/W}\); \(T_{amb} = 45\,°\mathrm{C}\), \(T_{j,max} = 175\,°\mathrm{C}\), derated design limit 135 °C. Find the allowed dissipation, and the new limit if a fan cuts \(R_{th,sa}\) to 0.6 K/W. challenge

Show solution
Chain: \( R_{th,ja} = 0.5+0.5+2.0 = 3.0\,\mathrm{K/W} \). \( P_{max} = (135-45)/3.0 = 30\,\mathrm{W} \). With fan: \( R_{th,ja} = 1.6\,\mathrm{K/W} \) → \( P_{max} = 90/1.6 \approx 56\,\mathrm{W} \). Note the diminishing returns: the fan cut the heatsink term by 70 % but total capability less than doubled, because the fixed junction-case and interface terms now dominate. Next lever: a better pad (\(R_{th,cs}\to 0.2\)) or two parallel devices (halving both \(P\) per device and effective \(R_{th,jc}\)). Also check: at 30 W and 3.0 K/W the heatsink surface sits at \(45 + 30\cdot 2.0 = 105\,°\mathrm{C}\) — untouchable; industrial rules require guards above ~70 °C.

E7A full-bridge inverter on a 400 V DC link uses sinusoidal PWM with \(m_a = 0.9\), \(f_1 = 50\,\mathrm{Hz}\), \(f_c = 10\,\mathrm{kHz}\). Find the RMS fundamental output voltage, and state where the first large harmonic groups appear. Could this inverter feed a 230 V mains-voltage load? challenge

Show solution
Fundamental peak: \( \hat u_1 = m_a U_{dc} = 0.9 \cdot 400 = 360\,\mathrm{V} \) → RMS \( 360/\sqrt2 \approx 255\,\mathrm{V} \). Yes — 230 V RMS needs \( \hat u_1 = 325\,\mathrm{V} \), i.e. \( m_a = 325/400 \approx 0.81 \), comfortably inside the linear range (this is why ~400 V is the standard single-phase DC-link voltage). Harmonics: clustered as sidebands around \(f_c = 10\,\mathrm{kHz}\) and multiples (20 kHz, …), i.e. at \(200\times\) the fundamental — easily removed by a small LC filter with cutoff around 1–2 kHz. Compare a square-wave inverter, whose 3rd harmonic at 150 Hz is essentially unfilterable at reasonable size: PWM does not remove harmonic energy, it relocates it to where filtering is cheap.
