AC Circuits & Phasors · Wechselstromtechnik

Every outlet on the planet delivers a sinusoid, and every filter, motor, transformer, and power line is designed in the language of this module: complex amplitudes. The phasor transform turns the differential equations of L and C into ordinary algebra — after this module, AC circuit analysis is Module 04 with complex numbers, and that is not a metaphor but a theorem.

Corresponds to: Grundlagen der Elektrotechnik II (2nd semester) Prerequisites: Modules 01, 04 Study time: ≈ 6–8 h

1. Why the world runs on AC

Direct current (Gleichstrom) came first. Yet from about 1890 onward the world electrified with alternating current (Wechselstrom) — current that reverses direction periodically, following a sine wave. Two physical facts decided this, and both are worth understanding rather than memorizing.

1. Rotation produces sinusoids for free. Spin a wire loop in a magnetic field (that is what every generator is, from a bicycle dynamo to a 1.5 GW nuclear turbo-generator). The magnetic flux through the loop varies as \(\cos(\omega t)\) with the rotation angle, and Faraday's law of induction (Module 07) makes the induced voltage its derivative:

\[ u(t) = \hat{u}\,\sin(\omega t). \]

A sinusoid is not a design choice; it is what rotating machinery naturally emits. Forcing a generator to produce DC requires a mechanical commutator — sparking brushes, wear, maintenance.

2. Only changing currents can be transformed. A transformer (Module 07) couples two coils through a shared magnetic flux, and induction only responds to change: \(u = N\,\mathrm{d}\Phi/\mathrm{d}t\). Feed it DC and it does nothing (worse: it overheats). Feed it AC and it converts voltage levels with ~99 % efficiency and no moving parts. Why does that matter? Transmission losses are \(P_{loss} = I^2 R_{line}\). To push 100 MW through a line, you want maximum voltage and minimum current: at 400 kV you need 250 A; at 230 V you would need 435 000 A — the line would vaporize. AC lets us step up to hundreds of kilovolts for transport and back down to 230 V at your wall, cheaply, at every substation.

📜The war of currents — and its modern plot twist

In the late 1880s, Thomas Edison (DC, low voltage, a power plant every few kilometers) fought George Westinghouse and Nikola Tesla (AC, transformable, long lines) in one of the nastiest marketing wars in engineering history — Edison publicly electrocuted animals with AC to brand it lethal. Physics won anyway: AC lit the 1893 Chicago World's Fair and harnessed Niagara Falls in 1895, and the grid went alternating worldwide. The twist: today, power electronics (Module 18) can transform DC too, and for very long or undersea links HVDC transmission actually beats AC (no reactive line losses — a concept you will understand by Section 8). Modern grids (Module 19) are AC with strategic DC arteries. Edison lost the war but is winning some late battles.

There is also a mathematical reason the sinusoid rules: it is the eigenfunction of linear time-invariant systems. Put a sine into any circuit of R, L, C and out comes a sine of the same frequency — only amplitude and phase change. No other waveform survives a circuit unchanged in shape. This single fact is what makes the phasor method of Section 4 possible.

2. The sinusoid — four numbers describe everything

The general sinusoidal voltage is

\[ u(t) = \hat{u}\,\sin(\omega t + \varphi_u), \]

and because a sine of known frequency is fully determined by so little, the entire waveform is pinned down by:

  • Amplitude \(\hat{u}\) (Scheitelwert/Amplitude) — the peak value. The hat \(\hat{\ }\) marks peak values throughout German literature.
  • Period \(T\) (Periodendauer) — time for one full cycle, in seconds.
  • Frequency \(f = 1/T\) (Frequenz) — cycles per second, in hertz (Hz), honoring Heinrich Hertz.
  • Phase angle \(\varphi_u\) (Nullphasenwinkel) — where in its cycle the wave sits at \(t=0\); it encodes timing relative to a reference.

Because the sine function needs its argument in radians, we bundle \(2\pi f\) into the angular frequency (Kreisfrequenz):

\[ \boxed{\;\omega = 2\pi f = \frac{2\pi}{T}\;} \qquad [\omega] = \mathrm{s^{-1}} \ \text{(rad/s)}. \]

For the European grid: \(f = 50\,\mathrm{Hz}\), so \(T = 20\,\mathrm{ms}\) and \(\omega = 2\pi \cdot 50 \approx 314.16\,\mathrm{s^{-1}}\). (North America uses 60 Hz — a historical accident of early equipment choices, frozen forever by installed base. Nothing fundamental prefers either.) The 50 Hz value itself was a compromise: low enough for early machines, high enough that lamps don't visibly flicker.

⚠️Phase is always relative — fix the convention now

A single sinusoid's phase is arbitrary (you choose when \(t=0\) is). What matters physically is the difference between two sinusoids. Throughout this module (and German convention) the phase shift is \(\varphi = \varphi_u - \varphi_i\): voltage phase minus current phase. Positive \(\varphi\) means the current lags the voltage (inductive behavior); negative \(\varphi\) means the current leads (capacitive). Getting this subtraction backwards flips the sign of reactive power in Section 8 — decide once, stay consistent forever.

3. Average and RMS — what "230 V" actually means

How do you state "the" value of a quantity that spends half its time negative? The arithmetic mean of a sine over a period is exactly zero — true, and useless. The meaningful average comes from asking about power: what DC voltage would heat a resistor equally hard as this AC waveform does on average?

Instantaneous power in a resistor is \(p(t) = u^2(t)/R\). Its average over one period:

\[ \bar{P} = \frac{1}{T}\int_0^T \frac{u^2(t)}{R}\,\mathrm{d}t \;\stackrel{!}{=}\; \frac{U^2}{R} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad U = \sqrt{\frac{1}{T}\int_0^T u^2(t)\,\mathrm{d}t}. \]

Read the recipe off the formula, right to left: square the waveform, take the mean, take the root — the root-mean-square or RMS value (Effektivwert).

3.1 Deriving the famous factor 1/√2

For \(u(t) = \hat{u}\sin(\omega t)\), use the identity \(\sin^2 x = \tfrac{1}{2}\bigl(1 - \cos 2x\bigr)\):

\[ U^2 = \frac{1}{T}\int_0^T \hat{u}^2 \sin^2(\omega t)\,\mathrm{d}t = \frac{\hat{u}^2}{T}\int_0^T \Bigl(\tfrac12 - \tfrac12\cos(2\omega t)\Bigr)\mathrm{d}t = \frac{\hat{u}^2}{T}\cdot\frac{T}{2} = \frac{\hat{u}^2}{2}, \]

because the \(\cos(2\omega t)\) term averages to zero over any whole number of periods. Hence

\[ \boxed{\;U = \frac{\hat{u}}{\sqrt{2}} \approx 0.707\,\hat{u}\;}\qquad\text{(sinusoid only!)} \]

So "230 V mains" is the RMS value. The actual peak at your wall socket is

\[ \hat{u} = \sqrt{2}\cdot 230\,\mathrm{V} \approx 325\,\mathrm{V}, \]

and the peak-to-peak swing is 650 V. This is why capacitors in mains-connected equipment are rated 400 V or more, and why touching "just 230 V" involves 325 V crests. Unless stated otherwise, every AC voltage or current you will ever quote — nameplates, multimeters, grid data — is an RMS value.

🔑Key idea: RMS makes the DC formulas survive

The entire point of RMS values is that the DC power formulas of Module 04 keep working: \(P = U I = I^2 R = U^2/R\) holds for AC in a resistor if U and I are RMS. One convention, and forty formulas carry over unchanged. (For L and C there is a twist — Section 8.)

The factor \(1/\sqrt{2}\) belongs to the sine alone. Run the square–mean–root recipe on other shapes (each with peak \(\hat{u}\)):

🚫Classic pitfall: "average-responding" meters

Cheap multimeters do not compute RMS. They measure the rectified average and multiply by 1.11 — the correct ratio for a sine. On any other waveform (dimmer outputs, inverter power, PWM) the reading is simply wrong, sometimes by 30 %+. Instruments that genuinely square-mean-root are labeled "True RMS". Also never confuse the RMS value with the arithmetic mean: for the grid, the mean is 0 V, the RMS is 230 V. Exam setters love this distinction.

4. The phasor transform — turning calculus into algebra

Now the intellectual centerpiece. Circuits with L and C obey differential equations, because \(u_L = L\,\mathrm{d}i/\mathrm{d}t\) and \(i_C = C\,\mathrm{d}u/\mathrm{d}t\). Solving coupled ODEs for every network would be miserable. Euler's formula from Module 01 rescues us:

\[ e^{jx} = \cos x + j\sin x . \]

(Electrical engineers write \(j\) for the imaginary unit — \(i\) is taken by current.) Any sinusoid can be written as the real projection of a rotating complex number:

\[ u(t) = \hat{u}\cos(\omega t + \varphi_u) = \operatorname{Re}\Bigl\{\underbrace{\hat{u}\,e^{j\varphi_u}}_{\hat{\underline{u}}}\; e^{j\omega t}\Bigr\}. \]

Picture \(\hat{u}\,e^{j(\omega t + \varphi_u)}\) as an arrow of length \(\hat{u}\) rotating counterclockwise in the complex plane at \(\omega\) rad/s; the physical waveform is its shadow on the real axis. Here is the crucial observation: in a linear circuit driven at one frequency, every voltage and current rotates at the same \(\omega\). The factor \(e^{j\omega t}\) is shared by everyone — so we agree to stop writing it. What remains is a complex constant per signal, the phasor (komplexer Zeiger, underlined in German notation):

\[ \underline{U} = U e^{j\varphi_u} \qquad \text{(complex RMS phasor: magnitude = RMS value, angle = phase)}. \]

Why go through all this? Because of what differentiation does to \(e^{j\omega t}\):

\[ \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}t}\, e^{j\omega t} = j\omega\, e^{j\omega t}. \]

Differentiation in time becomes multiplication by \(j\omega\) in the phasor domain; integration becomes division by \(j\omega\). And multiplying by \(j = e^{j\pi/2}\) is geometrically a 90° rotation. The whole apparatus of calculus collapses into complex multiplication:

\[ \boxed{\;\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}t} \;\longleftrightarrow\; j\omega\;} \]
💡Intuition: the phasor is a frozen snapshot

Imagine photographing all the rotating arrows of a circuit with a strobe light synchronized to \(\omega\). Every arrow appears frozen; only their lengths and relative angles remain — that photograph is the phasor diagram. Since all arrows rotate together, their relationships never change, so the frozen picture contains everything except the (shared, boring) rotation itself. Solve the geometry of the photo, then let it spin again to recover the time functions.

⚠️The fine print of the phasor method

Phasors are valid only under three conditions: (1) the circuit is linear (R, L, C, linear sources — no diodes), (2) everything oscillates at one single frequency \(\omega\), (3) we look at the steady state (eingeschwungener Zustand), after switch-on transients have died away. Transients get their own tools in Module 06, and multiple frequencies are handled by superposition — one phasor analysis per frequency (Fourier, Module 03).

5. Impedance — R, L and C get complex resistances

Define, in perfect analogy to Ohm's law, the impedance (Impedanz, Scheinwiderstand) of a two-terminal element as the complex ratio

\[ \underline{Z} = \frac{\underline{U}}{\underline{I}} = Z\,e^{j\varphi}, \qquad [\underline{Z}] = \Omega, \]

where \(Z = |\underline{Z}|\) scales the amplitude and \(\varphi = \varphi_u - \varphi_i\) is the phase shift the element imposes. Now derive \(\underline{Z}\) for each element from its physical law — this is the module's central calculation, so we do all three honestly.

5.1 The resistor: Z = R

The law \(u = R\,i\) contains no derivative, so in phasors simply

\[ \underline{U} = R\,\underline{I} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \boxed{\;\underline{Z}_R = R\;} \qquad \varphi = 0. \]

Voltage and current rise and fall in lockstep — in phase. A resistor is frequency-blind: 50 Hz or 5 MHz, same behavior.

5.2 The inductor: Z = jωL — current lags 90°

An inductor (Spule) opposes changes of current: \(u_L = L\,\dfrac{\mathrm{d}i}{\mathrm{d}t}\). Apply the transform (\(\mathrm{d}/\mathrm{d}t \to j\omega\)):

\[ \underline{U} = j\omega L\,\underline{I} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \boxed{\;\underline{Z}_L = j\omega L\;} \qquad Z_L = \omega L,\quad \varphi = +90^\circ. \]

The magnitude \(X_L = \omega L\) is the inductive reactance (Blindwiderstand): it grows linearly with frequency. At DC (\(\omega = 0\)) the inductor is a plain wire; at high frequency it chokes current off — hence the component name "choke" (Drossel). The factor \(+j\) says the voltage phasor points 90° ahead of the current phasor: the current lags the voltage by a quarter period. Physically: the voltage must first be applied, and only then does the current ramp up through it — the inductance resists the change.

5.3 The capacitor: Z = 1/(jωC) — current leads 90°

A capacitor (Kondensator) draws current only while its voltage changes: \(i_C = C\,\dfrac{\mathrm{d}u}{\mathrm{d}t}\). Transforming:

\[ \underline{I} = j\omega C\,\underline{U} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \boxed{\;\underline{Z}_C = \frac{1}{j\omega C} = -\frac{j}{\omega C}\;} \qquad Z_C = \frac{1}{\omega C},\quad \varphi = -90^\circ. \]

The reactance \(X_C = 1/(\omega C)\) falls with frequency: at DC a capacitor is an open circuit (blocks); at high frequency it approaches a short (passes). The \(-j\) says the current points 90° ahead of the voltage: the current leads. Physically: charge must flow first, and only the accumulated charge then builds the voltage.

💡Remembering who leads: "ELI the ICE man"

In an inductor (L), EMF E before current I: ELI. In a capacitor (C), current I before voltage E: ICE. The German mnemonic says the same: „Bei Induktivitäten die Ströme sich verspäten; im Kondensator eilt der Strom vor." Physically both follow from which quantity stores the energy: the inductor's energy sits in the current (\(\tfrac12 L i^2\)) — current cannot jump; the capacitor's sits in the voltage (\(\tfrac12 C u^2\)) — voltage cannot jump.

6. Combining impedances — the series RLC circuit

Impedances obey the same combination rules as resistances — series impedances add, parallel impedances combine reciprocally — because Kirchhoff's laws hold for phasors (sums of sinusoids of one frequency correspond to sums of their phasors). For the workhorse case, R, L and C in series:

\[ \underline{Z} = R + j\omega L + \frac{1}{j\omega C} = R + j\underbrace{\Bigl(\omega L - \frac{1}{\omega C}\Bigr)}_{X \;=\; \text{net reactance}}. \]

Real part: resistance (dissipates). Imaginary part: reactance (stores and returns). Magnitude and phase follow from the complex-plane geometry:

\[ \boxed{\;Z = \sqrt{R^2 + \Bigl(\omega L - \tfrac{1}{\omega C}\Bigr)^2}, \qquad \varphi = \arctan\frac{\omega L - \tfrac{1}{\omega C}}{R}\;} \]

Notice the sign war inside the bracket: \(\omega L\) grows with frequency, \(1/(\omega C)\) shrinks. At low frequency the capacitor dominates (\(X < 0\), current leads — capacitive); at high frequency the inductor dominates (\(X > 0\), current lags — inductive). At exactly one frequency they cancel:

\[ \omega_0 L = \frac{1}{\omega_0 C} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \omega_0 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{LC}}, \qquad f_0 = \frac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{LC}}. \]

There \(\underline{Z} = R\), purely resistive, and the current peaks: resonance (Resonanz). Module 06 builds radios, filters and quality factors on this single cancellation; here, just watch it happen in the widget below.

Worked example A series RLC at mains frequency

\(R = 100\,\Omega\), \(L = 200\,\mathrm{mH}\), \(C = 10\,\mathrm{\mu F}\) in series across the 230 V / 50 Hz grid. Find the impedance, the current, and the phase relationship.

Step 1 — the two reactances at ω = 314.16 s⁻¹: \( X_L = \omega L = 314.16 \cdot 0.2 \approx 62.8\,\Omega \), \(\quad X_C = \frac{1}{\omega C} = \frac{1}{314.16 \cdot 10^{-5}} \approx 318.3\,\Omega \).
Step 2 — net impedance: \( \underline{Z} = 100 + j(62.8 - 318.3) = (100 - j255.5)\,\Omega \). Magnitude: \( Z = \sqrt{100^2 + 255.5^2} \approx 274\,\Omega \); phase \( \varphi = \arctan(-255.5/100) \approx -68.6^\circ \).
Step 3 — current: \( I = U/Z = 230/274 \approx 0.84\,\mathrm{A} \), and since φ < 0 the circuit is capacitive: the current leads the voltage by 68.6°.
Step 4 — sanity check via resonance: \( f_0 = \frac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{0.2 \cdot 10^{-5}}} \approx 113\,\mathrm{Hz} \). We drive at 50 Hz, well below resonance — capacitive behavior expected ✓.
\(\underline{Z} \approx (100 - j255.5)\,\Omega = 274\,\Omega\,\angle{-68.6^\circ}\), \(I \approx 0.84\,\mathrm{A}\), current leads.
🚫Classic pitfall: adding magnitudes

\(|Z_1 + Z_2| \neq |Z_1| + |Z_2|\)! In the example above, \(100\,\Omega + 62.8\,\Omega + 318.3\,\Omega = 481\,\Omega\) is wrong — the true magnitude is 274 Ω, because the inductive and capacitive parts point in opposite imaginary directions and partially cancel. Always add impedances as complex numbers first, take the magnitude last. The same trap exists for power in Section 8: \(S \ne P + Q\).

7. Circuit analysis in the phasor domain — Module 04, upgraded

Here is the payoff, stated as a theorem: every DC analysis technique of Module 04 — Kirchhoff's laws, series/parallel reduction, voltage and current dividers, nodal analysis, Thévenin/Norton, superposition — remains valid verbatim if you replace real resistances by complex impedances and real values by phasors. The proofs carry over because all those techniques relied only on linearity, and complex arithmetic is linear.

The recipe for any steady-state AC problem:

  1. Transform: sinusoids → phasors, elements → impedances \(R,\; j\omega L,\; 1/(j\omega C)\) at the given ω.
  2. Solve with Module 04 machinery — now with complex numbers (Module 01 arithmetic: add in Cartesian form, multiply/divide in polar form).
  3. Transform back: \(\underline{U} = U e^{j\varphi}\) → \(u(t) = \sqrt{2}\,U \sin(\omega t + \varphi)\).

7.1 The complex voltage divider — and your first filter

The divider formula survives untouched:

\[ \frac{\underline{U}_2}{\underline{U}_{in}} = \frac{\underline{Z}_2}{\underline{Z}_1 + \underline{Z}_2}. \]

But something remarkable has happened: because impedances depend on ω, the divider ratio now depends on frequency. A humble two-component divider has become a filter. Take \(\underline{Z}_1 = R\) and \(\underline{Z}_2 = 1/(j\omega C)\) — the RC low-pass (Tiefpass):

\[ \underline{H}(j\omega) = \frac{\underline{U}_{out}}{\underline{U}_{in}} = \frac{1/(j\omega C)}{R + 1/(j\omega C)} = \frac{1}{1 + j\omega RC}. \]

At low ω: \(\underline{H} \to 1\) (capacitor ≈ open, everything reaches the output). At high ω: \(\underline{H} \to 0\) (capacitor ≈ short, output collapses). The boundary is the cutoff frequency (Grenzfrequenz) \(\omega_c = 1/(RC)\), where \(|\underline{H}| = 1/\sqrt{2}\). The full frequency-response story (Bode plots, decibels, cascading) is Module 06's opening act; here we nail one frequency by hand.

Worked example RC low-pass at one frequency, by complex divider

\(R = 1\,\mathrm{k\Omega}\), \(C = 100\,\mathrm{nF}\). The input is \(u_{in}(t)\) with RMS value 5 V at \(f = 1\,\mathrm{kHz}\). Find the output phasor.

Step 1 — capacitor impedance at ω = 2π·1000 ≈ 6283 s⁻¹: \( \underline{Z}_C = \frac{1}{j\,6283 \cdot 10^{-7}} = -j\,1592\,\Omega \).
Step 2 — divider (using ωRC = 6283 · 10⁻⁴ ≈ 0.628): \[ \underline{H} = \frac{1}{1 + j\,0.628}, \qquad |\underline{H}| = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 + 0.628^2}} \approx 0.847, \quad \varphi_H = -\arctan(0.628) \approx -32.1^\circ. \]
Step 3 — output: \( U_{out} = 0.847 \cdot 5 \approx 4.23\,\mathrm{V} \), lagging the input by 32.1°. In the time domain: \( u_{out}(t) = \sqrt{2}\cdot 4.23 \sin(6283\,t - 0.561) \,\mathrm{V} \).
Step 4 — sanity check: cutoff is \( f_c = \frac{1}{2\pi RC} \approx 1.59\,\mathrm{kHz} \); we drive below cutoff, so most (85 %) of the signal should pass ✓.
\(\underline{U}_{out} \approx 4.23\,\mathrm{V}\,\angle{-32.1^\circ}\) — one complex division replaced a differential equation.
🔑Key idea: one method, the whole curriculum

Phasor-domain analysis is arguably the highest-leverage technique in the EE curriculum. Filters (M06), transformer models (M07), amplifier frequency response (M09), transmission lines (M12), machine equivalent circuits (M17), grid load flow (M19) — all are "draw the circuit, insert impedances, crank the complex algebra". If Module 04's toolbox plus this module's transform sit firmly, roughly a third of the degree becomes routine.

8. Power in AC circuits — active, reactive, apparent

Multiply the instantaneous voltage and current — with a phase shift φ between them — and watch what the product does. With \(u(t) = \sqrt{2}\,U\sin(\omega t)\) and \(i(t) = \sqrt{2}\,I\sin(\omega t - \varphi)\):

\[ p(t) = u(t)\,i(t) = 2UI\sin(\omega t)\sin(\omega t - \varphi) \]

Using \(2\sin a \sin b = \cos(a-b) - \cos(a+b)\):

\[ \boxed{\;p(t) = \underbrace{UI\cos\varphi}_{\text{constant}} \;-\; \underbrace{UI\cos(2\omega t - \varphi)}_{\text{oscillates at }2\omega}\;} \]

Dissect this remarkable formula:

  • The power pulsates at twice the grid frequency — 100 Hz in Europe. (That is the hum of transformers and the flicker of cheap LED lamps.)
  • Its time average is \(P = UI\cos\varphi\) — the active power (Wirkleistung), in watts. Only this does net work: heat, light, torque.
  • If \(\varphi \neq 0\), \(p(t)\) dips negative twice per cycle: the load momentarily pushes energy back into the source. That returned energy sloshes back and forth, doing nothing useful — but the current carrying it is real and heats every wire it passes through.

The bookkeeping quantities for this sloshing:

\[ P = UI\cos\varphi \;\;[\mathrm{W}], \qquad Q = UI\sin\varphi \;\;[\mathrm{var}], \qquad S = UI \;\;[\mathrm{VA}], \qquad S^2 = P^2 + Q^2. \]

Reactive power \(Q\) (Blindleistung, unit volt-ampere-reactive) measures the oscillating, non-working exchange — positive for inductive loads (motors, transformers), negative for capacitive ones. Apparent power \(S\) (Scheinleistung, unit VA) is what the wiring actually experiences: the product of RMS voltage and RMS current, regardless of their timing. The ratio is the power factor (Leistungsfaktor) \(\cos\varphi = P/S\). Everything fits in one right triangle — and the elegant closed form is the complex power:

\[ \boxed{\;\underline{S} = \underline{U}\,\underline{I}^{\,*} = P + jQ\;} \]

The conjugate on \(\underline{I}\) is essential: it makes the angle of \(\underline{S}\) equal \(\varphi_u - \varphi_i = \varphi\), so inductive loads get \(Q > 0\) by convention. Without the star, the sign of Q would come out wrong.

8.1 Why utilities care about your cos φ

Suppose a factory needs \(P = 400\,\mathrm{kW}\) of real power. At \(\cos\varphi = 1\) the grid must deliver \(S = 400\,\mathrm{kVA}\); at \(\cos\varphi = 0.7\) it must deliver \(S = 571\,\mathrm{kVA}\) — 43 % more current through every transformer, cable and breaker between the power plant and the machine, and roughly double the \(I^2R\) line losses, for the same useful work. That is why utilities meter reactive energy for industrial customers and charge penalties, typically when the average \(\cos\varphi\) drops below about 0.9. Households are (so far) not billed for it — your washing machine motor's reactive power is a gift from the utility.

8.2 Power-factor correction — Blindleistungskompensation

The fix is elegant: inductive loads take \(Q > 0\), capacitors take \(Q < 0\) — so bolt a capacitor bank in parallel with the load and let the reactive power circulate locally between L and C instead of through 50 km of grid. The capacitor must supply the difference between the load's reactive power and the target:

\[ Q_C = P\,(\tan\varphi_1 - \tan\varphi_2), \qquad C = \frac{Q_C}{\omega U^2}, \]

where \(\varphi_1\) is the uncorrected and \(\varphi_2\) the target phase angle (the capacitor formula follows from \(Q_C = U^2/X_C = \omega C U^2\)).

Worked example Sizing a PFC capacitor for a motor

A single-phase induction motor draws \(P = 4\,\mathrm{kW}\) at \(\cos\varphi_1 = 0.70\) from 230 V / 50 Hz. Size the parallel capacitor to reach \(\cos\varphi_2 = 0.95\), and compare the line currents.

Step 1 — the two tangents: \(\varphi_1 = \arccos 0.70 = 45.6^\circ \Rightarrow \tan\varphi_1 = 1.020\); \(\quad\varphi_2 = \arccos 0.95 = 18.2^\circ \Rightarrow \tan\varphi_2 = 0.329\).
Step 2 — reactive power to compensate: \( Q_C = 4000\,(1.020 - 0.329) \approx 2.77\,\mathrm{kvar} \).
Step 3 — capacitance: \( C = \frac{Q_C}{\omega U^2} = \frac{2766}{314.16 \cdot 230^2} \approx 166\,\mathrm{\mu F} \) — a chunky motor-run capacitor, but standard.
Step 4 — payoff in current: before: \( I_1 = \frac{P}{U\cos\varphi_1} = \frac{4000}{230 \cdot 0.70} \approx 24.8\,\mathrm{A} \); after: \( I_2 = \frac{4000}{230 \cdot 0.95} \approx 18.3\,\mathrm{A} \).
\(C \approx 166\,\mathrm{\mu F}\) cuts the line current by 26 % — the motor works exactly as before, but the wiring, breaker and utility see a far friendlier load.
⚠️Don't overcompensate

Aiming for \(\cos\varphi = 1.00\) exactly is asking for trouble: if the motor is then unloaded or disconnected, the capacitor bank makes the site capacitive, which utilities dislike just as much, and the parallel LC combination can resonate with grid harmonics (Section 6's resonance, in the worst possible place). Practice targets 0.92–0.98 inductive, often with switched capacitor stages. Full compensation design lives in Module 19.

9. Three-phase systems — Drehstrom

Look again at the power widget: single-phase power pulsates at 100 Hz, dropping to zero twice per cycle. A motor fed this way delivers pulsating torque; a generator feels pulsating counter-torque. The fix, due largely to Dobrowolski and Tesla (~1890): generate three sinusoids, shifted by 120° each, on three conductors L1, L2, L3 (Außenleiter):

\[ u_1 = \hat{u}\sin(\omega t), \qquad u_2 = \hat{u}\sin(\omega t - 120^\circ), \qquad u_3 = \hat{u}\sin(\omega t - 240^\circ). \]

Three reasons this specific arrangement conquered the world:

  • Constant power. With balanced resistive loads, total instantaneous power is \(p(t) \propto \sin^2\!\omega t + \sin^2(\omega t - 120^\circ) + \sin^2(\omega t - 240^\circ) = \tfrac{3}{2}\) — constant. The 100 Hz pulsation of each phase cancels in the sum. Motors run smooth; generators load evenly.
  • The phasors sum to zero: \(\underline{U}_1 + \underline{U}_2 + \underline{U}_3 = 0\) (three unit vectors 120° apart close a triangle). Consequence: the return currents of three balanced loads cancel, and the return conductor can be thin — or absent. Three phases move triple power over three wires instead of six.
  • A rotating magnetic field for free. Three coils, 120° apart in space, fed with currents 120° apart in time, produce a magnetic field vector that physically rotates at ω. Put a piece of conductive metal in it and it spins: that is the induction motor, the workhorse of industry — built without brushes or contacts. Module 17 lives on this.

9.1 Star and delta — Stern- und Dreieckschaltung

Two vocabularies to keep apart (Stranggrößen vs. Leitergrößen):

  • Phase quantities \(U_{ph}, I_{ph}\): across/through one load or winding ("Strang").
  • Line quantities \(U_L, I_L\): between two lines / in one line conductor.

In a star, the line-to-line voltage is the phasor difference of two phase voltages. Two unit phasors 120° apart differ by a vector of length \(2\sin 60^\circ = \sqrt{3}\):

\[ \text{Star:}\quad U_L = \sqrt{3}\,U_{ph}, \quad I_L = I_{ph} \qquad\qquad \text{Delta:}\quad U_L = U_{ph}, \quad I_L = \sqrt{3}\,I_{ph}. \]

This is exactly the German low-voltage grid: phase voltage \(U_{ph} = 230\,\mathrm{V}\) (line to neutral — your socket), line voltage \(U_L = \sqrt{3} \cdot 230 \approx 400\,\mathrm{V}\) (line to line — your stove, heat pump, or workshop's "Starkstrom" outlet). The famous 230/400 V pair is one system described two ways, tied by \(\sqrt{3}\).

Balanced total power comes out identical for both connections when expressed in line quantities:

\[ P = 3\,U_{ph} I_{ph} \cos\varphi = \boxed{\;\sqrt{3}\; U_L\, I_L \cos\varphi\;} \qquad (\text{star: } 3 \cdot \tfrac{U_L}{\sqrt3} \cdot I_L = \sqrt3\,U_L I_L). \]
Worked example A three-phase motor's nameplate, decoded

An induction motor is rated \(P = 15\,\mathrm{kW}\) (mechanical ≈ electrical here for simplicity) at 400 V, \(\cos\varphi = 0.85\). Find the line current, apparent power, and reactive power.

Step 1 — line current: \( I_L = \frac{P}{\sqrt{3}\,U_L \cos\varphi} = \frac{15\,000}{1.732 \cdot 400 \cdot 0.85} \approx 25.5\,\mathrm{A} \).
Step 2 — apparent power: \( S = \sqrt{3}\,U_L I_L = 1.732 \cdot 400 \cdot 25.5 \approx 17.6\,\mathrm{kVA} \).
Step 3 — reactive power: \( Q = \sqrt{S^2 - P^2} = \sqrt{17.6^2 - 15^2} \approx 9.3\,\mathrm{kvar} \) (inductive, as for any induction motor).
\(I_L \approx 25.5\,\mathrm{A}\) per line — which is why this motor gets a 32 A three-phase breaker, and why its feed cable has 4–5 conductors but each is modest in cross-section.

9.2 The neutral conductor — hero of imbalance

In a balanced star system, the three load currents are equal in magnitude and 120° apart, so their phasor sum — the current returning through the neutral (Neutralleiter) — is exactly zero. The neutral is then redundant. Real buildings, however, are never perfectly balanced: apartments hang single-phase loads on L1, L2, L3 semi-randomly. The neutral carries the imbalance:

\[ \underline{I}_N = \underline{I}_1 + \underline{I}_2 + \underline{I}_3. \]

Lose the neutral in an unbalanced star (a classic, dangerous fault called Nullleiterbruch) and the star point drifts: lightly loaded phases see far more than 230 V — appliances burn out. This is why the neutral must never be fused or switched alone.

Balanced (slider at 100 %), the sum is a flat line at zero — three currents, no return current. 11. Exercises

Phasor fluency is finger memory — do these with a pencil and a polar–Cartesian-capable calculator before peeking.

E1The North American grid runs at 120 V (RMS) / 60 Hz. Find the peak voltage, the angular frequency, and the period. easy

Show solution
\( \hat{u} = \sqrt{2}\cdot 120 \approx 170\,\mathrm{V} \); \( \omega = 2\pi\cdot 60 \approx 377\,\mathrm{s^{-1}} \); \( T = 1/60 \approx 16.7\,\mathrm{ms} \). Sanity check: the US peak (170 V) is barely half the European one (325 V) — one reason US wiring uses larger currents for the same power, and why 120 V shocks are somewhat more survivable.

E2A \(2.2\,\mathrm{\mu F}\) capacitor is connected to 230 V. Find its reactance and current at 50 Hz, and its reactance at 5 kHz. easy

Show solution
At 50 Hz: \( X_C = \frac{1}{\omega C} = \frac{1}{314.16 \cdot 2.2\cdot 10^{-6}} \approx 1447\,\Omega \) → \( I = 230/1447 \approx 0.16\,\mathrm{A} \), leading the voltage by 90°. At 5 kHz (ω = 31 416 s⁻¹): \( X_C \approx 14.5\,\Omega \) — a hundred times smaller, because \(X_C \propto 1/f\). This 1/f behavior is why capacitors "short out" high-frequency noise in every power supply (decoupling capacitors, Module 08/10).

E3A coil with \(R = 40\,\Omega\) and \(L = 95.5\,\mathrm{mH}\) (its winding resistance and inductance in series) hangs on the 230 V / 50 Hz grid. Find \(\underline{Z}\), the current, cos φ, and the active power. medium

Show solution
\( X_L = \omega L = 314.16 \cdot 0.0955 \approx 30\,\Omega \), so \( \underline{Z} = (40 + j30)\,\Omega = 50\,\Omega\,\angle 36.9^\circ \) (the 3-4-5 triangle!). \( I = 230/50 = 4.6\,\mathrm{A} \), lagging by 36.9°; \( \cos\varphi = R/Z = 40/50 = 0.8 \). \( P = UI\cos\varphi = 230 \cdot 4.6 \cdot 0.8 \approx 846\,\mathrm{W} \). Cross-check: \( P = I^2 R = 4.6^2 \cdot 40 = 846\,\mathrm{W} \) ✓ — only the resistance dissipates; the inductance's 635 var just slosh.

E4Derive the RMS value of a symmetric triangle wave with peak \(\hat{u}\), and confirm the table entry \(\hat{u}/\sqrt{3}\). medium

Show solution
By symmetry, integrate over the first quarter period, where the wave is the ramp \( u(t) = \hat{u}\,\frac{t}{T/4} \): \[ U^2 = \frac{1}{T/4}\int_0^{T/4} \hat{u}^2 \frac{t^2}{(T/4)^2}\,\mathrm{d}t = \frac{\hat{u}^2}{(T/4)^3}\cdot\frac{(T/4)^3}{3} = \frac{\hat{u}^2}{3}. \] So \( U = \hat{u}/\sqrt{3} \approx 0.577\,\hat{u} \) ✓. The triangle spends more time near zero than the sine does, so its RMS is lower (0.577 < 0.707) — squaring punishes time spent at small values.

E5Design an RC low-pass with cutoff \(f_c = 1\,\mathrm{kHz}\) using \(R = 3.3\,\mathrm{k\Omega}\). Then compute the magnitude of the transfer function at 10 kHz. medium

Show solution
From \( f_c = \frac{1}{2\pi RC} \): \( C = \frac{1}{2\pi \cdot 3300 \cdot 1000} \approx 48\,\mathrm{nF} \) (pick the standard value 47 nF; \(f_c\) shifts to 1.03 kHz — fine). At 10 kHz, \( \omega RC = f/f_c = 10 \), so \( |\underline{H}| = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 + 10^2}} \approx 0.0995 \approx 1/10 \). A decade above cutoff, a first-order low-pass attenuates by a factor ≈ 10 (−20 dB) — the "20 dB per decade" slope you will meet formally in Module 06.

E6A workshop's single-phase machine park draws \(P = 11\,\mathrm{kW}\) at \(\cos\varphi_1 = 0.65\) from 230 V / 50 Hz, and the utility demands at least 0.92. Derive the correction formula \(Q_C = P(\tan\varphi_1 - \tan\varphi_2)\) from the power triangle, then size the capacitor. challenge

Show solution
Derivation: Active power P is untouched by a parallel capacitor (it draws ideally none). Before correction the load's reactive power is \( Q_1 = P\tan\varphi_1 \) (from the triangle: \(Q = P \tan\varphi\)); the target state needs \( Q_2 = P\tan\varphi_2 \). The capacitor must absorb the difference: \( Q_C = Q_1 - Q_2 = P(\tan\varphi_1 - \tan\varphi_2) \). With \( Q_C = U^2/X_C = \omega C U^2 \) follows \( C = Q_C/(\omega U^2) \).

Numbers: \( \tan(\arccos 0.65) = 1.169 \), \( \tan(\arccos 0.92) = 0.426 \). \( Q_C = 11\,000\,(1.169 - 0.426) \approx 8.2\,\mathrm{kvar} \); \( C = \frac{8170}{314.16 \cdot 230^2} \approx 490\,\mathrm{\mu F} \). Sanity check: line current falls from \( \frac{11000}{230\cdot0.65} \approx 74\,\mathrm{A} \) to \( \frac{11000}{230\cdot0.92} \approx 52\,\mathrm{A} \) — 30 % less copper heating. (Real installations this size would use a switched 400 V three-phase capacitor bank, but the physics is identical.)

E7An unbalanced star on 230/400 V feeds purely resistive loads: \(I_1 = 10\,\mathrm{A}\), \(I_2 = 8\,\mathrm{A}\), \(I_3 = 4\,\mathrm{A}\) (each in phase with its own phase voltage). Compute the neutral current as a phasor and its magnitude. challenge

Show solution
Take \(\underline{U}_1\) as reference; the currents are then \( \underline{I}_1 = 10\angle 0^\circ \), \( \underline{I}_2 = 8\angle{-120^\circ} \), \( \underline{I}_3 = 4\angle{+120^\circ} \) (A). Convert to Cartesian: \[ \underline{I}_N = 10 + 8\bigl(-\tfrac12 - j\tfrac{\sqrt3}{2}\bigr) + 4\bigl(-\tfrac12 + j\tfrac{\sqrt3}{2}\bigr) = (10 - 4 - 2) + j\,\tfrac{\sqrt3}{2}(4 - 8) = 4 - j\,3.46\,\mathrm{A}. \] Magnitude: \( I_N = \sqrt{16 + 12} = \sqrt{28} \approx 5.3\,\mathrm{A} \), at −40.9°. Sanity checks: were the loads balanced (10/10/10), the sum would vanish; and \(I_N\) (5.3 A) is smaller than the largest imbalance suggests naively (10−4 = 6 A would be the two-phase-only intuition) because the three directions partially cancel. Note \(I_N\) is not \(10-8-4\) — phasors, never bare magnitudes.
