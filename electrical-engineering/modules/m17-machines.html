🏭 Power Engineering · Module 17

Electrical Machines & Drives · Elektrische Maschinen & Antriebe

Roughly two thirds of all industrial electricity on Earth ends its life inside an electric motor, and essentially all of it began its life inside a generator — which is the same machine running backwards. This module builds the four classical machines (transformer, induction, synchronous, DC) from two laws you already own, then adds the power electronics that turned them from constant-speed workhorses into the precision actuators inside every EV, robot and wind turbine.

Corresponds to: Elektrische Maschinen und Antriebe (4th–5th semester) Prerequisites: Modules 05, 07 Study time: ≈ 7–9 h

1. From two laws to every machine

Module 07 gave you two facts about a conductor of length \(l\) in a magnetic field \(B\):

\[ F = B\,I\,l \qquad \text{(force on a current-carrying conductor)} \] \[ u_{\text{ind}} = B\,l\,v \qquad \text{(voltage induced in a moving conductor)} \]

Every rotating machine in this module is an industrial-scale packaging of these two equations. And here is the point students often miss: they always act together. Push current through a conductor in a field and it moves (motor); but the moment it moves, it induces a back-voltage that opposes the current. Drag a conductor through a field and it generates voltage (generator); but the moment you draw current, a force appears that opposes the dragging. The product tells the story of energy conversion:

\[ P_{\text{el}} = u_{\text{ind}}\, I = (Blv)\,I = (BIl)\,v = F\,v = P_{\text{mech}}. \]

Electrical power in equals mechanical power out (before losses) — the conductor in the field is a lossless power converter, and the direction of power flow is set purely by the operating point.

🔑Key idea: motor and generator are the same machine

There is no constructional difference between an electric motor and a generator (Motor / Generator). Every machine in this module runs in both directions of power flow. Your EV proves it several times per minute: accelerator down — the PMSM is a motor; brake pedal — the same machine becomes a generator and recharges the battery. Which mode you are in is a question of operating point, not of hardware.

2. The ideal transformer — induction without motion

Before anything rotates, meet the machine with no moving parts at all. Wind two coils, \(N_1\) and \(N_2\) turns, on a common iron core. The core guides essentially all the flux \(\Phi\) through both windings. Faraday's law (Module 07) applied to each winding:

\[ u_1 = N_1 \frac{\mathrm{d}\Phi}{\mathrm{d}t}, \qquad u_2 = N_2 \frac{\mathrm{d}\Phi}{\mathrm{d}t}. \]

Same \(\mathrm{d}\Phi/\mathrm{d}t\) in both — so dividing the equations eliminates it:

\[ \boxed{\;\frac{u_1}{u_2} = \frac{N_1}{N_2} = \ddot{u}\;} \]

with the turns ratio \(\ddot{u}\) (Übersetzungsverhältnis — yes, German notation really uses ü). An ideal transformer is lossless, so \(u_1 i_1 = u_2 i_2\), giving the current relation and, in one more step, the famous impedance transformation:

\[ \frac{i_1}{i_2} = \frac{N_2}{N_1}, \qquad Z_1 = \ddot{u}^2\, Z_2 . \]
💡Intuition: a gearbox for electricity

A mechanical gearbox trades speed for torque at constant power; a transformer (Transformator) trades voltage for current at constant power. The turns ratio is the gear ratio. And just as a gearbox lets a small fast engine drive a heavy slow wheel, the transformer lets a 400 kV transmission line feed your 230 V wall socket — the single trick that decided the "War of Currents" in favor of AC.

Note what the derivation required: \(\mathrm{d}\Phi/\mathrm{d}t \neq 0\). A transformer only works with changing flux — connect it to DC and it is just a heater (a very brief one: only the small winding resistance limits the current). This is why the transformer is an AC-only device, and why DC grids had no cheap way to change voltage levels in 1890.

3. The real transformer — equivalent circuit and how to measure it

Real transformers deviate from the ideal in four physically distinct ways, and each one becomes an element of the standard equivalent circuit (Ersatzschaltbild). Secondary quantities are referred to the primary side (denoted with a prime: \(R_2' = \ddot{u}^2 R_2\), \(u_2' = \ddot{u}\, u_2\)):

  • \(R_1,\; R_2'\) — the windings are copper wire with resistance: copper losses \(\propto I^2\).
  • \(X_{1\sigma},\; X_{2\sigma}'\) — leakage reactances (Streureaktanzen): a few percent of the flux takes shortcuts through air and links only one winding, acting like a small series inductance.
  • \(X_h\) — the magnetizing reactance (Hauptreaktanz): the core has finite permeability, so a (small) magnetizing current must flow to establish \(\Phi\) even with no load.
  • \(R_{Fe}\) — a fictitious parallel resistor representing iron losses (hysteresis + eddy currents), which flow whenever the core is magnetized, load or no load.

3.1 No-load and short-circuit test — extracting the parameters

The elegance of this circuit is that two cheap measurements separate all its parameters, because each test switches off one half of the physics:

TestSetupWhat dominatesYou obtain
No-load (Leerlaufversuch)Rated voltage on one winding, other winding openOnly tiny \(I_0\) flows → series drops negligible; full flux in core\(P_0 \approx P_{Fe}\) (iron losses), \(I_0\) → \(R_{Fe},\, X_h\)
Short-circuit (Kurzschlussversuch)Secondary shorted; primary voltage raised from zero until rated current flowsNeeded voltage \(U_{sc}\) is only 4–12 % of rated → core barely magnetized, iron losses negligible\(P_{sc} \approx P_{Cu}\) (copper losses), \(u_k = U_{sc}/U_N\) → \(R_1{+}R_2',\; X_\sigma\)

The relative short-circuit voltage \(u_k\) (relative Kurzschlussspannung) is stamped on every transformer nameplate (typically 4–6 % for distribution units, 10–15 % for large grid transformers). It is simultaneously (a) the series impedance in per-unit, (b) the key to voltage regulation, and (c) the inverse of the worst-case fault current: a transformer with \(u_k = 5\,\%\) delivers \(1/0.05 = 20\times\) rated current into a bolted short — the number your circuit breakers (Module 19) must be sized for.

Voltage regulation (Spannungsänderung): under load, the series impedance drops voltage. Splitting \(u_k\) into its resistive part \(u_R\) and reactive part \(u_X\), Kapp's approximation gives the relative sag at load fraction \(x\) and power factor \(\cos\varphi\):

\[ \Delta u \approx x\,(u_R \cos\varphi + u_X \sin\varphi). \]

Inductive loads (\(\sin\varphi > 0\)) sag the most; capacitive loads can even raise the secondary voltage — a genuine effect used (and feared) in grid operation.

4. Losses, efficiency — and why the AC grid exists

4.1 Iron vs. copper: two losses, two behaviors

Iron losses \(P_{Fe}\) (Eisenverluste) occur in the magnetized core, independent of load:

  • Hysteresis loss \(\propto f \cdot \hat{B}^{\,\approx 2}\): each cycle around the B–H loop dissipates the loop's area (Module 07). Cure: soft magnetic silicon steel with a skinny loop.
  • Eddy-current loss \(\propto f^2 \hat{B}^2 d^2\): the core is itself a conductor sitting in changing flux, so ring currents circulate in it. Cure: slice the core into thin insulated laminations (Blechung, 0.23–0.35 mm) perpendicular to the eddy paths — \(d\) shrinks, loss collapses quadratically. This is why every transformer and machine core you ever open is a stack of sheets, never a solid block.

Copper losses \(P_{Cu} = I^2 (R_1 + R_2')\) (Kupferverluste / Stromwärmeverluste) grow with the square of load current: \(P_{Cu}(x) = x^2 P_{Cu,N}\).

Efficiency at load fraction \(x\) and power factor \(\cos\varphi\), with rated apparent power \(S_N\):

\[ \eta(x) = \frac{x\,S_N \cos\varphi}{x\,S_N \cos\varphi + P_{Fe} + x^2 P_{Cu,N}} , \qquad \eta_{\max} \text{ at } x_{opt} = \sqrt{\frac{P_{Fe}}{P_{Cu,N}}} . \]

Maximum efficiency occurs where the constant loss equals the load-dependent loss — a pattern you will meet again in power electronics and even in economics. Distribution transformers idle most of the day, so they are deliberately designed with low \(P_{Fe}\), putting \(x_{opt}\) around 40–50 % load.

Worked example Efficiency of a 50 kVA distribution transformer

Nameplate: \(S_N = 50\,\mathrm{kVA}\), \(P_0 = 200\,\mathrm{W}\) (no-load test), \(P_{sc} = 1000\,\mathrm{W}\) at rated current (short-circuit test). Load: \(x = 1\) and \(x = x_{opt}\), both at \(\cos\varphi = 0.9\).

Step 1 — full load: output \(= 1 \cdot 50\,\mathrm{kVA} \cdot 0.9 = 45\,\mathrm{kW}\); losses \(= 200 + 1^2 \cdot 1000 = 1200\,\mathrm{W}\). \( \eta = \frac{45\,000}{46\,200} = 97.4\,\% \).
Step 2 — optimal load point: \( x_{opt} = \sqrt{200/1000} = 0.447 \). Output \(= 0.447 \cdot 45 = 20.1\,\mathrm{kW}\); losses \(= 200 + 0.447^2 \cdot 1000 = 400\,\mathrm{W}\) (note: exactly \(2 P_{Fe}\)). \( \eta_{\max} = \frac{20\,100}{20\,500} = 98.0\,\% \).
η ≈ 97.4 % at full load, peaking at 98.0 % near 45 % load — right where a residential transformer spends its day. Large grid transformers exceed 99.5 %: electrical machines are among the most efficient devices humanity builds.

4.2 The killer application: high-voltage transmission

Worked example Transmitting 100 MW at 400 V vs. 400 kV

A city needs \(P = 100\,\mathrm{MW}\) from a plant 100 km away. The three-phase line has \(R = 5\,\Omega\) per conductor (≈ 0.05 Ω/km, a realistic overhead line). Compare the line losses at transmission voltages of 400 V and 400 kV (line-to-line, \(\cos\varphi = 1\)).

Step 1 — current follows from power: \( I = \dfrac{P}{\sqrt{3}\, U} \). At 400 kV: \( I = \dfrac{100 \cdot 10^6}{\sqrt{3} \cdot 400\,000} \approx 144\,\mathrm{A} \). At 400 V: \( I \approx 144\,000\,\mathrm{A} \) — a thousand times more.
Step 2 — losses in the three conductors: \( P_{loss} = 3 I^2 R \). At 400 kV: \( 3 \cdot 144^2 \cdot 5 \approx 312\,\mathrm{kW} \) — just 0.3 % of the shipment. At 400 V: \( 3 \cdot (144\,000)^2 \cdot 5 \approx 312\,\mathrm{GW} \) — 3000 times the power we tried to send. The line would vaporize; physically, the city simply cannot be supplied.
Step 3 — the scaling law: at fixed power, \( P_{loss} \propto I^2 \propto 1/U^2 \). Raising the voltage 1000× cuts losses 10⁶×.
Loss ratio \((400\,\mathrm{kV}/400\,\mathrm{V})^2 = 10^6\). This is why the grid is AC: only the transformer could move power up to 400 kV for the journey and back down to 400 V for your socket — cheaply, passively, at 99+ % efficiency. Edison's DC had no such ladder in 1890 (modern HVDC does, using power electronics — Module 19).
📜1891: Lauffen → Frankfurt, the demo that built the modern world

At the Frankfurt Electrotechnical Exhibition, Oskar von Miller, Michail Dolivo-Dobrowolsky (AEG) and Charles Brown transmitted ~200 kW of three-phase power 176 km from a waterfall in Lauffen am Neckar to Frankfurt at 15 kV (stepped up from 55 V!), lighting a thousand bulbs and driving a pump for an artificial waterfall — at ~75 % overall efficiency, when skeptics predicted <50 %. Within a decade, three-phase AC with transformers became the world standard. Dolivo-Dobrowolsky had also just invented the squirrel-cage induction motor (Section 6) — the exhibition showcased the entire architecture of today's grid.

4.3 Three-phase transformers, vector groups, inrush

Three-phase transformers put three wound limbs on one core. Each side can be connected in star (Y) or delta (D), and the combination shifts the phase between primary and secondary in multiples of 30°. The vector group (Schaltgruppe) code, e.g. Dyn5, reads: primary Delta, secondary star (y) with neutral brought out, secondary lagging by \(5 \times 30° = 150°\). The classic European distribution transformer is Dyn5: the delta primary suppresses harmful third-harmonic currents, the star secondary provides the 230/400 V neutral. Rule of grid life: only transformers of compatible vector groups may be paralleled — a 30° mismatch is a permanent short circuit.

Inrush (Einschaltstromstoß): energize a transformer at the wrong instant (voltage near zero) and the flux, which must start from its remanent value rather than the steady-state offset, can transiently reach ~2× rated — deep into saturation. The magnetizing current then spikes to 5–10× rated current for several cycles. Harmless to the transformer, but protection relays must be designed not to trip on it.

5. The rotating field — the idea that runs the world

Now to rotation. The central trick of all AC machines is this: three stationary coils can create a rotating magnet — no moving parts, nothing spinning, yet a magnetic field that revolves at precisely controlled speed. This is the rotating field (Drehfeld), and it deserves a careful derivation.

Place three identical windings in the stator (Ständer), their magnetic axes 120° apart in space, and feed them the three-phase currents of Module 05, 120° apart in time:

\[ i_a = \hat{I}\cos(\omega t), \qquad i_b = \hat{I}\cos(\omega t - 120°), \qquad i_c = \hat{I}\cos(\omega t - 240°). \]

Each winding alone produces a pulsating MMF along its fixed axis, proportional to its current. Represent each axis as a unit phasor in the cross-section plane: \(1,\ e^{j120°},\ e^{j240°}\). The total MMF vector is

\[ \underline{F}(t) \;\propto\; i_a \cdot 1 + i_b\, e^{j120°} + i_c\, e^{j240°}. \]

Substitute \(\cos x = \tfrac12 (e^{jx} + e^{-jx})\) and collect terms. Each phase contributes a co-rotating part \(\tfrac12 e^{j\omega t}\) and a counter-rotating part; for phase b the counter-rotating part is \(\tfrac12 e^{-j(\omega t - 120°)} e^{j120°} = \tfrac12 e^{-j\omega t} e^{j240°}\), and similarly for c. The three counter-rotating parts are 120° apart — they sum to zero. The three co-rotating parts are all in phase and add:

\[ \boxed{\;\underline{F}(t) \;\propto\; \tfrac{3}{2}\, \hat{I}\, e^{j\omega t}\;} \]

A vector of constant magnitude \( \tfrac32 \hat I \), rotating at exactly the supply angular frequency ω. Three pulsating fields conspire into one perfectly smooth rotation. Swap any two supply leads and the sequence reverses — the field (and every AC motor) runs backwards. That is literally how you reverse a three-phase motor: exchange two wires.

💡Intuition: three friends pushing a merry-go-round

One person pushing a merry-go-round from a fixed spot delivers pulsating, jerky torque. Three people spaced 120° around it, each pushing in turn with sinusoidally rising and falling effort, timed 120° apart — the merry-go-round feels one perfectly constant push travelling around it. Nobody moves from their spot; the push rotates. That is the Drehfeld, and it is also why three-phase power is constant in time (Module 05) while single-phase power pulsates.

5.1 Pole pairs and synchronous speed

Repeat the winding pattern \(p\) times around the circumference and the field advances only \(1/p\) of a revolution per electrical cycle. With \(p\) pole pairs (Polpaarzahl), the field's mechanical speed — the synchronous speed (synchrone Drehzahl) — is

\[ \boxed{\; n_s = \frac{f}{p} = \frac{60 f}{p}\ \mathrm{rpm} \;} \]
Worked example The synchronous-speed menu at 50 Hz

A machine on the European 50 Hz grid can only choose its field speed from a discrete menu set by \(p\). Compute it.

Step 1 — apply \(n_s = 60 f / p\): with \(f = 50\,\mathrm{Hz}\): \(n_s = 3000/p\) rpm.

6. The induction machine — the world's workhorse

Put a shorted conductor cage inside the rotating field and something wonderful happens. The induction machine (Asynchronmaschine) has the simplest rotor imaginable: aluminum or copper bars, short-circuited by end rings — the squirrel cage (Käfigläufer). No windings to a power source, no brushes, no magnets. The chain of cause and effect:

  1. The stator field rotates at \(n_s\); suppose the rotor turns slower, at \(n\).
  2. Relative to the rotor, the field still moves — so it induces voltages in the bars (\(u = Blv\), with \(v\) the relative speed).
  3. The bars are shorted → currents flow.
  4. Currents in a field → force (\(F = BIl\)) → torque, pulling the rotor along with the field.

The rotor can never quite catch up: at \(n = n_s\) the relative motion vanishes, induction stops, currents and torque collapse. The machine needs to lag — asynchronously. The lag is the slip (Schlupf):

\[ \boxed{\; s = \frac{n_s - n}{n_s} \;} \qquad \text{rotor frequency: } f_2 = s \cdot f_1 . \]

At standstill \(s = 1\) (rotor sees full 50 Hz — the machine is momentarily just a shorted transformer); at rated load \(s \approx 0.02\ldots0.06\); at synchronism \(s = 0\), torque zero.

Worked example Reading a nameplate: slip and rotor frequency

A 4-pole (p = 2), 50 Hz induction motor's nameplate says 1440 rpm at rated load. Find slip, rotor frequency, and the speed of the rotor's own field.

Step 1 — synchronous speed: \( n_s = 60 \cdot 50 / 2 = 1500\,\mathrm{rpm} \).
Step 2 — slip: \( s = (1500 - 1440)/1500 = 0.04 = 4\,\% \).
Step 3 — rotor frequency: \( f_2 = s f_1 = 0.04 \cdot 50 = 2\,\mathrm{Hz} \). The rotor currents oscillate at just 2 Hz.
Step 4 — the beautiful consistency check: the rotor's current field rotates at \(s \cdot n_s = 60\,\mathrm{rpm}\) relative to the rotor, which itself turns at 1440 rpm — so relative to the stator: \(1440 + 60 = 1500\,\mathrm{rpm}\). Rotor field and stator field rotate in exact lockstep, at every load. That is why a steady torque is possible at all.
s = 4 %, f₂ = 2 Hz. Nameplate speeds just below a menu value (1440 → 1500, 2890 → 3000) are the fingerprint of an induction machine.

6.1 The torque–slip characteristic and Kloss's formula

Solving the machine's equivalent circuit (structurally the transformer's, with the rotor branch resistance \(R_2'/s\) — the slip-dependence encodes the mechanical load) yields the torque–slip curve, compactly written as Kloss's formula (Kloßsche Formel):

\[ \boxed{\;\frac{T}{T_k} = \frac{2}{\dfrac{s}{s_k} + \dfrac{s_k}{s}}\;} \]

with breakdown torque \(T_k\) (Kippmoment) at breakdown slip \(s_k\) (typically 0.15–0.3). Anatomy of the curve:

  • Small slip (\(s \ll s_k\)): \(T \approx 2 T_k s / s_k\) — linear. This is the steep, stable operating region: load increases → rotor slows slightly → torque rises to match. The machine self-regulates like a spring.
  • \(s = s_k\): maximum torque \(T_k\), typically 2–3× rated. Load beyond this and the motor stalls (kippt — tips over).
  • Large slip (\(s \gg s_k\)): \(T \approx 2 T_k s_k / s\) — falling. Starting torque at \(s=1\) is modest despite huge current.
  • Regions: \(0 < s < 1\) motor · \(s < 0\) (driven faster than \(n_s\)) generator — this is how most wind turbines of the 1990s fed the grid · \(s > 1\) (field vs. rotation opposed) brake (Gegenstrombremsung, plugging).

Since torque comes from \(B \cdot I\) and both scale with stator voltage, \(T_k \propto U^2\) — a 10 % undervoltage costs 19 % of breakdown torque, a classic cause of stalled motors on weak grids.

6.2 The starting problem

At switch-on, \(s = 1\): the cage looks like a shorted transformer secondary and draws 5–8× rated current while producing only ~1–1.5× rated torque. For big motors on weak grids this current is unacceptable. Classical remedies:

  • Star–delta starting (Stern-Dreieck-Anlauf): start with windings in star — each sees \(U/\sqrt{3}\) — then switch to delta. Current from the grid drops to 1/3… but so does torque (∝ U²). Only works for loads that start nearly unloaded (fans, saws).
  • Soft starter: thyristors ramp the voltage up smoothly. Same torque penalty, but no switching jolt.
  • Rotor resistance (slip-ring machines, Schleifringläufer): external resistors shift \(s_k\) toward 1 — full torque at start, resistors shorted once running. Elegant, but reintroduces brushes; now largely historical.
  • Frequency converter (Section 9): start at low frequency, keeping slip small throughout — full torque at rated current. The modern answer to everything.
🚫Classic pitfall: "star–delta reduces the current, great, no downsides"

Exam favorite: star–delta reduces line current to 1/3 — and torque to 1/3 as well, since each winding voltage falls by \(1/\sqrt3\) and \(T \propto U^2\): \((1/\sqrt3)^2 = 1/3\). Start a loaded conveyor in star and it simply won't turn; worse, the delta switchover under high slip produces a current spike that can exceed direct-on-line starting. Also: don't confuse \(n_s\) (field, e.g. 1500 rpm) with rated speed (rotor, e.g. 1440 rpm) — an induction motor listed "1500 rpm" would produce zero torque at exactly 1500.

Single-phase induction motors (washing machine, older fridge compressors): one winding alone makes a pulsating field — decomposable into two counter-rotating fields whose torques cancel at standstill. Such a motor hums but cannot decide a direction. Cure: an auxiliary winding fed through a capacitor fakes a second phase, creating an (elliptical) rotating field for starting. Three-phase machines need no such crutch — another gift of the Drehfeld.

🔑Why the induction machine rules industry

No brushes, no magnets, no electrical connection to the rotor whatsoever — just an aluminum cage die-cast into iron. It cannot lose excitation, barely wears, survives dirt, overload and abuse, and costs less per kW than any other machine. The price: speed chained to the grid frequency (until inverters, Section 9) and a mediocre power factor (it draws magnetizing reactive power from the grid — Module 05's cosφ penalty in the flesh). An estimated ~90 % of industrial motors are squirrel-cage induction machines.

7. The synchronous machine — the grid's backbone

Replace the cage with a rotor that carries its own DC-excited field winding (Erregerwicklung) — or permanent magnets — and the physics changes character. The rotor is now a magnet; it does not need slip to carry current. It locks magnetically to the rotating field and turns at exactly \(n_s\): the synchronous machine (Synchronmaschine).

Torque now comes from the angular offset between the rotor's field axis and the stator field axis: the load angle \(\delta\) (Polradwinkel). Think of rotor and field as coupled by a magnetic torsion spring: no load → they align, \(\delta = 0\); mechanical load → the rotor falls back by \(\delta\), and the twisted "spring" transmits torque. From the phasor diagram (round-rotor machine, per phase: internal EMF \(E\), terminal voltage \(U\), synchronous reactance \(X_d\)):

\[ \boxed{\; P = \frac{3\, U E}{X_d} \sin\delta \;} \]
  • \(\delta > 0\) (rotor ahead): generator, power flows to the grid. \(\delta < 0\): motor.
  • Power grows with \(\sin\delta\) up to the static stability limit \(\delta = 90°\). Push a generator beyond it and the magnetic spring "slips a tooth" — the machine pole-slips (außer Tritt fallen), a violent fault. Grid operators keep \(\delta\) at 20–30° for margin; the whole discipline of grid stability (Module 19) is about keeping thousands of these coupled rotors in step.
  • Bonus: the DC excitation is a free knob. Overexcite → the machine delivers reactive power; underexcite → absorbs it. Synchronous generators are the grid's voltage regulators, not just its power sources.

Grid synchronization (Synchronisation): before connecting a synchronous generator to the live grid you must match four things — voltage magnitude, frequency, phase sequence, and phase angle. Classically checked with the dark-lamp method (lamps across the breaker go dark when all match) or a synchroscope; today an automatic synchronizer closes the breaker within a fraction of a degree. Connect misaligned and the grid — effectively an infinite machine — slams the rotor into step with a torque transient that can shear the shaft.

Historically, essentially all classical grid power — coal, gas, nuclear, hydro — has come from synchronous generators, and their spinning mass provides the inertia that stabilizes grid frequency second-to-second. Meanwhile the permanent-magnet synchronous machine (PMSM) (permanenterregte Synchronmaschine), fed by an inverter that generates its own rotating field at any frequency, has become the premium traction motor: no rotor losses (no cage currents, no excitation), highest efficiency and torque density — the machine inside most EVs, from Zoe to Model 3 (rear), usually with rotor magnets buried in V-shapes (IPM) for extra reluctance torque and field-weakening capability.

8. The DC machine — the grand old lady

The oldest machine still deserves study, because inverter-fed AC drives are controlled to imitate it. Construction: a stationary field (wound poles or magnets) creates a fixed flux \(\Phi\); the rotor (Anker, armature) carries a winding whose current must always flow left under the N pole, right under the S pole to keep torque one-way. Since the rotor turns, each conductor alternately passes N and S — its current must alternate. The component that performs this switching is the commutator (Kommutator / Stromwender): copper segments on the shaft, wiping past stationary carbon brushes.

💡Intuition: the commutator is a mechanical inverter

Inside the rotor winding, the current is AC (frequency = rotation speed). At the terminals, it is DC. The commutator converts between them — it is a rotary power-electronic converter built from copper and carbon, invented half a century before the transistor. Modern "brushless DC" machines simply replace it with six transistors and a position sensor: same job, no wear, no sparks. Once you see this, the entire machine zoo becomes one family: every machine needs AC in its windings; they differ only in what does the inverting — the grid (induction, synchronous), a commutator (DC), or a transistor bridge (BLDC/PMSM).

8.1 Two equations describe everything

With machine constant \(k\) (geometry, turns) and flux \(\Phi\):

\[ \boxed{\; T = k\,\Phi\, I_a \;} \qquad \boxed{\; U = k\,\Phi\,\omega + R_a I_a \;} \]

Torque proportional to current; terminal voltage = back-EMF plus resistive drop. Rearranged, the speed–torque line:

\[ \omega = \frac{U}{k\Phi} - \frac{R_a}{(k\Phi)^2}\, T \]

— a straight, gently drooping line. Two independent control knobs fall out immediately:

  • Armature voltage control (\(U \downarrow\uparrow\)): shifts the line vertically. Speed ∝ voltage, full torque available at every speed — the constant-torque region below base speed.
  • Field weakening (Feldschwächung, \(\Phi \downarrow\)): with \(U\) at maximum, reducing flux raises the no-load speed \(U/k\Phi\) — speeds above base speed. But \(T = k\Phi I_a\): less flux means less torque per ampere, so this is the constant-power region. Every EV uses exactly this strategy (electronically) above ~50 km/h.

Characteristics by field connection: the shunt/separately-excited machine (Nebenschlussmaschine, Φ constant) gives the stiff, nearly constant-speed line above — the precision drive of the pre-inverter era. The series machine (Reihenschlussmaschine, field carries \(I_a\), so \(\Phi \propto I_a\) and \(T \propto I_a^2\)): brutal starting torque, speed soaring dangerously at no load — the classic traction and starter motor; its AC-tolerant sibling, the universal motor, still screams away at 20 000+ rpm in your drill and vacuum cleaner. Beyond those niches (plus automotive 12 V auxiliaries and precision legacy servos), brushed DC has been displaced by inverter-fed AC — the commutator's wear, sparking and maintenance lost to six silent transistors.

9. Drives — power electronics sets the machines free

9.1 Why variable speed is an energy revolution

Pumps and fans obey affinity laws: flow \(\propto n\), pressure \(\propto n^2\), hence power

\[ P \propto n^3 . \]

Run a pump at 80 % speed and it consumes ~51 % power; at half speed, 12.5 %. Yet for a century, flow was regulated by throttling — running the motor flat out against a partly closed valve, like controlling a car's speed with the brake while flooring the accelerator. The variable-frequency drive (VFD, Frequenzumrichter) replaces the valve with the frequency knob:

Worked example Retrofitting a 15 kW pump with a VFD

A 15 kW pump runs 4000 h/year, but the process needs only 70 % of full flow. Compare (a) throttling — motor stays near full speed, drawing ≈ 85 % of rated power against the valve — with (b) a VFD at 70 % speed (drive efficiency 97 %). Electricity: 0.30 €/kWh.

Step 1 — throttled power: \(P_a \approx 0.85 \cdot 15 = 12.8\,\mathrm{kW}\). (The valve converts the excess head straight into heat and noise.)
Step 2 — VFD power via the cube law: \( P_b = 15 \cdot 0.7^3 / 0.97 = 15 \cdot 0.343 / 0.97 \approx 5.3\,\mathrm{kW} \).
Step 3 — annual savings: \( \Delta P = 7.5\,\mathrm{kW} \) × 4000 h = 30 000 kWh/a → \(30\,000 \cdot 0.30 = 9000\) €/year.
A ~1500 € drive pays for itself in about two months, then saves ~9000 €/year for decades. Multiply by the millions of pumps and fans worldwide — motor-driven systems consume ~2/3 of industrial electricity — and VFDs are one of the largest levers in industrial energy efficiency, full stop.

9.2 V/f control and field-oriented control

V/f control (U/f-Kennliniensteuerung): the induction machine's flux is set by the ratio of voltage to frequency (\(\Phi \propto U/f\), from \(u = N\,\mathrm{d}\Phi/\mathrm{d}t\)). So a VFD that lowers \(f\) must lower \(U\) proportionally, keeping flux — and thus available torque — constant from a few Hz up to base speed. Above base speed, \(U\) has hit its ceiling: \(\Phi\) necessarily falls as \(1/f\) — field weakening again, constant power. V/f is open-loop, dead simple, and runs every fan and pump drive on the planet.

Field-oriented control (FOC) (feldorientierte Regelung), honestly in one paragraph: the reason a DC machine is easy to control is that flux (field winding) and torque (armature current) are separate knobs at 90° to each other. In an AC machine both hide inside the same three stator currents. FOC mathematically rotates the measurement frame so it spins along with the machine's flux (the d/q transformation); in that frame the AC currents become two DC quantities — \(i_d\), which sets flux, and \(i_q\), which sets torque. Two ordinary PI controllers regulate them independently, and the inverter transforms the result back into three phase voltages, thousands of times per second. Requires rotor position/flux knowledge (encoder or estimation), but delivers DC-machine-grade dynamics from a cageful of aluminum: torque steps in milliseconds. Every EV, servo and modern crane drive runs FOC.

9.3 Four quadrants and regenerative braking

Plot speed (±) against torque (±): four quadrants. Q1 forward motoring, Q3 reverse motoring; Q2/Q4 — torque opposing motion — are braking, and since our machines convert both ways, braking means generating (Nutzbremsung / Rekuperation). A modern inverter passes this recovered energy back to the DC link and thence to the battery (EV) or grid (trains, cranes, elevators). An ICE train throws its kinetic energy into brake dust; an electric one sells it back — regeneration recovers on the order of 20–40 % of traction energy in stop-and-go rail service. Where no return path exists, a brake chopper burns the energy in a resistor — functional, but the accountant weeps. Servo drives (PMSM + encoder + cascaded current/speed/position loops) are the four-quadrant idea perfected: positioning to arc-seconds with torque reversals hundreds of times per second, in every robot joint and CNC axis.

Thermal limits & duty cycles: a machine's rating is a temperature rating: insulation classes B/F/H permit 130/155/180 °C, and a rule of thumb halves insulation life for every ~10 K sustained over-temperature. A crane motor rated S3-40 % would cook in an S1 pump application — always match the duty cycle, not just the kilowatts. This is also why overload capability is a matter of minutes, set by thermal mass, while breakdown torque is a hard electromagnetic ceiling.

12. Exercises

Machines reward number-sense: always sanity-check against the 50 Hz speed menu and typical slips before trusting your algebra.

E1A transformer has \(N_1 = 800\) turns on the 230 V side. How many turns give 11.5 V out? A 2 A load flows on the secondary — what is the primary current (ideal transformer)? easy

Show solution
\( \ddot u = 230/11.5 = 20 \Rightarrow N_2 = 800/20 = 40 \) turns. Currents transform inversely: \( I_1 = I_2 / \ddot u = 2/20 = 0.1\,\mathrm{A} \). Check via power: \(230 \cdot 0.1 = 23\,\mathrm{W} = 11.5 \cdot 2\) ✓. Low-voltage side: thick wire, few turns; high-voltage side: thin wire, many turns — same copper cross-section·turns product on both.

E2A hydro generator must turn at exactly 750 rpm on the 50 Hz grid. How many poles does it need? What if the plant exported to a 60 Hz grid instead? easy

Show solution
\( p = 60 f / n_s = 3000/750 = 4 \) pole pairs = 8 poles. At 60 Hz the same 8-pole machine would run at \(3600/4 = 900\,\mathrm{rpm}\); to keep 750 rpm you'd need \(p = 3600/750 = 4.8\) — not an integer! No synchronous machine can link 750 rpm to 60 Hz directly; you'd change the turbine speed or use a converter. Speeds are quantized by the grid — the deep reason variable-speed hydro and wind use power electronics.

E3A 6-pole, 50 Hz induction motor delivers 11 kW at 960 rpm. Find synchronous speed, slip, rotor frequency, and shaft torque. medium

Show solution
\( p = 3 \Rightarrow n_s = 3000/3 = 1000\,\mathrm{rpm} \). Slip \( s = (1000-960)/1000 = 4\,\% \); rotor frequency \( f_2 = 0.04 \cdot 50 = 2\,\mathrm{Hz} \). Torque: \( \omega = 2\pi \cdot 960/60 = 100.5\,\mathrm{s^{-1}} \), so \( T = P/\omega = 11\,000/100.5 \approx 109\,\mathrm{N\,m} \). Sanity: rated slip of a few percent ✓; ~110 N·m from an 11 kW machine at ~1000 rpm is the right order (rule of thumb: \(T \approx 9550\, P[\mathrm{kW}]/n[\mathrm{rpm}]\,\mathrm{N\,m}\)).

E4Short-circuit test on a 10 kVA, 400 V transformer (rated current 25 A): rated current flows at \(U_{sc} = 20\,\mathrm{V}\), absorbing \(P_{sc} = 300\,\mathrm{W}\). Find \(u_k\), the series impedance \(Z\), its parts \(R\) and \(X\), and the worst-case fault current. medium

Show solution
\( u_k = 20/400 = 5\,\% \) — a typical distribution value. \( Z = U_{sc}/I_N = 20/25 = 0.8\,\Omega \); \( R = P_{sc}/I_N^2 = 300/625 = 0.48\,\Omega \); \( X = \sqrt{0.8^2 - 0.48^2} = 0.64\,\Omega \). Bolted short at rated voltage: \( I_{sc} = I_N/u_k = 25/0.05 = 500\,\mathrm{A} \) — 20× rated. Your breaker and cabling must interrupt and survive exactly this. (Note the test never exposed the transformer to danger: only 20 V were applied.)

E5A separately excited DC motor: \(U = 220\,\mathrm{V}\), \(R_a = 0.5\,\Omega\), drawing \(I_a = 20\,\mathrm{A}\) at 1500 rpm. Find the back-EMF, \(k\Phi\), and torque. The load then drops so that \(I_a = 15\,\mathrm{A}\) — what is the new speed? medium

Show solution
Back-EMF: \( E = U - R_a I_a = 220 - 10 = 210\,\mathrm{V} \). With \( \omega = 2\pi \cdot 1500/60 = 157.1\,\mathrm{s^{-1}} \): \( k\Phi = E/\omega = 1.337\,\mathrm{V\,s} \). Torque: \( T = k\Phi I_a = 1.337 \cdot 20 = 26.7\,\mathrm{N\,m} \). At 15 A: \( E' = 220 - 7.5 = 212.5\,\mathrm{V} \Rightarrow \omega' = 212.5/1.337 = 158.9\,\mathrm{s^{-1}} \Rightarrow n' \approx 1518\,\mathrm{rpm} \). Only ~1 % speed rise for a 25 % load drop — the separately excited DC machine is naturally speed-stiff, which is why it was the precision drive for a century.

E6An induction motor has \(s_k = 0.20\) and \(T_k = 2.5\,T_N\). Using Kloss's formula, find the rated slip and the starting torque (as a fraction of \(T_N\)). challenge

Show solution
Set \(T = T_N\): \( \frac{1}{2.5} = \frac{2}{s/s_k + s_k/s} \Rightarrow \frac{s}{s_k} + \frac{s_k}{s} = 5 \). With \(x = s/s_k\): \(x^2 - 5x + 1 = 0 \Rightarrow x = \frac{5 - \sqrt{21}}{2} \approx 0.209\) (small root = stable branch; the large root \(x \approx 4.79\) is the same torque on the unstable side). \( s_N = 0.209 \cdot 0.20 \approx 4.2\,\% \) ✓ (right in the typical range — Kloss is self-consistent). Starting torque, \(s = 1\): \( T_1 = \frac{2 \cdot 2.5}{1/0.2 + 0.2} T_N = \frac{5}{5.2} T_N \approx 0.96\,T_N \). So despite drawing 5–8× rated current, the machine starts with less than rated torque — the starting problem of Section 6.2 in one number.

E7A round-rotor synchronous generator: \(X_d = 2\,\Omega\) per phase, terminal voltage 400 V line-to-line (star), internal EMF \(E = 290\,\mathrm{V}\) per phase. (a) Power delivered at \(\delta = 25°\)? (b) Maximum possible power? (c) The turbine suddenly supplies 120 kW — what happens? challenge

Show solution
Phase voltage: \( U = 400/\sqrt3 = 231\,\mathrm{V} \). (a) \( P = \frac{3UE}{X_d}\sin\delta = \frac{3 \cdot 231 \cdot 290}{2} \cdot \sin 25° = 100.5\,\mathrm{kW} \cdot 0.423 \approx 42.5\,\mathrm{kW} \). (b) \( P_{max} = 100.5\,\mathrm{kW} \) at \( \delta = 90° \). (c) 120 kW > \(P_{max}\): no load angle can transmit it. The rotor accelerates, δ passes 90°, transmitted power falls, the rotor accelerates harder — pole slipping. Protection must trip the machine within fractions of a second. In real grid operation you would first raise the excitation (E ↑ → \(P_{max}\) ↑); this coupling between excitation, reactive power and stability is the daily bread of Module 19.
