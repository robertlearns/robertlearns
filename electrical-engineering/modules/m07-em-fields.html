⚡ Fundamentals of Electrical Engineering · Module 07

Electromagnetic Fields & Maxwell's Equations · Elektromagnetische Felder

Circuits are a brilliant abstraction — but underneath every resistor, capacitor and transmission line there are fields: invisible conditions of space that store energy, exert forces, and race outward at the speed of light. This module builds the field picture from Coulomb's law to Maxwell's four equations, and ends with the most beautiful punchline in physics: light itself falls out of the math.

Corresponds to: Theoretische Elektrotechnik / Elektromagnetische Felder (3rd–4th semester) Prerequisites: Modules 02, 04 Study time: ≈ 7–9 h

1. The field concept — how forces cross empty space

Coulomb's experiments (1785) show that two charges attract or repel each other across empty space. Taken literally, that is deeply strange: how does charge A "know" that charge B moved? Newton had the same discomfort about gravity and called action at a distance an absurdity he left "to the consideration of my readers".

The modern answer, due largely to Michael Faraday — a bookbinder's apprentice with no formal mathematics — is the field (Feld): a charge does not act on distant charges directly. Instead it modifies the space around itself, creating a condition — an electric field \(\vec{E}\) — and a second charge placed anywhere responds to the field at its own location. The interaction is local, always:

charge → field → force on other charge.

For static problems the field looks like mathematical bookkeeping. It stops being optional the moment things change in time: wiggle charge A, and charge B a distance \(r\) away feels nothing until a time \(r/c\) later. During that delay the energy and momentum are not in either charge — they are demonstrably in the field, travelling. Radio, radar, fiber optics and this module's finale all live in that delay.

💡Intuition: the field as a landscape

Think of a field as a weather map for space. A scalar field assigns each point a number (temperature map, or our potential \(\varphi\)). A vector field assigns each point an arrow (wind map, or \(\vec{E}\) and \(\vec{B}\)). A test charge is a tiny weather balloon: release it anywhere and the local arrow tells you the force it feels. All the vector calculus in this module — flux, circulation, divergence, curl — is just precise language for reading such maps: flux asks "how much arrows flow out through a closed surface?", circulation asks "how much do the arrows swirl around a closed loop?".

2. Electrostatics — Coulomb's law and the field of charges

2.1 Coulomb's law

Two point charges \(q_1, q_2\) at distance \(r\) exert on each other the force

\[ F = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\,\frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2}, \qquad \varepsilon_0 = 8.854\cdot 10^{-12}\,\frac{\mathrm{As}}{\mathrm{Vm}} \]

where \(\varepsilon_0\) is the permittivity of free space (elektrische Feldkonstante).

Like charges repel (\(F>0\), pointing apart), unlike charges attract. The prefactor \(1/4\pi\varepsilon_0 \approx 8.99\cdot 10^9\,\mathrm{Vm/As}\) is enormous: two charges of one coulomb each at 1 m distance would push apart with \(9\cdot10^9\) N — the weight of a small mountain. That is why macroscopic matter is so scrupulously neutral, and why the coulomb quantities we met in Module 04 flow as current but never pile up in one place.

2.2 The electric field E

Factor Coulomb's law into "source" and "victim". The electric field strength (elektrische Feldstärke) created by the source charge \(Q\) is defined as force per unit test charge:

\[ \boxed{\;\vec{E} = \frac{\vec{F}}{q}\;} \qquad [E] = \frac{\mathrm{N}}{\mathrm{C}} = \frac{\mathrm{V}}{\mathrm{m}}, \qquad \text{point charge: } E(r) = \frac{Q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r^2}\ \text{(radial)}. \]

The V/m unit is no accident — we will connect \(E\) to voltage in Section 4. Typical magnitudes: ~100 V/m in fair-weather atmosphere, ~10 kV/m under a 400 kV line, 3 MV/m breakdown strength of air (sparks!), ~10 GV/m holding electrons inside atoms.

Superposition (Überlagerungsprinzip): fields of several charges simply add as vectors, \(\vec{E} = \vec{E}_1 + \vec{E}_2 + \dots\) — the electrostatic field is linear in its sources, exactly like the circuits of Module 04 were linear in theirs.

2.3 Field-line pictures

Field lines (Feldlinien) are curves that follow the local field direction. Three rules make them quantitative: they start on + charge and end on − charge (or at infinity); their local density encodes field strength; they never cross (the field has one direction per point). 🚫Classic pitfall: field lines are not trajectories

A field line shows the direction of the force on a positive test charge at each point — not the path a released charge follows. A charge has inertia: like a ball rolling in a bowl, its velocity and the force generally point in different directions. Only for a charge released from rest in a homogeneous field do line and trajectory coincide.

3. Gauss's law — counting field lines through closed surfaces

3.1 Electric flux

Define the electric flux (elektrischer Fluss) through a surface as "field strength × perpendicular area", summed over the surface:

\[ \Psi = \oint_A \vec{E}\cdot \mathrm{d}\vec{A} \]

Picture it as counting field lines poking outward through the surface (outward crossings count +, inward −). Gauss's law (Satz von Gauß) is then a single powerful statement:

\[ \boxed{\;\oint_A \vec{E}\cdot\mathrm{d}\vec{A} = \frac{Q_{\text{enc}}}{\varepsilon_0}\;} \]

The net number of field lines leaving any closed surface is proportional to the charge enclosed — nothing else matters. Charges outside the surface send lines in on one side and out on the other: net contribution zero. This is the integral way of saying "field lines begin and end only on charges". The local, per-point version of the same statement is the divergence: \(\operatorname{div}\vec E = \rho/\varepsilon_0\) — divergence measures how strongly a point acts as a source (a "sprinkler head") of field lines. We will not need the differential machinery here; the picture is enough.

🔑Key idea: symmetry does the integral for you

Gauss's law is always true but only useful when symmetry lets you pull \(E\) out of the integral: choose a surface on which \(|\vec E|\) is constant and \(\vec E\) is perpendicular (or parallel, contributing zero). Three geometries deliver: spheres around point/spherical charges, cylinders around line charges and wires, pillboxes straddling charged planes. These three solved examples carry you through most of electrostatics.

Worked example Gauss's law for a charged sphere

A metal sphere of radius \(R = 10\,\mathrm{cm}\) carries \(Q = 1\,\mathrm{\mu C}\). Find the field at the surface and at \(r = 30\,\mathrm{cm}\), and check against air breakdown (3 MV/m).

Step 1 — choose the surface: a concentric sphere of radius \(r\). By symmetry \(\vec E\) is radial with the same magnitude everywhere on it, so the flux integral collapses to \(E \cdot 4\pi r^2\).
Step 2 — apply Gauss: \[ E \cdot 4\pi r^2 = \frac{Q}{\varepsilon_0} \;\Rightarrow\; E(r) = \frac{Q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r^2} \] Outside any spherically symmetric charge, the field is exactly that of a point charge at the center — a free theorem from Gauss.
Step 3 — numbers: at the surface: \( E = 8.99\cdot 10^9 \cdot \frac{10^{-6}}{0.1^2} \approx 9.0\cdot 10^5\,\mathrm{V/m} = 0.9\,\mathrm{MV/m} \). At 30 cm: \( E = 8.99\cdot10^9 \cdot \frac{10^{-6}}{0.09} \approx 1.0\cdot 10^5\,\mathrm{V/m} \) — nine times weaker at three times the distance, as \(1/r^2\) demands.
\(E_{\text{surface}} \approx 0.9\,\mathrm{MV/m}\) — below the 3 MV/m breakdown of air, but only by a factor 3. Roughly 3 µC on this sphere would spark. This is why high-voltage hardware uses large, smooth spheres: bigger \(R\) means lower surface field for the same charge.

The same pillbox argument in Fig. 3.1 gives the field of a large charged sheet with surface charge density \(\sigma\): flux \(2AE\) leaves through the two faces, enclosing charge \(\sigma A\), so \(E = \sigma/2\varepsilon_0\) — independent of distance. Put two oppositely charged sheets face to face (a capacitor!) and the fields add between the plates and cancel outside:

\[ E_{\text{between plates}} = \frac{\sigma}{\varepsilon_0}, \qquad E_{\text{outside}} = 0. \]

Keep this result warm — it is the seed of Section 5.

4. Potential — the energy landscape of the field

4.1 From force to energy

Push a charge \(q\) against the field from point A to point B and you do work. Per unit charge, that work defines the voltage between the points — the same \(U\) we used throughout Module 04, now revealed as a line integral over the field:

\[ U_{AB} = \int_A^B \vec{E}\cdot\mathrm{d}\vec{s} \]

For electrostatic fields this integral is path-independent (walk any route from A to B, same energy — a conservative field, zero circulation: \(\oint \vec E\cdot\mathrm d\vec s = 0\)). Path independence lets us define a scalar potential (Potential) \(\varphi\) at every point, an "electric altitude", with \(U_{AB} = \varphi_A - \varphi_B\). For a point charge, with the conventional zero at infinity:

\[ \varphi(r) = \frac{Q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r}. \]

4.2 E = −grad φ and equipotentials

Conversely, the field is the downhill slope of the potential landscape:

\[ \boxed{\;\vec{E} = -\operatorname{grad}\varphi\;} \qquad \text{homogeneous field: } E = \frac{U}{d}. \]

The minus sign says the field points from high potential to low — "downhill". Surfaces of constant \(\varphi\) are equipotentials (Äquipotentialflächen), the contour lines of the landscape; field lines cross them at right angles, like water flowing perpendicular to contour lines. Metal conductors in electrostatics are always equipotential volumes (otherwise their free electrons would still be moving), so field lines meet metal surfaces perpendicularly, and the field inside a closed metal shell is zero — the Faraday cage (Faradayscher Käfig), the reason your car protects you from lightning and your phone loses reception in an elevator.

The handy formula \(E = U/d\) for homogeneous fields explains everyday numbers: a 1 mm capacitor gap at 230 V holds \(E = 230\,\mathrm{kV/m}\); cell membranes (~5 nm, ~70 mV) sustain a staggering 14 MV/m.

⚠️Volts don't hurt you — fields and energy do

A rubbed balloon sits at ~10 kV, a taser at 50 kV, both mostly harmless; a 230 V outlet can kill. Potential alone is meaningless without asking how much charge/energy is behind it and what field (and hence current) it can drive through you. This is the physics behind "it's the amps that kill" — and behind birds sitting happily on a 20 kV line: both feet at the same potential, so no voltage across the bird.

5. Capacitance — the capacitor derived from its field

5.1 The parallel-plate formula

Since Module 04 you have used \(C = Q/U\) as a given. Now we can derive it. Take two plates of area \(A\) at spacing \(d\) (small compared to the plate size), charged to \(+Q\) and \(-Q\):

  1. Field from Gauss (Section 3): \(E = \sigma/\varepsilon_0 = Q/(\varepsilon_0 A)\), homogeneous between the plates.
  2. Voltage from the line integral: \(U = E\,d = Q d/(\varepsilon_0 A)\).
  3. Take the ratio:
\[ \boxed{\;C = \frac{Q}{U} = \frac{\varepsilon_0\varepsilon_r A}{d}\;} \]

Big plates and a small gap make a big capacitor — and the geometry, not the circuit, sets the value. The \(\varepsilon_r\) appears in Step 1 once a dielectric fills the gap, as follows.

5.2 Dielectrics and εr

Fill the gap with an insulator (Dielektrikum) and \(C\) jumps by a factor \(\varepsilon_r\) (relative permittivity, Permittivitätszahl). Why: the field polarizes the material — molecules stretch or rotate into tiny dipoles whose surface charge partially opposes the plate charge. The net field inside drops to \(E = E_0/\varepsilon_r\) for the same \(Q\), so \(U\) drops and \(C = Q/U\) rises. Typical values: air ≈ 1.0006, paper ≈ 3, glass/epoxy (FR-4) ≈ 4.5, ceramics 10–10 000, water ≈ 80. Ceramic class-2 dielectrics (X7R, barium titanate) are how a fingernail-sized MLCC reaches microfarads.

5.3 Where the energy lives: ½εE²

Charging a capacitor to voltage \(U\) stores \(W = \frac{1}{2}CU^2\) (Module 04). Substitute \(C = \varepsilon A/d\) and \(U = Ed\):

\[ W = \tfrac12 \frac{\varepsilon A}{d}(Ed)^2 = \tfrac12\,\varepsilon E^2 \cdot (A\,d) \;\Rightarrow\; \boxed{\;w_e = \frac{W}{V} = \tfrac12\,\varepsilon E^2\;} \]

The energy is proportional to the volume of field \(A\,d\) — it is stored in the field itself, at density \(\frac12\varepsilon E^2\) joules per cubic meter, not "in the plates". This is the first hard evidence that fields are physically real energy carriers.

📜From Leyden jar to supercapacitor

The 1745 Leyden jar (a glass bottle with foil inside and out) stored about 1 nF — enough to knock a row of monks off their feet in a famous demonstration for Louis XV. Modern supercapacitors reach thousands of farads in a soda-can volume by making \(A\) gigantic (porous activated carbon, ~2000 m² per gram) and \(d\) molecular (the electrochemical double layer, < 1 nm). Same formula, twelve orders of magnitude apart. Capacitors as circuit elements return in transient analysis (Module 05) and power electronics (Module 18).

6. Steady currents as a field — J and continuity

Current in a wire is a river of charge; the field view zooms into the cross-section. The current density (Stromdichte) \(\vec J\) gives amperes per square meter at each point:

\[ I = \int_A \vec J \cdot \mathrm d\vec A, \qquad \vec J = \sigma \vec E \ \ \text{(Ohm's law, local form; } \sigma = 1/\rho \text{ conductivity)}. \]

The local Ohm's law \(\vec J = \sigma\vec E\) is Module 04's \(U = RI\) shrunk to a point: an E-field inside a conductor drives a proportional current density. Integrate it over a wire of length \(l\) and cross-section \(A\) and \(R = \rho l/A\) reappears.

Charge conservation becomes a field statement, the continuity equation (Kontinuitätsgleichung):

\[ \oint_A \vec J\cdot\mathrm d\vec A = -\frac{\mathrm dQ_{\text{enc}}}{\mathrm dt} \]

Net current flowing out of a closed surface drains the charge inside. For steady currents (\(\mathrm dQ/\mathrm dt = 0\)) the outflow is zero through every closed surface — which is exactly Kirchhoff's current law, now recognized as a special case of a field law. Typical design values: household wiring runs at \(J \approx 1\!-\!6\,\mathrm{A/mm^2}\); IC interconnects tolerate ~10⁴ A/mm² before electromigration starts eating the metal.

7. Magnetostatics — currents make magnetic fields

7.1 The B-field

In 1820 Hans Christian Ørsted noticed, mid-lecture, that a compass needle twitches next to a current-carrying wire: moving charge creates a magnetic field. We describe it by the magnetic flux density (magnetische Flussdichte) \(\vec B\), unit tesla (T = Vs/m²), and its companion the magnetic field strength (magnetische Feldstärke) \(\vec H\) with \(\vec B = \mu_0\mu_r \vec H\), where

\[ \mu_0 = 4\pi\cdot 10^{-7}\,\frac{\mathrm{Vs}}{\mathrm{Am}} \]

is the permeability of free space (magnetische Feldkonstante).

Magnetic field lines have a defining peculiarity: they always close on themselves — there are no magnetic charges to start or end on. Gauss's law for magnetism: \(\oint \vec B\cdot\mathrm d\vec A = 0\) through every closed surface. Cut a bar magnet in half and you get two smaller magnets, never a lone north pole. Scale of things: Earth's field ~50 µT, a fridge magnet ~10 mT, an electric machine's air gap ~1 T, an MRI scanner 1.5–7 T, the record steady lab field ~45 T.

7.2 Ampère's law and the straight wire

The magnetic counterpart of Gauss's law swaps flux for circulation. Ampère's law (Durchflutungsgesetz) says: walk around any closed loop, summing the component of \(\vec H\) along your path — the total equals the current threading through the loop:

\[ \boxed{\;\oint \vec H\cdot\mathrm d\vec s = I_{\text{enc}}\;} \]

Where Gauss counts sources, Ampère counts swirl: currents are the vortices of the magnetic field (the differential version says \(\operatorname{curl}\vec H = \vec J\): curl measures local swirl the way divergence measures local sourcing). The classic application: an infinite straight wire carrying \(I\). By symmetry, \(\vec B\) forms concentric circles around the wire (direction: right-hand rule — thumb along \(I\), fingers curl along \(\vec B\)). Choose the loop to be such a circle of radius \(r\):

\[ H \cdot 2\pi r = I \;\Rightarrow\; \boxed{\;B = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi r}\;} \]

Numbers: 10 A at 2 cm gives \(B = 4\pi\cdot10^{-7}\cdot 10 /(2\pi\cdot 0.02) = 100\,\mathrm{\mu T}\) — twice Earth's field. This is why a compass near a live cable lies, and why measurement labs twist their wire pairs (opposite currents at nearly the same place: fields cancel).

7.3 The solenoid — and where inductance comes from

Wind the wire into a long coil (Zylinderspule) of \(N\) turns over length \(l\). Inside, the fields of all turns reinforce into a nearly homogeneous axial field; outside, the field is weak. An Ampère loop running length \(l\) inside the coil and closing far outside encloses the current \(N I\):

\[ H\,l = N I \;\Rightarrow\; B = \frac{\mu_0 N I}{l} \quad\text{(air core).} \]

Now compute the flux through the coil's cross-section \(A\): \(\Phi = B A\), and each of the \(N\) turns links it, giving flux linkage (verketteter Fluss) \(\Psi = N\Phi\). The inductance is defined as flux linkage per ampere:

\[ L = \frac{N\Phi}{I} = \frac{N \cdot \frac{\mu_0 N I}{l} A}{I} \;\Rightarrow\; \boxed{\;L = \frac{\mu_0 \mu_r N^2 A}{l}\;} \]

The \(N^2\) is worth a pause: doubling the turns doubles the field and doubles the turns that link it — inductance grows quadratically. Just like \(C = \varepsilon A/d\), inductance is pure geometry (times material). And the field stores energy here too, mirror-image to the capacitor:

\[ W = \tfrac12 L I^2, \qquad w_m = \frac{B^2}{2\mu} \ \ \mathrm{J/m^3}. \]
Worked example A real solenoid, end to end

A coil: \(N = 500\) turns on a plastic (air-core) tube, length \(l = 10\,\mathrm{cm}\), diameter \(2\,\mathrm{cm}\), carrying \(I = 2\,\mathrm{A}\). Find \(B\), \(L\), and the stored energy.

Step 1 — field: \( B = \frac{\mu_0 N I}{l} = \frac{4\pi\cdot10^{-7}\cdot 500\cdot 2}{0.1} \approx 12.6\,\mathrm{mT} \) — 250× Earth's field, enough to visibly attract a paper clip at the opening.
Step 2 — inductance: \( A = \pi r^2 = \pi (0.01)^2 = 3.14\cdot 10^{-4}\,\mathrm{m^2} \), so \( L = \frac{4\pi\cdot10^{-7} \cdot 500^2 \cdot 3.14\cdot10^{-4}}{0.1} \approx 0.99\,\mathrm{mH} \approx 1\,\mathrm{mH} \).
Step 3 — energy: \( W = \tfrac12 L I^2 = \tfrac12 \cdot 10^{-3}\cdot 4 = 2\,\mathrm{mJ} \). Sanity check via density: \( w_m = B^2/2\mu_0 \approx 63\,\mathrm{J/m^3} \) over the core volume \(A\,l \approx 3.1\cdot10^{-5}\,\mathrm{m^3}\) also gives ≈ 2 mJ ✓
\(B \approx 12.6\,\mathrm{mT},\; L \approx 1\,\mathrm{mH},\; W = 2\,\mathrm{mJ}\). Slide an iron core in (\(\mu_r \sim 1000\)) and \(L\) jumps toward a henry — that is how chokes, relays and contactors are built.

8. Magnetic forces — motors before Maxwell

8.1 Force on a current-carrying conductor

A magnetic field exerts force on moving charge — and a current-carrying wire is exactly that. A straight conductor of length \(l\) carrying \(I\) perpendicular to a field \(B\) feels

\[ \boxed{\;F = B\,I\,l\;} \]

direction by the right-hand rule (or the German "UVW rule": Ursache–Vermittlung–Wirkung — cause \(I\), mediation \(B\), effect \(F\), on thumb–index–middle finger). This single formula is the electric motor: put many conductor lengths in a ~1 T field, feed them tens of amperes, and torque appears (Module 17). It is also the loudspeaker, the galvanometer, and the reason busbars must be braced, as follows.

8.2 Force between parallel conductors

Wire 2 sits in the circular field of wire 1, \(B_1 = \mu_0 I_1/(2\pi a)\), so it feels \(F = B_1 I_2 l\) per length \(l\):

\[ \frac{F}{l} = \frac{\mu_0\, I_1 I_2}{2\pi a} \]

Parallel currents attract, antiparallel currents repel. (Until 2019, this formula defined the ampere.) In everyday electronics the force is negligible — until a short circuit makes it terrifying:

Worked example Busbar forces in a short circuit

In a substation, two busbars run in parallel at spacing \(a = 30\,\mathrm{cm}\). A fault drives a short-circuit current of \(I = 50\,\mathrm{kA}\) through them (out on one bar, back on the other). Find the force per meter, and at the first asymmetric peak (\(\hat I \approx 2.5\,I\)).

Step 1 — steady value: \[ \frac{F}{l} = \frac{\mu_0 I^2}{2\pi a} = \frac{2\cdot10^{-7}\cdot (5\cdot10^4)^2}{0.3} \approx 1.7\,\mathrm{kN/m}. \] Antiparallel currents: the bars are pushed apart with the weight-force of ~170 kg per meter.
Step 2 — peak: force scales with \(I^2\), so the 2.5× asymmetric current peak gives \(2.5^2 = 6.25\)× the force: \( F/l \approx 10\,\mathrm{kN/m} \) — a tonne-weight per meter, as an impulsive blow within the first 10 ms.
≈ 1.7 kN/m steady, ≈ 10 kN/m at the peak. This is why switchgear busbars sit in massive cast supports, and why standards (IEC 61439) demand a mechanical short-circuit withstand rating. Unbraced bars bend like spaghetti in fault videos — the magnetic force is very real.

8.3 The Lorentz force on a single charge

Underlying \(F = BIl\) is the force on each individual charge carrier, the Lorentz force (Lorentzkraft):

\[ \boxed{\;\vec F = q\,(\vec E + \vec v \times \vec B)\;} \]

The magnetic part is always perpendicular to the velocity — it does no work, it only steers. A free charge in a uniform \(B\) therefore runs in circles (radius \(r = mv/qB\)): this steering deflected the electron beam in every CRT television, guides particles around the LHC's 27 km ring, and traps the plasma in fusion tokamaks. In a current-carrying conductor in a field, the steered carriers crowd toward one side, producing a measurable transverse voltage — the Hall effect (Hall-Effekt), the working principle of the position sensors and clamp-on current probes you will meet in Modules 10 and 17.

9. Magnetic materials — iron, hysteresis and why transformers hum

Materials respond to \(\vec H\) with \(\vec B = \mu_0\mu_r\vec H\). For almost everything \(\mu_r \approx 1\) (diamagnets slightly below, paramagnets slightly above). The exception that powers civilization is ferromagnetism (Ferromagnetismus): in iron, nickel, cobalt and their alloys, atomic magnetic moments align spontaneously in microscopic domains (Weiss'sche Bezirke). An external field grows the aligned domains, multiplying the flux density by \(\mu_r \sim 10^2\ldots 10^5\). Iron is to magnetic flux what copper is to current: a low-resistance path that lets us guide flux through cores and air gaps.

Ferromagnets are neither linear nor memoryless. Cycle \(H\) up and down and \(B(H)\) traces a loop — hysteresis (Hysterese):

Read three engineering facts straight off the loop:

  • Loop area = energy lost per cycle per unit volume (\(\oint H\,\mathrm dB\), in J/m³). A 50 Hz transformer traverses the loop 50 times per second, so wide loops mean hot cores. Transformer laminations use soft magnetic steel with a sliver-thin loop; permanent magnets (NdFeB) deliberately use enormously wide loops so they stay magnetized against opposing fields.
  • Saturation (Sättigung) at 1.5–2 T for iron caps every magnetic design: above it \(\mu_r\) collapses toward 1, inductance plummets, and magnetizing current spikes — the classic cause of transformer inrush current and of chokes that mysteriously stop choking at high load.
  • Remanence + coercivity are memory: magnetic tape, hard-disk platters and magnetic stripe cards store bits as the sign of \(B_r\).

The audible 100 Hz hum of a transformer is the core physically flexing (magnetostriction) twice per 50 Hz cycle — the hysteresis loop made audible. Core loss engineering returns in earnest with transformers and machines (Modules 17, 18).

10. Induction — changing flux makes voltage

10.1 Faraday's law

Electricity → magnetism was Ørsted's 1820 discovery. The reverse took Faraday eleven more years, because the effect only exists during change: a steady magnet near a coil induces nothing; move it, and a voltage appears. Define the magnetic flux (magnetischer Fluss) through a loop, \(\Phi = \int \vec B \cdot \mathrm d\vec A\) (unit weber, Wb = Vs). Then Faraday's law of induction (Induktionsgesetz):

\[ \boxed{\;u_{\text{ind}} = -\,\frac{\mathrm d\Phi}{\mathrm dt}\;} \qquad \text{coil of } N \text{ turns: } u_{\text{ind}} = -N\frac{\mathrm d\Phi}{\mathrm dt}. \]

Anything that changes \(\Phi = B A \cos\alpha\) works, giving the two practical cases:

  • Changing field, resting conductor (Ruheinduktion): \(B\) varies in time — transformers, wireless chargers, induction cooktops.
  • Moving conductor, static field (Bewegungsinduktion): area or orientation varies. A rod of length \(l\) sliding at speed \(v\) across a field \(B\) sweeps flux at rate \(B l v\), so \(u = B\,l\,v\) — microscopically this is just the Lorentz force \(qvB\) pushing carriers along the rod. A loop rotating at \(\omega\) has \(\Phi = BA\cos\omega t\), hence \(u = BA\omega\sin\omega t\): every generator on the planet, and the origin of the sine waves of Module 05.

10.2 Lenz's rule — the minus sign

The minus sign has a name and a personality. Lenz's rule (Lenzsche Regel): the induced voltage drives a current whose own magnetic field opposes the change of flux that created it. It must be so — the opposite sign would amplify every disturbance into a free-energy runaway; Lenz is energy conservation wearing a minus sign.

💡Intuition: the magnet in the copper tube

Drop a strong neodymium magnet through a vertical copper tube. It falls in eerie slow motion — seconds instead of a blink. The approaching magnet increases flux through each ring of tube below it; induced eddy currents circulate to oppose the increase, making each ring a magnet that repels the falling one (and attracts it from above, where flux is decreasing). Copper is not magnetic at all — the drag is pure induction, and the magnet's lost potential energy reappears as heat in the tube walls. The same physics, scaled up, is the eddy-current brake in ICE trains and drop-tower rides, damping in analog meters, and the reason induction cooktops heat pans directly.

The voltage is largest where the flux changes fastest. Crank the speed: \(\Phi\)'s peak height stays the same, but \(u\) grows — induction rewards rate, not amount.

10.3 Self-inductance, mutual inductance, transformer

A coil's own current creates flux through itself, so a changing current induces a voltage in the very same coil — self-induction (Selbstinduktion). With \(N\Phi = L i\) from Section 7:

\[ u_L = L\,\frac{\mathrm di}{\mathrm dt} \]

— the defining equation of the inductor as a circuit element (transients: Module 05). It also explains the fat spark when you break an inductive circuit: forcing \(\mathrm di/\mathrm dt \to -\infty\) demands unbounded voltage, which the circuit finds by arcing across the opening switch. Hence flyback diodes across every relay coil.

If a second coil shares part of the first coil's flux, the changing current in coil 1 induces voltage in coil 2 — mutual inductance (Gegeninduktivität) \(M\), with \(u_2 = -M\,\mathrm di_1/\mathrm dt\). Wind both coils on one iron core so essentially all flux is shared, drive coil 1 with AC, and each turn of each winding sees the same \(\mathrm d\Phi/\mathrm dt\). The winding voltages are then simply proportional to the turn counts:

\[ \frac{U_2}{U_1} = \frac{N_2}{N_1} \]

— the transformer (Transformator), the machine that made AC win the "War of the Currents" by letting grids step voltage up for low-loss transmission and back down for safe use. Full treatment in Modules 17 and 19; here it stands as induction's greatest hit.

11. Maxwell's equations — the whole theory on one page

11.1 The missing term: displacement current

By 1860 the four experimental laws (Gauss ×2, Ampère, Faraday) were known. James Clerk Maxwell, aged ~30, noticed Ampère's law is inconsistent for changing fields. The cleanest way to see it — the capacitor paradox: charge a capacitor through a wire and apply Ampère's law to a loop around the wire. The enclosed current is defined via a surface spanning the loop. Choose a flat disk pierced by the wire: \(I_{\text{enc}} = I\). Now balloon the surface out so it passes between the capacitor plates, where no charge flows: \(I_{\text{enc}} = 0\). Same loop, two answers — contradiction.

Maxwell's repair: between the plates the electric field is growing, and a changing electric flux acts exactly like a current — the displacement current (Verschiebungsstrom):

\[ I_d = \varepsilon_0\,\frac{\mathrm d\Phi_E}{\mathrm dt} \]

One can check it has the same value as the wire current \(I\) (since \(\Phi_E = Q/\varepsilon_0\) between the plates, \(I_d = \mathrm dQ/\mathrm dt = I\)). Adding it to Ampère's law makes both surfaces agree — and, far more importantly, makes changing E-fields create B-fields, the symmetric partner to Faraday. That closes a loop with world-changing consequences.

11.2 The four equations, in plain language

LawIntegral formPlain-language reading
Gauss (electric)\( \displaystyle\oint_A \vec E\cdot\mathrm d\vec A = \frac{Q_{\text{enc}}}{\varepsilon_0} \)Electric field lines start and end on charges; enclosed charge fixes the net outflow.
Gauss (magnetic)\( \displaystyle\oint_A \vec B\cdot\mathrm d\vec A = 0 \)Magnetic field lines never start or end — no magnetic charges; the lines always close.
Faraday\( \displaystyle\oint \vec E\cdot\mathrm d\vec s = -\frac{\mathrm d\Phi_B}{\mathrm dt} \)A changing magnetic flux stirs an electric vortex field around it (induction, with Lenz's minus).
Ampère–Maxwell\( \displaystyle\oint \vec B\cdot\mathrm d\vec s = \mu_0 I_{\text{enc}} + \mu_0\varepsilon_0 \frac{\mathrm d\Phi_E}{\mathrm dt} \)Currents and changing electric flux stir a magnetic vortex field around them.

Plus the Lorentz force \(\vec F = q(\vec E + \vec v\times\vec B)\) to connect fields back to mechanics, and the material relations \(\vec D = \varepsilon\vec E\), \(\vec B = \mu\vec H\), \(\vec J = \sigma\vec E\). That is, without exaggeration, all of classical electrical engineering: every circuit law you have learned is a limiting case (KCL from continuity, KVL from Faraday with negligible \(\mathrm d\Phi/\mathrm dt\), \(R\), \(L\), \(C\) from the three material/geometry formulas).

🔑Key idea: the self-sustaining loop

Look at the last two equations as a pair: a changing \(\vec B\) creates a curling \(\vec E\) (Faraday); a changing \(\vec E\) creates a curling \(\vec B\) (Maxwell's new term). Neither needs charges or wires — the fields can regenerate each other in empty space, each one's change sourcing the other, leapfrogging forward. That is an electromagnetic wave. Without the displacement current there is no leapfrog, no radio, no light.

⚠️When can you still use circuit theory?

Kirchhoff's laws assume fields stay inside the components and signals arrive everywhere in the circuit instantly. Rule of thumb: circuit theory is safe while the physical size of the circuit is far below the wavelength \(\lambda = c/f\) (say, < λ/10). At 50 Hz, λ = 6000 km — a whole national grid is a "circuit". At 2.4 GHz, λ = 12.5 cm — a 3 cm PCB trace is a transmission line, not a wire (Module 21). When the lumped picture fails, Maxwell is what remains.

12. Electromagnetic waves — and the identity of light

12.1 The speed falls out of the constants

Combine Faraday and Ampère–Maxwell in charge-free space and (two lines of vector calculus we happily skip) each field component obeys a wave equation with propagation speed \(c = 1/\sqrt{\mu_0\varepsilon_0}\). Maxwell computed that number in 1864 from two tabletop constants — one measured with capacitors, one with current balances:

Worked example Maxwell's 1864 punchline, recomputed

Compute \(c = 1/\sqrt{\mu_0 \varepsilon_0}\) from \(\mu_0 = 4\pi\cdot10^{-7}\,\mathrm{Vs/Am}\) and \(\varepsilon_0 = 8.854\cdot10^{-12}\,\mathrm{As/Vm}\).

Step 1 — the product: \( \mu_0\varepsilon_0 = 1.2566\cdot10^{-6} \cdot 8.854\cdot10^{-12} = 1.1127\cdot10^{-17}\,\mathrm{s^2/m^2} \). (Check the units: Vs/Am · As/Vm = s²/m² ✓)
Step 2 — invert the root: \[ c = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1.1127\cdot10^{-17}}} = \frac{1}{3.336\cdot10^{-9}\,\mathrm{s/m}} \approx 2.998\cdot10^{8}\,\mathrm{m/s}. \]
299 800 km/s — the measured speed of light, emerging from electrical bench measurements that had nothing to do with optics. Maxwell: "we can scarcely avoid the inference that light itself is an electromagnetic disturbance." Hertz confirmed it experimentally in 1887; radio, and everything wireless since, is the engineering follow-up.

12.2 Anatomy of a plane wave

The simplest solution is the plane wave (ebene Welle): \(\vec E\) and \(\vec B\) oscillate in phase, perpendicular to each other and to the propagation direction \(\vec k\) — a transverse wave, \(\vec E \perp \vec B \perp \vec k\). Their magnitudes are locked together, and their ratio in free space is a resistance:

\[ \frac{E}{B} = c, \qquad Z_0 = \frac{E}{H} = \sqrt{\frac{\mu_0}{\varepsilon_0}} \approx 377\,\Omega \]

— \(Z_0\) is the impedance of free space (Feldwellenwiderstand).

That 377 Ω is a real design quantity: antennas are impedance transformers between a 50 Ω cable and 377 Ω space (Module 21). The wave carries power — sunlight delivers ≈ 1.37 kW/m² above the atmosphere, corresponding to E-field amplitudes around 1 kV/m.

13. Fields in practice — skin effect and shielding

13.1 Skin effect

Feed AC into a thick conductor and induction acts inside the metal itself: the alternating flux inside the conductor drives eddy voltages that oppose current flow in the center and reinforce it at the surface (Lenz again). The current crowds into a surface layer — the skin effect (Skineffekt / Stromverdrängung) — with exponentially decaying density characterized by the skin depth (Eindringtiefe):

\[ \boxed{\;\delta = \sqrt{\frac{2\rho}{\omega\mu}} = \sqrt{\frac{\rho}{\pi f \mu}}\;} \]

Beyond a few \(\delta\) the conductor's interior is electrically dead weight: the effective resistance of a fat wire rises as \(\sqrt f\).

Worked example Skin depth in copper: 50 Hz vs 1 MHz

Copper: \(\rho = 1.78\cdot10^{-8}\,\Omega\mathrm{m}\), \(\mu \approx \mu_0\). Find \(\delta\) at 50 Hz and at 1 MHz.

Step 1 — 50 Hz: \[ \delta = \sqrt{\frac{1.78\cdot10^{-8}}{\pi \cdot 50 \cdot 4\pi\cdot10^{-7}}} = \sqrt{9.0\cdot10^{-5}\,\mathrm{m^2}} \approx 9.5\,\mathrm{mm}. \]
Step 2 — 1 MHz: \(\delta \propto 1/\sqrt f\), so scale by \(\sqrt{50/10^6}\): \( \delta = 9.5\,\mathrm{mm}\cdot 7.07\cdot10^{-3} \approx 67\,\mathrm{\mu m} \) — a hair's breadth.
At 50 Hz, wires up to ~2 cm diameter conduct almost uniformly — skin effect barely matters in your house. At 1 MHz only the outer 67 µm works. Engineering answers: litz wire (Hochfrequenzlitze: hundreds of individually insulated 50 µm strands, woven so each takes a turn at the surface) for HF transformers and induction cooktops; hollow tubes for high-power RF and grid busbars (the core would carry nothing anyway — save the copper); silver plating of microwave waveguides, where only the surface layer counts.

13.2 Shielding in one paragraph

Electric fields are easy to stop: any grounded conductive enclosure is a Faraday cage — surface charges rearrange and terminate the field lines (Section 4). Magnetic fields are the stubborn ones. Static/low-frequency \(B\)-fields must be detoured through high-\(\mu_r\) material (mu-metal cans around sensitive transformers and photomultipliers). At higher frequencies eddy currents come to the rescue: an AC field hitting a conductive sheet induces currents that oppose it (Lenz), and a shield a few skin depths thick reflects/absorbs the wave — which is why an ordinary aluminum enclosure that is useless at 50 Hz shields excellently at 100 MHz. Real-world EMC — apertures, seams, cable entries, why the mesh in your microwave oven door stops 2.45 GHz but passes light — is Module 21's territory.

15. Exercises

Sketch the field picture before touching a formula — in field problems, the drawing is half the solution.

E1Two small spheres carry \(+2\,\mathrm{\mu C}\) and \(-2\,\mathrm{\mu C}\) at 10 cm separation. Find the force between them and the E-field magnitude at the midpoint. easy

Show solution
Force: \( F = \frac{q_1 q_2}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r^2} = 8.99\cdot10^9 \cdot \frac{(2\cdot10^{-6})^2}{0.1^2} \approx 3.6\,\mathrm{N} \), attractive — the weight of a 370 g object, from micrograms of surplus electrons. Midpoint: both fields point the same way (from + toward −), each \( E = 8.99\cdot10^9\cdot\frac{2\cdot10^{-6}}{0.05^2} = 7.2\,\mathrm{MV/m} \), so \(E_{\text{total}} \approx 14.4\,\mathrm{MV/m}\) — far above air breakdown (3 MV/m): in reality this pair would spark long before reaching 2 µC.

E2A parallel-plate capacitor: \(A = 50\,\mathrm{cm^2}\), \(d = 0.5\,\mathrm{mm}\), FR-4 dielectric (\(\varepsilon_r = 4.5\)), charged to 12 V. Find \(C\), \(E\), \(Q\) and \(W\). easy

Show solution
\( C = \frac{\varepsilon_0\varepsilon_r A}{d} = \frac{8.854\cdot10^{-12}\cdot 4.5 \cdot 5\cdot10^{-3}}{5\cdot10^{-4}} \approx 398\,\mathrm{pF} \approx 0.4\,\mathrm{nF} \). \( E = U/d = 12/0.0005 = 24\,\mathrm{kV/m} \) — comfortably below FR-4's ~20 kV/mm rating. \( Q = CU \approx 4.8\,\mathrm{nC} \); \( W = \tfrac12 C U^2 \approx 29\,\mathrm{nJ} \). Remark: this is roughly the parasitic capacitance of two facing copper pours on a PCB — "capacitors" exist whether you draw them in the schematic or not.

E3An overhead line carries 1000 A. At what distance is its B-field equal to Earth's (~50 µT)? And what force per meter acts between two such conductors of a duplex bundle 40 cm apart carrying the same 1000 A in the same direction? medium

Show solution
\( B = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi r} \Rightarrow r = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi B} = \frac{2\cdot10^{-7}\cdot 1000}{5\cdot10^{-5}} = 4\,\mathrm{m} \). Under the tower (~10–20 m) the line's field is below Earth's own — one reason public-exposure limits (100 µT at 50 Hz) are rarely approached. Bundle force: \( F/l = \frac{\mu_0 I^2}{2\pi a} = \frac{2\cdot10^{-7}\cdot 10^6}{0.4} = 0.5\,\mathrm{N/m} \), attractive (parallel currents) — modest in operation, but the spacers holding bundle conductors apart are sized for the short-circuit case, where 20× the current means 400× the force.

E4A relay coil: \(N = 2000\) turns, core cross-section \(1\,\mathrm{cm^2}\), magnetic path length 5 cm, \(\mu_r = 800\). Find \(L\), and the induced voltage spike if opening the switch forces its 50 mA coil current to zero in 1 ms. medium

Show solution
\( L = \frac{\mu_0\mu_r N^2 A}{l} = \frac{4\pi\cdot10^{-7}\cdot 800 \cdot 4\cdot10^6 \cdot 10^{-4}}{0.05} \approx 8.0\,\mathrm{H} \) — iron makes henries easy. Spike: \( u_L = L\,\frac{\Delta i}{\Delta t} = 8.0 \cdot \frac{0.05}{10^{-3}} = 400\,\mathrm{V} \) across a coil fed from maybe 12 V! That spike destroys the transistor switching the relay — hence the flyback diode (Module 08), which gives the current a path and clamps the voltage to ~0.7 V. Sanity check: stored energy \( \tfrac12 L i^2 = 10\,\mathrm{mJ} \) has to go somewhere; the diode lets it dissipate gently in the coil resistance.

E5A generator loop: 200 turns of area \(80\,\mathrm{cm^2}\) rotate at 3000 rpm in a 0.9 T field. Find the peak induced voltage and the frequency. medium

Show solution
\( \omega = 2\pi n = 2\pi\cdot 50 = 314\,\mathrm{s^{-1}} \) (3000 rpm = 50 rev/s), so \( f = 50\,\mathrm{Hz} \) — not a coincidence: European grid frequency comes precisely from 3000 rpm two-pole machines. \( \Phi = BA\cos\omega t \Rightarrow u = N B A \omega \sin\omega t \), peak \( \hat u = 200 \cdot 0.9 \cdot 8\cdot10^{-3}\cdot 314 \approx 452\,\mathrm{V} \), i.e. \(U_{\text{rms}} \approx 320\,\mathrm{V}\). Note the design levers: more turns, more field, more area, or more speed — every generator engineer trades among exactly these four.

E6Using Gauss's law with a cylindrical surface, derive the capacitance per meter of a coaxial cable (inner radius \(r_i\), shield radius \(r_a\), dielectric \(\varepsilon_r\)), then evaluate for RG-58: \(r_i = 0.45\,\mathrm{mm}\), \(r_a = 1.47\,\mathrm{mm}\), \(\varepsilon_r = 2.3\) (PE). challenge

Show solution
Put line charge \(q'\) (C/m) on the inner conductor. A coaxial Gaussian cylinder of radius \(r\) and length \(l\) has flux only through its curved wall: \( E \cdot 2\pi r l = q' l/\varepsilon \Rightarrow E(r) = \frac{q'}{2\pi\varepsilon r} \). Integrate for the voltage: \( U = \int_{r_i}^{r_a} E\,\mathrm dr = \frac{q'}{2\pi\varepsilon}\ln\frac{r_a}{r_i} \). Hence \[ C' = \frac{q'}{U} = \frac{2\pi\varepsilon_0\varepsilon_r}{\ln(r_a/r_i)}. \] Numbers: \( \ln(1.47/0.45) = \ln 3.27 = 1.184 \), so \( C' = \frac{2\pi\cdot 8.854\cdot10^{-12}\cdot 2.3}{1.184} \approx 108\,\mathrm{pF/m} \) — within a few percent of the RG-58 datasheet value (~100 pF/m). The same logarithm reappears in the cable's inductance and combines into its 50 Ω characteristic impedance (Module 21): field theory quietly underwrites every datasheet.
