🔬 Electronics · Module 08

Semiconductor Devices · Halbleiterbauelemente

Every resistor obeys \(U = RI\); a diode does not — and that disobedience built the modern world. This module explains how a pinch of phosphorus turns near-insulating silicon into a material we command at will, how two such regions in contact create a one-way valve for current, and how three of them create the transistor: the most manufactured object in human history, at roughly 1022 units and counting.

Corresponds to: Werkstoffe der Elektrotechnik / Halbleiterbauelemente (3rd–4th semester) Prerequisites: Modules 04, 07 Study time: ≈ 6–8 h

1. Energy bands — why copper conducts and glass does not

Module 04 treated resistance as a given material property. Now we ask the deeper question: what decides whether a material conducts at all? The answer is quantum mechanics, but the working picture is simple enough to draw.

In an isolated atom, electrons occupy discrete energy levels. Bring \(N \sim 10^{23}\) atoms together into a crystal and the Pauli exclusion principle forbids them all from sharing identical states: each atomic level splits into \(N\) closely spaced levels — an effectively continuous energy band (Energieband). Between bands lie forbidden ranges of energy: band gaps (Bandlücken). Two bands matter for conduction:

  • The valence band (Valenzband): the highest band that is (nearly) full at low temperature — these electrons form the chemical bonds.
  • The conduction band (Leitungsband): the next band up — electrons here are detached from any particular bond and can move freely through the crystal.
🔑Key idea: a full band carries no current

Conduction requires electrons that can change state — accelerate, i.e. move to a neighboring energy level. In a completely full band there are no empty states to move into, so a full valence band contributes exactly zero current, no matter how many electrons it contains. Conduction needs either a partially filled band, or electrons lifted into the (empty) conduction band.

💡Intuition: the parking garage

A completely full parking deck allows no car to move — bumper to bumper, zero traffic, even though it holds hundreds of cars. A completely empty deck also has zero traffic — no cars. Traffic needs cars and free spaces. Lift one car to the empty upper deck (conduction band): it drives freely. And on the full lower deck, the gap it left behind can now be filled by a neighbor, whose spot is filled by the next neighbor… the empty space migrates like a positive particle. Hold that thought — it becomes the "hole" in Section 2.

The three classes of solids now follow from one number, the gap width \(E_g\), compared with the available thermal energy \(kT \approx 25.9\,\mathrm{meV}\) at 300 K:

  • Metal (Leiter): valence and conduction bands overlap (or the top band is half-full). Free electrons in abundance (\(\sim 10^{23}\,\mathrm{cm^{-3}}\)) at any temperature.
  • Insulator (Isolator): huge gap — quartz glass (SiO₂) has \(E_g \approx 9\,\mathrm{eV}\), some 350 times \(kT\). Thermal excitation across it is hopeless; essentially zero carriers.
  • Semiconductor (Halbleiter): a small gap — silicon has \(E_g = 1.12\,\mathrm{eV}\). Still ≫ \(kT\), so very few electrons make the jump… but not none, and the number responds violently to temperature, light, and — decisively — to impurities.

2. Silicon and intrinsic conduction

2.1 The crystal

Silicon sits in group IV of the periodic table: four valence electrons. In the crystal (diamond lattice), each atom bonds covalently (kovalente Bindung) to four neighbors, sharing one electron pair per bond. Every valence electron is spoken for: the valence band is exactly full, the conduction band exactly empty at \(T = 0\). Perfect cold silicon is an insulator. The atomic density is worth memorizing as a reference point:

\[ n_{\mathrm{Si}} \approx 5 \cdot 10^{22}\ \mathrm{atoms/cm^3}. \]

2.2 Electron–hole pairs

At room temperature, lattice vibrations occasionally concentrate enough energy (\(\ge E_g\)) on one bond to break it. The freed electron enters the conduction band; the broken bond it leaves behind is a hole (Loch, Defektelektron) — an empty state in the valence band. A neighboring bound electron can hop into the hole, moving the hole one site over: the hole migrates through the crystal like a positive mobile charge (\(+e\)), exactly like the empty parking spot. Both carrier types conduct; this is intrinsic conduction (Eigenleitung), and generation is balanced by recombination (Rekombination) — an electron falling back into a hole, annihilating both.

The equilibrium intrinsic carrier density (Eigenleitungsdichte) follows a Boltzmann-type law:

\[ n_i(T) \;\propto\; T^{3/2}\, e^{-E_g / 2kT}, \qquad n_i(300\,\mathrm{K}) \approx 10^{10}\ \mathrm{cm^{-3}} \ \text{for Si}. \]

Put that in perspective: one carrier pair per \(5 \cdot 10^{12}\) atoms. Intrinsic silicon has a resistivity of about \(2.3 \cdot 10^{5}\,\Omega\,\mathrm{cm}\) — closer to glass than to copper. And because of the exponential, \(n_i\) roughly doubles every 9 K near room temperature. That thermal sensitivity haunts every semiconductor device: leakage currents double per ~10 K, and it is why power transistors carry heatsinks.

3. Doping — the trick that built an industry

Here is the pivotal idea of the entire module. Replace one silicon atom in ten million with a group-V atom — phosphorus or arsenic, five valence electrons. Four electrons slot into the four covalent bonds; the fifth has no bond to join. It stays only weakly bound to its atom (~45 meV, easily freed by \(kT\)), so at room temperature essentially every such atom has donated one electron to the conduction band. Group-V dopants are therefore donors (Donatoren), and the result is n-type silicon (n for the negative carriers).

Symmetrically, a group-III atom (boron: three valence electrons) leaves one bond unsaturated — a ready-made hole that detaches at room temperature. Group-III dopants accept an electron from the valence band: acceptors (Akzeptoren), producing p-type silicon.

With donor density \(N_D \gg n_i\) (typical: \(10^{14}\)…\(10^{20}\,\mathrm{cm^{-3}}\)), the electron density is simply \(n \approx N_D\) — set by the chemist, not by temperature. The carriers supplied by doping are the majority carriers (Majoritätsträger). The opposite kind still exists in tiny numbers — the minority carriers (Minoritätsträger) — governed in equilibrium by the elegant mass-action law (Massenwirkungsgesetz):

\[ \boxed{\; n \cdot p = n_i^2 \;} \]

Doping one way up pushes the other kind down, like an acid-base equilibrium.

Worked example Carrier census in n-type silicon

A wafer is doped with phosphorus at \(N_D = 10^{16}\,\mathrm{cm^{-3}}\). Find the carrier densities and the doping dilution, at 300 K.

Step 1 — majority carriers: all donors ionized, and \(N_D \gg n_i\): \( n \approx N_D = 10^{16}\,\mathrm{cm^{-3}} \) — a million times more than intrinsic.
Step 2 — minority carriers (mass action): \( p = n_i^2 / n = (10^{10})^2 / 10^{16} = 10^{4}\,\mathrm{cm^{-3}} \). Twelve orders of magnitude fewer holes than electrons!
Step 3 — dilution: \( N_D / n_{\mathrm{Si}} = 10^{16} / (5\cdot 10^{22}) = 1 : 5\,000\,000 \). One foreign atom per five million silicon atoms rules the material's electrical personality.
\(n \approx 10^{16}\), \(p \approx 10^{4}\,\mathrm{cm^{-3}}\). Sanity check: \(n\,p = 10^{20} = n_i^2\) ✓ — and conductivity rose by a factor ~10⁶ from a 0.2 ppm impurity.
🔑Key idea: purity + precision = the industry

Doping only works because everything else is absent. If random impurities floated around at 1 ppm, a deliberate 0.2 ppm phosphorus dose would drown in noise. So the industry first purifies silicon to 99.999 999 999 % ("eleven nines" — electronic grade, the purest material mass-produced by humankind: one foreign atom per ~1011), pulls it into a single flawless crystal (Czochralski process, Kristallziehen), and only then re-contaminates it — on purpose, atom-species by atom-species, in patterned regions a few nanometers deep. A chip is precisely choreographed dirt in ultra-clean crystal. That, not any single device, is the semiconductor industry's core trick.

4. Two ways charge moves: drift and diffusion

Semiconductors support two distinct transport mechanisms, and the pn junction (next section) is a tug-of-war between them.

4.1 Drift — pushed by a field

Drift (Driftstrom) is the familiar mechanism behind Ohm's law: an electric field \(E\) accelerates carriers between collisions, producing an average drift velocity \(v = \mu E\), with mobility \(\mu\) (Beweglichkeit; Si: \(\mu_n \approx 1350\), \(\mu_p \approx 480\ \mathrm{cm^2/(V\,s)}\) — electrons are ~3× nimbler than holes, one reason n-channel devices dominate). The conductivity with both carrier types:

\[ \sigma = e\,(n\,\mu_n + p\,\mu_p). \]

4.2 Diffusion — pushed by crowding

Diffusion (Diffusionsstrom) needs no field at all: wherever carrier concentration is uneven, random thermal motion produces a net flow from crowded to empty regions — the same physics as perfume spreading through a room. The current density is proportional to the concentration gradient:

\[ J_{n,\text{diff}} = e\,D_n \frac{\mathrm{d}n}{\mathrm{d}x}, \qquad D = \mu \, U_T \ \ \text{(Einstein relation)}. \]

The Einstein relation smuggles in the single most important scale of this module, the thermal voltage (Temperaturspannung):

\[ \boxed{\;U_T = \frac{kT}{e} \approx 25.9\,\mathrm{mV} \ \text{at } 300\,\mathrm{K}\;} \]

Memorize "about 25 mV at room temperature". It will reappear in every exponential from here to Module 09.

5. The pn junction — what happens at contact

Now the decisive experiment: dope the left half of one crystal p-type, the right half n-type. At the metallurgical junction, an enormous concentration cliff appears — \(10^{16}\) electrons/cm³ on the right, \(10^{4}\) on the left. Diffusion does what diffusion does:

  1. Electrons pour from n to p, holes from p to n, annihilating each other near the junction.
  2. But every departing carrier leaves its ionized dopant atom behind — and those are locked in the lattice. A zone empty of mobile carriers but full of fixed charge forms: the depletion region or space-charge region (Raumladungszone), negative acceptor ions on the p side, positive donor ions on the n side.
  3. This charge double-layer creates an electric field pointing from n to p — which drives a drift current exactly opposite to the diffusion. The field grows until drift cancels diffusion: dynamic equilibrium, zero net current.

The equilibrium potential step, the built-in voltage (Diffusionsspannung), follows from balancing the two currents — and lands, satisfyingly, on our thermal voltage:

\[ \boxed{\; U_D = U_T \ln \frac{N_A N_D}{n_i^2} \;} \]
Worked example Built-in voltage and depletion width

Both sides doped at \(N_A = N_D = 10^{16}\,\mathrm{cm^{-3}}\), 300 K. Find \(U_D\) and the depletion width \(w = \sqrt{\tfrac{2\varepsilon_{\mathrm{Si}} U_D}{e}\left(\tfrac{1}{N_A}+\tfrac{1}{N_D}\right)}\), with \(\varepsilon_{\mathrm{Si}} = 11.7\,\varepsilon_0 \approx 1.04\cdot10^{-10}\,\mathrm{F/m}\).

Step 1 — built-in voltage: \( U_D = 0.0259 \cdot \ln\frac{10^{16}\cdot 10^{16}}{10^{20}} = 0.0259 \cdot \ln(10^{12}) = 0.0259 \cdot 27.6 \approx 0.72\,\mathrm{V}. \)
Step 2 — depletion width (SI units, \(N = 10^{22}\,\mathrm{m^{-3}}\)): \( w = \sqrt{\frac{2 \cdot 1.04\cdot 10^{-10} \cdot 0.72}{1.6\cdot 10^{-19}} \cdot 2\cdot10^{-22}} \approx 0.43\,\mathrm{µm}. \)
\(U_D \approx 0.72\,\mathrm{V}\), \(w \approx 0.43\,\mathrm{µm}\) — there is the famous "0.7 volts", hiding in a logarithm of doping densities. Note how weakly \(U_D\) depends on doping: 100× more dopant adds only \(U_T \ln 10^4 \approx 0.24\,\mathrm{V}\).
🚫Classic pitfall: "so a diode is a 0.7 V battery?"

No. You cannot measure \(U_D\) with a voltmeter across an unbiased diode, and it can never drive a current through an external load — the contact potentials where the probes touch the p and n material cancel it exactly. Otherwise a diode in a loop of wire would be a perpetual-motion machine. \(U_D\) is an internal barrier; energy conservation survives.

6. Bias and the diode equation

6.1 Tilting the barrier

Everything about the diode follows from one sentence: an external voltage adds to or subtracts from the barrier.

  • Forward bias (Durchlassrichtung, + on p): the external voltage \(U\) lowers the barrier to \(U_D - U\). Since the number of majority carriers able to climb a barrier follows a Boltzmann factor \(e^{-\Delta W/kT}\), the diffusion flood grows as \(e^{U/U_T}\) — exponentially. The depletion region narrows.
  • Reverse bias (Sperrrichtung, − on p): the barrier rises to \(U_D + |U|\), choking diffusion entirely. The depletion region widens. All that remains is the drift of thermally generated minority carriers that wander into the field and get swept across — a tiny, voltage-independent leakage: the saturation current \(I_S\) (Sättigungssperrstrom), picoamps to nanoamps for small silicon diodes.

Working out the minority-carrier diffusion quantitatively (Shockley, 1949) gives the celebrated diode equation:

\[ \boxed{\; I = I_S \left( e^{\,U / (n\,U_T)} - 1 \right) \;} \]

with the ideality factor \(n \approx 1\dots 2\) (Emissionskoeffizient) absorbing recombination effects in real devices. For \(U \gg U_T\) the −1 vanishes (pure exponential); for \(U \ll -U_T\), \(I \to -I_S\) (flat leakage floor).

🔑Key idea: 60 mV per decade

Take the equation's logarithm: every increase of \(U\) by \(n\,U_T \ln 10 \approx 60\,\mathrm{mV}\) (for \(n=1\)) multiplies the current by ten. From 0.5 V to 0.7 V — a mere 0.2 V — the current of a small diode grows by over three orders of magnitude, from microamps to milliamps. This violent steepness is why the diode acts as a switch, and why the "on-voltage" looks so constant: currents from 1 mA to 1 A all crowd into a ~120 mV window around 0.7 V.

6.2 The "0.7 V knee" — a convention, not physics

Datasheet and textbook culture speaks of a silicon diode "turning on at 0.7 V" (Schleusenspannung, knee/threshold voltage). Be clear about what that is: the exponential has no threshold — at 0.4 V a silicon diode conducts happily, just in microamps. "0.7 V" merely marks where the current reaches typical circuit magnitudes (mA…A) for typical silicon junction sizes. Germanium diodes "turn on" near 0.3 V, Schottky diodes near 0.3 V, LEDs between 1.8 and 3.3 V — same equation, different \(I_S\). The knee is a modeling convention we adopt in Section 9 because it makes hand analysis linear.

6.3 Temperature: the −2 mV/K rule

Two competing effects set the temperature behavior at constant current. \(U_T\) grows with \(T\) (would push \(U_F\) up), but \(I_S \propto n_i^2 \propto e^{-E_g/kT}\) grows much faster (pushes \(U_F\) down). The net result for silicon:

\[ \left.\frac{\mathrm{d}U_F}{\mathrm{d}T}\right|_{I=\text{const}} \approx -2\,\mathrm{mV/K}. \]

Heat a diode from 25 °C to 125 °C and its forward voltage drops ~0.2 V. Consequences: a forward-biased junction is a cheap, linear thermometer (used inside every CPU's thermal sensor, Module 15); and paralleling diodes is dangerous — the hottest one steals ever more current (thermal runaway, thermisches Weglaufen).

On the linear scale you see the apparent "knee"; switch to the log scale and the exponential becomes a perfect straight line at \(n\,U_T \ln 10\) mV per decade — proof there is no threshold. Then raise the temperature and watch the curve slide left (≈ −2 mV/K at fixed current): hotter diodes conduct at lower voltage because \(I_S\) explodes with \(T\).

7. Reverse breakdown and the Zener diode

Raise the reverse voltage far enough and the diode abruptly conducts again: breakdown (Durchbruch). Two distinct mechanisms, worth keeping apart:

  • Avalanche breakdown (Lawinendurchbruch): in lightly doped, wide depletion regions, a stray minority carrier gains enough kinetic energy from the field to knock a new electron–hole pair out of the lattice (impact ionization); the pair accelerates and ionizes further — a carrier avalanche. Dominant above ≈ 6 V. Breakdown voltage rises with temperature (hotter lattice → more collisions → carriers gain energy less easily).
  • Zener effect (Zener-Effekt): in heavily doped junctions the depletion region is so thin (< 10 nm) that valence electrons quantum-tunnel directly across the gap. Dominant below ≈ 5 V. Breakdown voltage falls with temperature.

Around 5.6–6.2 V both mechanisms mix and the temperature coefficients nearly cancel — which is why precision reference "Zeners" cluster at those voltages. Colloquially all breakdown diodes are called Zener diodes (Z-Diode) regardless of mechanism.

Breakdown is not destructive by itself — the diode is destroyed only if \(P = U_Z I_Z\) overheats the junction. Z-diodes are built to live there: their reverse characteristic is nearly vertical at \(U_Z\), i.e. the voltage barely changes over a wide current range. That makes an almost-ideal voltage clamp: the classic shunt regulator (Parallelstabilisierung) — series resistor \(R_V\) from the raw supply, Z-diode to ground, load in parallel with the diode. The resistor absorbs input swings; the diode absorbs load swings.

Worked example Designing a Zener shunt regulator

A 5.6 V rail must be derived from a raw supply of 12 V ± 2 V. The load draws 5…20 mA. The Z-diode (\(U_Z = 5.6\,\mathrm{V}\), dynamic resistance \(r_Z = 10\,\Omega\)) needs \(I_Z \ge 5\,\mathrm{mA}\) to regulate. Size \(R_V\), check worst-case dissipation, and estimate line regulation.

Step 1 — worst case for starving the diode: minimum input (10 V) and maximum load (20 mA). \(R_V\) must still supply 20 + 5 = 25 mA: \( R_V \le \frac{10 - 5.6}{25\,\mathrm{mA}} = 176\,\Omega \;\Rightarrow\; \) choose the standard value \(R_V = 150\,\Omega\).
Step 2 — worst case for heat: maximum input (14 V) and minimum load (5 mA): \( I_{R} = \frac{14-5.6}{150} = 56\,\mathrm{mA} \Rightarrow I_Z = 51\,\mathrm{mA} \), so \(P_Z = 5.6 \cdot 0.051 \approx 0.29\,\mathrm{W}\) → use a 0.5 W Zener. Resistor: \(P_R = 0.056^2 \cdot 150 \approx 0.47\,\mathrm{W}\) → 1 W part.
Step 3 — line regulation: for input ripple, the circuit is a voltage divider \(r_Z\) against \(R_V\): \( \Delta U_{out} = \Delta U_{in}\,\frac{r_Z}{R_V + r_Z} = 4\,\mathrm{V} \cdot \frac{10}{160} = 0.25\,\mathrm{V} \) — a 16:1 improvement.
\(R_V = 150\,\Omega\) (1 W), Zener ≥ 0.5 W, output 5.6 V ± ~0.13 V. Sanity check: efficiency at nominal is poor (~30 %) — Zener shunts are for low-power references; real supplies use them to feed a regulator, or use switching converters (Module 18).

8. The diode zoo — one junction, many personalities

8.1 Rectifier and signal diodes

The bread-and-butter rectifier (Gleichrichter) turns AC into DC (the circuits come in Module 18). Workhorse example 1N4007: 1000 V reverse, 1 A forward — but slow: stored minority charge takes microseconds to clear when switching off (reverse recovery, Sperrverzögerung), fine at 50 Hz, fatal at 100 kHz. Signal diodes like the 1N4148 trade current capability (200 mA) for 4 ns speed.

8.2 Schottky — drop the p, gain speed

Replace the p region with a metal contact and you get the Schottky diode (Schottky-Diode): still a rectifying barrier, but conduction is by majority carriers only. Two gifts follow: a low knee (~0.2–0.4 V, less conduction loss) and no stored minority charge — essentially zero reverse recovery, hence its home in every switch-mode power supply. The price: larger leakage \(I_S\) (that is why the knee is low — same Shockley equation, bigger \(I_S\)) and modest reverse-voltage ratings.

8.3 The LED — bandgap becomes color

When an electron recombines with a hole, energy \(\approx E_g\) is released. In silicon (an indirect-gap material) it is wasted as lattice vibration — there will never be a silicon LED. In direct-gap materials (GaAs, GaN…) it leaves as a photon. The emitted wavelength is set by the bandgap — chemistry chooses the color:

\[ \lambda = \frac{h c}{E_g} \approx \frac{1240\,\mathrm{nm}}{E_g / \mathrm{eV}}. \]

GaAs (1.42 eV) → 870 nm infrared; AlInGaP (~2.0 eV) → red; InGaN (~2.7 eV) → blue. The forward voltage tracks the bandgap — an LED's color tells you its \(U_F\). White LEDs are blue InGaN chips coated with a yellow phosphor. With luminous efficacies beyond 200 lm/W versus ~15 lm/W for incandescent bulbs, the LED quietly became one of history's great efficiency revolutions.

📜The thirty-year hunt for blue

Red LEDs existed from 1962 (Holonyak), green soon after — but white light needs blue, and growing p-type GaN defeated the industry for three decades. Isamu Akasaki, Hiroshi Amano and Shuji Nakamura cracked it in the early 1990s (buffer layers, magnesium activation, InGaN wells) and received the 2014 Nobel Prize in Physics. The committee's citation was unusually blunt about impact: "incandescent bulbs lit the 20th century; the 21st will be lit by LED lamps."

8.4 Photodiode and solar cell — the junction run backwards

The LED's inverse: a photon with \(h\nu \ge E_g\) absorbed in or near the depletion region creates an electron–hole pair, and the built-in field sweeps it apart before it can recombine — light in, current out. The photocurrent \(I_{ph}\) is proportional to illumination and simply shifts the whole diode characteristic downward:

\[ I = I_S\left(e^{U/(nU_T)} - 1\right) - I_{ph}. \]

Operated reverse-biased, it is a fast, linear light sensor: the photodiode (Fotodiode) in every optical receiver. Operated in the fourth quadrant (\(U > 0\), \(I < 0\): the device delivers power) it is a solar cell (Solarzelle). Its open-circuit voltage follows directly by setting \(I = 0\): \(U_{oc} = U_T \ln(I_{ph}/I_S + 1) \approx 0.55\dots0.7\,\mathrm{V}\) for silicon — which is why panels chain dozens of cells in series. Same junction as the LED, same equation, opposite energy flow: a small physics poem.

9. Analyzing circuits with a nonlinear element

Now a problem Module 04 never faced. Put a diode in series with a resistor \(R\) across a source \(U_q\). KVL still holds:

\[ U_q = R\,I + U_D(I), \qquad I = I_S\left(e^{U_D/(nU_T)}-1\right) \]

— one equation, but transcendental: no closed-form solution. Three practical attacks, in increasing order of laziness (a virtue in engineering):

9.1 The load line — solve by drawing

Rearrange KVL as \(I = (U_q - U_D)/R\): a straight line in the \(I\)–\(U\) plane — the load line (Arbeitsgerade) — from \((U_q, 0)\) to \((0, U_q/R)\). The circuit must satisfy this and the diode's curve; the intersection is the operating point (Arbeitspunkt). This graphical method predates calculators, but the picture it builds — component curve vs. circuit constraint — is how engineers still think about transistor biasing (Module 09).

🧪Interactive — load line & operating point simulation

Diode curve (blue) meets load line (amber); the dot is the operating point, computed numerically by bisection. Slide \(U_q\): the line shifts parallel. Slide \(R\): it pivots around \(U_q\) on the x-axis. Notice how weakly \(U_{OP}\) moves — the exponential pins it near 0.7 V, which is exactly why the constant-drop model (below) works so well. The readouts compare against the ideal "0.7 V model".

9.2 Iteration — solve by ping-pong

Guess \(U_D^{(0)} = 0.7\,\mathrm{V}\). Compute the current from the linear part: \(I^{(1)} = (U_q - U_D^{(0)})/R\). Update the diode voltage from the exponential part: \(U_D^{(1)} = n U_T \ln(I^{(1)}/I_S)\). Repeat. Because the logarithm is so flat, this converges to 4 digits in 2–3 rounds — SPICE does a Newton-flavored version of exactly this at every time step.

9.3 Piecewise-linear models — solve by simplifying

ModelForward behaviorUse when
Ideal switch\(U_D = 0\), any \(I \ge 0\)supply ≫ 0.7 V; topology questions
Constant drop\(U_D = 0.7\,\mathrm{V}\) (LED: 2.0 V etc.)standard hand analysis — the default
Drop + slope\(U_D = U_{F0} + r_D I\),  \(r_D = \dfrac{n\,U_T}{I_{OP}}\)ripple/small-signal around a known operating point (→ Module 09)

The small-signal resistance \(r_D = nU_T/I\) is a gem: at 1 mA a diode looks like ~26 Ω for small wiggles, at 10 mA like 2.6 Ω — derived by differentiating Shockley. This "linearize around the operating point" move is the master key to all of analog electronics.

Worked example The M04 LED resistor, revisited honestly

In Module 04 (E1) we sized \(R = 150\,\Omega\) for an LED on 5 V, assuming a constant 2.0 V drop at 20 mA. Reality: a red LED obeying Shockley with \(n\,U_T = 50\,\mathrm{mV}\) and exactly 2.000 V at 20 mA. What current actually flows if the supply sags to 4.5 V — ideal model vs. real diode?

Step 1 — constant-drop prediction: \( I = (4.5 - 2.0)/150 = 16.7\,\mathrm{mA} \).
Step 2 — iterate the real diode: at 16.7 mA the LED voltage is \( U^{(1)} = 2.0 + 0.05 \cdot \ln\frac{16.7}{20} = 2.0 - 0.009 = 1.991\,\mathrm{V} \). Recompute: \( I^{(2)} = (4.5 - 1.991)/150 = 16.73\,\mathrm{mA} \). Next update changes \(U\) by < 0.1 mV — converged after one round trip.
Step 3 — compare: ideal 16.67 mA vs. real 16.73 mA — a 0.4 % difference.
\(I \approx 16.7\,\mathrm{mA}\) either way. Moral: the constant-drop model is excellent because the exponential is steep — the diode voltage self-regulates. The resistor, not the diode model, dominates the answer. (This is also why you must never connect an LED straight to a stiff voltage source: with no \(R\), a ±5 % voltage error becomes a ±3× current error.)

10. The MOSFET — a capacitor that switches an ocean

Diodes conduct or block, but they cannot amplify — no third terminal. Enter the MOSFET (metal-oxide-semiconductor field-effect transistor, Feldeffekttransistor), today manufactured in greater numbers than any other object ever made. We describe the n-channel enhancement type (NMOS), the industry default.

10.1 Structure: gate oxide as a capacitor plate

Take a p-type substrate (Substrat, Bulk). Diffuse two heavily doped n⁺ islands: source and drain. Between them, on the surface, grow an ultrathin insulating SiO₂ layer (the 9 eV insulator of Fig. 1.1 — in modern CPUs barely 1–2 nm, a dozen atomic layers), and cap it with a conductive gate (Steuerelektrode). Gate + oxide + substrate form a plain plate capacitor (Module 07). With no gate voltage, source→drain sees two back-to-back pn junctions: no current path.

10.2 Inversion and the threshold voltage

Apply a positive gate–source voltage \(U_{GS}\). The capacitor charges: positive charge on the gate, and mirror-image negative charge in the silicon underneath — first by pushing holes away, then, beyond a critical voltage, by pulling a dense sheet of electrons to the surface. The p-type surface layer has been inverted to n-type: a conducting channel (Kanal) now bridges source and drain. The critical voltage is the threshold voltage \(V_{th}\) (Schwellenspannung): ~0.3–0.7 V in CPU logic, 2–4 V in power MOSFETs. The excess \(U_{GS} - V_{th}\) is the overdrive — it sets how much charge the channel holds.

10.3 The two operating regions

With the channel formed, apply drain–source voltage \(U_{DS}\) and current flows. Two regimes, with \(k = \mu_n C_{ox} \tfrac{W}{L}\) (the transconductance parameter — carrier mobility × oxide capacitance × geometry) :

  • Triode / ohmic region (Widerstandsbereich), \(U_{DS} < U_{GS} - V_{th}\): \[ I_D = k\left[(U_{GS}-V_{th})\,U_{DS} - \tfrac{1}{2}U_{DS}^2\right] \] For small \(U_{DS}\) this is a resistor whose value the gate controls: \(R_{DS(on)} \approx \dfrac{1}{k\,(U_{GS}-V_{th})}\).
  • Saturation region (Sättigungs-/Abschnürbereich), \(U_{DS} \ge U_{GS} - V_{th}\): the channel pinches off at the drain end (the local gate-to-channel voltage there drops below \(V_{th}\)); the current stops growing with \(U_{DS}\) and depends only on the gate: \[ \boxed{\; I_D = \frac{k}{2}\,(U_{GS} - V_{th})^2 \;} \] A gate-voltage-controlled current source — the amplifying regime (Module 09).

The boundary between the regions is the parabola \(I_D = \tfrac{k}{2}U_{DS}^2\). Plotting \(I_D\) vs. \(U_{DS}\) for several \(U_{GS}\) values gives the output characteristics (Ausgangskennlinienfeld); \(I_D\) vs. \(U_{GS}\) at fixed \(U_{DS}\) is the (quadratic) transfer characteristic (Übertragungskennlinie).

The dashed parabola is the triode/saturation boundary \(U_{DS} = U_{GS}-V_{th}\): left of it the MOSFET behaves as a gate-tunable resistor, right of it as a current source. Note the quadratic spacing of the curves — doubling the overdrive quadruples the current. Raise \(V_{th}\) and watch curves vanish as their gate drive falls below threshold.

10.4 Why it is the perfect switch

The gate is insulated by 9-eV-bandgap oxide: the DC gate current is femtoamps — the MOSFET is voltage-controlled, costing essentially zero drive power to hold on or off. (Charging/discharging the gate capacitance at every transition does cost energy — \(f\,C U^2\) switching loss, the battle of Module 11.) In the on-state a power MOSFET is just \(R_{DS(on)}\) — modern parts reach single-digit milliohms. Two practical details of discrete power MOSFETs: the body is internally tied to the source, which creates a parasitic body diode (Body-Diode) from source to drain — it means the switch blocks in only one direction, and power circuits exploit it as a free freewheeling diode (Module 18). And \(R_{DS(on)}\) roughly doubles from 25 °C to 125 °C — a rare mercy: paralleled MOSFETs share current stably (the hot one takes less), the exact opposite of diodes and BJTs.

⚠️Handle the gate with respect

The gift of the insulated gate is also its weakness. The oxide withstands only ±20 V or so (\(U_{GS,max}\) in the datasheet) and, being a near-perfect insulator, cannot bleed off static charge: a walk across a carpet delivers kilovolts of ESD — instant, invisible oxide puncture. Handle MOSFETs with ESD precautions, and never leave a gate floating in a circuit: an unconnected gate picks up stray charge capacitively and the transistor drifts half-on, overheats, and dies. Always give the gate a defined potential (a pull-down resistor costs one cent).

Worked example Switch shoot-out: MOSFET vs. BJT conduction loss

A 5 A load must be switched on the low side. Candidate A: MOSFET with \(R_{DS(on)} = 20\,\mathrm{m\Omega}\), driven from a logic pin (no DC gate current). Candidate B: BJT with \(U_{CE,sat} = 0.25\,\mathrm{V}\), needing \(I_B = I_C/10 = 0.5\,\mathrm{A}\) of base current from a 5 V pin through a resistor. Compare the losses.

Step 1 — MOSFET conduction loss: \( P = I^2 R_{DS(on)} = 25 \cdot 0.02 = 0.5\,\mathrm{W} \). Gate drive: ~0 W static.
Step 2 — BJT conduction loss: \( P = U_{CE,sat}\, I_C = 0.25 \cdot 5 = 1.25\,\mathrm{W} \) in the transistor.
Step 3 — BJT drive loss: the base path burns \( \approx 5\,\mathrm{V} \cdot 0.5\,\mathrm{A} = 2.5\,\mathrm{W} \) (base resistor + junction) — five times the MOSFET's total loss, spent merely on keeping the switch closed.
MOSFET: 0.5 W total. BJT: ≈ 3.75 W total. And the MOSFET's loss scales as \(I^2 R\) — halve the current or halve \(R_{DS(on)}\) (parallel two devices!) and it drops fast, while \(U_{CE,sat}\,I\) only falls linearly. This is why power switching is MOSFET country.

11. The BJT — the original transistor

The bipolar junction transistor (Bipolartransistor) came first (1947) and still owns niches the MOSFET cannot touch. Structure: three layers, npn (or pnp), i.e. two pn junctions back-to-back sharing an extremely thin middle layer — emitter (n⁺, heavily doped), base (p, ~0.1 µm thin!), collector (n).

Two diodes soldered back-to-back conduct nothing either way — the magic is entirely in the shared, thin base. Bias the base–emitter junction forward (\(U_{BE} \approx 0.7\,\mathrm{V}\)) and the base–collector junction in reverse. The emitter injects a torrent of electrons into the base, Shockley-style. Now the crucial geometry: the base is so thin that almost none of them find a hole to recombine with before they stumble into the reverse-biased collector junction — whose field gleefully sweeps them into the collector. This is transistor action: ~99+ % of the emitter current exits at the collector; only the ~1 % that recombines must be resupplied as base current.

Quantitatively, with the fraction \(\alpha \approx 0.99\dots0.998\) reaching the collector:

\[ I_C = \alpha\, I_E, \qquad I_C = \beta\, I_B \quad\text{with}\quad \beta = \frac{\alpha}{1-\alpha} \approx 100\dots300, \]

(\(\beta\), datasheet symbol \(h_{FE}\), Stromverstärkung) and the collector current still obeys the exponential of its controlling junction:

\[ I_C = I_S\, e^{\,U_{BE}/U_T}. \]

Operating regions: cutoff (Sperrbereich: BE off — no current), active (aktiver Bereich: BE on, BC reverse — current source, amplifiers live here), saturation (Sättigung: both junctions forward, \(U_{CE}\) collapses to \(U_{CE,sat} \approx 0.1\dots0.3\,\mathrm{V}\) — switch closed). Note the vocabulary trap: BJT "saturation" is the switched-ON region, whereas MOSFET "saturation" is the current-source region — a historical accident that costs exam points every year.

🚫Classic pitfall: designing around β

β is not a design parameter: it varies 3:1 between specimens of the same type, and further with current and temperature. \(I_C = \beta I_B\) tells you a guaranteed minimum drive for switching (use \(I_B \ge I_C/10\) to saturate hard), but analog bias points must never depend on β. Proper amplifier design (Module 09) uses feedback so that β only needs to be "large" — the exponential \(I_C(U_{BE})\) relation and the circuit's resistors set the operating point.

📜1947, Bell Labs — and where this module leads

Bardeen, Brattain and Shockley demonstrated the first (point-contact) transistor in December 1947 — Nobel Prize 1956; Shockley's junction theory gave us the equations in this module. 12. Datasheet literacy — reading the fine print

Real design starts at the datasheet (Datenblatt). The skills transfer across all devices:

  • Absolute maximum ratings (Grenzwerte) are destruction limits, not operating targets — exceed any one, even briefly, and the warranty (and often the die) is gone. Design to comfortable fractions: e.g. a 1N4007 rated \(U_{RRM} = 1000\,\mathrm{V}\) is used on 325 V peak mains with a 3× margin.
  • Derating (Leistungsminderung): ratings hold at 25 °C case temperature and shrink linearly above. The governing physics is a thermal Ohm's law: \(T_j = T_{amb} + P \cdot R_{th,ja}\) with junction limit \(T_{j,max}=150\dots175\,{}^\circ\mathrm{C}\). A "2 W" transistor in free air with \(R_{th,ja}=60\,\mathrm{K/W}\) may really handle only \((150-40)/60 \approx 1.8\,\mathrm{W}\) — and needs a heatsink for more.
  • Typical vs. guaranteed: "typ." columns are marketing; design against min/max. β of a BC547B: min 200, typ 290, max 450 — a 2.25:1 spread in the same grade.

14. Exercises

Work these on paper before opening the solutions — the struggle is the learning.

E1Compute the thermal voltage \(U_T\) at −40 °C and at 125 °C (automotive limits). By how much does a silicon diode's forward voltage change between 25 °C and 100 °C at constant current? easy

Show solution
\( U_T = kT/e \) with \(T\) in kelvin: at 233 K → \( U_T = 20.1\,\mathrm{mV} \); at 398 K → \( 34.3\,\mathrm{mV} \). Forward voltage: \( \Delta U_F \approx -2\,\mathrm{mV/K} \cdot 75\,\mathrm{K} = -150\,\mathrm{mV} \) — a "0.7 V" diode reads about 0.55 V when hot. Sanity check: this is why datasheet \(U_F\) values always state the test temperature, and why engine-bay electronics must be verified across the full range.

E2A silicon wafer is doped with boron at \(N_A = 10^{17}\,\mathrm{cm^{-3}}\). (a) What type is the material and what are the majority/minority densities? (b) What fraction of silicon atoms is a dopant? (c) What purity did the raw crystal need for undesired impurities to stay below 1 % of the doping? easy

Show solution
(a) Boron is a group-III acceptor → p-type. Majority: \( p \approx 10^{17}\,\mathrm{cm^{-3}} \); minority: \( n = n_i^2/p = 10^{20}/10^{17} = 10^{3}\,\mathrm{cm^{-3}} \).
(b) \( 10^{17} / (5\cdot10^{22}) = 1 : 500\,000 = 2\,\mathrm{ppm} \).
(c) Unwanted impurities < \(10^{15}\,\mathrm{cm^{-3}}\), i.e. < 1 atom in \(5\cdot10^{7}\) → purity better than 99.999 998 % — and for lightly doped power devices (\(N_D \sim 10^{14}\)) it must be ~1000× better still. That is the "eleven nines" business.

E3A diode (\(I_S = 10\,\mathrm{fA}\), \(n = 1\), 300 K) in series with \(R = 100\,\Omega\) hangs on \(U_q = 2\,\mathrm{V}\). Find the operating point by iteration, starting from \(U_D = 0.7\,\mathrm{V}\). medium

Show solution
Round 1: \( I = (2 - 0.7)/100 = 13\,\mathrm{mA} \); update \( U_D = U_T \ln(I/I_S) = 0.0259 \cdot \ln(1.3\cdot10^{12}) = 0.0259 \cdot 27.9 = 0.722\,\mathrm{V} \).
Round 2: \( I = (2 - 0.722)/100 = 12.78\,\mathrm{mA} \); update \( U_D = 0.0259\,\ln(1.278\cdot10^{12}) = 0.7216\,\mathrm{V} \).
Round 3 changes \(U_D\) by < 0.1 mV — converged: \( \boxed{I \approx 12.8\,\mathrm{mA},\; U_D \approx 0.72\,\mathrm{V}} \). Sanity check: the 0.7 V shortcut gives 13 mA — 1.7 % high. Iteration converges this fast because a 2 % current error shifts \(\ln I\) by only ~0.5 mV.

E4A 5.1 V Zener (min. regulating current 5 mA, max. power 1 W) must hold a rail from a 9 V ± 10 % source feeding a constant 30 mA load. Choose \(R_V\) and verify the Zener's power in the worst case. medium

Show solution
Starving case (8.1 V in): \( R_V \le \frac{8.1 - 5.1}{35\,\mathrm{mA}} = 85.7\,\Omega \) → choose \(R_V = 82\,\Omega\).
Heating case (9.9 V in): \( I_R = (9.9-5.1)/82 = 58.5\,\mathrm{mA} \); if the load ever disconnects, all of it enters the Zener: \( P_Z = 5.1 \cdot 0.0585 = 0.30\,\mathrm{W} \) < 1 W ✓ (with load attached: \(28.5\,\mathrm{mA} \cdot 5.1\,\mathrm{V} = 0.15\,\mathrm{W}\)). Remark: always check the load-disconnected case — shunt regulators dump the full series current into the diode when the load leaves.

E5A MOSFET has \(V_{th} = 2\,\mathrm{V}\), \(k = 0.5\,\mathrm{A/V^2}\), \(U_{GS} = 4\,\mathrm{V}\). Compute \(I_D\) for (a) \(U_{DS} = 1\,\mathrm{V}\) and (b) \(U_{DS} = 5\,\mathrm{V}\), identifying the region each time. (c) What is \(R_{DS(on)}\) for very small \(U_{DS}\)? medium

Show solution
Overdrive: \( U_{GS} - V_{th} = 2\,\mathrm{V} \).
(a) \( U_{DS} = 1 < 2 \) → triode: \( I_D = 0.5\,(2 \cdot 1 - 0.5) = 0.75\,\mathrm{A} \).
(b) \( U_{DS} = 5 \ge 2 \) → saturation: \( I_D = \tfrac{0.5}{2} \cdot 2^2 = 1.0\,\mathrm{A} \), independent of \(U_{DS}\) (to first order).
(c) \( R_{DS(on)} \approx \frac{1}{k (U_{GS}-V_{th})} = \frac{1}{0.5 \cdot 2} = 1\,\Omega \). Sanity check: at \(U_{DS}=0.1\) V the triode formula gives 97.5 mA ≈ 0.1 V / 1 Ω ✓. Driving the gate to 10 V would cut \(R_{DS(on)}\) to 0.25 Ω — gate drive voltage is loss money.

E6A silicon solar cell delivers photocurrent \(I_{ph} = 3\,\mathrm{A}\) under full sun; its junction has \(I_S = 1\,\mathrm{nA},\; n = 1\) at 300 K. (a) Open-circuit voltage? (b) At 10 % sun? (c) Why do "12 V" panels contain ~36 cells in series? challenge

Show solution
(a) \( U_{oc} = U_T \ln(I_{ph}/I_S + 1) = 0.0259 \cdot \ln(3\cdot10^{9}) = 0.0259 \cdot 21.8 \approx 0.565\,\mathrm{V} \).
(b) \( I_{ph} = 0.3\,\mathrm{A} \): \( U_{oc} = 0.0259 \cdot \ln(3\cdot10^{8}) = 0.505\,\mathrm{V} \) — 10× less light costs only ~60 mV (one decade × 60 mV/decade!). Voltage is robust; current carries the illumination information.
(c) The maximum-power voltage is ~0.45–0.5 V/cell; 36 cells give ~16–18 V, enough headroom to charge a 12 V lead-acid battery even warm (cells lose ~2 mV/K each — the diode law again). Series strings are mandatory because one junction can never exceed ~0.7 V.

E7Derive the "60 mV per decade" rule from the Shockley equation, then use it to explain quantitatively why the constant-0.7 V model works from 1 mA to 1 A for a diode with \(I_S = 10\,\mathrm{fA},\; n=1\). challenge

Show solution
For \(U \gg U_T\): \( U = n U_T \ln(I/I_S) \). Two currents a factor 10 apart differ by \( \Delta U = n U_T \ln 10 = 1 \cdot 25.9\,\mathrm{mV} \cdot 2.303 \approx 59.6\,\mathrm{mV} \) — the rule.
Endpoints: \( U(1\,\mathrm{mA}) = 0.0259\,\ln(10^{11}) = 0.656\,\mathrm{V} \); \( U(1\,\mathrm{A}) = 0.0259\,\ln(10^{14}) = 0.835\,\mathrm{V} \) (in practice higher still due to series resistance). Three decades of current fit inside 0.18 V, i.e. within ±13 % of 0.75 V. A model error of ±0.1 V in \(U_D\) changes a typical loop current \((U_q - U_D)/R\) by only a few percent when \(U_q \ge 5\) V — which is why the shortcut survives. Sanity check: this is the log-flatness that also made E3's iteration converge in two steps.
